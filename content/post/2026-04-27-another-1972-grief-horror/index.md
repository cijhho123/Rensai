A student in the Yomiyama North Middle School class 3-3 died mid-semester in 1972. His classmates refused to accept it. They kept his desk in place, kept calling his name during roll, kept treating him as though he were present. At graduation, the staff photographed the class and there he was — dead but somehow accounted for, present in the image.

This is where the curse begins.

Every year since, class 3-3 has suffered deaths: students, their family members, their teachers. Bodies accumulate through accidents that range from the banal to the spectacular. The only solution the class has found is the non-existence ritual — designating one student to be treated as though they do not exist, to balance the extra presence the dead student's ghost provides.

It is a horror about grief. Specifically, about the grief that will not let the dead be dead — and what that loving refusal costs everyone who comes after.

## The Act That Started It

Yukito Ayatsuji's novel *Another* (2009, adapted to anime by P.A. Works in 2012) is structured as a locked-room mystery in horror clothing. The curse is its central puzzle: what is it, where did it come from, how does it work, and how can it be stopped? The investigation proceeds across the series until the revelation that the "Another" — the ghost who integrates into the class roster each year — is someone who has forgotten they are dead. {{< citation >}}Ayatsuji, Yukito. *Another*. Kadokawa Bunko, 2009. Licensed in English by Yen Press (2013).{{< /citation >}}

But the origin story, the 1972 class and what they did, is the horror the puzzle rests on. The locked-room mystery can be solved — the curse can be stopped by forcing the Another to remember and accept their death. The origin cannot be undone.

What the 1972 class did was not malicious. It was the opposite: it was an act of love. A classmate died. They could not accept it. Rather than proceeding through the ordinary rituals of grief — acknowledging the absence, emptying the desk, adjusting the class photograph — they incorporated the absence into their ongoing life as if it were a presence. They spoke to him. They counted him. They kept his seat.

The horror premise of *Another* is that this worked.

Not metaphorically — literally. The collective refusal of a group of people to acknowledge a death generated, or attracted, or became a mechanism for, the ongoing incorporation of a dead person into the class each year as a living member who has forgotten their own death. The love that could not let go created a structural opening that death has been filling ever since. Every student who has died in class 3-3 from 1972 forward has died because of what those students chose, out of grief, to do.

## Grief as a Generative Act

The horror logic of *Another* is philosophically unusual. Most ghost stories work in one direction: a trauma or injustice in the past creates an ongoing supernatural presence. The mechanism is guilt, or revenge, or unfinished business. The dead return because they were wronged or because they wronged.

*Another*'s mechanism is different. There is no wrongdoing. The original Misaki who died in 1972 was not murdered, not betrayed, not left unavenged. He died, and the people who loved him could not accommodate that fact. Their love — specifically the form of love that expresses itself as refusal of loss — is the active ingredient. The curse is not punishment for a crime. It is the consequence of an ordinary emotional failure: the failure to let the dead be dead.

This gives *Another* a horror logic that is more disturbing than revenge or guilt, because it implicates something universal. Everyone who has loved someone who died has, at some point, refused to accept it. The impulse the 1972 class acted on is not pathological. It is the impulse that leads people to speak to the absent, to keep photographs out, to maintain routines as though the missing person might return. What the series' premise asks is: what if that impulse had traction? What if the world bent to accommodate it?

The answer, in Yomiyama, is ongoing catastrophe.

## The Logic of the Counter-Ritual

The ritual response — designating one student as non-existent — is the series' most discussed element, because it is so viscerally disturbing. A student is selected, often Mei Misaki by default of her social isolation, and the class agrees collectively to treat her as though she does not exist. Teachers do not call on her. Students do not speak to or acknowledge her. She is present but functionally erased.

This is, as the series itself eventually acknowledges, a reproduction of exactly the logic that created the curse. The 1972 class maintained a dead person in the living roster. The response ritual maintains a living person in the dead roster — or rather, maintains a living person in a state of socially enforced non-existence. Both practices involve a collective decision to override the obvious truth of someone's presence status. Both practices work by consensus. {{< citation >}}The parallel between the origin act (treating the dead as living) and the ritual response (treating the living as non-existent) is the series' core ethical problem. Ayatsuji's novel handles this with more ambiguity than the anime adaptation, which tends to rush toward the horror spectacle of the deaths themselves.{{< /citation >}}

The series never quite confronts this symmetry explicitly — it is embedded in the architecture rather than stated by any character. But it is there. The tool the class developed to address the grief that couldn't let go is itself a grief-like act: the refusal to acknowledge someone's presence, maintained collectively, as a social reality the individual has no power to override. The person designated non-existent is, from the class's operational perspective, dead.

## What It Costs to Keep Someone

The annual death toll exists at the series' edges as a kind of accumulating invoice. Each year the ritual fails or is not established in time, people die. Some years it fails for long enough that the deaths are numerous. The students and teachers and family members who die across the series' run are, in aggregate, paying for something that happened before they were born — for the love of a class in 1972 that refused to let a dead student go.

This is unusual as a horror premise because the entity responsible for the deaths is not a malevolent force. It is a form of love. The original 1972 class was not wrong to love their classmate. They were wrong to express that love in a way that required the world to accommodate what the world cannot accommodate: the ongoing presence of someone who is not present.

The cost of their choice is distributed across decades and people who had no part in making it. Students born in the late 1990s are dying because of what a class decided in 1972. Their deaths are not punishment for anything they did; they are the ongoing maintenance cost of a decision made before they were alive.

*Another* does not editorialize about this. The series presents the mechanics with the neutrality of a mystery novel setting out its rules. But the logic accumulates: the series is about what it costs to be in proximity to a love that will not accept its limit.

## The Photograph

The detail Ayatsuji preserves from the origin story — the graduation photograph in which the dead student Misaki appears — is the series' most economical horror image. {{< citation >}}The photograph motif appears in Ayatsuji, *Another*, chapter 1 ("Introduction"). It recurs in the 2012 P.A. Works anime adaptation, Episode 1, as the hook image that establishes the premise.{{< /citation >}}

A photograph is already a form of grief refusal. Every photograph of a dead person is a document of someone the world no longer contains. What the 1972 graduation photograph adds is the extra weight that the image includes someone who was, at the moment of its taking, not physically present — and yet is legible in the image as present. The photograph does not lie, or rather it cannot: it records what the camera could see, and the camera could see Misaki.

Whether this is read as supernatural (his ghost was literally visible) or psychological (the class's insistence produced a collective hallucination documented in the image) is less important than what the photograph communicates as a horror object. It is evidence that their love worked. It is also evidence that the world became worse because of it. The photograph that should have been a graduation memory became a document of the first instance of the curse's mechanism. It is the most beautiful and most damning thing the series imagines: love so total that it shows up in a photograph.

## The Limitation of Love

*Another* is often categorized as pure horror — slasher deaths, Final Destination-style accidents, a body count that escalates efficiently. This reading is not wrong about the surface. The deaths are engineered for maximum shock and often for dark comedy about the specificity of their arbitrariness.

But the series' core horror is quieter and lives in the 1972 origin. The premise is a meditation on a limitation that love in popular narrative is rarely asked to acknowledge: that love cannot override the fact of death. More than that — that love which tries to override the fact of death does not fail gracefully. It does not simply fail to keep the dead person present. It succeeds in keeping something where the dead person was, and that something exacts an ongoing cost from everyone around it.

The 1972 class loved Misaki, and because they did, students in the same classroom three decades later died in preventable accidents. Their love was real. The cost of that love was paid by people who had nothing to do with it. This is the horror that the deaths and the rituals and the final revelation are all in service of, even when the series loses track of it in favor of spectacular violence.

What *Another* believes about grief is this: grief that refuses its own limit is not a private matter. It does not stay inside the person feeling it. It spills into the world and creates conditions that others are required to survive.

The 1972 class held on to someone who was gone. Everything that followed is the price.
