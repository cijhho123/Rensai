The production brief for the JoJo's Bizarre Adventure anime, as producer Hiroyuki Omori put it to director Naokatsu Tsuda, was: ["Make JoJo."](https://jojo-portal.com/special/production-note/en/02/)

Two words. No elaboration. That's either the most useful directive a director has ever received or an invitation to catastrophic misunderstanding, depending entirely on whether you know what JoJo actually is.

[Two previous attempts had already failed to answer that question correctly](https://www.avclub.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-anime-history-manga-adaptation-davidpro). The A.P.P.P. OVAs from the 1990s-2000s adapted Stardust Crusaders but cut swaths of story and removed characters — producing a competent action anime that happened to use JoJo's character designs rather than something that felt like JoJo. The 2007 Phantom Blood film, attempting to compress the arc into 90 minutes, was pulled after limited screenings. [Shueisha, the manga's publisher, seized all materials and blocked any DVD release](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2020-08-03/the-lost-jojo-movie/.160477) — making it [effectively lost media](https://lostmediawiki.com/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure:_Phantom_Blood_%28partially_found_anime_film_based_on_manga;_2007%29), a circumstance without real parallel in major anime history.

David Production got "Make JoJo" right. The 2012 anime is now the baseline against which everything in the franchise is measured. Understanding why requires understanding what the question actually was.

## The 25-Year Gap

Before October 6, 2012, [JoJo's Bizarre Adventure was the longest Shueisha manga series without a television anime adaptation](https://jojowiki.com/JoJo's_Bizarre_Adventure:_The_Animation). The manga had launched in [Weekly Shonen Jump in 1987](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure) — the same year as Dragon Ball's final arc. By the time David Production's adaptation premiered, Araki had been running the story for 25 years across eight separate parts, with over 100 collected volumes in print.

That gap wasn't accidental. The OVAs existed. The film existed. But none of it captured the series correctly enough to justify a full television adaptation. The industry-level answer — "it's too expensive, too long, too weird" — was real but incomplete. The deeper answer was that nobody had correctly identified what kind of problem a JoJo adaptation is.

JoJo is not a standard shonen battle manga in a distinctive aesthetic costume. It is, as Tsuda later described it, [closer to professional wrestling or Kabuki theater](https://jojo-portal.com/special/production-note/en/02/) than to conventional anime action. The pleasure is spectacle, theatricality, the "groove" that emerges when timing, character voice, pose, and music hit simultaneously. You can faithfully adapt every plot point in Phantom Blood and produce something inert if you miss that groove.

## What the OVA Got Wrong

The A.P.P.P. Stardust Crusaders OVAs are not bad, strictly speaking. [They feature decent animation for their era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure_(OVA)) and capture some of the Stand battles reasonably well. What they lack is the specific quality that makes JoJo JoJo: the sense that the story is being performed rather than simply depicted.

Araki's manga is operatic in a way that has almost no parallel in shonen publishing. Characters strike poses — real, sustained poses, held for several panels — not as character tics but as theatrical statements. Dialogue is delivered as monologue even when it's technically conversation. Villains announce themselves; heroes commit. The visual language is designed around impact frames and dramatic contrast rather than the flowing action lines that define most manga of the era.

The OVA attempts to translate this into conventional animation and loses the quality in the process. Conventional animation wants movement. JoJo wants stillness held at the exact right moment, then explosion. The rhythm is different, and the OVA doesn't find it.

## "Study JoJo Scientifically"

Tsuda's response to Omori's mandate was to [adopt his own internal slogan: "Study JoJo scientifically."](https://jojo-portal.com/special/production-note/en/02/) This meant cataloging the specific elements that distinguish a JoJo panel from any other manga panel — the sound effects, the poses, the way dialogue is staged — and then identifying how to reproduce those qualities in animation rather than simply animating the content.

The specific solutions this produced are worth examining.

First, the **onomatopoeia**. Araki's manga famously rendered sound effects as large, stylized text integrated into the panels themselves — *DODODODO*, *GOGOGOGO*, *WRYYYY*. Previous adaptations had treated these as content to be dubbed over or subtitled. David Production kept them on screen as animated text elements, acknowledging that they were part of the visual language, not just information. When the dubbing and translation team eventually rendered ["MENACING" for the ominous hovering text above Dio](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/jojos-bizarre-adventure-menacing), it worked because the fundamental decision to keep the text as a visual component had already been made.

Second, the **color shifts**. Araki had never used fixed character color schemes across the manga's run. His color pages and cover illustrations changed the characters' palettes freely, not from inconsistency but from [deliberate intent to create different emotional registers through color](https://screenrant.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-jjba-anime-look-explained-unique/). David Production formalized this into "special scene coloring" — moments in which the color palette shifts entirely to mark an emotional peak or story climax. The effect is immediately recognizable and has become one of the adaptation's most celebrated visual decisions.

Third, and most important: **the music**.

## The Roundabout Question

[Hirohiko Araki wrote the original Phantom Blood arc](https://jojowiki.com/MTV_BACK_TO_80's_(March_2017)) while listening to Western rock music — the same music that runs through the DNA of the whole JoJo franchise in the form of character names based on bands and songs (Dio, Zeppelin, ACϟDC, Giorno Giovanna). The series was conceived in a specific cultural moment: 1980s Japan, when Western rock arrived with force, and Araki absorbed it.

[Choosing "Roundabout" by Yes as the first season's ending theme](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/yes-roundabout-to-be-continued) was not a random decision. Yes is exactly the kind of progressive rock that shaped Araki's musical vocabulary. And the specific quality of "Roundabout" — the acoustic guitar intro that appears at the precise moment a freeze frame locks and the "To Be Continued" arrow appears — turned out to be accidentally perfect for the series' cliffhanger structure.

The meme that followed was genuinely organic. The earliest precursor circulated [while Phantom Blood was still airing](https://gizmodo.com/to-be-continued-is-the-sleeper-meme-hit-of-the-summer-1781860391), borrowing the JoJo outro elements to draw a parallel with another show's cliffhanger. From there, the format spread: freeze frame at a moment of impending disaster, the Roundabout intro hit, the arrow lands. By 2016, it was one of the most recognized meme templates internationally — driven entirely by a 70-second clip from an ending sequence.

This is where the "Make JoJo" mandate reveals its full depth. The OVAs understood JoJo as a battle manga. The 2007 film understood it as a compressed narrative. David Production understood it as a work with a cultural identity — one that was partly Western before it was animated. Using Western music in the anime was not an experiment. It was a recognition that [JoJo had always been in conversation with Western culture](https://www.cbr.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-classic-rock/), and that a faithful adaptation had to acknowledge that conversation rather than filter it out. The meme's organic spread was the confirmation: millions of people recognized something true in that ending, and the recognition traveled because the music belonged there.

## What the 2012 Anime Made Possible

The international impact of the 2012 anime is not hard to document. [Several critics have credited it with the surge of Western franchise popularity](https://www.avclub.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-anime-history-manga-adaptation-davidpro) that continues today. [IMDB users rate the first season at 8.5](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt2359704/), and the franchise's [MAL score sits above 8.0](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/jojos-bizarre-adventure-2012) consistently across parts — numbers that almost certainly understate the series' influence, since its memes and poses circulate far beyond the viewing base.

But the more specific achievement is what the 2012 anime made possible for David Production itself. Because the first season was received as genuinely faithful — not just adequate — every subsequent part inherited that credibility. Each new story arc required David Production to reinvent the visual vocabulary for a new era, new characters, new tone — and they could do that because the original contract with the audience had been honored.

The [JoJo Wiki's production notes](https://jojo-portal.com/special/production-note/en/02/) describe the team's approach as combining "thorough research of the original work with additional design and staging intended to reflect the 'JoJo' that exists in fans' impressions." That's a careful and accurate description of what adaptation always requires: not transcribing the source but honoring the specific quality that made people care about it in the first place.

It took 25 years and three failed attempts. The solution, when it arrived, was the simplest possible answer to the question. It just took knowing what the question was.
