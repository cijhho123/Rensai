The Third Impact begins. The collective AT Field of humanity collapses inward. Billions of souls dissolve into LCL. And the song that plays over all of it is so catchy you might tap your foot.

That's not an accident. "Komm, süsser Tod" is not just a striking musical choice — it is a precision instrument built from three distinct layers, each doing different work, and the interaction between them is where the film's most unsettling meaning lives. To understand what Hideaki Anno was actually doing with this song, you need to take it apart.

## Layer One: The Beatles Problem

Anno originally wanted ["Hey Jude"](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod) to play during Third Impact. He didn't get it — a Beatles license in 1997 was prohibitively expensive. So he gave the problem to composer [Shiro Sagisu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiro_Sagisu): make something with the same emotional architecture.

This is not speculation. The structural resemblance between "Komm, süsser Tod" and the back half of "Hey Jude" is documented enough that fans have mapped them directly. Both songs build to a sustained communal section — the na-na-nas in Lennon's version, the cycling refrain in Sagisu's — designed to dissolve the boundary between performer and audience. The "Hey Jude" ending is specifically famous for being a participation trap: it sounds so simple and welcoming that listeners find themselves singing without meaning to.

Anno wanted that mechanism. The choice of "Hey Jude" wasn't random nostalgia — it was chosen because that song is about exactly what Instrumentality promises. Take a sad song and make it better. Abandon the self, join the chorus, feel the comfort of the collective.

Sagisu built the analog. The result is a song in a major key, with a light pop arrangement, that sounds — on a completely surface listen — like something you'd hear at a school recital.

## Layer Two: What Anno Actually Wrote

The original Japanese lyrics, which Anno wrote himself and titled *Amaki shi yo, kitare* ("Come, sweet death"), are nothing like the English version most audiences heard.

Where the English adaptation by Mike Wyzgowski abstracts the content into a kind of melancholy but portable sadness, Anno's Japanese source text is [raw and specific](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod). First person. Present tense. It reads as a direct transcript of the emotional logic of clinical depression: the fear of being a burden, the certainty that you've already hurt everyone who mattered, the conclusion that withdrawal — or extinction — is the only ethical move. "I've already hurt the people who matter most to me. That's why I can't let myself like other people. That's why I hurt myself."

Anno was in and out of severe depression throughout the production of *Neon Genesis Evangelion*. [His studio biography](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-06-19/hideaki-anno-after-evangelion/.148019) notes that after the series finished airing, he broke down. These lyrics are not character work written at a remove — they are a record of a specific psychological state, dressed up in a song structure that makes them easy to miss.

Wyzgowski's English adaptation keeps the theme — dissolution, death, the appeal of nothingness — but loses the confession. "I have no one left. I have nowhere to go." That's in both versions. But the original's specificity about self-harm, about having hurt the people who mattered, doesn't survive the translation intact. What enters Arianne's mouth for the film is already one step removed from what Anno put on paper.

## Layer Three: The Teenager Who Didn't Know

[Arianne Schreiber](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod) was a South African-born singer based in England when she was brought to Japan through composer Rob Davis and Shiro Sagisu. She spent three days recording. She had never seen *Evangelion*. She recorded the song without knowing what it was for.

This is worth sitting with. The voice carrying Anno's most personal text through the Third Impact belonged to someone who had no relationship to the material — a teenager performing unfamiliar English lyrics in a foreign country, with no access to the context that made the words matter.

![The End of Evangelion — 1997 theatrical poster](https://image.tmdb.org/t/p/w500/j6G24dqI4WgUtChhWjfnI4lnmiK.jpg)

The result is a performance of strange, clean sincerity. Arianne sings it like a pop song. She doesn't load it with the weight of what it means. And paradoxically, that's exactly right — because the film isn't asking you to feel the weight of the lyrics. It's asking you not to notice them.

## The Mechanism

Here is what "Komm, süsser Tod" actually does during the Third Impact sequence: it makes you complicit.

The melody is catchy enough that you may start tracking it. It sounds familiar — that Beatles structure Sagisu built in. As you lean into the song, as you start to follow it, you're doing a version of what the characters onscreen are doing. You're surrendering your critical distance. You're letting the individual self dissolve into the collective experience of the music. You stop reading and start swimming.

This is not a metaphor. Singing along to "Komm, süsser Tod" — and people do, even people who know what they're doing — is a small enactment of what the film is describing. You give up your individuality. You become a singer instead of a listener. The boundary between you and the song dissolves. Instrumentality isn't just happening on screen. It's being modeled in the audience's bodies.

This is the same trick Anno pulls elsewhere in the film — Beethoven's Ode to Joy plays over a massacre, a piece of music so loaded with connotations of collective triumph that it reprogrammes how you feel about what you're watching. The music doesn't comment on the image; it absorbs it.

## The Title It Borrows

The German title, "Komm, süsser Tod," comes from a 1736 [Johann Sebastian Bach composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Komm,_s%C3%BC%C3%9Fer_Tod) of the same name — a Lutheran chorale about the sweetness of death as relief from earthly suffering. Anno borrowed the name precisely. His song is doing the same theological work as Bach's: making death feel desirable, even comforting, by making it sound beautiful.

The rest of the [*End of Evangelion* score](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_(Soundtrack)) operates in the same register. Sagisu and Anno also use Bach's "Air on the G String" and "Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring" — pieces historically associated with death, last rites, and the peace that lies beyond individual suffering. The classical vocabulary of the film is entirely about release from selfhood. "Komm, süsser Tod" is the pop vernacular version of the same theology, designed to reach audiences who wouldn't recognize Bach but would still feel the pull.

The difference between Anno's version and Bach's is that Anno's is specifically about the death of the self in psychological terms — not bodily death but the dissolution of the isolated ego into something larger. Instrumentality is a theological concept rendered in anime, and the soundtrack borrows from the actual liturgy of surrender. That the [soundtrack peaked at number 3 on Japan's Oricon charts](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2008-06-10/1st-anime-album-in-11-years-to-rank-in-japan-top-3) — remaining the last anime album to do so for eleven years — tells you how effectively it disguised what it was doing. People bought it. They listened to it at home. The camouflage worked.

## What Remains

The reason "Komm, süsser Tod" has stayed with people for nearly thirty years isn't just that it's a good song. It's that it works on multiple frequencies simultaneously. Casual listeners get the catchy hook. Closer listeners get the disturbing lyrical content. Careful listeners get Anno's original confession underneath. And everyone, regardless of which layer they're on, ends up participating in the same mechanism — the surrender to the music that mirrors the surrender to Instrumentality.

It was almost "Hey Jude." The Beatles license was too expensive. What Anno built in its place turned out to be more intimate and more precise — because it was built from something he actually understood from the inside.

Whether Instrumentality is a horror or a release depends on where you're standing when the music starts. The song doesn't tell you which. It just makes you want to stay in it.
