On January 22, 2020, two Japanese idol groups debuted simultaneously under the same agency on the same physical CD. One side of the disc belonged to Snow Man; the other belonged to [SixTONES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SixTones). The A-side track was "Imitation Rain," and it was produced not by an in-house Johnny's producer but by [Yoshiki of X Japan](https://www.billboard.com/articles/news/international/8549736/5-things-j-pop-group-sixtones) — a composer and musician whose global credibility in rock music has no equal within J-pop's mainstream. By the time that single's first-day sales were tallied, SixTONES had moved [772,902 copies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SixTones), crossing one million within days, and landing at number one on the Oricon chart.

The numbers are impressive but they're not the point. The point is what that debut *said*: that SixTONES was going to be positioned differently from every idol group in the building.

![SixTONES debut single Imitation Rain](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/56/Sixtones_vs_snowman_single.jpg)

## The Versus That Wasn't a Battle

The "SixTONES vs Snow Man" double debut has been interpreted as a competition — two groups fighting for fan attention on the same release date. [Fan discourse across forums positioned them as complementary rivals](https://www.theidolcast.com/podcasteps/episode-23-sixtones-and-snowman), with SixTONES occupying the rock-vocal lane and Snow Man the dance-performance lane, comparing the dynamic to "the Rolling Stones to the Beatles." The framing of "versus" is interesting because it invites comparison while obscuring what the agency was actually doing, which was hedging its bets by releasing two completely different product categories simultaneously into the same market.

SixTONES got Yoshiki. Snow Man got a polished mainstream idol track. Johnny & Associates was offering one choice to fans who wanted artistic credibility and another to fans who wanted spectacle — and by branding the release as a competition rather than a bifurcation, the agency turned the difference into drama. Both groups benefited from the other's existence. The rivalry framing generated discourse that a straightforward solo debut wouldn't have.

But even within that calculated structure, something genuinely uncommon happened. SixTONES actually earned their rock positioning.

## Where the Credibility Came From

[SixTONES formed in 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SixTones), but the six members had been working together since the 2012 drama *Shiritsu Bakaleya Koukou*, where they played lead delinquent roles and [built a following informally called "the Bakaleya Boys"](https://blog.fromjapan.co.jp/en/music/sixtones-and-snow-man-enter-the-reiwa-era-of-johnnys.html). By the time they became an official unit, [they already had chemistry](https://www.theidolcast.com/podcasteps/episode-23-sixtones-and-snowman) and a reputation — not the blank-slate trainee image that new idol groups typically project.

The musical credibility wasn't just Yoshiki's reflected glory, either. A [January 2021 feature in Talking Rock!](https://6ix-neiro.livejournal.com/11859.html) — a magazine with no interest in flattering idol groups — showed what the members were actually listening to: Kyomoto Taiga citing Mr. Children, RADWIMPS, and The Pillows from genuine childhood exposure to Japanese rock bands; Tanaka Juri moving from hip-hop (Hannya, Zeebra) into ONE OK ROCK and coldrain; Jesse drawing on Bon Jovi alongside Michael Jackson and Eminem; Morimoto Shintaro gravitating toward Western music by ear rather than brand. These aren't carefully curated names dropped for credibility. The eclecticism is genuine, which is exactly what makes it land.

The A&R strategy, [described in detail in an internal interview translated by fan community j2translatones](https://j2translatones.livejournal.com/40591.html), was built around that authenticity. Black-and-white debut photography emphasized the members' silhouettes over conventional idol aesthetics. The musical approach evolved across releases — "Imitation Rain" established rock ballad credibility, ["NAVIGATOR" (an anime tie-in for *The Millionaire Detective Balance: Unlimited*)](https://aramajapan.com/news/music/sixtones-officially-releases-japonica-style-music-video-on-youtube/92783/) released without advance notice to test fan engagement, while more experimental tracks were embedded deep in albums for listeners who would find them. The philosophy was differentiation first: from Snow Man, from senior Johnny's acts, from the idol template itself.

[The agency gave them unusual creative autonomy](https://6ix-neiro.livejournal.com/11859.html): most coupling songs and album tracks were selected by the members themselves, with Sony staff providing feedback rather than direction. The result was what the Talking Rock feature described — a group that treated themselves as "perverts towards music," willing to explore all-English tracks and vocaloid-influenced material within an idol framework that traditionally allows almost none of that.

## What Yoshiki Actually Understood

The decision to hand "Imitation Rain" to Yoshiki deserves its own examination. [According to Sony's A&R](https://j2translatones.livejournal.com/40591.html), the intention was to let Yoshiki interpret SixTONES and present his vision of what they were — not to have him produce a track to their specifications. The result was a rock ballad that the A&R specifically noted "didn't feel the typical Johnny-ness," which in context is a form of praise.

Yoshiki had an insight: the group's strength was vocal. [SixTONES are a singing-focused group in a context where dance often dominates idol aesthetics](https://www.theidolcast.com/podcasteps/episode-23-sixtones-and-snowman). "Imitation Rain" — an orchestral rock ballad that builds to a cinematic crescendo — is designed to be sung, not danced. The debut announced their identity through what it asked of them, not just what it told audiences about them.

[The Billboard Japan coverage](https://www.billboard.com/articles/news/international/8549736/5-things-j-pop-group-sixtones) framed it as J-pop modernization — a group that used a rock icon to reposition what idol credentials could look like. Yoshiki's own statement at the time reportedly included the observation that SixTONES could "renew the image of J-Pop overseas," which may have been promotional language, but it reflected the genuine ambition behind the collaboration.

## Surviving the Collapse

In September 2023, the BBC documentary "Predator: The Secret Scandal of J-Pop" broke open the story of [Johnny Kitagawa's decades of sexual abuse of young male performers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_Kitagawa_sexual_abuse_scandal). [An independent probe established that Kitagawa committed abuse repeatedly from the early 1970s until the mid-2010s, with over 470 people coming forward](https://www.nippon.com/en/japan-topics/g02475/). The scandal was catastrophic for Johnny & Associates as an institution — within months, the agency had announced it would restructure into two separate entities: Smile-Up (handling victim compensation) and [Starto Entertainment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starto_Entertainment) (taking over talent management). By April 2024, [Starto had signed contracts with 295 individuals across 28 groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starto_Entertainment).

SixTONES transitioned to Starto. What is notable about that transition is what it didn't disrupt: the group's identity and trajectory. The [Starto concert series in April 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starto_Entertainment) featured SixTONES alongside every major act from the former agency, but by that point SixTONES had a musical identity independent of the "Johnny's" brand. Their discography, their Talking Rock credibility, their Sony deal — none of that was contingent on the agency name.

This is what separates groups with genuine identity from those that exist only within their institutional framework. When the institution collapsed, SixTONES continued. They had built something that was theirs.

## What the "Unpolished Stones" Metaphor Reveals

The name is explained as referring to [six unpolished stones, each with distinct personality and sound, refined through work](https://kprofiles.com/sixtones-members-profile/). This is a standard idol metaphor — the becoming, the process, the gradual polish — but SixTONES deployed it in a context that gave it unusual weight. Their pre-debut years in the Bakaleya drama, their long junior period, their creative arguments with themselves about musical direction — all of it backs the claim in a way that a group invented from scratch two years before their debut cannot.

The idol industry runs on the fiction of becoming. What SixTONES did differently was make the fiction legible — verifiable by the paper trail of Talking Rock interviews, of music choices made under their own authority, of a Yoshiki who read them correctly and handed them something they could actually inhabit.

Six years after their debut and three years after the agency that raised them became a liability, SixTONES remain one of the clearest examples of what the Japanese idol industry looks like when it allows artistic identity to precede institutional requirement.
