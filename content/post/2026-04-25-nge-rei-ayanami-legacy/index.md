Before the Neon Genesis Evangelion broadcast had even finished its run, [shops in Akihabara were struggling to keep Rei Ayanami figurines in stock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rei_Ayanami). This was unprecedented — Evangelion was a mecha anime, a genre where the robots are the product, where plastic robots fill the store shelves and drive the business. But Rei's action figures exceeded the series' robot sales, [creating what became known as the first and perhaps only case of a robotic anime "where reproductions of the human characters outsold those of the robots."](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Rei_Ayanami) Japanese media began calling her "the girl who manipulates magazine sales at will" and "the fastest route to the sold-out sign." [Over 600 different items](https://www.popmatters.com/71108-on-evas-and-angels-postmodern-fantasy-devotion-to-neon-genesis-evange-2496051663) were released to commemorate the series, and the inimitable bandaged Rei outsold them all.

Anno had designed her to be a disturbing presence. She became the most commercially processed character of her era.

![Rei Ayanami continues to generate merchandise decades after her creation — a fact that would have surprised the character's creators, who designed her to be unsettling rather than appealing](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/rei-ayanami-figure-1.jpg)

## The Design That Wasn't Supposed to Sell

Hideaki Anno's instructions to character designer [Yoshiyuki Sadamoto](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2013-09-19/interview-evangelion-character-designer-yoshiyuki-sadamoto) were precise and pointed in the opposite direction of commercial appeal: Rei should be painted in as "a bitterly unhappy young girl with little sense of presence." Anno conceived her as ["the unconscious Shinji"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:Rei_Ayanami_(In-Depth)) — a representation of his protagonist's repressed unconscious, something fundamentally alien to recognizable human personality.

Sadamoto's articulation of his own intent was equally telling. He designed Rei as the "Yin opposed to Asuka" — where Asuka was an "idol" figure, bright and aggressive and readable, Rei was her inversion. He described the finished character as ["locked in as translucent, like a shadow or the air. The kind of girl you can't touch. The girl you long for, but there is nothing about her that you can grab a hold onto."](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Rei_Ayanami)

"The kind of girl you can't touch." That's the design brief, offered by the person who drew her. She was intended to be unreachable — not appealing in the usual sense, but discomfiting. Not a character you would want to merchandise.

Anno later acknowledged Rei's popularity, connecting it to a cultural pathology rather than treating it as flattery. He suggested her appeal ["stems from a cultural deficit"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Rei_Ayanami_(In-Depth)) in Japanese society — "there's no reasonable model of what an adult should be like" — a reading of Rei's fandom as symptomatic of social disconnection rather than genuine affection for a well-realized character.

## What the Market Did With the Critique

The [Animage magazine survey for 1996](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rei_Ayanami) elected Rei the best female character in anime. She remained at the top of similar surveys into 1997. [Her merchandise made $400 million](https://screenrant.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-influence-impact-anime/) in the first two years alongside the series' other revenue. A dedicated art book, *Evangelion Photograph – Rei*, was published in 1997 for a character Anno had struggled to feel close to and who, as he later admitted, he had sometimes "forgotten to explore" during production because he didn't relate to her.

The audience had no such difficulty relating. They related intensely. They related in the one way that commodity culture knows how to measure: they bought.

Hiroki Azuma, in [*Otaku: Japan's Database Animals*](https://www.amazon.com/Otaku-Database-Animals-Hiroki-Azuma/dp/0816653526) ([published 2001, translated 2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otaku:_Japan%27s_Database_Animals)) — the most significant theoretical treatment of how otaku consumption shifted in the post-Evangelion era — provides the analytical framework that explains why this happened. Azuma argues that Evangelion marked a decisive shift from "narrative consumption" to "database consumption" in otaku culture. Where earlier fandoms consumed stories and built secondary narratives in extension of those stories (Gundam's fictional universe being the model), the Evangelion fandom consumed *elements* — discrete components of a character that could be extracted, recombined, and reproduced independently of any specific narrative.

Rei's "moe elements," in Azuma's framework: [pale skin, blue hair, red eyes, quiet personality, flat affect, mysterious presence](https://medium.com/read-event-horizon/explainer-2-hiroki-azuma-and-the-database-animal-2716b8e6ecd5). These could be lifted out of the context that gave them their specific meaning — the post-WWII Japanese cultural deficit Anno was diagnosing, the specific psychological wound that produced Rei's blankness — and reassembled into new configurations. [Azuma's summary is clinical: Rei's emergence "did not influence many authors so much as change the rules of the moe elements sustaining otaku culture."](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rei_Ayanami)

She didn't inspire imitators. She changed the grammar.

## The Expy as Industry Mechanism

[TV Tropes' entry on the "Rei Ayanami Expy"](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/ReiAyanamiExpy) is extensive — a formal taxonomy of a character archetype so thoroughly proliferated that it now requires its own critical literature. The template: quiet, pale, blue (or white or silver) hair, flat affect, emotionally inaccessible, often with mysterious origins or non-human elements, and a blank-slate quality that invites projection. The characters who follow this template include, most prominently, [Yuki Nagato from *The Melancholy of Haruhi Suzumiya*](https://gamerant.com/characters-based-on-neon-genesis-evangelion-rei-ayanami-anime/), Ruri Hoshino from *Martian Successor Nadesico*, and the countless lighter commercial copies across visual novels, light novels, and late-night anime — where the blue-pale-quiet combination became a standardized character slot.

The character trait Rei popularized is "mukuchi" — roughly "mouthless," referring to characters who speak minimally and display no legible emotion. [Critics and scholars of moe culture](https://jmpc-utokyo.com/keyword/moe/) identify Rei as the prototypical mukuchi character and point to the proliferation of this element in subsequent anime as evidence of how thoroughly her "database element" entered the standard vocabulary of character design. The Japanese term for imitator characters in this mode is "Ayanami type" — the fact that an entire character archetype in a living fandom is named after a specific person is a measure of how thoroughly the template displaced the original.

What the Rei Ayanami Expy is *not*, in most of its subsequent iterations, is the thing Rei herself was. Anno's Rei is specifically unsettling because she exists in a context that frames her blankness as damage — she is replaceable, she knows she is replaceable, she "doesn't value her life very highly" because she is aware that her death is simply an inconvenience that Gendo will address by activating the next version. Her unreachable quality is the product of a character who has been treated as an object for so long she has internalized it. The horror of Rei is that the blankness is accurate. There is nothing to grab onto because Gendo made sure there would be nothing.

Strip that context — the medical pods, the clones, the absent self — and what you have left is the surface: pale, quiet, blue-haired. Aesthetically appealing in the precise register the original was designed to make discomfiting. The imitators kept the look and discarded the wound.

![The End of Evangelion (1997) — Anno's response to fan rage at the original ending brought Rei back as the central figure of the Instrumentality sequence, again as vessel, again as projection surface](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/The-End-of-Evangelion-movie.jpg)

## The Feedback Loop

There's a moment in the mythology of Rei's reception that makes the full circuit visible. In 1997, Anno released *The End of Evangelion* as a response to [fan hostility toward the series' original ending](https://www.cbr.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-other-anime-ran-out-of-budget-quality-decline/). Fans had sent him death threats and vandalized the Gainax offices with messages threatening violence. What they wanted was more of the product — resolution, spectacle, commercial satisfaction.

What Anno gave them, among other things, was a Rei who becomes the vessel for the Third Impact, who absorbs Lilith, who offers all of humanity the choice of dissolution into collective being. The girl who was designed to have no selfhood of her own becomes the instrument through which selfhood itself is offered up. The ultimate blank slate, finally offered a function.

The industry's response to *End of Evangelion* and Rei's function in it was not to engage with its critique of the desire for absence. It was to produce more merchandise. More Rei figures. More pale-haired imitators in more series. The [proliferation of the Rei template in the late 1990s and 2000s](https://the-artifice.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-rei-ayanami-legacy/) is a document of an industry that found in her the most commercially reliable version of a specific desire and proceeded to manufacture that desire at scale.

Anno eventually registered this. [Across the Rebuild of Evangelion films (2007–2021)](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/18658/Did-Anno-Seek-to-Undermine-Rei-with-Rebuild/), he repeatedly destabilizes Rei's function as the archetype — giving her moments of genuine personality (the dinner scene in *2.0*, her desire to connect with Shinji), then resetting her to blank in *3.0*, then, in the final film, allowing the character to dissolve entirely into the earth and return to nature. The discourse around this reads as [Anno deliberately trying to make the archetype unappealing](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Rei_Ayanami/Rebuild) — to close the door on the commercial Rei. The merchandise kept selling anyway.

Sadamoto said she was "the kind of girl you can't touch." The industry heard that as a design specification and built a production line around it.

## What This Means

Anno's reading of Rei's popularity — that it reflects a cultural pathology, a failure of adult models in Japanese society — may be correct. It may also be an author's ex post facto rationalization of a character he initially struggled to understand. [He admitted](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff) he forgot to develop her for much of production; she was less designed than she was left open, and the audience filled her with what they brought.

This is, in its way, Azuma's point about the database: the "moe element" functions precisely because it does not insist on itself. The blank surface is not absence — it is an invitation. Rei works commercially not despite having nothing to grab onto but because of it. The less she insists on being a specific person, the more space there is for any person.

Anno wanted that to be troubling. He wasn't wrong that it should be. But wanting something to be troubling and making it so are different problems, and the industry, reading the cash registers, concluded correctly that it was not.

The girl who wasn't supposed to sell anything sold everything. The archetype designed to make audiences uncomfortable with their own desire became the foundational unit of a production system built to manufacture and service that desire. Rei Ayanami is still being produced, in her own figure forms and in the blue-haired, flat-affect daughters that trail behind her in the catalog. The irony is structural: the critique became the template because the critique was accurate.
