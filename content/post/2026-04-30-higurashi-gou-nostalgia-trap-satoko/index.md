In October 2020, two weeks into the airing of what everyone had been told was a remake of Higurashi: When They Cry, Hanyuu appeared on screen. Viewers who remembered the original recognized the problem immediately: Hanyuu doesn't show up until *Higurashi Kai*, the second cour, which aired in 2007. She has no business being in the first episode of what is supposed to be a faithful retelling of the original 2006 series.

That moment of disorientation — the sense of watching something familiar suddenly wrong — is the whole thesis.

![Higurashi: When They Cry – Gou key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1716/111533l.jpg)

## The Story Behind the Story

Ryukishi07 did not return to Hinamizawa because he had unfinished business with the world. He returned because of a specific message that kept arriving in his inbox for twenty years.

"I want to erase my memory so that I can experience that shock for the first time all over again."

Fans sent him [variations of this sentiment](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2021-09-30/ryukishi07-explains-how-higurashi-gou-and-sotsu-reinvent-the-franchise/.177492) repeatedly across the two decades since the original visual novel launched. They weren't asking for a remake, exactly. They were describing an impossible desire — to unknow something beloved enough to feel its impact fresh. To return to a beginning that had already ended.

Ryukishi07 told ANN that the idea for Gou emerged from [a conversation](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2021-09-30/ryukishi07-explains-how-higurashi-gou-and-sotsu-reinvent-the-franchise/.177492) about creating an anime that "would begin as a remake but then turn into something new from the middle." He was clear that he didn't want to replace the original — he liked the old anime — so a genuine remake was never on the table. What he wanted was something structurally analogous to the fans' request: a story that felt like a return to the beginning, before revealing that no such return was ever possible.

That's not a marketing strategy. That's the plot.

## The Architecture of the Trap

The [first episode of Higurashi Gou](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/higurashi-when-they-cry-gou/episodes-1-3/.165313) is meticulously constructed to feel like the 2006 original. It opens with the same summer in Hinamizawa, the same friend group dynamics, the same slow accumulation of menace beneath the surface of small-town life. The production even uses [Eiko Shimamiya's "Higurashi no Naku Koro ni"](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2020-10-08/new-higurashi-when-they-cry-anime-reveals-true-title-visual-songs/.165003) — the 2006 anime's opening theme — as the closing music for Episode 1, a detail so specific it reads less like nostalgia and more like manipulation, which is to say it reads like both simultaneously.

Then Episode 2 happens. [Hanyuu appears](https://cbr.com/anime-higurashi-when-they-cry-gou-suprise-sequel/). The subtitle changes from "New" to "Gou." The audience, both new viewers who had been told no prerequisites were needed and veterans who thought they were watching a remake, experiences the same thing: confusion about where they actually are in the story, and what they've actually been promised.

The production manager confirmed in a [March 2021 interview](https://07th-expansion.fandom.com/wiki/Higurashi_Gou_Interview_(3/23/21)) that the advertising was intentionally designed to let the series "pass by as a remake." The voice actors — handed scripts weekly, not the full text — [apparently thought they were working on a remake](https://gramarye.tumblr.com/post/646465870105853953) until Yui Horie (Hanyuu's VA) received her script and noted, puzzled, that she was appearing unusually early. The deception was pervasive.

This is worth sitting with: the production withheld the truth from the actors, from the marketing, from the audience. This is not how you launch a routine sequel. This is how you create an experience of betrayal by familiar things — which is also what the story is about.

## Satoko Is the Audience

The reveal in Episode 15, retroactively titled "Satokowashi-hen" (The Village-Destroying Chapter), is that Satoko Hojo has been the architect of the loops. She obtained the power to reset the world through contact with Eua — a new supernatural being — and has been using it not to escape Hinamizawa but to trap Rika inside it.

The reason: Rika finally left. After achieving the happy ending in the original series, after a century of dying and resetting and fighting toward freedom, Rika escaped Hinamizawa, went to St. Lucia Academy, and began building a life. And Satoko — who loved Rika in the absolute, unqualified way that children love their closest friends — couldn't accept it. She didn't want Rika to leave. She wanted to go back to the beginning.

Ryukishi07 told interviewers that he wasn't going to reveal Gou's themes explicitly, but he [observed](https://07th-expansion.fandom.com/wiki/Higurashi_Gou_Interview_(3/23/21)) that Satoko "is certainly a hindrance to the future Rika wants" and asked audiences to try to understand her emotions. The same qualities that made Satoko lovable in the original — her irrepressible optimism, her absolute refusal to give up on the people she loves, the [promise they'd always be together](https://thewhitefluffyhat.tumblr.com/post/647219694568357888/themes-and-parallels) — are exactly what transformed her into a villain. Her loyalty became imprisonment. Her love became a loop.

This is the structural parallel the series built and has been largely underdiscussed: Satoko is doing what the fans asked for. She found a way to erase the past — to keep resetting to a beginning she loves — so she could experience that closeness for the first time, again, and again, and again. Ryukishi07 couldn't give the fans their impossible wish. But he could show them what granting it would actually cost.

The audience watching Gou hoping for the familiar textures of the original was, in miniature, Satoko. The series used your nostalgia against you — [your desire to return](https://www.cbr.com/higurashi-gou-episode-19-rika-satoko-twist-villain/) to a Hinamizawa that already ended — to put you in the position of the person destroying Rika's life.

## What the Criticism Gets Right, and Where It Stops

The most [sustained criticism of Gou and Sotsu](https://infinitemirai.wordpress.com/2021/10/06/higurashi-sotsu-whole-series-review-and-reflection-remarks-on-the-curtainfall/) — and there is a lot of it, with Sotsu [widely described](https://www.animeherald.com/2022/04/02/in-the-aftermath-of-higurashi-sotsu/) as "one of the biggest anime-related disappointments and/or frustrations of 2021" alongside The Promised Neverland Season 2 — focuses on Satoko's characterization. Critics argue that reducing her motivation to possessive obsession over Rika ignores the complexity she had in the original, where she was a practical, trap-building, fiercely independent character who survived genuine abuse. The [Junkyard blog's review of Gou](https://junkyard.blog/2022/01/17/higurashi-when-they-cry-gou/) notes that the first twelve episodes, despite presenting new events from Rika's perspective, "inevitably revisit so many of the original storylines that it did feel more like a remake than something new" — and that the Satoko-as-villain pivot is "just about the only thing that justifies its entire existence." A villain who kills Rika every few episodes because she doesn't want her to go to a different school is, these critics argue, a villain without a commensurate crime.

This is a fair reading of how the execution works, particularly in Sotsu, where the narrative stalls in extended recaps of scenes already shown in Gou and the promised emotional payoff — the final confrontation between Rika and Satoko — lands as a physical fight that doesn't carry the weight the score implies. [Critics noted](https://infinitemirai.wordpress.com/2021/10/06/higurashi-sotsu-whole-series-review-and-reflection-remarks-on-the-curtainfall/) a "mismatch between what my ears told me and what my eyes saw." Ryukishi07's script and the animators weren't operating at the same level of intensity.

But the criticism usually stops at characterization without asking what the structural argument is, and the structural argument is stronger than the execution. The problem isn't that Satoko's motive is "childish" — it's that it's supposed to be. The original Higurashi is full of catastrophe driven by psychological wounds that look small from the outside. The syndrome, the paranoia, the violence — all triggered by mundane anxieties amplified into horror. Satoko's inability to accept that Rika might want a different future follows the same logic. The original thesis — that love without communication becomes a curse — is being applied to the relationship the series has always centered, and the result is genuinely uncomfortable.

Where the critics are right: the resolution is too clean. Ryukishi07 [told ANN](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2021-09-30/ryukishi07-explains-how-higurashi-gou-and-sotsu-reinvent-the-franchise/.177492) that the theme of Higurashi is "there is no sin that cannot be forgiven," and Sotsu ends with Rika and Satoko reconciling after a fraction of the real reckoning the plot demanded. The happy ending is not unearned in the sense of violating Higurashi's core philosophy — but it's unearned in the sense of having been rushed to a conclusion that should have taken longer.

## The Sequel That Interrogates Its Own Existence

The most uncomfortable thing about Gou is that it has genuine contempt for the desire that brought it into existence. Ryukishi07 couldn't give fans the blank-slate experience they wanted — can't give anyone that — so instead he wrote a story about what it looks like to want that blank slate badly enough to do terrible things to get it. He put the audience's fantasy in the body of the villain, ran it forward to its logical conclusion, and asked them to examine how it looked from the outside.

This is not a comfortable move. It's also not, despite what some critics suggest, a cheap one. The fake-remake architecture — the deliberate use of familiar scenarios, character beats, and even the 2006 opening theme in Episode 1 — exists to generate the specific emotional position of a Higurashi fan in October 2020, and then to complicate that position. [When a character you loved becomes a cage for another character you loved](https://www.cbr.com/higurashi-differences-gou-original-series/), you're being asked to examine the relationship between love and ownership. Between remembering something and returning to it.

The original Higurashi's answer to its question — can Rika's curse be broken through the power of genuine human connection? — was yes, with difficulty, through sacrifice and communication. Gou asks a harder question: what happens when the connection itself becomes the cage? What happens when the person who helped you survive your curse becomes the source of a new one?

It doesn't fully answer this. Sotsu flinches at the last moment, retreating into a reconciliation the emotional groundwork hadn't fully laid. But the question is real, and it is being asked by the writer of the original, about his own ending, using his own characters. That's not a failure of imagination. It's the most honest thing a creator can do with a beloved work — take it seriously enough to interrogate what it left unresolved.

The fans who wanted to forget Higurashi so they could experience it fresh got something stranger and more honest: a story that wouldn't let them forget, that weaponized what they remembered, and asked them to look directly at why returning to beginnings is always, in some ways, a form of trap.
