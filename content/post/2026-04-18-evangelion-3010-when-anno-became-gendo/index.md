Somewhere during the long, grief-stricken production of *Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time*, Hideaki Anno sat down with Megumi Ogata — the voice actress who had played Shinji Ikari for twenty-five years — and told her he had a problem. He could no longer understand Shinji. He no longer knew how that character thought or what he wanted. And then he said something that reframes the entire Rebuild tetralogy: by now, he felt closer to Gendo.

That's not a throwaway comment. That's the key.

![Gendo Ikari in Evangelion 3.0+1.0](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4f/Evangelion_3.0%2B1.0_film_poster.jpg)

## The Gendo Problem

In the original *Neon Genesis Evangelion* and in *The End of Evangelion*, Gendo Ikari is essentially a silhouette. He exists in the series as a gravity well — people orbit him, collapse under him, flee from him — but his interior is mostly sealed off. What we know is functional: he wants Third Impact, he wants Yui back, he will use his son as an instrument and discard him. We see him from Shinji's angle, which means we see him as Shinji experiences him — as a wall that occasionally moves.

This was, partly, a production necessity. The original TV series ran on fumes by the end, with Anno directing episodes from the depths of clinical depression. But it was also, unmistakably, a self-protective choice — and understanding why requires knowing something about Anno's real father. A [resurfaced interview](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/neon-genesis-evangelion-resurfaced-interview-creator-abusive-father/) documented the shadow of his father's behavior over his childhood. Writing Gendo's interiority in 1995 — truly inhabiting the psychology of the cold, withholding father who uses his son as a tool — might have required Anno to go somewhere he wasn't ready to go. So he didn't. He built Gendo as pure function: a mechanism that generates damage.

By 2021, he was.

## What Happened in the Seventeen Years Between

The [production history of *Thrice Upon a Time*](https://collider.com/hideaki-anno-evangelion-interview/) reads as its own kind of Eva narrative. The third Rebuild film, *3.0 You Can (Not) Redo*, released in 2012 to widespread bewilderment and some betrayal — audiences who had come aboard with the crowd-pleasing *2.0* found themselves dropped into a time-skip they weren't briefed on, watching a Shinji they didn't recognize navigate a NERV that had become a cult. Anno then [publicly stated he could not continue](http://www.evamonkey.com/hideaki-anno-apologizes-for-evangelion-delay/), describing the production of *3.0* as having left him burned out and depressed, uncertain he would ever return. He went and made *Shin Godzilla* instead — crediting that detour with saving the project entirely, since without it he might never have come back.

When production on *3.0+1.0* finally resumed after *Shin Godzilla* wrapped in late 2016, Anno was returning to Evangelion as a different person. He was in his mid-fifties. He had a marriage. He had built and broken and partially rebuilt his mental health over two decades. And he had, somewhere in there, aged out of being able to locate himself in a fourteen-year-old boy who can't stop crying.

He located himself in Gendo instead.

## The Therapy Scene

The film makes this explicit in a way that is almost shocking in how literal it is. During the climactic confrontation between Shinji and his father in the Anti-Universe — a space that strips reality down to its blank symbolic bones — Gendo is seated in a therapy chair. Actually in one. The staging is deliberate. What follows is not a battle but an excavation: Gendo explains, in full coherent sentences, what has been wrong with him his entire life. He was afraid of being hurt by others. He could not connect. He poured himself into science as a substitute for human contact because science, unlike people, could be controlled. When Yui died, the last tether broke.

This is not villainy. This is a diagnosis. It's the interiority that the original series never had the space — or the willingness — to write.

And Shinji doesn't fight him for it. He listens. He offers understanding. In the most quietly extraordinary beat of the film, a de-aged Gendo — restored briefly to the young, lost man he was before grief calcified into cruelty — weeps and is held. Shinji gives his father the thing Gendo could never give him.

Anno has said that by the time of making this film, [the story of Evangelion was becoming a story of Shinji and his father](https://collider.com/hideaki-anno-evangelion-interview/). What he didn't say explicitly — but what the film says — is that writing that story required Anno to look at Gendo from the inside. To ask: what does it feel like to be the father who failed? To have built walls so high you forgot why you built them?

## The Answer the Original Series Couldn't Give

*The End of Evangelion* offered Instrumentality as the logical endpoint of Gendo's project — and as the logical endpoint of a very specific kind of despair: Shinji's. The argument EoE makes, through Third Impact, is that if connection is pain and isolation is unbearable, the cleanest answer is to dissolve all boundaries, merge all souls, eliminate the AT Field that keeps you separate and therefore vulnerable. It's the fantasy of a person who has retreated from everyone and found no relief in solitude either. Shinji refuses it because he decides — barely, at the last second — that he wants the possibility of real contact even at the cost of real pain.

*Thrice Upon a Time* offers a different answer, and it comes through Gendo's arc. The solution to the wall isn't to tear down all walls — that's Instrumentality, that's the fantasy of frictionless dissolution. The solution is to lower the wall in front of one specific person, enough for them to see in. Gendo's catharsis doesn't come from merger with Yui or from Third Impact. It comes from being *understood* by his son.

This is, quietly, the film's most radical departure from its source material. Not the fights. Not the new timeline. The fact that Gendo Ikari is finally allowed to be comprehensible.

## Ube, and the Door Out

The film closes in Ube, Yamaguchi Prefecture — [Anno's actual hometown](https://midwestfilmjournal.com/2021/08/14/evangelion-3-0-1-0-thrice-upon-a-time/), recreated as it looked during his youth, used as the setting where adult Shinji finally meets a world that doesn't need saving. The animation bleeds into live-action footage — Anno's actual hometown, actual asphalt and daylight, the world outside the cockpit and the AT Field and the fiction. Shinji and Mari step into it. The frame that has contained him since 1995 simply stops.

This isn't a stylistic flourish. It's Anno making the argument structurally: Shinji belongs to the real world now. And so, implicitly, does Anno. [The story of Evangelion had ended for him](https://collider.com/hideaki-anno-evangelion-interview/), he told journalists. "I am not going to continue on, but now that it's ended, it's actually very peaceful for me."

You believe him. Not because *Thrice Upon a Time* is a tidy film — it's frequently maddening, its middle act crowded with mythology that resists easy parsing — but because the confession at its core has the unmistakable texture of something real. Anno made Gendo human because he had finally figured out how. And figuring out how, for a man who has spent his life oscillating between Shinji's paralysis and Gendo's armor, probably felt something like therapy.

The question the film leaves open is whether you can understand your own Gendo before you've spent twenty-five years not understanding him. Anno seems to think you can. He wrote the ending to prove it.
