Everyone who talks about *Insomniacs After School* eventually lands on Isaki Magari's insomnia. Her heart condition makes sleep terrifying in a clinically legible way, and the heartbeat-as-reassurance mechanic is genuinely elegant. She gets the attention. She gets the analysis.

But Ganta Nakami's insomnia is the stranger and more precise wound, and it is what the series is actually about.

![Insomniacs After School, Vol. 1 English cover — Ganta and Isaki in the observatory](https://static.animecorner.me/2023/03/insomniacs-after-school-vol-1-9781974736577_hr.jpg)

## What the Night Took

Ganta's insomnia was caused by his mother leaving in the middle of the night without warning — abandoning him and his father while they slept. The [fandom wiki entry](https://insomniacs-after-school.fandom.com/wiki/Ganta_Nakami) states it plainly: he has extreme separation anxiety from this event, and it produces a specific form of nocturnal dread. He confided to Isaki that every night he thinks the next morning will be worse than today, and that this is why he cannot sleep.

That phrasing is worth unpacking. He doesn't fear the night itself — he fears what the morning will reveal. His sleeplessness is a form of vigil. If he stays awake, he knows what's there. If he lets the night pass without watching, he might wake to find that someone has gone.

This is a different psychological architecture than Isaki's. Her fear runs inward: *I will not survive the night.* His runs outward: *the night will take someone from me.* Both fears are about continuity — about whether the world will be the same on the other side of sleep — but they locate the threat differently. Isaki fears her own disappearance; Ganta fears his loved ones'. Her insomnia is about mortality; his is about abandonment. Those are not the same condition in different clothes. They are structurally opposite fears that happen to share the symptom of sleeplessness. The series puts them together precisely because they have different etiologies, and what helps one is not automatically what helps the other.

What [a perceptive fan essay by Dave Dalessio](https://davedalessiowrites.wordpress.com/2025/08/03/the-power-of-two-insomniacs-after-school/) noted is that the series handles this asymmetry in a telling way: Isaki's medical condition gets clinical attention (she's in the care of a cardiologist, her condition is named and monitored), while Ganta's psychological wound gets essentially none. No therapist, no formal intervention. [Reviews of the manga](https://icv2.com/articles/reviews/view/53455/review-insomniacs-after-school-vol-1-manga) consistently identify Ganta's grumpiness and social withdrawal as character traits before identifying them as symptoms, which is actually the correct order of observation — most people who know him can't see the wound inside the presentation. Just the slow accumulation of nights spent differently.

That might read as an oversight, or it might be the manga's most honest choice about how psychological wounds actually heal in adolescence — not through treatment but through replacement experience.

## The Performance of Laziness

Before Isaki, Ganta reads as the grumpy, unmotivated student who sleeps through class. [Makoto Ojiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insomniacs_After_School) introduces him as an honor student who coasts on raw ability because investment in the future requires believing the future will be worth it. His exhaustion is real — chronic insomnia does that — but his social withdrawal is a choice built on the same foundation as his sleeplessness. If you don't let people close, they can't leave in the night.

He is, in the logic of his own psychology, doing exactly what his wound trained him to do. His mother left without warning, so warnings and proximity became the danger. He keeps people at arm's length not out of misanthropy but out of a child's very specific learning: *the night is when the people you love vanish, and the morning is when you find out.*

That learning produces a person who [came off as grumpy and unfriendly](https://insomniacs-after-school.fandom.com/wiki/Ganta_Nakami) to his peers before Isaki, someone whose insomnia appeared to others as mere laziness. The external presentation is almost perfectly designed to prevent the kind of closeness that could reopen the wound — or heal it.

## What Astrophotography Does

Ganta doesn't come to astrophotography through any pre-existing interest. He comes to it because Isaki introduces it, and because the astronomy club they accidentally inherit gives him a reason to be awake at night that isn't anxiety.

This transformation is structurally significant. [The club's activities](https://www.japanpowered.com/anime-articles/insomniacs-after-school) — stargazing, astrophotography contests, travel to rural dark-sky locations — reframe his insomnia as a qualification rather than a dysfunction. The night that was his affliction becomes his access. He has something the daytime world doesn't have, and for the first time that something is valuable.

But astrophotography specifically does something the other activities don't. Long-exposure photography is a practice of patience through darkness: you point the camera at what you want to capture, you hold it still, and you wait while the light accumulates. The images it produces — star trails over castle ruins, the Milky Way above a sleeping valley — are records of presence. The camera documents that these things were there, that the night contained something real and beautiful. [Ojiro grounds this in specific technical reality](https://shapes.inc/fandom/insomniacs-after-school/astronomy-log), with the manga's astronomical content accurate to the real night sky as it appeared during serialization. These aren't generic nightscapes — they're evidence.

The child who learned that the night takes things away now photographs what the night contains. The symmetry is probably not conscious on Ganta's part, and Ojiro doesn't announce it as symbolism. It simply emerges from who Ganta is and what activity he's taken up, and it gives the astrophotography arc an emotional register beyond the surface romance. [Ojiro's draftsmanship](https://nikolasyoffie.journoportfolio.com/articles/insomniacs-after-school/) is unusually attentive to detail across the series — the realistic depiction of specific real-world locations, the accurate astronomical content — so the astrophotography thread doesn't read as allegorical convenience. It reads as logical consequence, which is how the best character-driven symbolism always works: the image earns its resonance by being truthful first.

## The Specific Cure

The reason Isaki works as Ganta's cure — not a generic cure, but his specific one — is structural. She needs to be there at night. Her condition requires the reassurance of his heartbeat. Which means she has a reason to stay, a reason that preexists romance and outlasts the ordinary reasons people leave.

He cannot offer her his presence as proof without receiving her presence in return. The circuit closes not through declaration but through mutual need. [Anime Corner's review](https://animecorner.me/insomniacs-after-school-review-a-beautiful-tale-about-finding-your-safe-space/) describes the chemistry as "wholesome and not superficial at all," and what makes it not superficial is precisely this: it's not just two people who like each other but two people whose specific damages lock together in a way that accidentally constitutes treatment.

Her insomnia needed his stability; his insomnia needed her permanence. The romance is the delivery mechanism for both.

[Critics who found the series boring](https://www.cbr.com/insomniacs-after-school-review-why-boring-not-popular/) — and [there were others](https://butwhytho.net/2023/07/insomniacs-after-school-season-1-review/) who found it "a relaxing read" precisely because of what others called slowness — were both responding to the same pacing, disagreeing only on whether trust accumulation is worth watching in real time. You cannot rush Ganta out of his vigilance. The months of shared nights have to happen at the speed they happen, because that's how long it takes for a nervous system to learn that the morning is not always an aftermath of loss.

## What the Manga Actually Heals

[Insomniacs After School ended in chapter 125](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-07-17/insomniacs-after-school-manga-ends-in-2-chapters/.200331), serialized in Weekly Big Comic Spirits from May 2019 to August 2023. The [ending](https://animecorner.me/insomniacs-after-school-manga-officially-ends/) shows Ganta as club president, boyfriend, a person who has taken on the role of staying rather than bracing for departure. That is what recovery looks like in this series: not the elimination of the wound but the slow reversal of the behaviors it produced. [Readers on Goodreads and fan forums](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/62919674) noted that the series earned its ending by refusing to rush the path to it — a rare quality in romance manga, which more typically accelerates toward its conclusion once the emotional premise is established.

He is still probably an insomniac. The manga doesn't claim to cure his psychology through the mechanism of romance. What it does is give him four years of nights that ended in mornings where Isaki was still there, where the observatory was still there, where the [accumulation of presence](https://www.romancemangarecs.com/kimi-wa-houkago-insomnia-manga-review/) proved more durable than his mother's absence.

What the manga heals is not the insomnia. It's the belief — learned in a single night, practiced every subsequent night for years — that morning always means someone is gone.

That's a more modest claim than most romances make, and a more honest one.
