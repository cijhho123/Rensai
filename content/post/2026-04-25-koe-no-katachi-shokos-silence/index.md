There is a specific kind of cruelty built into the structure of *Koe no Katachi*. Not the cruelty of Shoya Ishida pulling Shoko Nishimiya's hearing aids out, destroying six of them over the course of elementary school, not the cruelty of classmates who joined in or looked away. The cruelty I mean is quieter and more architectural: in a story ostensibly about a deaf girl, that deaf girl is given the least access to her own inner life.

Yoshitoki Oima was explicit about her intentions in interviews. ["The main theme was not bullying or disability,"](https://fandomwire.com/a-silent-voice-creator-yoshitoki-oima-it-was-not-the-main-theme-on-her-movies-real-theme-that-was-born-out-of-regret/) she told Kodansha — the work grew from a personal regret about failing to truly hear another child, about the difficulty of communication between people. Her mother worked as a [Japanese Sign Language interpreter](https://japanpastandpresent.org/en/teaching-aids/lesson-plans/a-silent-voice-deafness-japanese-sign-language-and-pop-culture/), and Shoko's deafness was chosen as a concrete representation of that broader theme: the gap between what we mean and what another person receives.

That's a genuinely interesting premise. The problem is that Oima chose to tell this story almost entirely from the perspective of the person who couldn't be bothered to listen.

## The Perspective Problem

Shoya Ishida is the protagonist of *Koe no Katachi*, and Oima commits to that choice with full structural consequences. We are inside his head, processing his guilt, riding his suicidal ideation, watching him decide — slowly, imperfectly — to try to live again. This is not inherently a bad decision. Literature is full of stories told from the wrong person's point of view as a deliberate artistic move, and some of them are among the most powerful works of fiction we have.

But *Koe no Katachi* does not seem aware that it has made this choice. It treats Shoya's perspective not as a lens that necessarily distorts, but as a neutral window. The result is that Shoko, the person whose life was most directly damaged, exists primarily as a function of Shoya's healing. She is patient where he is volatile. She forgives where she has every reason not to. She smiles — that iconic, unreadable smile — in moments where rage or grief would be the honest response. As [one analysis notes](https://videogamesmakemehappy.wordpress.com/2021/12/04/a-silent-voice-koe-no-katachi-is-a-terrible-representation-of-the-deaf-community-lets-talk-about-why/), Shoko "barely gets a voice in her own narrative," a fact made more pointed by the mechanics of her deafness — she communicates in sign language, in writing, in hesitant speech, and the story consistently treats those communications as touching details of her character rather than as the actual substance of her interiority.

We know what Shoya thinks about Shoko constantly. We almost never know what Shoko thinks about anything.

## What the Manga Does Better — and Where It Still Falls Short

The manga, across [seven volumes serialized in Weekly Shōnen Magazine from 2013 to 2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)), has more room to breathe than the 2016 Kyoto Animation film, and it uses that room in ways that matter. The side characters — Yuzuru, Nagatsuka, Sahara, the complicated and damaging Ueno — all receive backstories that make them legible as people rather than as functions in Shoya's social rehabilitation. The bullying sequences are harder, more graphic, more difficult to aestheticize. The film project that brings the friend group together feels organic rather than convenient.

And Shoko does get slightly more space. Her relationship with her sister Yuzuru, her ambitions after high school (she moves to Tokyo to study hairdressing in the manga's later chapters), her anger — the manga renders these in more detail. [Comparisons between the two formats](https://www.cbr.com/a-silent-voice-anime-manga-comparisons/) consistently note that the manga's expanded scope lets the secondary cast breathe — and that breathing room makes the central relationship feel more earned than the film's tighter focus allows. The [AniList community discussions](https://anilist.co/forum/thread/52252) around the manga reinforce this: readers who came to the manga after the film frequently report that it made them care more about characters the film had flattened.

But the fundamental structure doesn't change. Shoko's emotional processing is still largely invisible. The scenes that most directly concern her inner life — her suicide attempt, her reaction to discovering her sister's self-harm, her complex feelings about forgiveness — are filtered through other characters' perceptions rather than shown from the inside. When she tries to throw herself off a balcony, the scene's emotional center is Shoya's desperate response, not whatever drove her there. [Critics have noted](https://shuubah.wordpress.com/2021/04/06/koe-no-katachi-a-silent-voice-in-the-eyes-of-a-physically-disabled-bully-victim/) that for all the manga's emotional power, it is consistently "his fear of losing her" and not "her reasons for wanting to jump" that the narrative dwells on. We know what saving her means to him. We do not really know what she meant to do.

This is the core of the structural problem: *Koe no Katachi* consistently interrupts Shoko's emotional experience to show us how it lands on Shoya.

## The Forgiveness Economy

Oima's stated intent — to examine communication and connection — is most visible in the way the manga handles forgiveness. Shoko's capacity for forgiveness is positioned as one of her defining qualities, practically a superpower, and the narrative treats it as a gift she offers Shoya rather than as something that requires examination.

[Book Riot's piece on bullying and redemption in the story](https://bookriot.com/bullying-redemption-silent-voice/) puts it plainly: "the entire situation winds up being all about him — his struggle, his redemption, his healing." This is not entirely unfair. The manga's climax involves Shoya finally removing the X-shaped marks he has placed over people's faces in his imagination, learning to see them as fully present humans — and the emotional payoff of that moment is calibrated to his experience, not to theirs.

Shoko's forgiveness of Shoya is never really interrogated. We don't see the cost of it, or whether it costs anything, because her interiority has been so consistently elided that by the time the emotional resolution arrives, she is less a character than a position — the forgiving victim, the patient recipient of the protagonist's growth.

This is a live debate in the fandom, not a fringe reading. [The Zetsubō Free Zone essay on who forgives whom](https://zetsubofreezone.wordpress.com/2021/09/12/a-silent-voice-who-forgives-the-bully-bullies/) notes the asymmetry clearly: Shoya is punished socially and emotionally for years; the other bullies largely face no consequences; and Shoko absorbs the burden of reconciliation. In a story that claims to be about the difficulty of real communication, the distribution of that difficulty falls almost entirely on the person who communicates differently.

## The Disability Question

The deafness representation debate around *Koe no Katachi* runs parallel to the structural critique and often intersects with it. [A University of Alabama thesis analyzing the manga through disability studies frameworks](https://louis.uah.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1450&context=uah-theses) finds that while the work depicts ableism in Japanese school culture with unusual frankness, Shoko herself fits troubling patterns: her deafness becomes her personality, her suffering is framed as the generative source of Shoya's growth, and her own community and culture as a deaf person is largely invisible.

The positive case for the manga's representation is real. [Oima's use of accurate Japanese Sign Language](https://japanpastandpresent.org/en/teaching-aids/lesson-plans/a-silent-voice-deafness-japanese-sign-language-and-pop-culture/), her mother's professional expertise informing the depiction, the care taken with the physical and social experience of a deaf child in a mainstream school — these are not nothing. Compared to how disability functions in most shōnen manga, *Koe no Katachi* is doing something more considered. The [Accessible Japan review](https://www.accessible-japan.com/a-silent-voice-review/) praises the film adaptation specifically for its sound design choices, which attempt to render Shoko's auditory experience with some fidelity.

But the critique from disability scholars and advocates — that Shoko's deafness is instrumentalized to produce a hearing character's redemption arc — lands because the text supports it. [One analysis in a San Diego State University academic project](https://soundofcomics.sdsu.edu/disability-and-sound/) notes that the story consistently focuses on what Shoko's deafness costs the hearing people around her, rather than what it means to Shoko. That's not a peripheral observation; it follows directly from the POV structure.

## What Oima's Own Irony Reveals

The strangest thing about *Koe no Katachi* is how fully it demonstrates its own stated theme while undermining it. Oima wanted to write about the failure of communication — about how difficult it is to actually reach another person, to hear what they mean rather than what you project onto them. She built a story about exactly that failure: a boy who could not hear a girl who was right in front of him.

And then she wrote the story from his perspective.

The result is a manga that performs the very problem it's diagnosing. The reader is positioned to inhabit Shoya's consciousness, to process Shoko through his eyes, to experience her healing as his achievement. The communication gap Oima wanted to illuminate becomes structurally reproduced in the reading experience. We, too, see Shoko from the outside. We, too, interpret her silence and her smile and her patience without having access to what they actually cost.

This might be intentional. It might be brilliant. A story that forces you to feel the limits of your perspective by giving you only the wrong one — that would be a sharp piece of meta-commentary. But *Koe no Katachi* doesn't read as though it knows it's doing this. The ending is too comforting, Shoya's breakthrough too redemptive, Shoko's interiority too consistently absent for the structural problem to feel like a choice rather than a blind spot.

What Oima built was a story about the impossibility of truly hearing another person that accidentally confirms the impossibility by not letting you hear Shoko. Whether that makes *Koe no Katachi* a failed work or an accidentally brilliant one depends on how charitably you're willing to interpret structures that don't seem aware of themselves.

The [fandom discourse](https://anilist.co/forum/thread/18949) is, predictably, split. For many readers, Shoya's arc is deeply felt and earned — they see their own patterns in him, find something useful in watching a person try to become less cruel. That response is not wrong. For others, the story's emotional architecture is inseparable from its ideological problem: it teaches you to care about the bully's healing while leaving the victim's inner life conspicuously blank.

Both readings are accurate. That's the exact shape of the problem.

## The Story That Isn't There

What *Koe no Katachi* in manga form never gives us is the story from Shoko's side — not as a correction or supplement, but as the primary text. What did it mean to arrive at a new school already knowing you would be a target? What does it feel like to forgive someone not because the hurt is gone but because you have decided to try? What does the smile mean?

We don't know. The story that could answer those questions — the story of a deaf girl navigating a world built for hearing people, finding ways to communicate across the gap, carrying the weight of others' limitations while building her own life — that story exists somewhere adjacent to *Koe no Katachi*, implied by its edges, but never told.

What we have instead is the story of the boy who finally learned to look at her. It's a good story. But Oima's own theme suggests it was always the less important one.
