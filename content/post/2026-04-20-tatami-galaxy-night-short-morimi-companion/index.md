The Senpai in *The Night Is Short, Walk On Girl* is essentially Watashi from *The Tatami Galaxy* on the other side of the loops.

Not literally — they're different characters, in nominally different stories, separated by an anime TV series and a theatrical film. But the resemblance is structural. Both are [unnamed Kyoto University young men](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_Is_Short,_Walk_On_Girl) consumed by the desire for a specific dark-haired girl they cannot bring themselves to approach directly. Both narrate their own paralysis with tremendous verbal energy. Both stand at the threshold of an experience they want desperately and keep finding reasons not to enter.

The difference is this: Senpai already knows his own faults. [He is "painfully aware of his own faults, and doesn't really feel any ill will towards others."](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Anime/TheNightIsShortWalkOnGirl) Watashi is not. The whole Tatami Galaxy is the process of getting Watashi to that point. Night Is Short begins where Tatami Galaxy ends. Together, they constitute Tomihiko Morimi's complete argument about how young people live and fail to live their own lives.

## The Shared World Morimi Built

Morimi [spent his own student years in Kyoto](https://www.studykyoto.jp/en/magazine/2022/10/12/kyoto-through-novels/), and the city's university life became the setting for the universe he would spend his career inhabiting. *The Tatami Galaxy* (novel: 2004, anime: 2010), *The Night Is Short, Walk On Girl* (novel: 2006, anime film: 2017), and *Tatami Time Machine Blues* (novel: 2004 sequel, anime: 2022) are set in the same Kyoto, with the same geography — the Kamo River bridge, the Kyoto University clocktower, the labyrinthine secondhand book market — and share minor characters who drift between stories as evidence of a shared continuity.

[Both anime adaptations were directed by Masaaki Yuasa](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=11200), working from the same creative team: screenwriter Makoto Ueda, character designer Yusuke Nakamura's angular, vivid aesthetic, the same Noitamina sensibility about adult-coded anime that refuses conventional expectations. They look and feel like siblings because they are — made by the same people from the same author for largely the same audience, separated by seven years and a shift in medium.

The shared creative DNA is worth naming because the thematic relationship is not accidental. Morimi wrote both novels within two years of each other. The Senpai of Night Is Short is explicitly [compared in fan analysis](https://medium.com/@daleetah/how-the-night-is-short-walk-on-girl-course-corrects-the-tatami-galaxy-22a3eed0214c) to Watashi: the same archetype, but revised. Higuchi and Hanuki, supporting characters from Tatami Galaxy, [reappear in Night Is Short](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_Is_Short,_Walk_On_Girl) in significant roles. This isn't intertextual Easter-egging — it's Morimi building a coherent world in which the same types of people keep reappearing and making the same mistakes with slightly different degrees of self-awareness.

## What The Tatami Galaxy Cannot Show

*The Tatami Galaxy* is a first-person narrative about a young man who cannot see past himself. This is not a criticism — it is the precise formal premise. Watashi narrates everything through his own interpretive filter, and the show's visual and structural choices (as I've written about separately) are built to make you feel this filter as a physical constraint. The flat art, the rapid voiceover, the looping structure: all of it is Watashi's psychology made external.

The consequence of this premise is that *The Tatami Galaxy* cannot show you anything Watashi doesn't perceive. And Watashi, crucially, cannot perceive Akashi — the woman he nominally loves — as a full person. She is [described by critics as "flat"](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/the-tatami-galaxy) because she is flat in Watashi's eyes. He sees her in abstract terms: dark-haired, quietly competent, available in his peripheral vision across every timeline. What she actually thinks, what she wants, what her life looks like outside of his orbit — none of this is accessible to him, so none of it is accessible to the viewer.

This is honest but limiting. *The Tatami Galaxy* makes a strong argument about male self-absorption but can only demonstrate it from within. You understand what Watashi cannot see, but you cannot see it either.

Night Is Short changes the equation. Otome — the equivalent of Akashi, the dark-haired girl the male protagonist cannot approach — [is the co-protagonist](https://medium.com/@daleetah/how-the-night-is-short-walk-on-girl-course-corrects-the-tatami-galaxy-22a3eed0214c). She narrates her own night, pursues her own pleasures, makes her own decisions, forms her own relationships. She is not in Senpai's story; Senpai is in hers. The structural correction is so complete that it retroactively changes how you read the earlier work: Akashi, you realize, was always doing her own thing too. Watashi just couldn't see it.

## The Gender Shift as Argument

The most important difference between the two works is not the gender of the protagonist — it's what that shift reveals about the *world* both protagonists inhabit.

In *The Tatami Galaxy*, the Kyoto university world is rendered entirely through Watashi's male, self-absorbed gaze. The clubs are sites of male ambition and male disappointment. Akashi exists at their margins. When the show reveals that Akashi noticed Watashi's "wasted effort" across timelines — that she was watching too — it's played as a surprise, because Watashi's gaze had never turned outward enough to imagine she had a perspective at all. This is the show's most quietly devastating observation: [the girl he was ostensibly pursuing has been living her own life the whole time](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/the-tatami-galaxy), and he is shocked to learn she has one.

In Night Is Short, [Otome moves through the same world](https://www.screenslate.com/articles/night-short-walk-girl) as an agent rather than an object. She drinks, negotiates, reads, dances, confronts harassment, forms alliances with strangers. The world is not organized around her desire; she moves through a world that has its own logic and makes her own place within it. When Todo-san gropes her — a moment [the "Night Is Short corrects Tatami Galaxy" essay identifies as a direct revision of how the earlier work treated Jougasaki's predatory behavior toward female club members](https://medium.com/@daleetah/how-the-night-is-short-walk-on-girl-course-corrects-the-tatami-galaxy-22a3eed0214c) — she responds with immediate, clear consequence. The film does not narratively suppress this, does not make her experience invisible to make the male lead more sympathetic. It happened; she handled it; she moved on.

In Tatami Galaxy, [Jougasaki's predatory behavior toward women in the tennis circle is acknowledged but treated as background color](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/TheTatamiGalaxy) — context for Watashi's disillusionment with that club, not a thing the show is interested in processing. This isn't a critique unique to Tatami Galaxy; it's structural to a first-person narrative that cannot see past its narrator. Night Is Short simply refuses that structure. Otome's experience is the story.

This is not a retroactive condemnation of *The Tatami Galaxy* — it is a completion. The limitation may be intentional: Morimi and Yuasa arguably built the constraint into the first work knowing they would address it in the second. Two novels, two years apart, the same city, the same creative team. This reads like design, not oversight. The first work showed the trap from inside. The second showed the world the trap was built in.

## Kyoto as the Third Character

Both works are specifically, precisely Kyoto — not a generic Japanese university city but the actual city, with [actual Kyoto University buildings](https://jamesofwalsh.com/2015/05/17/tatami-galaxy-and-kyoto/), actual landmarks, actual geographical logic. The Kamo River bridge appears in both as the site of crucial decisions. Kyoto University's clocktower represents the perpetual reset of Watashi's loops.

![Kyoto University Clock Tower — the real-world anchor for both Morimi adaptations](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Kyoto_University_clocktower_from_north.JPG/1280px-Kyoto_University_clocktower_from_north.JPG)

Morimi has spoken directly about this: his [novels are interwoven with his actual university experiences](https://www.studykyoto.jp/en/magazine/2022/10/12/kyoto-through-novels/), and the Kyoto setting allows him to make the fantastical feel familiar, grounded in spaces his readers may have visited or can imagine visiting. The setting matters because what Morimi is writing about is not generic "youth" — it's a specific institutional context, the Japanese university, with its specific culture of clubs and circles, senpai and kohai, the social pressure of performing the right kind of student life.

The concept that organizes both works is what the series calls "bara iro no kyampasu raifu" — a rose-colored campus life, the fantasy of what university is supposed to be. [This fantasy is not personal to Watashi](https://bewilderedgalaxy.wordpress.com/2019/04/21/romanticization-of-an-ideal-college-life-tatami-galaxy/); it is cultural. Japanese young people, particularly young men entering university, are handed a script: join the right club, meet the right people, fall in love with the right person, graduate properly socialized. The script is the problem. The clubs in Tatami Galaxy fail not because Watashi chooses wrong ones — they fail because the script itself is a fantasy, and no amount of correct club selection can fulfill it.

Night Is Short doesn't argue with the script; it simply ignores it. Otome is not trying to achieve a rose-colored campus life. She is having whatever night she's actually having, which turns out to be genuinely extraordinary precisely because she is not pursuing an ideal version of it. The absence of the script is what enables the experience.

## Why They Need Each Other

*The Tatami Galaxy* is a remarkable work on its own. Its formal precision is genuinely singular — [the first TV anime to win the Japan Media Arts Festival Grand Prize](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2010-12-08/tatami-galaxy-historie-win-media-arts-awards) was not a coincidence, and its influence on how anime can use narrative structure and visual grammar to enact psychological argument is real if underacknowledged.

But it is incomplete in the way that any first-person narrative locked inside a self-absorbed perspective is incomplete. It can show you the prison from inside. It cannot show you what the prison looks like from outside, or what exists in the world the prison is built within.

*The Night Is Short, Walk On Girl* completes it. Senpai's self-awareness, compared to Watashi's total self-absorption, suggests a progression: this is what you get when the loops end and you can see yourself. Otome's vitality, compared to Akashi's peripheral presence, suggests an alternative: this is what it looks like when a person is not organized around another person's fantasy. The shared Kyoto geography suggests continuity: same city, different consciousness, radically different experience.

The [Chicago Review of Books review of the Tatami Galaxy novel](https://chireviewofbooks.com/2023/02/20/multiversal-revelations-in-the-tatami-galaxy/) notes that Morimi's thesis is ultimately about acceptance — accepting that circumstances will not produce happiness, that self must produce it. Night Is Short shows this in its affirmative register: a protagonist who does not wait for happiness to arrive from the right configuration of external circumstances, who goes out into the night and makes something happen through sheer presence and openness.

Read alone, each is excellent. Read together — which Morimi clearly intended, which Yuasa clearly understood — they constitute a complete argument about how the fantasy of a perfect student life, held too tightly and pursued too narrowly, closes off the actual life happening around you.

Watashi needed eleven episodes of loops to figure this out. Otome didn't need to be told.
