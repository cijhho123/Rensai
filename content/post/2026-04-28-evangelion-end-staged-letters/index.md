At the end of *The End of Evangelion*, just before the final scene of Shinji and Asuka on a barren shore, the screen fills with letters. Fan correspondence, held up and photographed — some expressing appreciation for the film, some containing hostility, one ending with the words "Anno, I'll kill you!!!" For years, these were cited as the most concrete evidence of what kind of film End of Evangelion actually was: Anno's direct response to the threats and hatred his fanbase had sent him after the controversial TV series finale in 1996. The creator, the argument went, had received hatred, and the film was his answer.

The letters, it turned out, were almost entirely written by Gainax staff.

Anno confirmed in a [later interview documented by the Evangelion fan community](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) — specifically in the making-of materials for *Shiki-Jitsu* — that he couldn't use real fan correspondence for legal reasons. "If you write the real thing," he said, "it causes a lot of trouble." The most aggressive letter, the one that ends with a death threat, was written by someone he described as "a really old friend of mine." The theatrical booklet that accompanied the film's release described the sequence as "simulations to explore the relationships between a work and its admirers."

This is the part that gets ignored in every "toxic fandom" retrospective that cites End of Evangelion as Exhibit A. Anno didn't document his audience's hostility. He constructed a version of it, staged it with people he trusted, and then projected that construction at the people he was addressing. That choice — staging over documentation — is the most revealing thing about what the film was actually trying to do.

## What Actually Happened

The timeline is important to establish clearly, because the myth version has overwhelmed the documented version. [*Neon Genesis Evangelion*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/30/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) concluded its television run in March 1996 with episodes 25 and 26, which replaced the expected external action climax with an internal psychological journey — Shinji working through his trauma in an abstract mind-space, and ending with a simple "Congratulations" as everyone around him applauds his choice to continue living.

Fan response was severe. [Gainax's offices were vandalized](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) with graffiti — the specific messages included both sexual slurs directed at franchise characters and expressions of contempt for the studio. Anno received hate mail. The severity of the backlash registered at the level of genuine threats. These are documented facts, not the mythology that grew around the film.

Anno was not in a position to weather this calmly. He had already been working through severe depression during the production of the original series — during episode 16's production, he encountered a book that articulated his own psychological condition in clinical terms for the first time, [as he described in multiple interviews](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-interview-mental-health/). He later confirmed he "was almost mentally broken after the end of the Evangelion TV series." His own words about the original series: ["I tried to include everything of myself in Neon Genesis Evangelion — myself, a broken man who could do nothing for four years. A man who ran away for four years, one who was simply not dead... 'You can't run away,' came to me, and I restarted this production. It is a production where my only thought was to burn my feelings into film."](https://medium.com/@cyberdank/be-good-to-yourself-hideaki-annos-gospel-in-neon-genesis-evangelion-329ed9fd4300)

So the context is real. The hatred was real. The breakdown was real. But the document of it — the letters on screen — was constructed.

## What the Staging Means

There is a version of this story where Anno's decision to stage the letters rather than use real ones is a legal necessity with no artistic significance. That version is wrong.

Anno knew what he was making. The theatrical booklet's description of the letters as "simulations to explore the relationships between a work and its admirers" isn't a legal disclaimer — it's a description of the film's actual project. [*The End of Evangelion*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/32/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion_The_End_of_Evangelion) wasn't a documentary of what his fans had done to him. It was an examination of *what it meant* for that dynamic to exist at all.

This is the distinction that matters. A documentary of fan hatred would have been personal and specific — here are the people who threatened me, here is what they said. What Anno made was a model of the creator-audience relationship itself, populated with controlled actors (a trusted friend) and staged scenarios (letters that span appreciation and hostility in proportion Anno chose). The film doesn't ask: "Look what these particular fans did." It asks: "What does it mean to have your work owned by an audience that feels entitled to it?"

The [EvaWiki documentation of the sequence](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) notes that most of the letters were actually positive — expressions of appreciation for the film. The one genuine death threat message is buried at the very end. The balance is significant. Anno wasn't primarily constructing a record of hostility. He was constructing a full picture of the relationship — which contains both genuine love and genuine threat, often from the same people.

## The Hospital Scene

The film's opening scene makes this argument more directly than the letters do, and it's not a documentary at all — it's pure construction.

Shinji visits Asuka in the hospital. She's unconscious, in a coma. He pulls back her gown and masturbates over her unresponsive body, then looks at his hand and says "I'm the lowest." The scene is deliberately designed to lure the audience into the same viewing position as Shinji — [critics have noted](https://epicstream.com/article/what-is-end-of-evangelion-hospital-scene-significance) that Anno "uses viewers' expectations against them — luring them into a voyeuristic gaze at Asuka's exposed body, only to twist it into Shinji horrifically assaulting her, confronting the audience with their own implicit preconceptions and desires towards anime women."

The scene was the most controversial moment in a film designed to be controversial. When it aired on television in 2014, [broadcaster edits prompted fan backlash](https://soranews24.com/2014/08/27/youre-so-messed-up-complaints-come-after-broadcaster-edits-infamous-evangelion-scene/) of its own — fans objecting to the editing of a scene in which Shinji assaults an unconscious woman, incensed that their experience of the film was being altered. The recursiveness of that response — defending the inviolability of a scene that criticizes exactly that kind of audience ownership — is not lost on anyone paying attention.

Anno's intent with the scene wasn't simply shock. His argument is structural: the audience has spent twenty-five episodes watching Asuka and being invited to have feelings about her. In the film's first minutes, he makes that watching into something Shinji does, in its most degraded form. The viewer is not separate from Shinji here. The viewer has been doing a version of what Shinji is doing for the whole series. The scene's horror is about complicity.

This is the same move as the staged letters, more concentrated. Anno is not showing you what happened to him. He's showing you what you're doing.

## Komm, Süsser Tod

The film's most famous piece of music — the cheerful, upbeat song that plays over Third Impact, as humanity is dissolved into collective LCL — was written by Anno himself. The [original Japanese lyrics](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod) are a poem expressing extreme anxiety about human connection: fear of being disliked, fear of hurting others, a wish to dissolve back into nothingness and escape the pain of being an individual.

The [Hyperallergic analysis](https://hyperallergic.com/the-uneasy-heartbreak-of-hideaki-anno-end-of-evangelion/) identifies this as the film's emotional center: Anno's own voice, embedded in the apocalypse, confessing the feelings that the whole franchise was built around. The song's upbeat arrangement — cheerful, almost children's-music in its register — against its content of personal dissolution is the same formal move as the staged letters: controlled construction of something that could easily have been raw, made strange through aesthetic distance.

The [CBR production overview](https://www.cbr.com/end-of-evangelion-production-trivia/) describes EoE as fundamentally "about Hideaki Anno's Stress and Breakdown." This is true, but incomplete. The stress and breakdown are materials. What Anno made from them is a formal examination of why art produces that stress — what happens when the thing you made from your own pain becomes the property of people who have no interest in your pain, only in what the property does for them.

## The Argument Behind the Film

[*End of Evangelion*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion) was released in July 1997, slightly over a year after the TV ending's controversial reception. It [grossed ¥2.47 billion at the Japanese box office](https://www.the-numbers.com/movie/End-of-Evangelion-The-(1997-Japan)), becoming the fourth-highest-grossing Japanese feature film of that year — which is its own kind of irony, given that the film was partly about what an audience's appetite for something does to the person who made it. It has since accumulated a reputation as one of the most formally radical theatrical anime releases ever made. It also, through the medium of that reputation, became exactly the kind of object it was criticizing — a possession that fans would defend violently, claim with intensity, and use as identity.

What Anno did wasn't just survive the hostility and move on. He turned the experience itself into the architecture of the film, staging his own harassment in controlled conditions in order to make the dynamic visible. [The film's confrontational approach](https://jonwooder.medium.com/end-of-evangelion-death-threats-and-author-audience-relationships-9d3c7ea02552) anticipated by decades the pattern of creators being threatened by audiences that have confused love with ownership — but unlike most of those creators, Anno chose to analyze the structure of the relationship rather than simply endure it.

The staged letters aren't a legal workaround. They're the film's thesis in document form: what we're looking at isn't any specific fans' actual hatred, it's the *structure* of a relationship between a creator and an audience that has confused love with ownership. The friend who wrote the death threat letter knew exactly what they were writing. Anno chose what to include and in what proportion. The sequence is a curated model of the relationship, not a record of what happened.

That might be more disturbing than real hate mail would have been. Real hate mail comes from specific people being terrible. A simulation of hate mail comes from someone who understands it well enough to construct it — and has chosen to confront you with your own capacity for it, whether or not you ever actually sent anything.

Shinji, at the shore's end, asks Asuka: "I'm the lowest." She says: "Disgusting." He cries. The [final scene's analysis](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:Final_Scene_in_End_of_Evangelion) remains contested — theories range from Asuka accepting him to rejecting him to something in between. What's not contested is that Anno chose to put a human interaction at the end of an apocalypse film, two people alone in the wreckage of everything, unable to do anything except respond to each other honestly. After everything the film has said about the audience's relationship to its characters, after staging that relationship's worst possibilities — this is what it looks like when the staging stops.

Two people. No audience. Whatever happens between them is between them.
