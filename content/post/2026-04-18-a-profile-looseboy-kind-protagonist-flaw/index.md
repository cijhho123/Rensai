*A Profile* opens with a trick. Before it gives you the protagonist's perspective, it shows you the heroines'. [Three women](https://the1000thsummer.wordpress.com/2010/09/15/a-profile/), early in the game, are shown in the aftermath of pain — memories of something that went wrong, of trust that proved costly. Then the game drops you into Hayama Masayuki's point of view, and he is relentlessly, systematically optimistic. He studies. He sees the good in everyone around him. He believes in his friends. He is, by surface assessment, exactly the kind of visual novel protagonist the genre has trained readers to trust.

The dramatic irony is built in from the opening frame. You already know, because the game told you, that these women have been damaged by something. You already know, because the game told you, that Masayuki's perspective is not the full story. The question [*A Profile*](https://vndb.org/v1894) spends its three routes answering is: what exactly does it cost to be the kind of person who only sees the best?

## The Architecture of Blindness

Masayuki is described, in the game's own framing, as someone who "always does his best to see the best parts of everyone." This is not presented as a strength. [The game's thematic framing](https://theglorioblog.com/2013/07/06/exploring-visual-novels-6-a-profile/) is explicit: the story revolves around lies and trust, and specifically around "the way the protagonist only sees what he wants to see." That qualifier — *wants to see* — is the tell. This is not naïve optimism. It is selective vision. Masayuki is not failing to notice the negative; he is choosing not to look.

The distinction matters because it charges his gentleness with a moral quality the genre usually treats as neutral or positive. The gentle VN protagonist is a fixture: he is kind, self-sacrificing, believes in others, helps where he can. These traits are typically his virtues and the source of his narrative reward. Looseboy, in his first visual novel, takes this exact package and asks: what if this is how someone avoids responsibility? What if "seeing the best in people" is how a person insulates themselves from the discomfort of acknowledging what others are actually experiencing?

The answer is in the routes. Rizu's route — the forced-first entry — shows Masayuki failing to see how his adopted sister is actually responding to the world around her. Miku's route explores a heroine with an apparent multiple personality structure, and the layers of truth underneath what she presents to him. But the case that most sharply indicts Masayuki's defining trait is Miou's.

## Miou and the Consequence of Belief

[Miou Souma](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/VisualNovel/AProfile) was Masayuki's childhood friend. They dated. He, caught up in concern for Rizu, essentially abandoned her — not maliciously, but through the mechanism of seeing her as fine, as capable, as someone who didn't need his attention. She wasn't fine. She sought connection and attention elsewhere, eventually entering sex work. When her story surfaces in the final route, the full cost of Masayuki's optimistic blindness becomes auditable: his refusal to see what she was actually experiencing wasn't neutrality. It was abandonment performed in the register of trust.

This is [the game's central moral move](https://durimudream.wordpress.com/2012/10/22/visual-novel-sono-yokogao-wo-mitsumete-shimau-a-profile-kanzenban/): to reveal that Masayuki's characteristic gentleness — his belief in people, his trust, his refusal to impute bad motives or see deterioration — functions as a mechanism for not having to act. Seeing the best in someone can be a way of loving them. It can also be a way of never having to engage with the reality of their experience, which requires something more than goodwill. The game never explicitly frames this as cruelty. It is, in a way, worse: it treats his behavior as understandable, even sympathetic, and still insists on showing you what it cost.

[The 1000th Summer review](https://the1000thsummer.wordpress.com/2010/09/15/a-profile/) praises Masayuki as "one of my favorite eroge protagonists" and compares him to Shirou from [*Fate/Stay Night*](https://vndb.org/v11) — kind, altruistic, believes in others. That comparison is apt, and not necessarily as a compliment. Shirou's defining characteristic is also a refusal to see what he doesn't want to see; his idealism about heroism consistently produces harm. Both are gentle heroes whose gentleness is a form of evasion.

## The Opening That Deconstructs Itself

The narrative technique that makes this work is the pre-protagonist framing. By showing us the heroines' painful memories before granting us access to Masayuki's perspective, [the game establishes its ironic register from the first pages](https://the1000thsummer.wordpress.com/2010/09/15/a-profile/). We are never fully inside his optimism. We are always watching it from a position that knows more than he does.

This is a craft choice with consequences. The dramatic irony prevents the reader from inhabiting Masayuki's worldview as most VN readers inhabit a protagonist's perspective — as a neutral lens on reality rather than a partial and distorted one. The genre convention of protagonist-POV as reliable narrator is disrupted before it's established. We are positioned to see through him, which means we see his blindness as something active and particular rather than as simple naïveté.

[The Glorio Blog review](https://theglorioblog.com/2013/07/06/exploring-visual-novels-6-a-profile/) calls this "dramatic irony that sometimes feels forced and at other times is nothing short of brilliant." The uneven execution is real — Rizu's route is [widely criticized](https://vengilikes.wordpress.com/2015/02/20/short-review-a-profile/) as the game's weakest entry and functions primarily as scaffolding for Miou's payoff — but the core technique works precisely because it refuses the genre's standard offer of protagonist identification. You cannot fully trust Masayuki, which means you cannot fully trust his readings of the women around him. Everything is viewed through a lens you know is warped.

## Looseboy's First Argument

*A Profile* is [Looseboy's debut visual novel](https://animeataraxia.wordpress.com/2015/12/17/12-days-of-visual-novel-reviews-day-4-sharin-no-kuni-himawari-no-shoujo/), the first public statement from a writer who would go on to [*Sharin no Kuni*](https://vndb.org/v57) (a dystopian bildungsroman about a protagonist who enforces an unjust system he believes in) and [*G-senjou no Maou*](https://vndb.org/v213) (a thriller whose protagonist is morally compromised in ways the game withholds then reveals). The through-line is visible in retrospect: Looseboy is consistently interested in protagonists whose virtues are also their failures, whose defining good qualities are structurally entangled with the harm they do.

In *A Profile* the harm is intimate and personal. In *Sharin no Kuni* it is systemic. In [*G-senjou*](https://furusami.wordpress.com/2014/09/14/review-g-senjou-no-maou/) it is operatic. But the basic move is the same: take the trait the genre typically codes as simply positive (kindness, idealism, loyalty), and show what that trait looks like from the outside, from the perspective of people affected by its blind spots. Masayuki sees the best in everyone. The heroines' memories, shown to us before his perspective begins, are the ledger of what that cost.

The [6.67 VNDB score](https://vndb.org/v1894) from 1,415 votes accurately reflects *A Profile*'s uneven execution. The forced route order, the imouto content, the variable quality across routes — these are real limitations. But the game earns its place in Looseboy's catalog precisely because it is where this argument is stated most nakedly: the gentle hero who only sees what he wants to see, and the women who lived with the consequences of what he refused to look at.

Kindness, in *A Profile*, is never simply kind. It is also a way of not having to act. The game knows it. Masayuki doesn't. That gap is where the story lives.
