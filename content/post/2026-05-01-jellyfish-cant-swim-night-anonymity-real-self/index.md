Mahiru Kozuki painted a jellyfish mural when she was a child. Friends saw it, didn't know she made it, and mocked it. She heard every word. That moment—her work existing in public, her authorship hidden, and the verdict coming anyway—is the founding wound of *Jellyfish Can't Swim in the Night*, and it gets at something the show understands with unusual clarity: the problem with creating under your own name is that the criticism lands differently. It doesn't just critique the work. It critiques *you*. And in an era where your name is permanently attached to everything you've ever posted, that risk has become something that stops people from creating at all.

![Jellyfish Can't Swim in the Night key visual](https://images.amcnetworks.com/amcnetworks.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/03152024-JCSITN-193x300.jpg)

Doga Kobo's [Spring 2024 original anime](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=27601) — produced to mark the studio's 50th anniversary — follows four girls who form an anonymous multimedia collective called JELEE: Mahiru, a visual artist paralyzed by that childhood incident; Kano, a disgraced ex-idol trying to build something honest from the wreckage of her public persona; Kiui, a VTuber whose digital avatar enables an identity her physical body cannot yet claim; and Mei, a composer so deliberately opaque that her thinly drawn arc becomes the show's own blind spot — the one character the ensemble thesis can't quite account for. Together they make music, art, and a shared screen name. The hook is that none of them put their real names on any of it.

The show is often framed as being about [creative collaboration overcoming self-doubt](https://harperanimereviews.com/drifting-artists-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-spoiler-review/), which is accurate but undersells what it's actually arguing. The thesis isn't "working together builds confidence." The thesis is more specific and more unsettling: the anonymous self is the real one. The person Mahiru is when she's painting jellyfish and no one knows it's her is closer to who she actually is than the carefully managed version she presents in daily life. JELEE doesn't help these characters find themselves — it gives them somewhere to *put* themselves, for the first time.

## The Digital Permanence Problem

Series composition by [Yūki Yaku](https://collider.com/jellyfish-cant-swim-night-anime-everything-we-know/) (light novelist behind *Bottom-Tier Character Tomozaki*) gives the show a specific Gen Z texture that most anime about creative youth don't bother with. The characters aren't afraid of failure in the abstract. They're afraid of failure with receipts. Every bad take, every cringeworthy post, every publicly mocked piece of art is permanently searchable, permanently attached to your name, permanently part of the record. The show captures something real about what it means to grow up knowing that your embarrassments don't expire. The scene where Mahiru, watching a classmate casually share her art online without credit, freezes and says nothing — not because she's passive, but because claiming it would mean becoming the person who made the thing people might not like — is small and precise in the way only a creator who knows this feeling could write it.

This is why JELEE's anonymity isn't presented as a coping mechanism or a stepping stone to eventual disclosure. As one analysis of the series put it, it "[purports that maybe the greatest fantasy is being able to cast off both your old posts and the role society slots you into](https://shapes.inc/fandom/jellyfish-can-t-swim-in-the-night)" — a fantasy of starting over, of having the work evaluated without the accumulated weight of everything you've been before. The jellyfish name is deliberate: jellyfish don't have brains, don't have an official identity, don't swim so much as drift. They just glow.

## Each Mask and What It Enables

Director Ryōhei Takeshita — working with art director Yuji Kaneko on a [bioluminescent Shibuya](https://www.comicsbeat.com/anime-review-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-is-the-most-exciting-anime-you-probably-passed-over/) that exists one step beyond reality — gives each character's relationship to their mask its own visual grammar. Mahiru's art shows up in the world before she does, jellyfish glowing on walls that she walks past as if encountering a stranger. Kano's voice exists on the internet stripped of the idol identity that was destroying her. The show uses [split-second flashback frames and smartphone footage aesthetics](https://www.comicsbeat.com/anime-review-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-is-the-most-exciting-anime-you-probably-passed-over/) to indicate how perception shapes reality — not just what characters see, but how they've encoded their own traumas into how they see.

Kano's arc is the cleanest version of the argument. As a former idol, she had a persona that was professionally manufactured, commercially optimized, and ultimately used against her when her label dropped her. What she does inside JELEE is the opposite: she makes what she wants and attaches no name to it. The [Oshi no Ko comparison](https://harperanimereviews.com/drifting-artists-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-spoiler-review/) reviewers reach for is instructive — both are Doga Kobo productions exploring the gap between the idol on stage and the person doing the performing, but where *Oshi no Ko* is forensic and merciless, *Jellyfish Can't Swim* is warmer, more willing to believe the industry is reformable from within. Whether that's maturity or naivety is the show's central unresolved tension.

## The VTuber Case

Kiui Watase is where the show's argument about the anonymous self becomes sharpest and most politically legible. Her VTuber persona — Nox Ryugasaki, the self-proclaimed strongest VTuber — is visually and linguistically masculine. In [Episode 11](https://yuriempire.wordpress.com/2024/06/16/jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-episode-11-call-of-the-vtuber/), Kiui explicitly reveals that she refers to herself in masculine terms, that the VTuber avatar allows her to be recognized the way she actually experiences herself, and that she's exhausted by a world that refuses to take that seriously.

This isn't incidental. The show is threading VTubing and trans identity through the same needle: both are about using a constructed digital presentation to express something the physical, socially-legible body cannot say without risk. The VTuber avatar — usually discussed as parasocial entertainment or a business model — becomes in this reading a [form of self-authorship](https://press.invincible.ink/story-pile-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night/) that the show treats with genuine seriousness. Kiui doesn't hide behind Nox. Nox is closer to the truth of who Kiui is.

The fan discourse around this reading has been pointed. Comments across [Anime-Planet](https://www.anime-planet.com/forum/threads/jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night.345554/), [TV Tropes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Anime/JellyfishCantSwimInTheNight), and fan blogs describe the show as "nakedly queer" in its ensemble, with multiple characters' relationship to performance mapping cleanly onto trans and lesbian experience. This isn't a stretch reading — the show invites it explicitly through Kiui's storyline. The [Afictionado's essay on the series](https://theafictionado.wordpress.com/2024/09/05/the-mortifying-ordeal-of-being-known-art-and-identity-in-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night/) frames the core tension as "the desire to be witnessed, understood, and appreciated, and the desire to never be perceived, ever" — which is a pretty accurate description of both creative anxiety and the experience of being queer in public.

## Where the Show Flinches

*Jellyfish Can't Swim in the Night* doesn't entirely deserve the goodwill it earns. Two problems gnaw at it.

The first is structural. As one critic frames it through Stephen Greenblatt's "invisible bullets" concept — [the show's challenge to the idol industry is ultimately absorbed by it](https://press.invincible.ink/story-pile-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night/). The narrative builds toward a conclusion that JELEE should reveal itself, go public, and enter the entertainment machine it spent twelve episodes hiding from. The resolution is reconciliation rather than rejection — with the industry, with parental control, with the visibility these characters spent the whole show escaping. It stops short of the radical implications of its own premise. If anonymity is genuine liberation, then revealing who you are shouldn't feel like the natural conclusion. But the show arrives there anyway, because idol anime always do.

The second is harder to dismiss. Director Takeshita's previous work is [*Eromanga Sensei*](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=18836), and reviewers on [The Glorio Blog](https://theglorioblog.com/2024/04/10/first-look-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night/) noted — with some frustration — that the voyeuristic framing of its teenage characters sits uneasily beside the show's earnest message about dignity and creative vulnerability. This isn't a minor stylistic complaint. When a show is explicitly about artistic exposure, about the terror of having your private self examined in public, and it simultaneously frames its characters as objects of the camera's uninvited gaze, the contradiction says something uncomfortable about what "authentic creation" actually means in this medium. [The Comics Beat](https://www.comicsbeat.com/anime-review-jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night-is-the-most-exciting-anime-you-probably-passed-over/) review flagged it early, and it doesn't get less true as the show progresses.

## What Survives

None of that undoes what the show understands. [The ANN episode reviews](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/jellyfish-cant-swim-in-the-night/episodes-1-2/.209862) flagged it as among Spring 2024's strongest premieres, and that initial energy was built on something real: the show has a specific, articulable thesis about creative fear that most anime in this space don't have. Most music or idol anime tell you that passion wins, that hard work pays off, that friendship makes you better. *Jellyfish Can't Swim in the Night* tells you something more uncomfortable: that the version of yourself that exists without your name attached to it might be the most honest version you've ever been.

The jellyfish can't swim on its own. It glows in the dark. The show knows the difference between those things, and it knows that glowing without swimming is not failure. It's just a different way of being real in the water.
