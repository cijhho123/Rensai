The name that made them famous was not their own. "KinKi Kids" was given to them in 1992 by Masahiro Nakai of SMAP during a television appearance — two teenage Johnny's juniors from Kansai who had been performing backup for years under various temporary labels were handed an identity on live TV by someone who had the power to make it stick. They kept that name for 31 years and became one of Japan's best-selling artists of all time under it. Then, on New Year's Eve 2024, they announced they were abandoning it.

The name they chose to replace it was [DOMOTO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domoto_(band)) — their shared surname, which they had always had, which they are not related by, which they share only because they both come from families with that name. After three decades operating under an institutional identity handed to them by the industry's most powerful gatekeepers, they have decided that what they will be called going forward is just themselves.

This is the most compressed possible summary of what KinKi Kids' career has actually been about: 48 Guinness-record number-ones built by institutional compliance, and then the system breaking apart while the two people inside it kept choosing each other.

## The Machine and Its Product

[KinKi Kids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KinKi_Kids) debuted on July 21, 1997, with "Garasu no Shōnen" — a single that sold over [1.7 million copies](https://www.generasia.com/wiki/Glass_no_Shounen), charted at number one, and initiated what became the longest consecutive run of number-one singles from a debut in Japanese chart history. As of 2025, [DOMOTO holds the Guinness World Records](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/most-consecutive-no1-singles) for most consecutive number-one singles since debut (48 works) and most consecutive years with a Japanese number-one single (28 years, 1997-2024). [More than 30 million physical copies sold](https://starto.fandom.com/wiki/KinKi_Kids) — one of the best-selling acts in Asian music history.

These numbers are real and they are staggering. They are also, in large part, the product of a system rather than of any particular artistic vision. Johnny & Associates at the height of its power in the late 1990s was arguably the most effective pop manufacturing operation in Japanese entertainment history, and it deployed that power on KinKi Kids with extraordinary precision: [legendary songwriters](https://appears.wordpress.com/2022/08/06/johnnys-macro-snapshot/) like Tatsuro Yamashita, Kyohei Tsutsumi, and Koji Makaino were brought in to write their singles, ensuring each release carried the weight of established names alongside the excitement of a new act. The debut was not just a commercial launch — it was the inaugural release of [Johnny's Entertainment as a new record label](https://www.generasia.com/wiki/KinKi_Kids), making KinKi Kids both product and institutional proof-of-concept.

One [music blogger who analyzed their catalog](https://appears.wordpress.com/2022/08/06/johnnys-macro-snapshot/) described the effect precisely: despite the roster of legendary contributors, their songs carry "the same style and feel to them, like being run under day-one Instagram filters, rather than bearing the distinctive thumbprints of their creators." Tatsuro Yamashita wrote and arranged [the debut "Garasu no Shōnen"](http://kayokyokuplus.blogspot.com/2014/04/kinki-kidstatsuro-yamashita-glass-no.html) — one of the defining songs of his career as a producer — and its sound is identifiably Yamashita. But listen to it alongside six more consecutive Yamashita-adjacent KinKi Kids singles and the house style becomes legible above the individual fingerprints. This is not a failure of taste. It is the system working exactly as intended. The Johnny's house style absorbed the songwriters rather than the other way around. What emerged was identifiably, impeccably, KinKi Kids — which meant it was identifiably, impeccably, the institutional sound of late-1990s Johnny's pop.

## The One Who Kept Rewriting Himself

The paradox at the center of KinKi Kids is that one half of the duo has spent most of their career visibly straining against exactly this kind of institutional smoothing. [Tsuyoshi Domoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsuyoshi_Domoto) released his first solo single in 2002 — five years into KinKi Kids' Guinness record run — and penned and composed all of its tracks himself. He called his solo project [Endlicheri-Endlicheri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsuyoshi_Domoto) and began releasing work that had no particular relationship to the pop framework that made KinKi Kids famous. Then he renamed it 244 Endli-x. Then renamed it again to Tsuyoshi (剛紫). Each renaming marked a new phase of musical direction, each further from the institutional sound of the duo's official work.

This is not unusual behavior for an artist who feels constrained by their commercial identity. What is unusual is that Tsuyoshi was doing it while continuing to hold the Guinness record. The number-one singles kept coming for KinKi Kids while Tsuyoshi was releasing funk-inflected, self-composed solo work under names that most KinKi Kids fans may not have followed. He was living two simultaneous careers: the record-holding institutional idol and the restless solo artist who kept refusing to settle into a fixed identity.

[Koichi Domoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koichi_Domoto), by contrast, channeled his artistic energy into a different kind of commitment. The Endless SHOCK stage musical series — which he created, directed, starred in, and sustained for over 1800 performances at the Imperial Theatre — is [the most-performed musical in Japan](https://www.generasia.com/wiki/KinKi_Kids) and made him the youngest *zachō* (chairman-lead performer) in the Imperial Theatre's history. It is the opposite of the idol recording pipeline: a theatrical work with a single creative vision, performed live, controlled entirely by its creator. Koichi built an institution of his own, parallel to the one that made him famous, on terms he set himself.

Two responses to the same constraint, both remarkably generative.

## The Collapse of the Container

[Johnny Kitagawa's sexual abuse scandal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_Kitagawa_sexual_abuse_scandal), which became fully public in 2023, changed the structural reality in which KinKi Kids operated. The industry's power to suppress this knowledge had held for decades — [media silence was maintained](https://www.nippon.com/en/in-depth/d00945/) through the implicit threat of losing access to Johnny's talent roster, a form of commercial censorship that shows up as a case study in media power. When that silence broke, and the institutional reckoning arrived, [Johnny & Associates split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_%26_Associates) into Smile-Up (handling reparations) and Starto Entertainment (managing talent going forward).

Tsuyoshi [departed from Smile-Up in March 2024](https://cinemadailyus.com/news/kinki-kids-tsuyoshi-domoto-to-leave-ex-johnny-associate/) — the stated reason was his health, specifically a hearing impairment that had been affecting him for years. He did not join Starto. Koichi remained with Starto. The institutional separation was total: the two halves of KinKi Kids now have no common agency, no common corporate umbrella, no shared institutional identity beyond the work they choose to do together. The two halves of KinKi Kids now operate under different management structures, and they are continuing anyway.

This is the detail that matters most. The institutional container that produced KinKi Kids has broken apart, and KinKi Kids — now DOMOTO — is still functioning. They performed year-end dome concerts together at [Kyocera Dome Osaka](https://nantejapan.com/kinki-kids-renames-themselves-domoto/) in December 2024. They announced the name change together. The name change announcement at the end of 2024, made on the night that marked 31 years as KinKi Kids, [was framed by the members themselves](https://neotokyo2099.com/2025/01/01/kinki-kids-surprise-announcement-after-25-years-will-change-their-name-to-domoto-starting-in-the-summer-of-2025/) as: "we happened to meet with the same surname, and we came to the conclusion that we wanted to take a new step while cherishing this thing that is more than just words or letters."

What they were saying is that what they have is more fundamental than the name the industry gave them. They share a surname by coincidence, but they have made it mean something by choice.

## What the Record Actually Proves

The [48 consecutive Guinness-record number-one singles](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/504699-most-consecutive-years-with-a-japanese-no-1-single) are real, but they prove something specific: that a well-resourced institutional system, with the right promotional infrastructure and the right calibrated talent, can produce sustained commercial dominance. This is not a dismissal of KinKi Kids — what they were asked to do is extraordinarily difficult, the commercial longevity reflects genuine audience connection across nearly three decades, and their concerts [regularly fill domes](https://www.tokyohive.com/article/2024/11/kinki-kids-to-reunite-with-year-end-and-new-year-dome-concerts-new-song-release-anticipated). But the record reveals what the system produces when it succeeds, not what either member of the duo would have built entirely on their own.

Tsuyoshi's multiple self-reinventions suggest someone who, given full latitude, operates very differently from the institutional product. Koichi's SHOCK theater career — 1800+ performances of a musical he controls — suggests someone whose creative energy is directorial and auteurist rather than promotional. What KinKi Kids the duo produced was not exactly either of these things. It was the shared artifact of two distinct artists operating under a single commercial identity provided by the industry's most powerful institutional force.

That institution is now restructuring around its own reckoning. The name KinKi Kids is being retired. The duo continues as DOMOTO. In the larger story of [Johnny's and the J-pop idol system](https://www.japansubculture.com/the-pioneer-and-the-serial-predator-of-the-japanese-idol-industry-johnny-kitagawas-legacy-of-exploitation-and-control/), this is one of the more honest endings: the machine built something that outlasted the machine. The two people it produced are still choosing to work together, but now under their own name, after the institutional framework that defined them collapsed into its own contradictions.

The record stays in the Guinness Book. The name is their own now. These are not unrelated facts.
