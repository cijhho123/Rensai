"A normal rom-com is embarrassing," [Kotoyama said in an interview](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html), "and so I can't draw it." He said this while working on *Call of the Night*, a manga about a sleepless middle schooler who falls into the orbit of a vampire who needs to make him love her in order to feed. He was explaining, without irony, why vampirism was the natural approach.

This is useful information about everything he has ever made.

![Nazuna Nanakusa - Call of the Night character poster](https://i0.wp.com/news.qoo-app.com/en/wp-content/uploads/sites/3/2022/02/Nazuna-Nanakusa.jpg?resize=640%2C907&ssl=1)

Kotoyama has two major manga series: [*Dagashi Kashi*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagashi_Kashi), which ran in *Weekly Shonen Sunday* from 2014 to 2018 and [reached four million copies in print](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/manga.php?id=17671), and [*Call of the Night*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night), which ran in the same magazine from 2019 to 2024 and reached 5.2 million copies. On the surface these works seem as different as a dagashiya shop and a 3 AM street lamp. One is a candy comedy set in the rural summer. The other is a vampire romance set in late-night Tokyo. But both are built from the exact same structural DNA, and both exist for exactly the same creative reason.

## The Template

The blueprint runs like this. A young male protagonist has a thing he's supposed to want and doesn't. An eccentric female outsider arrives, passionately committed to a specific, niche obsession. She begins the slow work of dismantling his resistance. Romance develops sideways — through the medium of her obsession rather than through any conventional romantic staging. The series asks whether genuine connection is possible when it's mediated by something so strange.

In *Dagashi Kashi*, [Kokonotsu Shikada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagashi_Kashi) is the reluctant heir to a dagashiya — a traditional Japanese sweet shop that has been in his family for nine generations. He wants to become a manga artist. Hotaru Shidare arrives from the city, a daughter of a major confectionery company, unhinged in her devotion to cheap candy. She wants to recruit his father to her company; she will succeed if she can first make Kokonotsu love dagashi enough to inherit the shop. The entire series is their argument about what cheap candy means, conducted with Hotaru's operatic intensity and Kokonotsu's perpetual bewilderment.

In *Call of the Night*, Ko Yamori can't sleep. He wanders out of his house at midnight and meets Nazuna, a vampire who makes her living from blood-drink cuddling. Ko wants Nazuna to turn him into a vampire. She can only do so if he genuinely falls in love with her — and she is, Kotoyama confirmed in the same interview, deliberately inexperienced in affection, because "I thought the story would end instantly if Nazuna was experienced in being affectionate." The entire series is their argument about what the night means, conducted with Nazuna's strange mix of professional distance and dawning vulnerability.

Replace dagashi with vampirism and the template is identical.

## What's Disappearing

Neither series is playing with a neutral subject. In both cases, Kotoyama picks an obsession that exists in friction with the mainstream of contemporary Japan — something the modern world has either left behind or refused to look at directly.

[Dagashiya shops date back to the Edo period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagashi) and were genuine institutions of Japanese childhood through the postwar boom era. They were where after-school hours went. Then the bubble economy arrived, convenience stores proliferated, the birthrate fell, and dagashiyas began to disappear. Around [fifty dagashiya](https://metropolisjapan.com/those-dang-dagashiya/) remain in the Tokyo area today, down from what were once thousands across the country. *Dagashi Kashi* arrives into this gap. The candy Hotaru is so manic about is already a dying thing. Her passion is a form of grief that hasn't recognized itself yet, and her mission — to make a young man care about something his generation has mostly abandoned — carries a cultural weight the comedy never fully names but always holds.

[The night in *Call of the Night*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/50190) is under a similar kind of pressure. Ko's insomnia isn't framed as a disorder — it's framed as a correct response to a world that doesn't fit him. Kotoyama is writing about a Japanese school system that demands total compliance: the entrance exams, the uniform rules, the studied friendships managed for social outcome rather than actual affinity. Ko's daytime life was suffocating him before the series began. The night is where the pressure lifts. But the night, as an unstructured social space — as a place for people who don't fit anywhere — is increasingly marginal. Convenience stores have colonized it. Surveillance has followed. The specific freedom of 3 AM Tokyo that Ko discovers with Nazuna is real, but it's also fragile and already half-gone.

Both series are elegies written in the form of rom-coms.

## The Oblique Approach

[Kotoyama has cited](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) Dragon Ball, Ping Pong, and Katsuhiro Otomo's *Akira* as formative influences. That last one is telling. *Akira* is a work about alienated youth in a future Tokyo that has failed them — about what happens when the institutions stop working and the gap between the world's promises and its realities becomes too wide. It is not a quiet manga. But its sensibility — the urban night as both danger and liberation, young people who don't fit the system finding their own compressed communities — runs underneath *Call of the Night* in ways that Kotoyama's cleaner, lighter aesthetics deliberately soften.

He says he was drawn to *Call of the Night* because he wanted to draw multi-level apartment buildings. That the vampire appeared "just — poof, for no reason." What this tells you is that Kotoyama builds from specific visual and atmospheric desires rather than from plot or theme. He wanted the look of late-night urban Japan; he found the content that justified drawing it. This is also true of *Dagashi Kashi*, which is fundamentally a series built around Kotoyama's desire to draw Hotaru's face while she delivers speeches about candy. The emotional texture comes first. The narrative framework is what makes it publishable.

He is a mangaka who cannot approach what he cares about directly. He has to come at it sideways. In *Dagashi Kashi* the approach is comedy and candy trivia; in *Call of the Night* it's vampire mythology and the erotic geometry of night-street encounters. In both cases, the real subject — which is something like: *how do you find genuine connection in a world designed to prevent it?* — never gets stated outright. It lives in the margins of the obsession.

## The Visual Signature

![Dagashi Kashi manga cover](https://blog.alltheanime.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/Dagashi-Kashi-cover.jpg)

The [Kotoyama 10th anniversary exhibition](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2024/7/10/dagashi-kashi-call-of-the-night-manga-author-kotoyama-0th-anniversary-exhibition) — which traveled from Tokyo to Osaka to Nagoya between 2024 and 2025, celebrating both series under a single retrospective — made the creative continuity legible in physical form. Standing in a room where Hotaru Shidare and Nazuna Nanakusa occupied the same walls, you could see the shared visual logic: both characters have what Kotoyama describes as a [distinctive "crazed look"](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kotoyama), a slight unhinging in the eyes that marks them as outside the register of ordinary social presentation. Both operate through intensity that most people around them can't fully parse.

Hotaru is the prototype. She is a girl from the legitimate world who has nonetheless refused to be normal about the thing she loves. She knows candy the way scholars know texts — with the specific feverishness of someone who has made an arbitrary thing carry all the weight of what matters. Nazuna is what happens when Kotoyama takes that same character and gives her something to lose. The manic energy is still there. But now there's the love condition working against her, now there's the existential loneliness of a vampire who built her whole professional life around controlled transactions because genuine intimacy keeps going wrong.

The exhibition existed to argue, visually, that Kotoyama has been drawing one character across two series. The argument is correct.

## The Embarrassing Rom-Com

What's interesting about "a normal rom-com is embarrassing and so I can't draw it" is that it's also a description of a constraint and a creative solution. Kotoyama is not saying he doesn't want to write romance. He writes almost nothing else. He's saying he cannot access the emotional material directly — it requires cover. Candy. Vampires. Something strange enough that the feelings can sneak in under the weirdness before anyone notices what the series is actually doing.

*Dagashi Kashi* is about a young man learning to care about something he was told to care about, and discovering that the caring is genuine. [*Call of the Night*](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/people.php?id=142614) is about a young man who stops performing the life he was assigned and finds out who he actually is in the space left over. Both stories are, at root, about the specific and ordinary human problem of figuring out what you actually want from a world that keeps pre-deciding for you. That *Call of the Night* ended in 2024 and Kotoyama [immediately launched a new short series in its universe](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-06-30/kotoyama-launches-new-short-series-for-the-call-of-the-night-manga/.226129) suggests he is not done with the night world yet — which makes sense, because the night world is not done with the problem.

Kotoyama cannot draw this straight. So he draws it as candy trivia, and as vampires who need your love to feed. The trick has always been the same: find something strange, put two people in it, and let the strangeness hold the weight of everything he can't say directly. That is what the embarrassing rom-com looks like when someone who understands himself makes it.
