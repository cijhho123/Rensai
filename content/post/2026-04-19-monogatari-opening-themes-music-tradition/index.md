["Renai Circulation"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renai_Circulation) went viral on Chinese TikTok in 2018. Then again, globally, in 2021 via an English cover. A Bakemonogatari character poll held by anime merchandise retailer Toranoana found that some voters cited the song as the reason they voted for Nadeko. The opening theme for a single two-episode arc from a 2009 anime became, over the course of a decade, one of the most recognizable anime songs on the internet — not because it was the catchiest song or had the biggest budget, but because it was doing something specific and unusual: it was a psychological portrait of a character disguised as a cute J-pop number.

That's what every Monogatari opening does. Not just aesthetically. Formally. And fifteen years later, [YOASOBI's "Undead"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undead_%28Yoasobi_song%29) — the ending theme for Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season — is the clearest proof that the franchise understands exactly what it's been doing all along.

![Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season key visual](https://static.animecorner.me/2024/03/1711264240-94259.jpg)

## The Tradition, Briefly

Every Monogatari story arc has its own opening theme song. Every song — with [one notable exception](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Category:Opening_Theme_Songs) — is performed in-character by the voice actress of that arc's female protagonist. Every song was composed by [Satoru Kōsaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satoru_K%C5%8Dsaki) with lyrics by Meg Rock. Every opening sequence is storyboarded and directed by a different creator, each with their own visual signature.

The exception proves the rule: "White Lies," the opening for the Shinobu Time arc in Monogatari Second Season, was not performed by Maaya Sakamoto — who voices Shinobu — because [Sakamoto does not do in-character singing work](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Category:Opening_Theme_Songs). The series had to find an alternative. That the production team treated this as a meaningful absence, something that required explicit workaround rather than just assigning a different singer, demonstrates how central the in-character tradition was to the series' understanding of what these songs are supposed to do.

What they're supposed to do is give you the character's internal monologue in a form the character would never state outright. [As one fan analysis of the visual direction observes](https://boosmahn.wordpress.com/2021/06/22/creator-styles-in-the-openings-of-the-monogatari-series/), the Monogatari openings function as "meaningful narrative extensions" rather than promotional tools — transforming opening sequences into character studies set to music. Each opening is, in a specific and technically precise way, an unreliable narration. The character is singing their own story in a key that reveals what they're concealing.

## How It Works

"Staple Stable," Hitagi Senjougahara's opening for the first Bakemonogatari arc, is polite, controlled, slightly cold. It matches her personality exactly as she presents it in the first episode — the girl who warns Araragi not to tell anyone about her condition, who maintains a careful emotional distance, who deploys words like weapons. The song doesn't break the mask. It demonstrates the mask.

"Ambivalent World," Suruga Kanbaru's opening, is frenetic and layered with barely-controlled energy. Kanbaru in the series presents as exuberant and open, but the opening captures the tension underneath — the thing about loving Senjougahara that she can't quite manage to talk about cleanly. The musical anxiety is doing what the character's dialogue redirects.

"Renai Circulation" is the canonical example. [Kana Hanazawa performs it as Nadeko](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Renai_Circulation) in the most deliberately cute voice she can manage — bouncy, innocent, saccharine. It is Nadeko's performance of being a girl with a simple, sweet crush, delivered with such precision that it becomes ironic on its face. [As DoctorKev's analysis of the Monogatari Season One music notes](https://medium.com/anitay-official/the-music-of-monogatari-season-one-4083c44ce846), the opening "reflects her horrible personality" — which is to say, it reflects the performance of personality she has constructed to avoid being seen. The song is not Nadeko; it's Nadeko's mask. Which is exactly what makes it so effective when the mask comes off in Otorimonogatari three seasons later.

This is the formal move the tradition enables: the opening tells you who the character thinks they are, or who they're performing themselves to be. The subsequent arc tells you who they actually are. The gap between those two things is where the story lives.

## Off & Monster Season's Continuation

Off & Monster Season uses the tradition with self-awareness. [The first opening, "icecream°," is performed by Yuka Iguchi (Tsukihi Araragi) and Saori Hayami (Yotsugi Ononoki)](https://animeliryca.com/iguchi-yuka-icecream/) — the two characters at the center of the Orokamonogatari arc. Tsukihi is volatile and affectionate; Yotsugi is a corpse puppet who insists she has no feelings. Together they perform a light, floating pop confection that sits oddly against both characters' self-presentations. The arc that follows is about Yotsugi discovering she does need things — that a constructed thing can become real through being tested. The opening gives her a song that doesn't match who she claims to be, before the arc demonstrates that she was wrong about herself.

"caramel ribbon cursetard," the second opening, [features both Nadeko (Kana Hanazawa) and Yotsugi (Saori Hayami)](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-06-30/monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-anime-reveals-promo-video-opening-theme-song-for-/.212633) performing together for the Nademonogatari arc. Two characters who have both claimed a kind of emotional absence — Nadeko's cultivated passivity, Yotsugi's programmed inexpressiveness — singing together. The pairing is thematically precise: both are being asked, in this arc, to reckon with what they've actually become.

"Banshi no Theme," the third opening for Shinobumonogatari, carries a different weight. Shinobu's opening tradition has always had to navigate the gap between her two identities — Kiss-Shot Acerola-Orion Heart-Under-Blade, the godlike 600-year-old vampire, and Shinobu Oshino, the child-form companion. [The series has treated her arcs with particular care](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobu_Oshino) because no other character spans so much history. "Banshi no Theme" is structured to feel like a reckoning, not an introduction — a song that knows what Shinobumonogatari is asking of her and doesn't soften it.

## YOASOBI and What It Means

Then there is ["Undead."](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/UNDEAD)

[YOASOBI became Japan's biggest pop act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idol_%28Yoasobi_song%29) in 2023, when their Oshi no Ko opening "Idol" became the fastest Japanese song to reach 300 million YouTube views, topped five Billboard Japan charts simultaneously, and won Song of the Year at the 38th Japan Gold Disc Award. This is not a niche act. This is the act that defined Japanese mainstream pop for a year. Bringing them to Monogatari is the most high-profile musical collaboration the franchise has ever undertaken.

["Undead" was written based on two short stories by Nisio Isin himself](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-07-01/monogatari-off-and-monster-season-anime-gets-new-short-stories-about-yoasobi-theme-song/.212650): "Nadeko Past" and "Shinobu Future." YOASOBI's typical working method involves Ayase composing music based on original short fiction — the "Idol" track was based on the Oshi no Ko manga's conceit. Nisio Isin commissioned specifically for the song means the "Undead" lyrics are not an adaptation of something existing; they are new canonical Monogatari fiction, written specifically for YOASOBI to set to music.

The specific structure of the song: [it is narrated from Yotsugi Ononoki's perspective](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/UNDEAD), with the first verse telling the story of Nadeko Sengoku (based on Nisio Isin's short story "Nadeko Past") and the second verse telling the story of Shinobu Oshino (based on "Shinobu Future"). A doll — something not designed for emotions, the series' meditation on constructed interiority — watching and describing two characters who spent decades defining themselves by absence and pain, and who are now, in Off & Monster Season, finding out who they are on their own terms.

This is not a coincidence. The entire run of Off & Monster Season is about the female characters asking what they've become after Araragi left. Yotsugi Ononoki has, over the course of her arcs, become the series' most concentrated exploration of that question. And the ending theme — the song that sends each episode off — is Yotsugi, watching, describing, singing. The character who was never supposed to have a perspective is the one who synthesizes the season's meaning.

## What the Music Proves About the Series

[The "Renai Circulation" phenomenon](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/renai-circulation) — an opening from a 2009 anime going viral on TikTok twelve years later — happens because the song is doing real work. It's not just a catchy anime OP. It's a character study encoded as pop music, designed to mean one thing to viewers watching the Bakemonogatari arc and something entirely different once they've seen Otorimonogatari. It rewards the kind of repeat engagement that builds genuine fandom, because every time you hear it with more of the series' context, the mask in the performance becomes more legible.

Off & Monster Season's musical choices — both the continuation of the character-opening tradition and the YOASOBI ending — demonstrate the franchise's confidence in that formal approach. Bringing in Japan's biggest pop act is a mainstream signal, yes. But the song YOASOBI was asked to write is not mainstream pop for a mainstream anime. It's a doll's-eye-view meditation on survival and self-knowledge, narrated by a corpse puppet, structured around two characters' journeys toward accepting that their lives are their own now.

That's the Monogatari tradition, fifteen years in, doing exactly what it was always doing: using music to say what the story can't say directly, and trusting the audience to hear both things at once.
