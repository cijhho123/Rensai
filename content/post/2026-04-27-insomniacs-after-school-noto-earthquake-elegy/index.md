The manga ended in August 2023. Five months later, the place it was set in was hit by Japan's most powerful earthquake since 2011.

That is not a metaphor. *Insomniacs After School* (*Kimi wa Hōkago Insomnia*) — Makoto Ojiro's four-year romance about two insomniacs finding each other under the dark skies of the [Noto Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noto_Peninsula) — concluded its final chapter on August 7, 2023. On January 1, 2024, a [7.5-magnitude earthquake struck the Noto Peninsula in Ishikawa Prefecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Noto_earthquake), killing more than 700 people, destroying over 200,000 structures, and physically reshaping the coastline of the peninsula the series spent years meticulously documenting. Nanao City — the real-world basis for the fictional setting — recorded 76 confirmed deaths. The Noto Railway's Anamizu Station, featured in the series' location-specific background art, was severely damaged.

What was already a beautiful romance became something else: an archive.

## Why Setting Is the Argument

Most anime and manga set in real places treat those places as backdrop. The Shibuya scramble, Kyoto's temple districts, Tokyo Tower at sunset — these appear because they are legible symbols, shorthand for "modern Japan" or "traditional Japan." They are not chosen because the work has something to say about them specifically.

*Insomniacs After School* was built on a fundamentally different logic. Ojiro chose Nanao City — a coastal city of fewer than 50,000 people on the Noto Peninsula — not for symbolic resonance but for a specific, technical reason: the peninsula sits on the Sea of Japan coast, far from the light domes of major urban centers, and [offers amateur astrophotographers some of the best dark skies in the Hokuriku region](https://www.animepilgrimage.com/Locations/insomniacs-after-school). The series needed those skies. The story could not have happened anywhere else, because the story was, in part, about what that sky makes possible — about what it feels like to see the Milky Way from a high school rooftop, to photograph star fields over a Jōmon-era timber circle, to have the cosmos become intimate.

This produced a level of location specificity that is genuinely unusual in manga. [Fans and researchers have documented over 40 real-world pilgrimage locations from the series](https://seichi.moe/media/2033/), including Nanao Station, the Misaki-machi coastline, and the rocky shores of the Noto-Kongo Coast. The fictional Kuyou High School is transparently based on the real Ishikawa Prefectural Nanao High School. The pivotal scene where Ganta photographs the Milky Way rising over ancient wooden poles — one of the series' most reproduced images — was drawn from the [Mawaki Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mawaki_Site) in Noto Town, a real National Historic Site where reconstructed Jōmon-period timber poles stand in a coastal meadow. Ojiro was not gesturing at Noto. She was documenting it.

The anime adaptation, produced by [Liden Films and directed by Yūki Ikeda](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=25119), aired in Spring 2023 and extended this commitment. Background art was praised across multiple reviews for its "[truly exquisite](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/01/anime-analysis-insomniacs-after-school-2023-by-yuki-ikeda/)" rendering of the specific landscape — coastlines, town streets, the particular quality of the peninsula's night skies. [The Noto Railway collaborated with the production to run themed train wraps](https://epicstream.com/article/insomniacs-after-school-promotes-nanao-city-train-collab) on rolling stock in Nanao City, turning the actual trains that appear in the series into mobile advertisements for a show that had depicted them lovingly and accurately. The local city had, by the end of 2023, embraced the series as a civic document.

## The Specificity Was Always the Theme

There is something important to understand about what *Insomniacs After School* was actually doing with its precise attention to place. The series is not just set in Noto — it is, at its thematic core, about the irreproducibility of particular moments and places. Ganta is a photographer. Isaki is dying — not in melodramatic terms, but in the low-level mortality anxiety of someone with a heart condition who has learned, at a young age, that the present is not permanent. The series' most significant artistic choice is that [Ojiro refuses to use Isaki's condition for tragedy](https://www.yalsa.ala.org/thehub/2023/11/09/great-graphic-novels-ggn2024-featured-review-insomniacs-after-school-by-makoto-ojiro/). In a genre where illness consistently serves as countdown clock — *Your Lie in April*, *A Silent Voice*, a dozen lesser imitators — the heart condition here is not a death sentence deployed to generate pathos. Instead, it becomes the lens through which the present is experienced as worth preserving.

Ganta's photography is the mechanism for this. Photographs are, by definition, documents of the irretrievable — light captured at a moment that cannot be repeated. The Milky Way over the Mawaki timber poles. The Sea of Japan at three in the morning. A specific face in a specific light. The series was always arguing that these particular things — this place, this person, this sky — matter and are worth capturing before they change. The aesthetic philosophy was embedded in the medium the protagonist practiced.

The earthquake made this reading impossible to ignore. When the Noto Railway's infrastructure was damaged, when buildings that had appeared in the series' backgrounds were destroyed, when the geography of the coastline was literally altered by the seismic event — the photographs Ojiro had spent four years taking through her characters suddenly meant something different. They had documented a world that no longer fully exists.

## When Fiction and Place Become Inseparable

The response from Ojiro and publisher Shogakukan was immediate and significant. [The Insomniacs After School Disaster Area Support Event ran from May 3 to June 2, 2024](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2024/4/17/insomniacs-after-school-event-in-nanao-will-raise-funds-for-noto-earthquake-relief), replacing the deferred Seihakusai Festival in Nanao. [Proceeds from manga sales were donated to the Nanao City Disaster Relief Fund](https://otakuusamagazine.com/insomniacs-after-school-manga-creator-doing-special-event-to-help-with-earthquake-relief/) for an entire year — from April 2024 to April 2025. A [special video combining scenes from the manga, the anime adaptation, and the 2023 live-action film](https://www.imdb.com/news/ni64413989/) was released, subtitled *"You Are Not Alone."*

That subtitle matters. It is addressed not primarily to the characters — neither Ganta nor Isaki is alone in the story — but to the people of Nanao, reading or watching a series that depicts their home, after that home had been catastrophically damaged. The fiction became a form of solidarity because the fiction had always been, implicitly, a love letter to a specific place and its people.

![The Noto Peninsula coastline, the geographical setting that defines the series' aesthetic](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/The_Noto_Peninsula%2C_Ishikawa_Prefecture%3B_September_2019_(08).jpg)

This kind of relationship between fiction and place — where the fiction becomes genuinely useful to the place's residents after a disaster — is rare. It happens when a work has been specific enough, attentive enough, that the people who live in that place recognize themselves in it. The Noto Railway collaboration and the civic embrace of the series before the earthquake were evidence that this had already happened. The relief efforts after the earthquake confirmed it in a different register.

[Fan reports from pilgrimage sites note specific damage](https://seichi.moe/media/2033/): the pharmacy building that appeared in one scene has been torn down, possibly earthquake-related; the game center shown in another scene needed repairs after the New Year's disaster; a gate that appears in the background of early chapters collapsed. These are not consequential facts in isolation — buildings are replaced and damaged all the time. But in the context of a series whose entire aesthetic project was the precise documentation of these specific places, they register as something closer to loss.

## What Place-Specific Fiction Does

There is a broader argument to be made here about what it means to set fiction in real, specific, carefully observed places — and what that fiction becomes when those places change.

Most anime settings that achieve this kind of documentary specificity do so in major cities: Makoto Shinkai's [meticulous Tokyo renderings in *Your Name* and *Weathering With You*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makoto_Shinkai), the Washinomiya shrine in *Lucky Star*, the Yotsuya locations in *Monogatari*. Urban landmarks are resilient; they are maintained, reconstructed, defended. When those settings are disrupted, it is usually by fictional means — the city destroyed in *Your Name* exists only in the film, so the grief is entirely contained.

*Insomniacs After School* chose a small, economically vulnerable, seismically precarious coastal city on a peninsula that geologists had been [warning was overdue for a major earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Noto_earthquake) for years. The series arrived, became beloved, sparked tourism, collaborated with local infrastructure — and then concluded five months before the worst natural disaster in that region in modern history.

[The live-action film adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insomniacs_After_School_(film)), directed by Chihiro Ikeda and released in June 2023, spent an entire month filming on location in Nanao City. It was selected for the Fantasia Film Festival 2023. The production crew documented the city with the high-resolution specificity that live-action location shoots require. Within eight months of the film's release, the location that had been so carefully captured was under rubble.

[The manga won the Mandō Kobayashi Manga Grand Prix in 2023](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/manga.php?id=25118). It was listed in the [2024 YALSA Great Graphic Novels for Teens top ten](https://www.yalsa.ala.org/thehub/2024/02/22/2024-great-graphic-novels-for-teens/), [recognized by the ALA alongside graphic novels for its unusual emotional precision](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-03-11/insomniacs-after-school-manga-makes-yalsa-2024-great-graphic-novels-for-teens-top-10-list/.208521). These are recognitions of craft and storytelling. But what the earthquake revealed is that the series had also been doing something that awards and ratings cannot fully capture: it had been paying attention to a place that deserved attention, documenting what was there before it changed.

## The Light Already Left the Source

There is a fact about stargazing that the series uses deliberately. When you look at the night sky above Noto — or anywhere — you are not seeing the stars as they are. You are seeing light that left those stars years, centuries, or millennia ago. The star might already be dead. The light you are receiving is a record of something that no longer exists in its original form.

Photography works the same way. The photographs Ganta takes throughout *Insomniacs After School* are records of light that has already moved on by the time anyone looks at them. This is not a melancholy observation in the series — it is a reason to keep taking photographs. The record is valuable precisely because the moment it captures cannot be held in any other way.

[Makoto Ojiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makoto_Ojiro) built a series around this understanding of time and place, and that series became, in the event, a document of a place that would soon need to be remembered. The light above Nanao's fishing harbors, the specific way it fell on the Noto Railway's rolling stock, the dark field around the Mawaki timber circle at two in the morning — all of it was captured in the panels and animation frames that exist now in volumes and streaming libraries, regardless of what happened to the physical places they depicted.

That is not consolation. It does not rebuild houses or return what was lost. But it is, in its modest way, exactly what the series always claimed photography was for: holding something true about a particular place and moment, before the light moves on.
