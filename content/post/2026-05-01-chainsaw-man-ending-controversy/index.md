The chapter dropped on March 24, 2026, and within hours it had crashed multiple manga reading sites. By the next morning, the discourse was everywhere: *Chainsaw Man* had ended, [no Part 3 was announced](https://animecorner.me/chainsaw-man-manga-officially-ends-no-part-3-announced/), and roughly half the fandom felt robbed. The other half felt something harder to name — an emotion that Japanese readers described, in discussions across social media, as a kind of ["empty awe."](https://jp.ibtimes.com/chainsaw-man-finale-japanese-fans-left-state-empty-awe-100308)

The Evangelion comparison came almost immediately. Not because the two series are structurally similar, but because they share a specific kind of ending: the kind where the author stops telling the story you thought you were reading and starts talking to you directly. [Multiple critics noted](https://gizmodo.com/chainsaw-man-chapter-232-ending-evangelion-2000737905) that *Chainsaw Man*'s finale felt like what Anno did with the original TV ending of *NGE* — an abandonment of the plot in favor of something more interior, more uncomfortable, and considerably less satisfying on first contact.

I want to argue that this comparison is exactly right, and that understanding it is the key to reading what Fujimoto actually accomplished.

![Denji and Asa in Chainsaw Man's final chapter](https://comicbook.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2026/03/Chainsaw-Man-Ending-Denji-Asa.jpg?w=1024)

## What Happened in Chapter 232

First, what the chapter actually contains. After Pochita erases himself — and the Chainsaw Devil along with him — [Denji resets](https://gamerant.com/chainsaw-man-ending-explained/). He wakes up in the shack from Chapter 1. Blood on his hands. The zombie encounter plays out again, except this time it's Power who saves him, not Pochita. She gives him some of her blood, claims ownership of his life until she dies, and the series ends.

Denji has a vague sense of having "a good and a bad dream." He doesn't remember any of it. The eight years of story are gone. Major subplots — the search for Power, [Nayuta's fate, the Fake Chainsaw Man](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-harsh-realities-reading-part-2/) — are left unaddressed.

Critics of the ending have a point: [fan forums filled with threads](https://www.resetera.com/threads/chainsaw-man-part-2-to-end-with-chapter-232.1458760/) cataloguing the threads Fujimoto dropped. This is not a neat resolution. If you read *Chainsaw Man* for the mythology, for the devil system, for Makima's legacy playing out across Part 2, the finale is a door that doesn't open.

But the people asking "what about the Fake Chainsaw Man" are, in the gentlest possible terms, missing the point.

## The Evangelion Problem

When *Neon Genesis Evangelion* aired its final two episodes in 1996, Anno's production had run out of resources and, by his own admission, he was in the grip of a serious depressive episode. The result was a finale that abandoned the Angel War entirely and retreated into Shinji's psychology — a series of internal monologues, abstract imagery, and an ending that culminated in "Congratulations" while the promised catastrophe went unshown.

Fans were furious. Death threats were sent to Gainax. [Anno later made *End of Evangelion*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion) partly in response to that pressure. But here's what happened over time: the original ending is now widely considered not just defensible but arguably superior. Its refusal to deliver spectacle in favor of interior resolution became legible as exactly what it always was — a work about depression making an ending out of depression.

*Chainsaw Man*'s ending is the same structural move, made from a completely different trauma.

[As several critics have argued](https://gamerant.com/chainsaw-man-ending-evangelion-similarities/), what both endings share is their metatextual layer: a creator using the work's conclusion to address their own relationship with the work itself. Anno was dealing with burnout and self-loathing. Fujimoto, after eight years and massive global success, was dealing with the specific madness of becoming a cultural phenomenon — something his own interviews make clear without him ever having to say it directly.

The clue is in Pochita's farewell. Before he erases himself, he tells Denji — and through Denji, tells the reader — something like this: *even after getting everything you wanted, you were still unhappy*. The simple life, the shack, the hunger and the cold and the single dog companion: that was the version of Denji who knew how to be at peace. Everything the series gave him only broke him further.

[As Isaiah Colbert writes at *Aftermath*](https://aftermath.site/chainsaw-man-part-two-tatsuki-fujimoto-shonen-jump/), this is Fujimoto's own experience projected outward: the realization that achieving massive acclaim created an unsustainable loop, and that sometimes the only escape is to let the dream go.

There is a through-line here that goes beyond interpretation. [In interviews](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-creator-shonen-jump-career/), Fujimoto has talked about how Denji's desire to "become a normal person" mirrors his own experience growing up as an otaku in a rural community where that identity was a social liability. [In his ORICON interview](https://us.oricon-group.com/news/864/), he revealed that in his early twenties he drew manga to pay off student loans, and after that "because it's fun" — the creative drive initially stripped of romance and ambition, then reclaimed as something genuinely personal. *Look Back*, the one-shot he made between *Chainsaw Man*'s two parts, was explicitly [autobiographical processing](https://animehunch.com/tatsuki-fujimoto-reveals-look-back-is-based-on-his-own-past-experiences/) — Fujimoto working through feelings of helplessness and artistic identity by splitting himself into two characters. The desire/recognition dynamic has been at the center of his work for years. The ending of *Chainsaw Man* is not a sudden departure into autobiography. It's the culmination of something he's been building toward the whole time.

## Why Denji Was Never Supposed to Grow

Standard shonen narrative requires a protagonist arc. The hero starts low, is tested, grows, and through that growth achieves a resolution that justifies all the suffering. It's so deeply embedded in the genre that any deviation reads as failure.

*Chainsaw Man* has been systematically subverting this formula since chapter one. [Denji's motivations were never heroic](https://the-artifice.com/chainsaw-man/) — he wanted food, a bed, and physical intimacy. The series was praised for this groundedness, and many readers took it as a setup: *eventually* he would grow beyond these desires, *eventually* the series would reward him with something more.

It doesn't. And [that's deliberate](https://www.soapcentral.com/anime/denji-s-trauma-lack-character-development-chainsaw-man-explained). Fujimoto built a protagonist whose trauma functions not as fuel for growth but as a ceiling — a structural limit on how much change is possible. Denji is not a hero who fails to grow. He is a portrait of what trauma actually does: it loops. It confines. It makes you repeat the same choices in different clothing.

[The *Broken Hands Media* essay on Chainsaw Man](https://www.brokenhandsmedia.com/blog/2025/1/28/theses-on-chainsaw-man) puts it well: the manga operates through "diabolic realism" — fantastical premises treated with an unglamorous, realistic narrative logic. The consequence is that no amount of fighting, winning, or accumulating power actually saves Denji, because the thing he needs saving from isn't external. It's the engine of desire itself.

Part 2's entire architecture supports this. Denji as public celebrity, loved at a distance, incapable of forming the close connections that would actually matter. The escalating sense that the more he gets, the worse off he is.

Asa Mitaka, Part 2's actual protagonist, deserves more attention than she usually gets in ending discussions. Her power — the ability to [create weapons from the things she cares about](https://hero.fandom.com/wiki/Asa_Mitaka), which grow stronger through guilt — is not a superpower so much as a thesis statement about attachment. The things you love become the instruments of your destruction. The closer you hold something, the more effectively it can be used against you. [Her Falling motif](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-asa-mitaka-falls-explained/), built from a childhood of rejection and survivor guilt, mirrors Denji's trauma-loop exactly. Both characters are defined by what they cannot let go of, even when holding on is the thing that's killing them.

When Asa saves Denji in the final chapter and calls him "Chainsaw Man" — causing his heart to race with a hint of something that might be Pochita's residue — it's not a tease for Part 3. It's the whole argument in miniature: the name "Chainsaw Man" means desire, means wanting, means the chain that breaks you. And even in a reset world, even with his memory gone, something in Denji responds to it.

![Denji and Power in Chainsaw Man's ending](https://comicbook.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2026/03/Chainsaw-Man-Ending-Denji-Power.jpg?w=1024)

The reset isn't defeat. It's mercy.

## The "Empty Awe" Is Correct

When Japanese readers described the ending with that phrase — empty awe — I think they were naming the sensation of having the work's thesis land directly in the chest. Not disappointment, exactly. Not satisfaction. A hollow space where the expected emotional payoff should be, and the slow recognition that the hollow space *is* the point.

The *Chainsaw Man* you thought you were reading — the mythology-dense, escalating power-fantasy with a hidden masterplan — was always being critiqued from inside. [The devil-as-fear system](https://jurnal.ppjb-sip.org/index.php/jpdr/article/view/347) is not setup for a final confrontation with the ultimate fear; it's a lens for looking at what we're actually afraid of and what that fear costs us. Fujimoto wasn't building toward a climax. He was building toward a diagnosis.

The ending delivers it: wanting more is the disease. The cure, such as it is, is the shack and the dog and the simple dream.

[The NGE comparison holds up](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-neon-genesis-evangelion-new-anime-successor/) in one final way: both endings got angrier reactions than they deserved, from audiences who had invested years into a story and felt entitled to a specific kind of payoff. Anno's depression made a depression ending. Fujimoto's relationship with fame made a fame ending. In both cases, the ending is the most honest thing the creator could have made at that moment — and in both cases, the audience wanted something else.

That tension is not the work's failure. It's the subject.

## What This Generation Demanded

There is a real critique buried in the dissatisfaction. *Chainsaw Man* Part 2 had genuine structural problems. The Academy Saga's middle sections sprawled — the Typhoon arc, the Justice Devil subplot, the Chainsaw Man Church — in ways that complicated rather than deepened the central argument. The [Fake Chainsaw Man arc](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-chapter-231-review/) built an expectation of confrontation and mythology that the ending simply walks away from. A tighter version of this — one that interrogated why the public built a fake Chainsaw Man before dismantling the desire for Chainsaw Man entirely — would have been a stronger ending for the same thesis.

But that's a craft critique, not an ideological one. The people arguing the ending was bad because it didn't resolve the mythology are arguing something different: that a long-running serialized manga owes its readers a certain kind of closure, and that Fujimoto refused to pay that debt.

He did refuse. [He made a series about the trap of wanting more and then refused to give you more](https://sequentialplanet.com/manga-review-chainsaw-man-chapter-232/). That's not incidental. It's the final chapter of the argument he's been making since Denji killed his father's debt collector with a chainsaw and thought that was the worst thing that would ever happen to him.

The *Chainsaw Man* ending is not this generation's *Evangelion* ending because it provoked controversy. It's this generation's *Evangelion* ending because in twenty years, we're going to look back and realize it was always exactly what it needed to be.

Some of us already do.
