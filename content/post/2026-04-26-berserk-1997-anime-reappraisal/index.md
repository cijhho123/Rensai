The 2016 Berserk anime is more faithful to the manga than the 1997 anime. It covers arcs the original skipped, includes characters the original cut, and depicts events the original couldn't touch. It is also, by nearly universal agreement, unwatchable. The 1997 anime omits the Skull Knight entirely — causing what fans have called [one of the biggest plot holes in anime history](https://www.cbr.com/everything-the-original-berserk-anime-left-out-from-the-manga/). It drops Donovan, leaves Guts' post-Eclipse survival unexplained, and ends on a note that technically doesn't resolve anything. And it is, by that same near-universal agreement, the only adaptation that has ever made the Eclipse feel like what it's supposed to feel like: the end of the world.

The question of why is the question of what Berserk actually is.

## What the 1997 Anime Chose Not to Be

The [1997 adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berserk_(1997_TV_series)), directed by Naohito Takahashi at [Oriental Light and Magic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OLM,_Inc.) and broadcast on NTV, ran 25 episodes and covered the Lost Children chapter briefly before settling into the Golden Age arc — the Band of the Hawk years, Griffith's political ascent, the war with Tudor, and finally the Eclipse. It cut the Black Swordsman prologue entirely. It cut Puck. [It cut the Skull Knight](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Skull_Knight). It cut Donovan.

These are not small omissions. The Skull Knight is Berserk's connective tissue between the earthly and the supernatural — his absence means the anime has no framework for what the God Hand is, no anchor for the story's cosmological scope, and no one to pull Guts and Casca out of the Eclipse at the end. His absence is a structural wound.

What the anime got instead: 25 episodes to do almost nothing but watch three people become inseparable, and then watch that inseparability destroyed.

That's the trade. And it was the right trade to make.

## Why Puck's Absence Was a Gift

Puck is critical to the manga's tonal architecture. He is the elf who travels with Guts in the post-Eclipse years — small, comedic, providing emotional warmth in a story that would otherwise be relentless suffering. The manga needs him because the manga extends for hundreds of chapters into Guts' survival. Without tonal relief, the post-Eclipse sections would be unendurable.

The 1997 anime doesn't need Puck because [it isn't about Guts' survival](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Berserk_(1997_Anime)). It's about what he lost. The anime ends at the moment of loss. It doesn't need a safety valve for extended darkness because there's no extended darkness after the Eclipse — there's only the Eclipse, and then credits, and then you sitting there.

Every character the 1997 anime cuts was a character the post-Eclipse story needs. The pre-Eclipse story doesn't need them. It needs Guts, Griffith, and Casca. It needs the Band of the Hawk as a family. It needs the specific weight of watching Griffith choose the dream over the people who made the dream worth having. That's what the show has, and that's essentially all it has, and that turns out to be enough.

## What the Direction Actually Did

Takahashi's adaptation is not stylistically impressive in the way that Miura's manga is. The animation [is visually inconsistent](https://jonahwrites.blog/2023/06/10/berserk-1997-the-strongest-attempt/) — backgrounds are gorgeous, action sequences work, but mid-range shots can be stiff and poorly executed. This is not a show that attempts to translate Miura's architectural line work, his Dürer-sourced crosshatching, his Bosch-derived grotesquerie. It is a show on a TV budget adapting a story for television audiences in 1997, and it knows what that means.

What it does instead is invest absolutely in performance — in the voice work, in the use of silence, in the pacing of conversations. The script, as one reviewer put it, [reads like a Shakespearean play](https://www.animationforadults.com/2021/04/berserk-1997.html): heavy with monologue, demanding close attention. Griffith's speeches about dreams, Guts' increasingly broken relationship with belonging — these land because the show has spent episodes building the relationships they depend on. [THEM Anime Reviews' Carlo Ross](https://www.theanimereview.com/reviews/berserk.html), who initially found the series too bleak, came to praise it precisely for this quality: the cast transcended typical fantasy archetypes through their nuanced portrayals, and the violence was inseparable from its emotional consequences in a way reminiscent of Kurosawa.

The show also makes a specific directorial choice that the 2016 anime and the Golden Age film trilogy both got wrong: it takes its time with the ordinary. A meal in camp. Soldiers talking around a fire. Casca and Guts failing to talk to each other about what they actually want. The horror of the Eclipse is inseparable from how good everything was before it. The 1997 anime understands this and paces accordingly. The 2016 anime, covering more arcs with more events, [treated scenes like checkboxes](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-2016-anime-controversy-explained/) rather than weight-bearing moments.

## Hirasawa and the Sound of Inevitability

The other thing the 1997 anime got right that its successors haven't is the music. [Susumu Hirasawa's score](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susumu_Hirasawa) is the show's emotional spine. Hirasawa came to the project because Miura himself had been listening to Hirasawa's work while drawing Berserk — in Hirasawa's own words in a [2000 interview](https://rocketbaby.net/interviews_hirasawa_1.html), Miura revealed that "he made Berserk listening to my music." The music and the manga were already in dialogue before the adaptation existed. When Hirasawa was commissioned, Miura almost never requested specific compositions. He didn't need to. The sound was already there.

"BERSERK -Forces-" — the next-episode insert theme that plays every time the show wants you to feel that something is being built toward — is an extraordinary piece of television music. It doesn't sound triumphant. It sounds like something moving toward an outcome that cannot be changed. The melody is martial but hollow, propulsive but resigned. Hirasawa has described composing for Berserk as ["creating the music for Berserk is similar to the sensation of unlocking one of the restraints I've built up within myself. Once unlocked, and the censoring part of me removed, the music becomes 'Berserk'"](http://hirasawalyrics.tumblr.com/post/25712429750/susumu-hirasawa-aria-interview) — and Forces sounds like that: controlled chaos, the sensation of momentum without control.

By contrast, the 2016 anime's score by Shiro Sagisu is technically competent. It is also [completely interchangeable](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-1997-2016-differences/) with a dozen other dark fantasy soundtracks. It does not have a voice. Hirasawa's score has a voice that sounds like the story's voice, because the story's creator made them together. As [ANN's Mike Toole noted in his retrospective](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/watch/2022-01-07/does-it-hold-up-berserk-97-98/.181319), the 2D presentation of 1997 aged better than any of the CGI adaptations that followed — not just technically, but atmospherically.

## The Plot Hole That Doesn't Matter

The biggest criticism of the 1997 anime is the Skull Knight problem. By cutting him entirely, the show creates an unresolved question: how do Guts and Casca escape the Eclipse? The manga answers this. The anime does not. Fans who came to the manga through the anime hit this immediately — something is missing, someone must have intervened, and the show never tells you who.

This is a real flaw. It is also, in practice, almost irrelevant to the experience the show delivers.

The anime ends during the Eclipse. It ends on the image of Guts losing his arm, losing his eye, losing Griffith to whatever Femto is. It ends on Casca, on what happens to Casca, on the thing the anime can't fully show but makes you understand anyway. It ends on the complete destruction of everything that has been built over 25 episodes. The question of how Guts escapes is a narrative question. The emotional question — did this land, does the Eclipse feel like the end of the world — is not a narrative question. It is answered independently of the Skull Knight.

The [fan community's response to finishing the 1997 anime](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-5-years-hiatus-tragedy/) is consistent across decades of viewers: shock, devastation, and an immediate need to read the manga. Not because the ending was unsatisfying in the cheap sense, but because it was satisfying in the correct sense — it delivered exactly what a tragedy is supposed to deliver, and then left you wanting more of the world it destroyed.

## Why the Later Adaptations Couldn't Find This

The [Golden Age film trilogy](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-every-anime-adaptation-chronological-order/) (2012-2013) is the most technically accomplished Berserk adaptation. Studio 4°C produced it with significantly higher budgets and more faithful visual rendering of the source material. The Eclipse sequence in the third film is visually closer to Miura's designs than anything the 1997 show attempted. It's also less devastating. Consider the scene of Guts leaving the Hawks after his fight with Griffith — in the 1997 anime, this spans two episodes, dwells on the silence between people who cannot say what they mean, and ends with Guts simply walking away while Griffith watches from the castle window. In the film, the same sequence takes minutes. The emotional throughline is technically present. What's missing is the time — the accumulated weight of watching two people fail to communicate across hundreds of small scenes — that makes that departure feel like an amputation rather than a plot transition.

The 2016 anime started after the Eclipse, which meant it needed to carry the weight of what happened without having shown it. It failed on multiple levels: the [CGI animation was widely criticized](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-2016-anime-controversy-explained/) for its stiffness, the pacing was poor, and the censorship on broadcast television further stripped out the darkness the story depends on. But the root problem was structural: the show couldn't lean on what the 1997 anime had built, and it didn't have the patience to build it again.

## What This Says About Adaptation

The 1997 Berserk anime is the textbook case for an adaptation principle that sounds obvious but gets violated constantly: adapt for what your medium can do, not for coverage of the source. The films and the 2016 anime both tried to be more comprehensive versions of Berserk. They got more plot and less story.

The 1997 show chose a specific thing — the human tragedy inside the Golden Age arc, the three-way relationship between Guts, Griffith, and Casca, the corruption of idealism into catastrophe — and gave that thing everything it had. It worked because that specific thing is what Berserk is fundamentally about. All the supernatural infrastructure, the God Hand, the Idea of Evil, the Skull Knight's cosmic game — those are the architecture. The betrayal at the center is the building.

Every adaptation after 1997 got more of the architecture. None of them rebuilt the building.

Miura reportedly kept Hirasawa's music on while he drew. Perhaps that's the truest statement about what the 1997 show was: not an adaptation of the manga, but something that briefly occupied the same emotional frequency, produced by someone who understood what frequency that was. The cuts weren't failures of fidelity. They were a translator recognizing which sentences do the work and which ones are scaffolding.

The [1997 series currently holds an 8.7 on IMDB](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0318871/) from over 30,000 ratings — higher than any subsequent Berserk adaptation. That's not just nostalgia. It's the audience recognizing, across nearly three decades, that something was right about what that show chose to be.
