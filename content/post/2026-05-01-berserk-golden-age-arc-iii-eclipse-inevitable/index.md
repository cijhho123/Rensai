About two-thirds through *Berserk: The Golden Age Arc III — The Advent*, the film does something the previous two movies never attempted: the animation style changes. The cel-shaded CG and the 2D hybrid that defines the trilogy dissolves into something painterly — textured, impressionistic, operating in a completely different visual grammar than anything that came before it. This is the moment Griffith, lying broken and mute at the center of an interdimensional sacrificial ceremony, chooses to become Femto.

The shift is brief. It lasts only as long as the transformation requires. Then the film returns to its usual register, and the Eclipse continues with full technical ferocity. But that moment — those seconds where the animation tells you something the narrative hasn't yet said aloud — is the thesis of the entire film, and arguably of the entire Golden Age arc. The painterly style represents Griffith moving beyond the human plane entirely, becoming something divorced from human senses and comprehension. The film's production materials note that the visual shift was designed to show his "senses and understanding expanding beyond human limits" — not a metaphor but a literal change in what the camera can see. It codes the transformation not as a decision but as a revelation: this was always there. The man who led the Band of the Hawk was the surface. This is what was underneath.

## The Eclipse as Endpoint, Not Twist

[Kentaro Miura revealed that his friendship with fellow manga artist Kōji Mori partially inspired the Guts and Griffith dynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Griffith_(Berserk)). The relationship is the engine of the Golden Age arc: two men who bring out something essential in each other, one of whom has a dream that cannot include the other as an equal. [Griffith viewed the relationship as both his most genuine bond and a possession](https://theberserkmanga.com/griffith/) — he could not fully distinguish between these two things, which is precisely the point. When Guts leaves to find his own dream rather than serve Griffith's, Griffith's response is so disproportionate that it breaks the character open: his self-destruction, the sexual humiliation of Princess Charlotte, his torture at the hands of the Midland military. By the time the Eclipse arrives, Griffith is not making a choice from strength. He is choosing from the bottom of a psychological pit that the previous two films spent their runtime excavating.

The Eclipse, understood this way, is not a twist. It is a destination. [The betrayal of the Band of the Hawk is not sudden but the inevitable outcome of ambition that allowed no equal](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/why-griffith-betray-guts-berserk-explained). The film earns this reading through the painterly frame: by changing the visual language at the exact moment of transformation, it signals that what Griffith reveals is not new information but the truth of what was always there, surfacing now because nothing human is left to contain it.

## What the Film Does That the 1997 Anime Can't

The perennial debate in the Berserk fandom is which Eclipse is better — the 1997 TV anime's or the film's — and the honest answer is that they're doing different things, with different advantages. [The 1997 anime's slower pacing allowed for more breathing room in relationships, more character investment, a stronger sense of what is about to be destroyed](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-reasons-90s-anime-best-adaptation-movies-better/). If you wanted to understand *why* the Band of the Hawk's massacre hits as hard as it does, spending twenty-five episodes building the bonds is a legitimate answer.

But the film's version of the Eclipse is [more disturbing in motion than the 1997 version](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-reasons-90s-anime-best-adaptation-movies-better/). The manga pages are devastating, but they are static. The 1997 anime filmed them with restraint — the scene is harrowing but managed, fit for a TV broadcast slot. The film was not made for broadcast. [The audio-visual combination — Susumu Hirasawa's score paired with the animation in full motion — creates something the reviewer at Kotaku described as "nearly unbearable,"](https://kotaku.com/the-third-berserk-movie-is-by-far-the-most-violent-thin-5981641) not violence for its own sake but violence as the language of what Griffith's choice costs.

[Hirasawa's approach to composing for Berserk has always been described in terms of unlocking something — he said composing for the series is like the sensation of unlocking one of his inner restraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susumu_Hirasawa). That description fits the Eclipse sequence precisely: the score doesn't describe the event, it is the event, running alongside the animation rather than underlining it. The film understood that the Eclipse required a different kind of horror from the kind you can achieve through narrative build — it required an overwhelming sensory argument.

## The Hybrid That Finally Worked

The trilogy's CGI was a genuine problem for the first film. [The cel-shaded CG in *The Egg of the King* frequently broke immersion](https://otakuusamagazine.com/review-berserk-golden-age-arc-iii-descent/), creating action sequences where characters moved in ways that felt disconnected from the emotional register the story required. [The Fandom Post's review of *The Advent* noted the improvement specifically](https://www.fandompost.com/2014/05/07/berserk-golden-age-arc-iii-the-advent-blu-ray-anime-review/) — by the third film the balance had shifted enough to make the hybrid approach work rather than fight against itself. The second film improved it. The third film, by prioritizing 2D animation for the sequences that required emotional nuance and deploying CG for the scale that 2D couldn't achieve in budget, found the balance the trilogy had been searching for. [The Eclipse benefits from this: faces are traditionally animated, preserving the expressiveness that the scene's horror depends on, while the environment and the apostle forms that fill it are rendered with the CG's capacity for overwhelming visual density](https://kotaku.com/the-third-berserk-movie-is-by-far-the-most-violent-thin-5981641).

This is also where the painterly Femto sequence becomes more than a stylistic choice. The animation shift is only coherent because everything surrounding it is playing by consistent rules. When those rules break — when the human visual grammar of the film's normal style gives way to something else — the break registers as meaning, not error. Studio 4°C understood that the transformation required not a better version of the film's existing visual approach but a fundamentally different one. The argument is visual: what Griffith becomes cannot be represented in the same medium that represented who he was.

## What the Compression Costs

The film's weakest point is the Guts and Casca relationship prior to the Eclipse, and the weakness is a structural one. The scene depicting Guts' childhood trauma — which grounds his response to what happens to Casca during the Eclipse in something psychologically specific — was omitted, [a cut noted by multiple reviewers](https://www.fandompost.com/2014/05/07/berserk-golden-age-arc-iii-the-advent-blu-ray-anime-review/) as the film's most significant narrative loss. The reason is not that the filmmakers missed its importance but that the running time didn't permit it. The consequence is that the scene in the eclipse that deals most directly with Guts' past lands without the foundation it needs.

This is the honest cost of the film format. The 1997 anime had twenty-five episodes to build toward the Eclipse. The film had three films of roughly ninety minutes each. What the film gains in intensity — in the ability to deliver the Eclipse as a concentrated shock — it pays for in the character scaffolding that makes the shock meaningful. [The Guts-Casca relationship is there, it's earned, but not as fully as in the manga or the 1997 series](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-reasons-90s-anime-best-adaptation-movies-better/). The film knows this and front-loads the Eclipse itself with enough technical force to compensate. The compensation is incomplete — and precisely because the Eclipse is framed as inevitable, as revelation rather than decision, the missing backstory matters less to the Griffith-as-monster reading and more to the Guts-as-survivor one.

## Legacy and What Survived

*The Advent* was supposed to be the first of a continuing series. [Studio 4°C intended to adapt the Black Swordsman arc and the story beyond the Eclipse, but declining financial returns cancelled the continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berserk:_The_Golden_Age_Arc). The trilogy ends on a cliffhanger that will never be resolved in this format. The [2022 Memorial Edition](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2022-09-24/berserk-the-golden-age-arc-memorial-edition-tv-anime-3rd-promo-video-features-new-song-by-susumu-hirasawa-/.190100) reedited the films into a TV series with new Hirasawa-composed material and additional scenes, addressing some of the pacing problems the film format imposed.

What it did not change is the painterly Femto transformation. The Eclipse sequence in the Memorial Edition preserves the exact visual grammar shift that defines *The Advent*'s reading of Griffith's character. Whatever else the reedits adjusted — and they adjusted enough to make the Memorial Edition a meaningfully different viewing experience — the argument at the center of the trilogy survived intact. That argument is that Griffith's betrayal was not a choice the Eclipse forced out of him but the truth the Eclipse allowed him to become. The painterly frame holds that reading. Every subsequent version of this scene has kept it.

Griffith didn't choose to become Femto in the Eclipse. The Eclipse chose him, the way causality chooses everyone in Berserk — as a structure that was already there, waiting for the moment when the human surface wore away enough to reveal it. The painterly frame understands this. It is the most honest frame in the trilogy.
