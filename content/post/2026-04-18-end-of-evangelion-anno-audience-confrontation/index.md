In the summer of 1997, two films competed for Japanese cinema screens. One carried the tagline "生きろ" — *Live.* That was Miyazaki's *Princess Mononoke*. The other's tagline: "みんな死んじゃえばいいのに" — *Wouldn't it be nice if everyone would just die?* That was Hideaki Anno's [*The End of Evangelion*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion).

The contrast was not accidental. Miyazaki was making a film for an audience. Anno was making a film *at* one.

![The End of Evangelion promotional artwork](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1314/108941.jpg)

## The Fans Asked for This

The origin story of *The End of Evangelion* is well-documented enough to feel almost mythological: the TV series' final two episodes turned inward, abandoning the mecha apocalypse fans had been promised for intimate psychological theater set entirely inside Shinji's fractured mind. The response was immediate and violent. The Gainax studio building was [defaced with graffiti](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats). Anno received death threats. The fandom, a constituency that had made the series a cultural phenomenon, was furious.

And so Gainax made the film they demanded. What they delivered was, depending on your tolerance for irony, either the most generous thing a creator has ever done for a hostile audience, or the most vicious.

The key detail that tends to get lost: Anno didn't capitulate. He escalated. The film contains the "real" ending — the giant robots, the Angels, the Instrumentality sequence with its full nightmarish visual logic. It delivers everything the TV finale withheld. And then, precisely because it delivers all of that, it makes you feel complicit in watching.

## The Form Is the Argument

*The End of Evangelion* opens in the real world and it ends in the real world, but the middle is a controlled demolition of the boundary between them. As Third Impact begins, the film's animation starts dissolving — colors strip away, cels reduce to pencil sketches, motion becomes static frames. This is usually described as an artistic choice, a reflection of Anno's mental state during an extraordinarily difficult production. Both things are true, but the formal logic goes further.

What Anno is doing is making the seams visible. He is reminding you that you are watching drawings. That the apocalypse you're witnessing has the same material reality as a school sketchbook. And then, as if that weren't enough, he cuts to live action.

The live-action sequence — an empty cinema, Tokyo streets in real afternoon light, a playground, the sky — arrives in the middle of Third Impact like a fire alarm. Shinji's voiceover continues, but his words now play over actual physical space. The effect is disorienting in a way that's almost confrontational. According to [analysis at Animation Studies](https://blog.animationstudies.org/?p=2012), the sequence works to invite viewers to "examine their own reality and their relationship to the fictional world of the series" — but that framing undersells how aggressive it actually is. Anno isn't inviting. He's insisting.

Then comes the shot of a movie theater audience watching *End of Evangelion* itself. The camera finds them in the dark, staring at a screen we can't see. The film you are watching contains footage of people watching the film you are watching. The loop is deliberate. You are in that theater. Anno knows you are in that theater.

## Graffiti in Public Toilets

After *Evangelion* ended, Anno was asked about the fan reaction — not the death threats specifically, but the broader phenomenon of internet discourse around the series. His response has become infamous: he compared those opinions to [graffiti in public toilets](https://amaelcognacq.medium.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-the-trojan-horse-of-otaku-culture-cc8e041862e9). Anonymous, performative, disconnected from any real engagement with what the work was doing.

This is the context in which the film's montage of fan letters needs to be understood. During Third Impact, the screen fills with rapid-fire flashes of correspondence — handwritten notes, messages, some hostile, many earnest. For years, fans assumed these were the actual death threats Anno had received. They weren't. In an interview, Anno confirmed that [the letters were written by studio staff for legal reasons](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) — real mail would have created liability. But that doesn't make the sequence less pointed. Anno staged his own hate mail. He fabricated the evidence of his audience's rage, reproduced it on film, and then broadcast it back to them in a theater.

The officially stated purpose, per the film's theatrical pamphlet, was to explore the relationship "between a work and its admirers." What it actually does is collapse that relationship into something uglier and more honest: the relationship between a creator breaking down under public pressure and the public that applied it.

## What Anno Thought He Was Saying

Anno has been relatively open about his intentions, at least in retrospect. His stated goal was to "purify" viewers — to use the film as an experience that would wake people up and push them back toward the real world. He considered *Evangelion* a critique of otaku culture from the inside, made by someone who understood it precisely because he was part of it. The Human Instrumentality Project, in this reading, isn't just a plot device. It's a literalization of the otaku fantasy of total connection without risk — a world where the boundary between self and other dissolves, where loneliness is solved not by human contact but by the erasure of human separateness.

Instrumentality is comfortable. That's the horror. Shinji almost chooses it.

What pulls him back — what pulls Asuka back — is the thing that otaku fantasy specifically works to avoid: the friction of actual other people, who can hurt you, reject you, express disgust at the sight of you. Asuka's final line, the film's last word, is a wound. Anno [reportedly asked actress Yūko Miyamura](https://www.cbr.com/end-of-evangelion-production-trivia/) what she would say if she were assaulted, and her response became the script. It is one of the few moments in the film where something crosses from fiction into something that feels genuinely unprocessed — raw in the way that only real discomfort produces.

The film wants you to feel that discomfort. It wants you to understand that you cannot opt out of it. Instrumentality was a lie. The theater you are sitting in is real.

## The Film the Audience Deserved

There's a version of this reading that makes Anno look punitive — a creator so contemptuous of his fans that he used their demands as an excuse to attack them. I don't think that's right, or at least not complete. Anno was in genuine psychological collapse during this period; the film's formal deterioration is, among other things, an honest document of a person barely holding it together. The cruelty in *The End of Evangelion* is not the cruelty of contempt. It's the cruelty of someone who knows what the audience is hiding from, and loves them enough not to let them hide.

The film gives fans everything they asked for — the big ending, the spectacle, the resolution — and embeds inside that resolution the argument that wanting those things the way they wanted them was itself a symptom. Not of being bad fans. Of being lonely people who found in a work about lonely people the companionship they couldn't locate anywhere else, and then demanded that companionship be permanent, comfortable, and safe.

Anno understood that demand because he had the same one. That's what makes the ending — Shinji and Asuka alone on the shore of a ruined world, the human race reduced to LCL, Asuka expressing disgust, Shinji crying — feel less like a middle finger than like a diagnosis. The illness is mutual. The cure is the same for everyone: return to the world, which is difficult, which is real.

Twenty-nine years later, that diagnosis still lands. Which probably says more about the world the fandom built than about the film.
