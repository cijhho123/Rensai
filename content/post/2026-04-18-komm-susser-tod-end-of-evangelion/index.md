Hideaki Anno wanted to end the world to "Hey Jude."

The Beatles track was his original choice for the Third Impact sequence in *The End of Evangelion* — the seven-minute stretch in which humanity's collective AT Fields dissolve, people melt into orange LCL fluid, and Rei-Lilith's giant form bleeds light across a dying sky. The coda of "Hey Jude" — that compulsive, escalating, four-chord release — was, apparently, what he heard when he imagined the apocalypse.

He couldn't get the rights. What he commissioned instead, out of necessity, became one of the most analyzed, memed, covered, and emotionally devastating pieces of music in anime history. "Komm, Süsser Tod" did not replace "Hey Jude." It became something far stranger: an accidental self-portrait, a pop song built around Anno's own depression, deployed against images of civilizational death and dressed in a sound that makes you want to clap along.

## Three People in a Room

The song has three credited authors: Anno, [Shiro Sagisu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiro_Sagisu) (the series' composer), and Mike Wyzgowski — and understanding who each one contributed matters for what the song actually is.

Sagisu composed the music. Wyzgowski, a British songwriter and Sagisu's longtime English-language collaborator, adapted the lyric text. His working method, described in an interview with Cartoon Milk, is important: "Shiro generally gives me a brief outline or a few buzzwords for whichever piece we're working on and just leaves me to get on with it... I think *interpretation* would be more accurate." He did not translate Anno's Japanese poem word-for-word. He interpreted it — which means the English lyrics are a creative reimagining, filtered through another writer's sensibility, of something Anno wrote in Japanese.

The singer is [Arianne Schreiber](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Arianne_Schreiber), a South African-born British teenager credited simply as ARIANNE, who at the time of recording was on the verge of being signed by George Michael's label. She traveled to Japan for three days specifically to record the track. She was entirely unfamiliar with Evangelion. She later described the experience as "amazing" — she felt honored to be part of something she didn't know anything about. Her identity was unknown to fans for years until they tracked her down.

This is worth sitting with. One of anime's most emotionally precise musical pieces was written by a depressed Japanese director, arranged by his longtime British collaborator, adapted into English through a writer's interpretation rather than a translation, and performed by a teenager who had no frame for what she was singing into. The result is somehow completely coherent.

## The Title Is Already Doing Work

The song takes its name from Johann Sebastian Bach's 1736 cantata "Komm, süsser Tod, komm sel'ge Ruh" — *Come, sweet death, come blessed rest*. In Bach's original, sweet death is a gateway: the release from earthly suffering that leads toward Heaven and divine peace. The baroque tradition frames death as transition.

Anno's version keeps the phrase and strips out the destination. There is no Heaven at the end of the Third Impact sequence. There is LCL — the primordial fluid from which life emerged, pooling in oceans that used to be people. The "sweet death" is not a passage. It is erasure.

The structural echo of "Hey Jude" in Sagisu's arrangement — that driving, building rhythm in the chorus that feels like it should resolve into something communal and uplifting — reinforces the dissonance. The song sounds like it is heading somewhere redemptive. It is heading toward annihilation. The musical grammar says celebration; the content says dissolution; the images playing onscreen say the world is over.

## The Poem Underneath the Pop Song

Anno's original Japanese text — from which Wyzgowski worked — has been quoted and circulated extensively in fan communities:

*"I'm uneasy. I'm afraid of being disliked by everyone. I'm afraid of being hurt. But I'm even more afraid of hurting other people."*

This is a lyric written by a man who spent four years immobilized by depression after *Nadia: The Secret of Blue Water*, who identified his own illness mid-production of the TV series by reading a clinical description of depression, who received death threats mailed to Gainax after the controversial original ending and described contemplating suicide. The "I" of Anno's poem is not abstractly Shinji Ikari, humanity, or a fictional narrator. It is Hideaki Anno in 1996, transcribing his own psychology into a song that would be sung over images of the world ending.

The English lyrics that Wyzgowski built from this poem map the same emotional territory: a narrator exhausted by their failures, unable to face the process of repairing relationships or changing themselves, choosing the path of least resistance — which is ceasing to exist. [Multiple analyses of the song](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod) have noted that it represents specifically the voice of nihilistic capitulation. This is not grief, not rage, not even tragedy. It is the voice of someone who has decided that continuing is not worth the cost, and who has dressed that decision in an upbeat melody so it goes down easier.

The seven minutes and forty-six seconds of the song's runtime — across which humanity dissolves and the AT Fields collapse — are Anno's private ideation made public spectacle.

## The Anno Signature: Music That Lies About Its Mood

"Komm, Süsser Tod" is the most extreme example of a technique Anno had been using throughout Evangelion: pairing music with a contradictory emotional register. Earlier in the TV series, Beethoven's "Ode to Joy" — the ur-text of communal uplift, the musical gesture toward humanity's highest aspiration — plays under a scene of catastrophic death. [EvaWiki catalogs this as Anno's directorial trademark](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod): "celebratory music paired with tragic events."

The technique is not ironic in the cheap, winking sense. It is genuinely destabilizing. Irony at least tells you which register is correct — this is the real one, that is the fake one. Anno's version does not resolve the contradiction. The music is really upbeat; the images are really apocalyptic. Both are happening simultaneously, and the viewer has to hold both at once without a key for which to trust. This is formally identical to the experience of dissociation that depression produces: the surface register of the moment does not match the internal one, and there is no way to make them cohere.

## 14.4 Million Views and a Meme That Won't Die

"Komm, Süsser Tod" was probably always going to have a cult following. What nobody could have predicted is that it would become [4chan's official soundtrack for internet-era catastrophe](https://knowyourmeme.com/photos/957324-neon-genesis-evangelion).

Over the 2010s, the song calcified into a specific meme format: any event perceived as the end of something beloved — a platform's acquisition, a beloved creator's departure, a community's implosion — got posted with the song playing in the background. When Facebook acquired Oculus Rift, the song played. When moot retired as 4chan's administrator, the song played. By the 2020s it had migrated to New Year's Eve memes cataloguing the previous year's disasters. The YouTube upload accumulated over 14.4 million views over eight years. Fan covers now exist in medieval bardcore, synthwave, full orchestral, and metal arrangements.

What the meme logic grasped, probably without conscious analysis, is that the song is *about* watching something end and choosing to feel it as both devastating and somehow darkly okay. The upbeat melody gives you permission to not quite fall apart. That is exactly what Anno built into it — the nihilistic embrace of dissolution that feels, for a few minutes, like relief.

The joke, if there is one, is that Anno wrote this song because he couldn't end the world to the Beatles. What he wrote instead keeps getting used to process the small-scale endings of contemporary internet life: the death of a meme, the end of an era, the moment a community finally accepts something is over. The scale is radically different. The emotional structure is identical.

Anno's private breakdown, outsourced to a British lyricist and sung by a South African teenager in a Tokyo recording studio, is now the internet's most reliable tool for collective mourning. He wrote a song about wanting to stop existing. Thirty years later, everyone else uses it to say goodbye.
