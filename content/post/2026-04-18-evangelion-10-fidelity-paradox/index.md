In December 2006, Hideaki Anno told [Newtype magazine](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff) that he was happy to finally recreate Evangelion "as he wanted it to be in the beginning" — freed at last from the technological limitations and budget chaos that had defined the original 1995–96 production. He had founded his own studio, [khara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khara_(studio)), specifically to control this project. He had the tools. He had the money. He had the time.

The resulting film, *Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone*, is [almost shot-for-shot identical to episodes 1 through 6 of the original series](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Differences_between_Evangelion_1.0_and_Neon_Genesis_Evangelion).

This is worth sitting with. After a decade away, with total creative control and modern production infrastructure, Anno made the same film. Not a reimagining. Not a radical reinterpretation. The same film, cleaned up. The dialogue is tightened. The pacing loses its dead air. The animation is immaculate. But the bones? Identical.

What Anno's "as I meant it to be" accidentally reveals is a profound and uncomfortable truth: the original NGE was not broken in the ways he seemed to think it was.

## The Live Concert

Anno has described the production of the original Neon Genesis Evangelion as a "live" concert — story and character development without theory, improvised collaboratively in the manner of a jazz ensemble, someone playing the guitar and the drums and bass finding their parts in response. This was not a metaphor for creative freedom. It was a description of controlled desperation. [The show aired weekly from October 1995 to March 1996](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion), and as the deadline pressure escalated and the budget collapsed, the animation increasingly became stick figures over static backgrounds, recycled shots, and long close-ups of mouths not moving.

The famous psychological episodes near the end — 25 and 26, largely composed of sketches, stills, and text — have been attributed variously to budget exhaustion and to deliberate artistic choice. Anno himself has given different accounts at different times. What is documented is that the production ran so close to the deadline that the final episodes' content had to be redesigned in the last three months, and whatever combination of necessity and intent shaped them, the result was radically unlike anything television anime had done before.

And yet. The madness of those conditions produced something that no amount of craft could manufacture deliberately: rawness. Anno's breakdown was in the pixels. The dead air in conversations, the lingering shots of empty rooms, the silence that made the dialogue hit harder when it finally arrived — these weren't features of a well-designed script. They were symptoms of a creator filming his own psychological state because he had no other material left to work with.

The original NGE is powerful precisely because it was not made the way Anno wanted.

## The Studio Recording

*1.0* corrects the original's "problems" methodically. The pacing is tightened — the awkward silences compressed, the conversations made more legible, the exposition smoother. Lilith, whose presence in Terminal Dogma was kept mysterious through most of the original series, is shown and explained to Shinji before Operation Yashima, giving the climax more dramatic stakes and coherence. The city of Tokyo-3 is populated with actual pedestrians and traffic. The oceans are now a gorgeous, deliberate blood-red — Second Impact rendered globally, beautiful from orbit. Ramiel, the fifth Angel, doesn't just float — it shapeshifts into extraordinary geometric configurations, a genuinely spectacular sequence that replaces the original's static blue octahedron.

These are all improvements. The film is, by any conventional craft metric, better than episodes 1–6.

And then, in the film's final scene, a new element: Kaworu Nagisa awakens on the moon and looks toward Earth. In the original series, Kaworu didn't appear until episode 24, arriving without warning, providing Shinji an intimacy the show had denied him before tearing it away seventeen minutes later. His appearance is one of the most disorienting pivots in the medium — it changes the show's entire register without preparation, and the devastation that follows lands harder because of it. *1.0*'s moon scene telegraphs Kaworu's arrival. You could argue this creates dread instead of surprise — that knowing he's coming makes every subsequent moment with him weighted with dramatic irony. That's a legitimate reading. My view is that it trades the gut-punch for the slow-burn, and the original earned its gut-punch specifically because it didn't warn you.

This is the tell. Every change in *1.0* is rational. The Kaworu foreshadow is smart, coherent storytelling. It's also the wrong call.

## What Choosing Costs

The original NGE's greatest structural decisions weren't made — they arrived under pressure. The psychological episodes at the end exist because the production collapsed; if Anno had had the time and money, we would have gotten action sequences, a conventional resolution. The show's psychological interiority — the thing that makes it feel like no other anime — was partly a function of necessity. When you can't animate a battle, you film Shinji's internal monologue instead.

*1.0*'s blood-red oceans are a perfect example of the difference. They're beautiful. They're symbolically precise. They were chosen. Compare that to the original series' ocean shots, shot in a relatively normal blue — the devastation of Second Impact is mostly implied, described in dialogue, felt through absence rather than spectacle. Which conveys more?

The red ocean is *the right answer*. It's also the answer you'd give if you were designing the image rather than living it.

Anno's problem in 1995 was that he was too depressed, too exhausted, and too close to his subject to make clean artistic choices. That proximity was the product's secret ingredient. When he returned in 2006 with total control, he could finally make the choices he'd always wanted to make — and those choices are, by and large, the right ones. But the rightness is exactly what's missing.

## What 1.0 Is Actually For

None of this is a knock on *1.0* as a film. It works. As an entry point for viewers who bounced off the original series' pacing, it's excellent — the same essential story, modernized and accessible, with an animation quality that holds up alongside anything being made in 2007. As a gateway drug, it might be the most effective piece of media the franchise ever produced.

But Anno's statement — that he was finally making Eva "as he wanted it to be" — implies that he was correcting a deficiency. The actual record suggests something stranger: that he was simply making the same work again, with better tools, and discovering in the process that what he thought was wrong with the original was actually where all the power lived.

The studio recording is technically flawless. But sometimes what you wanted to record was the sound of the room falling apart.
