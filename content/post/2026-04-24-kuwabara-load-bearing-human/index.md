The running joke about Kazuma Kuwabara is that he's the dead weight. The guy who trips, loses, gets hospitalized, cracks bad jokes, and holds the team back. Yusuke is the main character. Hiei is the cool one. Kurama is the elegant one. Kuwabara is the one who's supposedly just there.

This reading is wrong in a way that matters. Not because Kuwabara is secretly stronger than everyone thinks (though he is), but because the entire moral logic of *Yu Yu Hakusho* collapses without him. He isn't the weak link. He's the load-bearing wall.

![Kazuma Kuwabara in Yu Yu Hakusho](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2021/01/Yu-Yu-Hakusho-Kuwabara-Featured-Image.jpg)

## The Team Nobody Actually Looks At Closely

Here is a fact about Team Urameshi that tends to get glossed over: [Yusuke Urameshi is the descendant of the demon lord Raizen](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Yusuke_Urameshi), one of the Three Kings of Makai, and carries latent demonic power that eventually transforms him into a Mazoku — a demon-human hybrid — after his death at Sensui's hands. [Kurama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurama_(YuYu_Hakusho)) is a demon fox spirit, Yoko Kurama, who has been inhabiting a human body since birth and whose true self is a powerful demon with thousands of years of history. [Hiei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiei_(YuYu_Hakusho)) is a full demon, born of a Koorime ice maiden, carrying the Jagan Eye and eventually mastering the Darkness Flame.

And then there is Kuwabara: a human teenager from Sarayashiki who gets into street fights after school.

The four of them are assigned to protect the human world. Three of them are, in whole or part, the thing they're supposedly protecting it from. The one member of this team who is unambiguously, unreservedly *human* is the guy everyone treats as the group's weak link. That is not a coincidence. It is the show's architectural choice, and it has consequences that ripple across every arc.

## What Humanity Actually Requires

The premise of *Yu Yu Hakusho* is that humans are worth protecting. Not because they're powerful, not because they're spiritually advanced, but because they're human. The show makes this case repeatedly across its run — through Yusuke's instinct to throw himself in front of a car to save a child in the very first episode, through Genkai's insistence on training human potential rather than supernatural power, through the horror with which the cast responds to demons preying on ordinary people.

But a premise needs a representative. If your story's thesis is that humanity has intrinsic worth, you need a human present who is not secretly a demon, not inhabited by a demon spirit, not the genetic descendant of a demon lord. You need someone whose humanity is the whole story, not just the surface.

Kuwabara is that person. He's the one who reacts to supernatural violence the way an actual human would — with fear that gets converted into stubbornness through sheer force of will. When the rest of the team calculates, Kuwabara feels. When they adapt, he simply refuses to stop. [Fan discourse has called him the most underrated character in the series for precisely this reason](https://gamefaqs.gamespot.com/boards/2000121-anime-and-manga-other-titles/78780531) — his worth isn't legible in the show's power ranking logic, so it gets dismissed. As the [Kazuma Kuwabara character page on the Heroes Wiki](https://hero.fandom.com/wiki/Kazuma_Kuwabara) notes, he is the most empathetic to the plights of others and "will even save an enemy if he believes they are worth saving." That's not weakness. That's the show's moral center wearing a delinquent's uniform.

## The Dimension Sword Problem

Here is where the "Kuwabara is the weak one" reading gets genuinely awkward: by Chapter Black, Kuwabara wields what is functionally the most theoretically powerful weapon in the entire series.

The [Dimension Sword](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Dimension_Sword) — the Jigen Tou — can cut through dimensional barriers, psychic territory, and space itself. It is the weapon that Sensui needs Kuwabara to develop, because [only the Dimension Sword can open the barrier between the human world and the demon world](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-spiritual-dimension-sword-explained/). Sensui, who dispatched Hiei, Kurama, and Yusuke without difficulty — who absorbed assault after assault with his Sacred Energy and took zero meaningful damage — [refused to take a direct hit from the Dimension Sword](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Dimension_Sword). Not because he couldn't survive it. Because the sword's nature, cutting through dimensions and the fabric of space, was something even his body couldn't absorb and shrug off.

The weakest member of Team Urameshi developed the one weapon that the arc's villain — the most powerful human-turned-demon in the series — would not stand in front of. [As Spencer Irwin observed on his Substack analysis of Kuwabara](https://spencerirwin.substack.com/p/not-your-typical-token-weak-teammate), the show constantly undercuts this potential through narrative choices that soften or sideline his victories. But the potential is real. Kuwabara is not weak. The show just has structural reasons to keep him from fully flowering, and those reasons are more interesting than the "comic relief" dismissal suggests.

## The Chapter Black Excision

The most revealing thing *Yu Yu Hakusho* ever does with Kuwabara is take him out of Chapter Black.

When Sensui and his seven associates make their move against the human world, Kuwabara ends up hospitalized — specifically manipulated into a position where Sensui can use him as a tool (for his Dimension Sword) and then remove him from the board as a combatant. He's present for the opening acts, used as the key that unlocks the barrier, and then effectively neutralized for the final confrontation. Yusuke fights Sensui without Kuwabara. [The climax of the arc, Yusuke's death and demonic resurrection, happens with Kuwabara sidelined.](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Chapter_Black_Saga)

This is notable because Chapter Black is the arc where the show most seriously entertains Sensui's thesis: that humanity is irredeemably corrupt, that humans deserve what demons will do to them. [Sensui was himself a Spirit Detective](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobu_Sensui) — someone who spent his life protecting humans — who broke when he witnessed the Black Black Club's atrocities and decided that humans were beyond redemption.

That is precisely the question Kuwabara's existence poses a problem for. His function in the show is to be the proof that ordinary humans are worth protecting — to embody the decency, the loyalty, the stubbornness that Yusuke fights to preserve. But you cannot have that argument running in the background when your villain's entire thesis is that human decency is an illusion. The show can't simultaneously have Sensui argue that humans are irredeemable and have Kuwabara standing there being unambiguously decent.

Togashi's solution — intentional or not — was to take Kuwabara off the board. [Togashi had intended to end the series after Chapter Black anyway](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-final-arc-sudden-ending/), meaning this was the arc he designed as his true finale. And in that finale, the character who most represents the show's optimistic premise about humanity disappears from the battlefield. That's not sloppy writing. It's an inadvertent structural admission.

## The Ending He Deserved

[Kuwabara's conclusion in the series](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-kuwabara-facts-trivia/) is that he decides to leave demon-fighting behind and focus on university entrance exams. He becomes an excellent student. He moves on.

This is treated, in most readings, as the final joke at his expense — the normal one going back to normal life because he can't keep up. But that reading is backwards. Every other member of Team Urameshi ends up drawn back into demon politics or demon-world dynamics. Yusuke gets entangled in the Three Kings arc. Kurama and Hiei remain enmeshed in Makai. Kuwabara is the one who gets to *leave*. The human gets to return to human life. The delinquent becomes a serious student, on his own terms, without supernatural power or demon heritage to prop up his identity.

The ending works precisely because Kuwabara was never trying to become something other than human. He fought to protect a world he actually belongs to, and when the immediate threat was over, he went back to it. That's not weakness. That's the only fully coherent arc in the show — the one character whose conclusion matches his premise.

## What Gets Lost in Translation

*Yu Yu Hakusho*'s influence on modern shonen is well-documented. [Gege Akutami explicitly cited Togashi's work as an influence](https://screenrant.com/hunter-x-hunter-inspired-jujutsu-kaisen-sukuna-possessing-megumi/) on *Jujutsu Kaisen*, and the power system, villain motivations, and dark tonal register of JJK all trace clearly back to Chapter Black. [Masashi Kishimoto has acknowledged the same lineage for Naruto.](https://screenrant.com/hunter-x-hunter-yuyu-hakusho-similarities-togashi/)

But *Jujutsu Kaisen* doesn't have a Kuwabara. Its main characters — Itadori, Megumi, Nobara — are all exceptionally powerful sorcerers from the start. The world is populated by sorcerers and curses, with ordinary humans serving primarily as collateral damage. Nobara comes closest to a human anchor, but she's already a formidable fighter by episode one and is defined by her power, not by her ordinariness. There's no one in the cast whose function is simply to be the ordinary person whose existence proves the fight is worth having.

This is why [JJK can afford a nihilism about human casualties that YYH structurally cannot](https://screenrant.com/hunter-x-hunter-inspired-jujutsu-kaisen-sukuna-possessing-megumi/). The deaths in *Jujutsu Kaisen* register as tragedy within a closed system of power — awful, but contained. When ordinary humans suffer in *Yu Yu Hakusho*, Kuwabara's presence means you feel it as a violation of something that actually matters to the narrative. He's the difference between a world of supernatural power and a world that humans actually inhabit. Take him out, and the show's emotional stakes collapse into the same nihilism that JJK embraces by design.

[The anime ran from October 1992 to January 1995 across 112 episodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YuYu_Hakusho_(1992_TV_series)), directed by Noriyuki Abe at Studio Pierrot. What it built during that run was not just a tournament arc template or a villain motivation framework — it was a model for how to keep a supernatural action story grounded in human stakes. [Key animators like Norio Matsumoto who cut their teeth on the series went on to define the visual language of digital-era action anime](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/10/14/yu-yu-hakushos-exceptional-production/), but its structural insight was softer and more philosophical: put an actual human in the room where the monsters fight, and don't let him leave.

The rest of shonen borrowed the fights. It mostly forgot Kuwabara.
