The Major's "I Love War" speech, delivered aboard his airship as London burns below him, is frequently cited as one of anime's great villain monologues. The production team [reportedly spent three days just directing the English dub recording](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/The_Major/I_Love_War), with voice director Taliesin Jaffe calling it one of the hardest sessions of his career. What makes it worth that effort is not what everyone usually says it is.

People praise the speech for its rhetoric — the cascading repetitions, the crescendo structure, the honest declaration of a love for war divorced from any ideology. "I love war!" he says, over and over, naming each specific kind of war, each phase and texture of it, until the catalogue becomes a kind of ecstasy. It is a great speech. But the reason it is great is not the rhetoric. It is that every single thing the Major says about himself is also true of Alucard.

## The Mirror

Alucard loves killing. The show does not hide this. [He arrives at combat scenes with a smile that has nothing to do with duty](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/Alucard). He is not restrained violence pointed at an objective; he is violence that has been given a direction and a master and is grateful for both. Every scene in which he deploys full power is a scene in which the animation takes evident pleasure in depicting him — the dissolution into shadow, the multiform body, the army of familiar spirits consuming everything in range. Alucard does not fight for England. He fights because fighting is what he is.

Father Anderson loves the battle with Alucard specifically. His choice to be stabbed with the Nail of Helena — to give up his humanity for the power to fight Alucard on equal terms — is presented in the show as a sin, but it is a recognizable one. [Anderson chose war over his own salvation](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/HellsingAlucard), the same fundamental exchange the Major made and the same one Alucard made six centuries earlier. What Anderson could not bear was the idea that the great fight with Alucard would end before either of them got what they came for. He chose to extend the battle. The show does not excuse this, but it does make it comprehensible — because every character with a genuine will in *Hellsing Ultimate* makes some version of this choice.

The Hellsing organization is an institution whose entire purpose is organized, perpetual war against the supernatural. [Sir Integra](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/Integral_Fairbrook_Wingates_Hellsing) is a commander who inherited this war at thirteen, who has never known a life outside it, and whose authority is exercised entirely through controlling the most powerful weapon ever built — a vampire who loves to fight. The distinction between her "righteous purpose" and the Major's nihilism is not that she wants peace and he wants war. It is that she gives the warmonger drive a direction and a legitimating structure. [As the blog analysis at The Cozy Lair argues](https://thecozylair.wordpress.com/2019/09/06/incorrigible-warmongers-the-moral-backdrop-of-hellsing-ultimate/), the "common denominator in all the supposedly good characters" is devotion to Integra — which allows them to channel their bloodlust toward a "righteous purpose" while the boundary separating them from the Major remains deliberately thin.

The Major says what everyone else will not say. He is not wrong about himself. He is simply honest about the thing the other characters dress up in other clothes.

## The Difference Is the Years

What separates Alucard from the Major is not ideology or principle. It is time.

The Major is a human who made a deliberate choice — he joined the Waffen-SS [not for any ideological commitment to National Socialism but to have access to war](https://villains.fandom.com/wiki/Major_(Hellsing)), then made himself a cyborg to better serve a life dedicated to it. He is at the beginning of his understanding of what that choice is. He dies happy. The speech is the speech of someone who has not yet lived long enough to find out what loving war costs when it never ends.

Alucard made the same choice, or its equivalent, at his defeat by Abraham Van Hellsing. Vlad Dracula, Prince of Wallachia, having been conquered, chose to become a monster rather than accept the death that defeat implied. He chose power over ending. He chose an indefinite continuation over the finite life he was losing. [Six centuries later](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/Alucard), he wants to die.

This is the show's central irony. The most powerful being in *Hellsing Ultimate* has only one genuine desire, and the thing he has chosen to be makes that desire impossible to fulfill. He cannot be killed by anything except a worthy human opponent — and no human opponent is worthy enough. He has spent centuries hoping someone will be. Van Hellsing himself was the last one, and Alucard has been locked in the Hellsing basement ever since. 

The Major loves war because he has not been doing it for six hundred years. Alucard loves it too — but he is also exhausted by it in the way only an immortal can be: not tired in the body, but tired of the loop. He wants what the Major gets to have: an ending.

## Kouta Hirano's Refusal

Author Kouta Hirano was [unhappy enough with the 2001 TV anime](https://www.cbr.com/anime-hellsing-ultimate-ova-manga-differences/) — which had to diverge from his manga partway through when the chapters ran out — that he refused to allow any further adaptation until the manga was finished. The 2006 OVA series — [ten episodes across six years, produced across three animation studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellsing) (Satelight for episodes 1-4, Madhouse for 5-7, Graphinica for 8-10) — exists specifically because Hirano wanted the complete argument adapted, not just its opening.

That insistence matters structurally. The Major's speech lands in episode eight of ten, after [seven hours of watching Alucard fight](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=5114) — after the audience has seen what he does, fully, with no ambiguity about his enthusiasm for it. By the time the Major stands up and describes his love of war with total transparency, the viewer has already been watching Alucard enact that love for eight episodes. The speech is not revelatory in the way a standard villain monologue is. It is confirmatory. It names something the show has been showing you.

A 2001-style adaptation, truncated mid-story, would have cut this off before the Millennium arc resolves and before Alucard's Schrödinger dissolution. The 2006 OVA has the full ending: the dissolution, the thirty-year absence, the return. Only because the argument was complete could the adaptation be.

What the Major says is the suppressed backstory of the protagonist. Alucard once felt exactly this way. [When Vlad Dracula chose to become a vampire](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/Alucard/History) — chose power and continuation over defeat and death at Van Hellsing's hands — he was, in some sense, saying: "I love war." He chose the war over the warrior's ending. [As Gabriel Mohr observes](https://gabrielmohr.com/the-alucard-effect-whats-great-about-hellsing-ultimate/), what makes Alucard compelling is not his power but the self-awareness that keeps coming through it: he knows what he is, knows what it cost, and knows what he wants now. The Major is where that story begins. Alucard is where it arrives after you have won every fight for six centuries and still have not found the one thing you actually want.

## The Schrödinger Problem

The final arc gives Alucard a resolution that is simultaneously a punishment and a reward. After he absorbs Schrodinger — a catlike being whose power is to exist simultaneously everywhere and nowhere, contingent on self-recognition — he stops existing as a singular entity. [He has consumed too many souls](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger), and now the self that would recognize itself as Alucard is distributed across all of them. He spends thirty years in this quantum dissolution, which the show presents as a kind of death, before returning — after having eliminated all the absorbed souls except one, his own — to Integra, who is old now.

The logic of this ending is the logic of the show's central argument about Alucard. He spent centuries accumulating power by absorbing the souls of those he drank. Every life he took became part of him, until the crowd of them made it impossible to be himself. He was, in a very literal sense, destroyed by his own power — not by an enemy, not by a worthy human, but by the mass of everything he had consumed in service of his immortality and his capacity for war.

He survives by becoming less. By giving everything back. The ending is Alucard finding the reduction he wanted — not death exactly, but the elimination of the surplus self that the six centuries of killing had built up. He returns as Alucard, singular, holding the person who gave him the last thing he needed: someone to come back for.

The Major never lives to find this out. He dies happy in his war. His cyborg body is destroyed, he smiles, and that is the end of his story. [He got everything he wanted](https://hellsing.fandom.com/wiki/The_Major) because he never wanted to survive his wanting.

## Why This Is the Show

*Hellsing Ultimate* is often treated as a great action anime with outstanding villain design — which it is — or as a faithful adaptation of Hirano's manga after the 2001 anime's disappointments — which it also is. But the combination of the Major's speech and Alucard's arc makes it something more specific: an argument about what it costs to make violence your nature.

The Major is correct in his speech. War is something that can be loved. The speech is not satirized; it is presented with full rhetorical investment. Alucard is not presented as a refutation of the Major's thesis. He is the demonstration of where the thesis leads if you keep living.

They are the same argument. One of them is six hundred years younger and does not know it yet.
