The conventional reading of Yu Yu Hakusho is that it starts strong and ends weakly. The Spirit Detective arc is charming. The Dark Tournament is genuinely one of the finest sustained stretches of action anime in the medium's history. Chapter Black is psychologically ambitious, built around a villain so complex he nearly breaks the moral architecture the show depends on. And then comes the Three Kings arc — rushed, underpopulated, anticlimactic — and people generally shrug and say Yoshihiro Togashi ran out of steam.

That reading is wrong, or at least incomplete. What happened to the Three Kings arc was not a creative failure. It was an industrial one. And understanding the distinction is the prerequisite for understanding not just [Yu Yu Hakusho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YuYu_Hakusho) but the entire shape of Togashi's career in the thirty years since.

## What Togashi Actually Wanted

The evidence is unambiguous. According to [Screen Rant's account of the YYH ending](https://screenrant.com/yu-yu-hakusho-ending-heartbreaking-manga/), Togashi originally wanted to end the series at the conclusion of the Chapter Black arc — specifically after Sensui's defeat and death. He had said what he wanted to say. The narrative had reached a natural terminus. In a doujinshi called *Yoshirin de Pon!*, he was explicit: while he still cared for the work, the stress of production had dominated his life in ways he couldn't sustain, and he wanted to end it with a finality that would prevent continuation.

[CBR's retrospective on the Three Kings arc](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-final-arc-sudden-ending/) documents what happened instead: editorial pressure from Shonen Jump, the same machine that had forced him to allow as little as [five hours of sleep per night](https://screenrant.com/yu-yu-hakusho-ending-heartbreaking-manga/) at the series' peak, kept him going. The original plans for the Three Kings saga — a full-scale war between demon factions, the kind of sprawling conflict that would have justified Kuwabara's narrative absence and made Yusuke's demon heritage matter structurally — were dropped in favor of a compressed tournament format. Togashi's ideas for "deconstructing" the characters were rejected. What aired was the skeleton of a story that had been planned to be much more.

This is not rumor. This is what Togashi told people.

## The Scar as Document

The Three Kings arc is commonly treated as a disappointment to be moved past. But it is more useful to read it as evidence — specifically, as the most visible artifact of how the manga industry operated in 1994-1995 and how it treated its creators.

[Yoshihiro Togashi's Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoshihiro_Togashi) records that he developed persistent back problems during this period that have afflicted him ever since. The five-hours-of-sleep schedule was not exceptional. [Left Voice's documentation of manga industry labor conditions](https://www.leftvoice.org/exploitation-in-storytelling-the-conditions-of-manga-artists-in-japan/) notes that weekly serialization in a major magazine like Jump requires fifteen to twenty pages every seven days with no downtime — one Piece's Eiichiro Oda famously operated on three hours of sleep per night at his peak. [HoneyAnime's overview of manga work schedules](https://honeysanime.com/the-insane-work-schedules-of-your-favorite-manga-artists/) situates Togashi within a broader pattern of Jump mangaka whose health broke down under identical conditions. The editorial relationship between Jump and its properties was famously extractive: a popular series was not a creator's property in any meaningful sense but a revenue stream, and the creator was the labor input required to maintain it.

What makes YYH unusual is not that it ended this way. Many series ended this way, or ended worse. What makes it unusual is that Togashi talked about it, and then spent the next thirty years making different decisions.

## Chapter Black as the Real Ending

If you want to understand what Togashi was trying to do with Yu Yu Hakusho, Chapter Black is the argument. [As CBR's analysis notes](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-dark-tournament-chapter-black-best-arc/), the arc represents a deliberate tonal shift from the physical contests of the Dark Tournament toward psychological and moral complexity. The antagonist is not a demon but a human — or rather, a former Spirit Detective, someone who occupied Yusuke's exact position and broke under the weight of what that position revealed.

Shinobu Sensui is one of the great underappreciated villains in shonen anime, a figure whose complexity [the CBR feature on YYH's antagonists](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-sensui-perfect-villain-after-toguro/) describes as a complete inversion of the show's moral framework. He was a dedicated hunter of demons who became radicalized by witnessing human cruelty — specifically, the [Feast of Human Vices](https://yuyuhakusho.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobu_Sensui), a Black Black Club event where humans tortured demons for sport. The discovery destroyed his simple moral architecture (humans good, demons evil) and replaced it with its inverse. His multiple personalities — developed over years of isolation — represent different ways of surviving a broken worldview, none of them functional.

![Yu Yu Hakusho Chapter Black - Sensui](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/57/YuYuHakusho.jpg/220px-YuYuHakusho.jpg)

What Sensui confronts Yusuke with is not a fight but a question: if the humans you protect are capable of the same atrocities as the demons you fight, what is the point of the job? The Chapter Black tape — a recording of human violence — is the key artifact of the arc, a piece of media that Koenma has kept hidden because it proves too much. The concealment is the indictment. [The ResetEra discussion of YYH's arcs](https://www.resetera.com/threads/yu-yu-hakusho-lets-talk-about-chapter-black-and-three-kings.21874/) observes that many fans consider the series to have reached its true resolution here, before the Three Kings arc began.

Togashi agreed. That's the important part.

## Yusuke's Purpose

One of the ways Chapter Black earns its weight is that it puts the right character at the center of its moral question. Yusuke Urameshi is not a typical shonen protagonist — this point has been made repeatedly, but it remains worth examining specifically. He is not Goku or Naruto, characters whose moral goodness is constitutive of their identity. He is a [juvenile delinquent who barely attended school](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-yusuke-great-deliquent-main-character/), raised in unstable circumstances, without the foundational moral certainty that conventional shonen heroes carry by default.

This matters enormously for the Chapter Black confrontation. Sensui expects Yusuke to be the kind of person who would be shattered by the same revelation that broke him. He's wrong — not because Yusuke is more virtuous, but because Yusuke was never invested in the moral binary that Sensui's worldview depended on. Yusuke's response to finding out that humans can be as cruel as demons is essentially: yes, obviously, I've met humans. His moral world was never organized around species affiliation. It was organized around *his* people — specific individuals he has chosen to protect.

[The Wikipedia entry on Yusuke Urameshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yusuke_Urameshi) notes that Togashi designed him to evolve from a slice-of-life character into an action protagonist. What that design produces is a hero whose moral growth is not about becoming good in the abstract but about expanding which specific people he considers worth protecting. He doesn't resolve Sensui's question philosophically. He just doesn't accept its terms.

## What Togashi Took With Him

The direct line from Yu Yu Hakusho's ending to Hunter x Hunter's structure is not subtle. [The Looper article on Togashi's health history](https://www.looper.com/1084068/hunter-x-hunter-writer-yoshihiro-togashis-health-struggles-help-explain-the-mangas-long-hiatus/) documents that the back problems that began during YYH never resolved, and that Hunter x Hunter's notorious hiatus pattern — often years between chapters — is a direct consequence of chronic pain that makes sitting at a drawing desk impossible for extended periods.

But the hiatus pattern is also a creative decision, not merely a medical one. Togashi's approach to HxH from the beginning signaled a refusal of the production conditions that broke YYH: he writes at whatever pace allows him to produce work he's satisfied with, irrespective of publication schedules. The result is a manga that has been running since 1998 with [no confirmed completion date](https://www.cbr.com/manga-industry-burnout/) and whose most recent arc (the Succession War) is the most structurally complex thing he has ever written.

[CBR's feature on manga industry burnout](https://www.cbr.com/manga-industry-burnout/) uses Togashi as the paradigm case — a creator whose talent was never in question but who was ground down by the industrial conditions attached to producing popular work on Weekly Shonen Jump's timetable. The lesson he drew from YYH was that those conditions were non-negotiable within Jump's structure, so negotiation had to happen at the level of what he was willing to give.

## The Legacy in the Wrong Place

[TIME's overview of the YYH Netflix live-action adaptation](https://time.com/6485553/yu-yu-hakusho-netflix/) positions the series' legacy primarily through its influence on later shonen — the direct ancestry between YYH's structure and the DNA of Naruto, Bleach, and explicitly Jujutsu Kaisen. Gege Akutami has been direct about this: [ComicBook.com documents](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/jujutsu-kaisen-bleach-yu-yu-hakusho-homages-creator-explains/) his stated homages to both YYH and Bleach, with [FandomWire's interview coverage](https://fandomwire.com/gege-akutami-admitted-yu-yu-hakushos-character-was-the-blueprint-for-suguru-geto/) confirming that Suguru Geto was designed as a deliberate echo of Sensui — a character who becomes disillusioned with humanity by witnessing its capacity for cruelty. [As CBR's retrospective notes](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-dark-fantasy-shonen-anime-1990s/), the "Dark Trio" of modern Jump — Chainsaw Man, Jujutsu Kaisen, Hell's Paradise — can all be traced through YYH's willingness to use violence and psychological darkness in a genre that had been built on optimism and transformation sequences.

That legacy is real and worth honoring. But it focuses on Yu Yu Hakusho's influence as a successful finished product rather than as a process that broke its creator.

The more honest legacy is this: YYH is the series that taught Yoshihiro Togashi, through direct and painful experience, that the industrial model of serialized shonen manga would consume exactly as much of a creator as that creator permitted. The Three Kings arc is not an unfortunate postscript to a great series. It is the record of the lesson learned. And Hunter x Hunter — chaotic, brilliant, perpetually unfinished, produced on terms that prioritize the creator's survival over the audience's schedule — is the forty-year response.

Togashi did not fail to complete Yu Yu Hakusho. He completed it in the most important sense, and at considerable cost, and then spent the rest of his career making sure he never ended up in that position again.

Whether that choice produced a better outcome for Hunter x Hunter, or just a different kind of damage, is a question the series has not yet answered.
