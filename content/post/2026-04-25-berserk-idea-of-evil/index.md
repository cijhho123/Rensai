On July 12, 1996, [Young Animal magazine published Chapter 83 of Berserk](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_83_(Manga)), titled "God of the Abyss (2)." In it, Griffith — on the verge of his transformation into Femto, having just sacrificed the Band of the Hawk — sinks through the void and encounters a being that identifies itself as the Idea of Evil. The entity explains that it was created by humanity's collective unconscious desire for meaning: humans needed reasons for their suffering, and that need became a god. This god has been managing causality ever since, engineering Griffith's lineage and existence specifically to produce the Eclipse. "Do as you will, chosen one," it tells him. The chapter implies that Griffith's choice at the Eclipse was not really a choice — it was the culmination of a plan centuries in the making, executed by a force assembled from human desire.

Miura then requested that the chapter be removed from every collected volume of the manga, and it has never been officially reprinted.

![Berserk Volume 14 cover by Kentaro Miura — Guts in the immediate aftermath of the Eclipse, the Brand on his neck, equipped as the Black Swordsman](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/the-cover-of-berserk-volume-14.jpg)

## What Miura Said and What He Didn't Say

In 2009, thirteen years after the chapter's removal, Miura addressed it directly — responding to questions submitted by the community at [SkullKnight.net](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/what-up-with-the-lost-chapter.2371/), Berserk's largest dedicated English-language fan forum. ["The appearance of God in the manga conclusively determines its range," he said. "I thought that might limit the freedom of the story development."](https://screenrant.com/berserk-chapter-83-lost-miura-forbidden-factoid/) He added that he did not know whether the Idea of Evil would ever appear in the manga again.

This is a careful statement. Miura did not say the chapter was wrong. He did not say it was non-canonical. He did not say the Idea of Evil didn't exist or that its revelations were inaccurate. He said that revealing it had determined "the range" of the story, and that determination troubled him. The problem wasn't that Chapter 83 was false — it was that it was too true, too early.

The chapter exists in a peculiar ontological state. It was published in a magazine. It is technically part of the serialized manga. The events it depicts — Griffith meeting the Idea of Evil, the revelation that his life was engineered — are not contradicted by anything that appears in the collected volumes. They simply do not appear in the authoritative version of the text. [Multiple fan analyses have noted](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/09/28/fate-causality-why-was-the-lost-chapter-removed/) that the Idea of Evil's logic remains consistent with everything in the volumes — the chapter reads as true whether or not it is "officially" there.

Miura hid a revelation that he could not unwrite.

## The Theology That Was Removed

What the chapter reveals, if you read it, is the story's answer to the question Berserk has always been asking: why does this happen? Why do people suffer? Why is the world structured to allow what happened to Guts and Casca to happen?

[The Idea of Evil is a primal entity born from humanity's collective unconscious](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Idea_of_Evil) — the dark side of the human desire for transcendental meaning. People wanted reasons for pain, death, and suffering, and that wanting accumulated into something that could provide reasons by manufacturing them. The entity manages causality to produce suffering that feels meaningful — and in doing so, ensures that meaning is always paid for in pain. The God Hand and the apostles are its instruments. Griffith was its masterwork.

This is a complete metaphysics. [CBR's analysis of Berserk's causality system](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-rules-of-causality-explained/) notes that the God Hand operates through what the narrative calls "the flow of causality" — not by forcing individual choices but by engineering the conditions under which those choices feel inevitable. The Idea of Evil is the engine behind that flow: a god created by collective human desire that then acts on that collective to produce the conditions that sustain the desire. The suffering creates the need for explanation; the need for explanation creates the Idea; the Idea creates more suffering. [Epoché Magazine's philosophical analysis of Berserk](https://epochemagazine.org/41/berserk-metaphysics-on-the-idea-of-evil/) identifies this as the closed loop at the story's metaphysical foundation: evil as the infinitely self-sustaining production of its own conditions.

If that's the answer, it makes Griffith's "choice" at the Eclipse something closer to destiny than decision. The Idea of Evil tells him it cultivated his lineage, structured the historical context of his life, brought him to this exact point. His answer — ["I want wings"](https://archive.org/details/GodOfTheAbyss2English) — is framed as desire rather than deliberation. [The Eclipse becomes the event the universe was engineered to produce](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon), with Griffith as the avatar of human collective desire for salvation-or-destruction.

Miura removed that answer from the book. And in doing so, he preserved the question.

## What Stays When the Chapter Leaves

The reason Miura's removal is interesting rather than simply pragmatic is what it does to the rest of the text.

[Analyst studies of the Idea of Evil and causality in Berserk](https://epochemagazine.org/41/berserk-metaphysics-on-the-idea-of-evil/) note that its presence in the hidden chapter is consistent with everything visible in the collected volumes — the God Hand's behavior, the structure of behelits, the rules of the Eclipse, the logic of apostles. The theology of suffering-as-meaning is already embedded in the story even without the chapter that names it. Removing Chapter 83 didn't change the story's metaphysics. It just removed the moment where those metaphysics were spoken aloud.

This has a specific effect on how the story reads. Without Chapter 83, the source of the God Hand remains mysterious. Griffith's ascension looks like a terrible choice made in extremity by a man whose world collapsed — motivated by human failure, ambition, and desperation, rather than cosmic engineering. The universe's cruelty is ambient rather than administered. Whether or not Griffith had genuine free will is an open question that the text doesn't resolve.

With Chapter 83, the answer is: no. Or at least, not entirely. He was made for this. The universe specifically produced him.

Miura decided that readers should hold both possibilities. His specific creative move — removing the chapter that closes the question while keeping everything that makes the question live — is a choice to maintain the story's operative ambiguity. [As the Berserk analysis community has noted](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/09/28/fate-causality-why-was-the-lost-chapter-removed/), this shifts moral responsibility back onto individuals: without the Idea of Evil's explicit confirmation that everything was fated, you cannot simply attribute the Eclipse to cosmic mechanics. Griffith still made whatever choice he made, even if its full weight remains contested.

## The Structural Echo

The most interesting thing about this is not what the chapter says about Berserk's metaphysics. It's what Miura's decision says about how the story is structured.

The Idea of Evil is an entity that operates invisibly — [it shapes causality from below the level of perception](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Idea_of_Evil), visible to no one in the main timeline except Griffith in a moment that exists in a chapter no one officially has. It is the force that structures everything but that the story as published never acknowledges. It's present in the logic of every event without being present in the text of those events.

And Miura's editorial decision — to publish the chapter and then make it officially absent — reproduces exactly this structure at the level of authorship. The Idea of Evil is the thing that explains the story while being missing from the story. Chapter 83 is the chapter that explains the story while being missing from the story. They are the same kind of object: present in the logic, absent in the text, shaping everything, never named.

This is not a coincidence that Miura engineered consciously. It is the kind of deep structural rhyme that happens when a creator is thoroughly inhabited by their own work — when the patterns of the story start to operate at the level of craft decisions. Miura hid the answer because he was afraid of limiting himself. In hiding it, he created a situation where the most important piece of information in Berserk is held in exactly the same position as the most important piece of information in Berserk's world: true but inaccessible, structuring but unverifiable, canonical but deliberately kept out of the authoritative text.

## What Kouji Mori Inherited

When [Kentaro Miura died in May 2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berserk_(manga)), the Idea of Evil had not reappeared in the manga's collected volumes. [Kouji Mori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kouji_Mori), his longtime friend and the person continuing the story based on notes and conversations with Miura, now holds the question of whether and how to reintroduce it. In a [CBR interview on continuing the series](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-new-writer-manga-continue/), Mori stated that he would "only write the episodes that Miura talked to me about" and would not flesh out or invent anything Miura did not specifically describe. He framed the continuation as an act of fidelity to a friend's intentions rather than an authorial takeover. [SkullKnight.net has summarized Mori's public statements](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/summary-of-kouji-moris-statements-on-the-berserk-continuation.16634/) in detail — he has confirmed that his knowledge of Miura's intended story extends to the ending, but has not addressed whether Chapter 83's theology is part of what he was told.

[The Popverse has reported](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon) that the chapter's fate — whether the Idea of Evil returns, whether Chapter 83 is ever reprinted, whether Mori is even working with that material — is uncertain. Mori knows what Miura told him. He knows what the chapter contains. Whether he considers it usable is an open question.

What Miura left him is not just an unfinished manga. He left him an unresolved act of authorial censorship — a chapter that exists and doesn't exist, a revelation that shapes the story while being formally hidden from it. Mori inheriting Berserk means inheriting not just the plot but this specific problem: what to do with the thing that explains everything, the god that lives below the text, the chapter that Miura published and then hid.

Berserk has always been about things that are present in the structure of the universe but absent from anyone's ability to name them. It turns out the story is made the same way.
