In 2018, Kodansha [announced that Oh! Great — the artist behind Air Gear and Tenjho Tenge — was drawing a manga adaptation of Bakemonogatari](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2018-02-28/air-gear-artist-oh-great-revealed-as-bakemonogatari-manga-artist/.128394). The announcement surprised people. Oh! Great had made his reputation on kinetic action and elaborate erotic detail — beautifully rendered bodies in motion or in states of undress, the physicality of inline skating and martial arts turned into visual argument. Bakemonogatari is famously a franchise about talking. The bodies are almost beside the point.

Almost.

The [reaction to the announcement](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2018-02-28/air-gear-artist-oh-great-revealed-as-bakemonogatari-manga-artist/.128394) ranged from bemused to enthusiastic, but the underlying question — whether Oh! Great was the right fit — quietly reveals something important about the franchise itself. Because SHAFT had already answered that question by making the opposite choice, and the two answers are at war with each other in a way that goes deeper than style.

## What SHAFT Chose to Remove

The received argument about SHAFT's Bakemonogatari is that the abstract backgrounds, geometric compositions, and empty spaces were budget-saving measures that became accidentally perfect — a studio too broke for backgrounds accidentally discovering that removing the world focuses everything on the characters. This is partially true and mostly wrong. The budget constraints existed; the specific visual choices that emerged from them were [deliberate directorial decisions encoded into the series' final form](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Shaft).

What SHAFT actually did was make a formal argument. The [franchise wiki notes](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(anime_series)) that only two or three characters are ever on screen at once, that locations appear abandoned, that background figures are simply absent. The [visual analysis blog "Mistakes of Youth"](https://blog.mistakesofyouth.com/2010/09/24/visualized-bakemonogatari-15-part-i-lighting-and-setting-2/) documents this precisely: the landscape consists of wide blank spaces punctuated by copy-pasted objects in bold, solid colors. Senjougahara's house is "small, rather empty, open, and kind of broken." The school exists as isolated architectural fragments — a staircase here, a window array there — never as a coherent space.

SHAFT was encoding the series' central thesis in its visual grammar. Araragi's world is empty because Araragi has been empty. His apartment during Golden Week, where most of Bakemonogatari is set, is a space of withdrawal. The girls he encounters each inhabit their own isolated fragment of the world — their problems exist in architectural bubbles, severed from a social fabric the anime refuses to show existing. [The emptiness isn't representing isolation; it is isolation, made legible.](https://ponzianialexvic107.wordpress.com/2018/10/30/bakemonogatari-when-art-and-surreal-design-meet-story-telling-and-animation/)

The bodies got the same treatment. SHAFT famously converts physical moments — the stapler scene, the "toothbrush scene," every ecchi moment in the franchise — into angular compositions, symbolic imagery, and strategic abstract cuts. The [Shaft head tilt](https://gamerant.com/what-is-the-shaft-head-tilt/) became a meme precisely because it takes a body and makes it impossible: the neck doesn't move that way, the angle is inhuman, the shot refuses anatomical realism. You are looking at a diagram of desire, not the thing itself.

This was a choice. And it says something specific: the erotic content in NisiOisin's text is not what it appears to be. When Araragi describes Senjougahara's body in detail as she holds a stapler to his throat, the physical description is functioning as displacement. Something else is being processed. SHAFT read this and decided to encode it formally: the body becomes geometry because what the narrator is actually handling is not a body.

## What Oh! Great Chose to Restore

The manga [tells a different story about the same text](https://www.cbr.com/bakemonogatari-manga-differences-light-novels-anime/).

[In Oh! Great's version](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(manga)), Araragi "looks more like a suave movie star than the scruffy loner seen in the anime." The characters receive "a fresh new feel" — cleaner, more conventionally attractive, more physically present. Where the anime had Araragi as a somewhat rumpled teenager, Oh! Great draws him as a figure with genuine charisma, someone who looks like he belongs in the kind of story NisiOisin is nominally telling. The sexual content remains, with [brand-new scenes added for the opening arc](https://www.cbr.com/bakemonogatari-manga-differences-light-novels-anime/) that don't appear in the anime or light novel. The [manga is less comedically playful than the anime](https://theturnaroundblog.com/2019/10/03/bakemonogatari-manga-1-review-a-new-take-on-the-acclaimed-light-novels/), but more directly invested in the physical register.

The specific visual choices Oh! Great makes are worth examining closely, because he is not simply illustrating the existing text. [He plays with camera angles for visual excitement and deliberately references SHAFT's own visual grammar](http://mangatestdrive.blogspot.com/2019/12/holiday-review-bakemonogatari.html) — including the signature head tilt — but these are quotations, nods to a style he's working alongside rather than within. When Oh! Great does a head tilt, it's a clean reference; when SHAFT does it, it's the franchise's native language. His "stark inking" and fine detail work render the characters' bodies as precise and immediate, where SHAFT left them diagrammatic. The speech bubble placement in the manga even [performs the text's censorship as visual comedy](http://mangatestdrive.blogspot.com/2019/12/holiday-review-bakemonogatari.html) — a formal wit that differs from SHAFT's, which performed it as philosophical displacement.

Oh! Great is reading NisiOisin's physical descriptions at face value. The novels are explicitly detailed about women's bodies — not metaphorically detailed, not ironically detailed, but cataloguing detail in a way that is clearly meant to be received as erotic. Oh! Great, as an artist who has built [an entire career on exactly this kind of rendering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oh!_great), accepts this as the text's primary investment and illustrates accordingly.

The [manga was serialized in Weekly Shōnen Magazine from 2018 to 2023](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(manga)) — over two million copies in circulation by 2020 — and Oh! Great draws the adaptation with genuine care for the source. [He adapted Kizumonogatari and Nekomonogatari's material specifically so that the Tsubasa Cat arc would land with proper emotional weight](https://www.cbr.com/bakemonogatari-manga-differences-light-novels-anime/). He is not simply providing illustrations for a text he doesn't understand; he has engaged with it seriously. His reading just begins from a different premise: the bodies matter, and they should be present in the frame as bodies.

## What NisiOisin Actually Wrote

The problem is that NisiOisin wrote both things at once, and the tension between them is not a flaw. It's the mechanism.

The [Nisemonogatari brush scene](https://subtlechaos.wordpress.com/2012/03/04/on-nisemonogatari-8-and-fanservice/) — an extended sequence of Araragi brushing his sister's teeth that became one of the most discussed sequences in the franchise — is the clearest articulation of this mechanism. The physical content is explicit and intentional. The conversation happening during it is about authenticity, imitation, and whether the act of perfect simulation constitutes the real thing. The erotic and the philosophical are not layered on top of each other; they are the same object viewed from different angles. Remove either and you don't get the other one more clearly — you get nothing.

SHAFT understood this enough to know that translating it literally would collapse the tension. By converting the body into geometry, they force the viewer to confront the philosophical content directly, without the erotic displacement the narrator is using. The result is that the franchise's central argument — about desire, about whether desire can see clearly what it wants — becomes visible. Araragi wants to help these girls. He also wants to look at them. SHAFT's visual language refuses to let these two things coexist comfortably. The abstract space insists: the desire is a displacement, a substitute for something else. [Critics who read the franchise as primarily a male-gaze exercise](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2015-08-07/monogatari-is-a-disaster/.90575) are responding to a real feature of the text — but what they're naming is the layer Oh! Great illustrates, not the layer SHAFT chose to show. The argument isn't that the franchise has no male gaze; it's that SHAFT's visual choices are specifically structured to make that gaze visible as a mechanism, rather than merely comfortable to experience.

Oh! Great's manga takes the erotic content at face value and produces something that is technically faithful and emotionally different. Manga Araragi is suave. The new scenes feel less like the narrator's anxious cataloguing and more like straightforward fantasy. The franchise's characteristic ambivalence — is Araragi a selfless helper or a voyeur in a savior's costume? — is harder to maintain when the visual language has decided he's the former.

## The Adaptation That Reveals the Source

This is not an argument that the manga is bad. [It sold over two million copies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(manga)); it's clearly doing something right for a large readership. And Oh! Great's engagement with the character dynamics is genuine — [Senjougahara's "complexities take on new levels" in manga form](https://theturnaroundblog.com/2019/10/03/bakemonogatari-manga-1-review-a-new-take-on-the-acclaimed-light-novels/), and his ability to render her stationer's-grade sadism with visual weight is one of the things Oh! Great is uniquely positioned to deliver.

But the comparison is useful for what it reveals about the anime's choices. SHAFT's Bakemonogatari looks strange because it refuses to be comfortable. It refuses to give the viewer the bodies the narrator keeps describing. That refusal is the argument. The franchise is about a narrator who talks about bodies as a way of avoiding what he actually feels, and a visual language that performs the same operation — converting physical presence into abstraction, desire into geometry, the seen into the diagrammed — is not failing to translate the text. It is translating the text at a deeper level than illustration can reach.

The relationship between the two adaptations is stranger than rivalry. In 2022, [SHAFT itself produced an anime promotional video for Oh! Great's manga](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2022-02-17/bakemonogatari-manga-gets-anime-promo-video-by-shaft/.182729) — the studio using its own visual language to promote a version of the same franchise that makes opposite visual choices. The two adaptations are in dialogue, not in competition, and SHAFT's willingness to support the manga suggests an awareness that different readers find different things in the same text.

Oh! Great's manga is the franchise without that refusal. It's interesting for that reason. Every choice the manga makes to restore bodies, to make Araragi attractive, to add erotic content, is simultaneously a choice to take NisiOisin at his most surface-legible. SHAFT chose to read what's underneath the surface. And that difference — between reading the letters of a text and reading its silences — is the entire argument about adaptation.

Bakemonogatari was always a franchise about what you won't look at directly. The anime turned that into a method. The manga turned it into a story. Both are right about what's there. Only one is right about what it means.
