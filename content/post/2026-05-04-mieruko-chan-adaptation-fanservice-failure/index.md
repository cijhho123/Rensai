The problem with the Passione anime adaptation of *Mieruko-chan* is not that it added fanservice. The problem is that it added fanservice the [source manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mieruko-chan) was actively shedding.

Tomoki Izumi's webcomic-turned-manga started, in its earliest incarnations, with a significant fanservice component — camera angles focused on Miko's body, leering shots inserted during mundane scenes, the kind of cheesecake material that functions in seinen horror as a palette cleanser between scares. By Chapter 17, that material had [almost completely disappeared](https://www.goodreads.com/questions/1969839-how-bad-is-the-fan-service-i-m). The manga evolved. Izumi understood something through the process of making it: that the fanservice and the horror were in competition, and the horror was winning.

Passione's 2021 anime adaptation, directed by Yuki Ogawa, chose the other side of that competition. The [anime included significantly more male gaze than the original manga](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Webcomic/MierukoChan), particularly focusing the camera on Miko's legs and body in sequences where the manga had moved past that approach. Multiple critics noted the tension, with ANN's Caitlin Moore describing being haunted by "gaze-y camera angles" and "[random, subtle out-of-place fanservice shots](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2020-11-20/the-fall-2020-manga-guide/mieruko-chan/.166181)" that she couldn't stop noticing. This is not a reading comprehension problem on Moore's part — it is a description of how the adaptation chose to frame its protagonist.

The result is an anime that undermines its own best moments by refusing to look at its protagonist the way its premise requires.

![Mieruko-chan key visual](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/72/Mieruko_chan.jpg)

## Why the Gaze Is the Problem

*Mieruko-chan*'s premise depends on a specific relationship between viewer and protagonist. Miko Yotsuya sees ghosts — horrifying, elaborate, grotesque spirits that follow her and the people she cares about. She cannot make them go away, and she cannot reveal that she sees them, because [in the show's internal logic](https://mierukochan.fandom.com/wiki/Spirit), awareness invites attention. To survive, she must perform complete indifference while internally enduring what the viewer can see around her.

This is psychological horror. The dread is not "will the monster appear" — it appears immediately, constantly, in broad daylight — but "will Miko's composure break." The viewer watches her hold herself together under sustained, unrelenting supernatural assault. What makes that work is empathy: the viewer needs to be with Miko, inhabiting her perspective, sharing the weight of what she cannot acknowledge.

Sexual objectification of the protagonist destroys that relationship. You cannot inhabit a person's perspective while simultaneously positioning yourself as a voyeur surveilling their body. The male gaze, in the technical sense — the camera as an instrument of evaluation and desire directed at the female subject — creates a categorical distance between viewer and viewed. The moment the camera lingers on Miko's legs during a horror scene, you are no longer inside her experience. You are outside it, looking at her as an object. That is the opposite of what the show needs.

Horror has understood this for decades, imperfectly. The slasher genre's use of the female body as simultaneously victim and spectacle is well-documented and extensively criticized. *Mieruko-chan*'s specific horror variant — which requires sustained, empathetic identification with a protagonist enduring invisible-to-others dread — makes the problem more acute than usual. The [AniTAY review](https://medium.com/anitay-official/some-thoughts-on-mieruko-chan-b7101d6057d4) that criticized the show for removing tension was describing this failure, even if it located the problem in the premise rather than the adaptation: when the monster is always visible and the protagonist is always shot as a spectacle, the viewer floats detached from both, relating to nothing.

## What the Manga Understood

Izumi's decision to phase out the fanservice wasn't subtle. The transition is visible and deliberate across the manga's early volumes, as the series starts taking its ghost lore and character dynamics seriously enough that the leering camera became tonally incompatible with what the story was becoming.

The [manga's ghost designs](https://mierukochan.fandom.com/wiki/Spirit) reflect a serialized mythology: the spirits range from tragic echoes of human death to active supernatural predators to beings that function like kami — spirits powerful enough that the shrine spirits Miko inadvertently binds herself to have their own hierarchy and agenda. [The fan analysis on Tumblr](https://www.tumblr.com/mask131/670200805711904768/analysis-of-mieruko-chan-ghosts-chap-1) that broke down each ghost's design in the first chapters identified the specific J-horror grammar Izumi was drawing on: water association with supernatural activity, the logic of bakemono (shape-changed ghosts), cat spirits as nekomata variants. This is a horror manga building its internal consistency with real folklore vocabulary.

That project — making the supernatural rules feel lived-in and real — requires the reader's credulity. Credulity is undermined by the author elbowing you and winking while a ghost eats someone's soul nearby. The fanservice was shedding precisely because Izumi was building something that couldn't survive it.

## Why Passione Made the Choice It Made

Understanding why the Passione adaptation amplified what the manga was reducing requires thinking about how anime studios pitch and produce source material for seinen audiences.

[Passione](https://anilist.co/studio/858/Passione), a studio whose catalog includes *Uzaki-chan Wants to Hang Out!* and *High School DxD New* among others, has a production history oriented toward fanservice-friendly seinen content. Taking on *Mieruko-chan* at the point in its serialization where the manga had moved away from fanservice, they appear to have adapted from an earlier understanding of the property — the early webcomic and first volumes, where the leering camera still existed — rather than from the direction the series was actually heading.

There is also a commercial logic — though this is inference rather than documented intent, since no Passione production interview addresses the decision directly. The [manga's early fanservice material](https://www.goodreads.com/questions/1969839-how-bad-is-the-fan-service-i-m) represents how the series initially built its seinen readership before the horror took over as the primary hook. An anime adaptation looking to capture maximum audience at launch might reasonably identify that early material as a revenue-generating hook, particularly when the horror genre's mainstream anime audience was not the most obvious target demographic in 2021. The fanservice is, in this reading, a risk-mitigation strategy: bring in the viewers who might not have come for horror, then convert them.

The problem is that this strategy was applied to a property that had already proven it did not need it. The manga's readership was following the horror, not the fanservice — as evidenced by [continued critical interest in the manga's monster lore](https://sequentialplanet.com/manga-review-mieruko-chan-volume-one/) and the series running to [fourteen tankōbon volumes as of March 2026](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mieruko-chan), still serializing. A [live-action film released in June 2025](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mieruko-chan) confirms the property's commercial durability. That audience was cultivated by the property's evolution toward horror, not by preserving its earliest impulses.

## The Specific Failure Mode

What makes the Passione adaptation worth examining beyond "studio made bad creative choices" is the pattern it represents: an anime adaptation preserving or amplifying a phase of a property that the source material had already moved past.

This happens regularly. A manga or webcomic starts with a certain formula — fanservice, comedy beats, a lighter tone — and evolves over time toward something more ambitious. When an adaptation is produced, the studio reads the existing volumes but often processes them through the lens of the property's initial identity rather than its trajectory. The adaptation freezes the property at a point the creator had already left.

![Mieruko-chan manga volume cover](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/36/Mieruko-chan_volume_1_cover.jpg)

*Mieruko-chan* is an unusually clear case because the shift is documented and the direction is unambiguous. Izumi did not gradually reduce fanservice because the manga got more popular and he was targeting a different audience. He reduced it because the story he was actually telling — of a girl surviving supernatural dread through sheer will — could not be told while the camera was also appraising her body. He recognized an incompatibility and resolved it in favor of the story.

Passione's adaptation chose not to make that resolution. It tried to have both, in a premise where having both is precisely the problem. The result is an anime that often works — the [monster designs by Makoto Uno](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2021/fall/mieruko-chan/.177541) are genuinely disturbing, the core performance of sustained composure under dread works, and there are sequences that hit exactly the register the premise promises — but that consistently undercuts itself at its most effective moments by repositioning the viewer as a voyeur rather than a companion.

*Mieruko-chan*'s horror works when you are in it with Miko. When the camera reminds you that you are looking *at* her instead, the ghost ceases to be terrifying and becomes merely disgusting — a CGI creature in a show that has already told you, with its camera, that the person it's terrorizing is here for your visual consumption. The manga understood what that does to the reader. The anime did not.
