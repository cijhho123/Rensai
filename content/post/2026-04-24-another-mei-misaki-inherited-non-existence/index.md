The class doesn't choose Mei Misaki arbitrarily. There's something in the mechanics of [*Another*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/11111/Another) that requires a specific type of person for the countermeasure to work: someone who can be collectively erased and stay erased, someone who won't fight the institutional forgetting, someone who already knows, on some level, how to not be there. The show never names this explicitly. It just hands you the facts of Mei's life and lets you work out the logic yourself.

Once you do, the horror of *Another* gets sharper and uglier. The curse didn't create Mei's non-existence. It inherited it.

## The Girl Who Replaced a Loss

Start at the beginning. [Mei Misaki](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki) was born a twin. Her biological mother, Mitsuyo Fujioka, gave her to her younger sister Yukiyo Misaki shortly after birth — because Yukiyo had recently miscarried, was still in grief, and needed a child to raise. So Mei was transferred. She entered the world as a functional substitute for someone else's dead baby. Not an adoption driven by love or circumstance, but a replacement, one grieving woman filling another's loss with a living infant she had to spare.

This is the foundational fact of Mei's existence: she did not arrive in her family as herself. She arrived as a solution to an absence. Her name, her legal identity, her entire family situation were constructed to cover over a loss that predated her. She has always been, at some level, someone else's proxy.

Yukiyo Misaki runs a doll shop. When Mei loses her left eye to disease, her mother gives her a prosthetic — a [doll's eye](https://www.cbr.com/another-main-characters-trivia-hidden-details/). Not a glass eye of the kind you'd get from an ophthalmologist; a doll's eye, made from the same material as the figures her mother creates for a living. The symbolism here isn't subtle, but it's precise. Yukiyo has a particular relationship with beautiful objects that substitute for living things. She makes dolls. She raised a daughter who replaced a miscarried child. When her daughter's body needs repair, she reaches for the tools of her trade. Mei gets a doll's eye because, on some register, her mother has always seen her as a doll — something you can fix, something beautiful and still and usable.

The eye does give Mei a power: she can see the [color of death](https://hero.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki) hovering over people who are about to die or who have died but are still walking around. The show treats this as a horror element, a tool for the plot's resolution. But structurally it's also a statement about what it means to be raised as an object. The thing that objectified her is also what makes her uniquely capable of perceiving death's presence in the living. You can read that as the show giving her a compensatory gift, or you can read it as the show saying that her particular kind of formation — as substitute, as repaired object — gave her a mode of perception nobody else in the story has access to.

## The Surplus Twin

The second layer of her non-existence comes from the twin she didn't know she had.

Mei grew up believing [Misaki Fujioka](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Misaki_Fujioka), the girl she visited and loved, was her cousin. She discovers in fifth grade that Misaki is actually her twin sister — the one who stayed with their biological mother after Mei was given away. Then Misaki dies of leukemia. The show eventually reveals that Misaki Fujioka's death is what triggered the 1998 curse: she was a student of Class 3-3 that year, and the class's grief over losing her brought her back as the [undead extra](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/The_Calamity) embedded in the classroom roster.

Work through the implications. Mei is the surviving member of a divided twin pair. Her twin was taken by the curse that now endangers Mei and everyone around her. The dead twin's return is the mechanism by which Mei gets designated as non-existent. In other words: the sister who died is literally the reason Mei gets erased. The loss that originally displaced Mei at birth — her mother's need to give her away, her aunt's need to receive her — echoes forward twenty years into a supernatural mechanism that enacts the same gesture, just collectively rather than privately. The family gave her away as surplus once. The [curse makes her surplus again](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)).

This is not coincidence. Ayatsuji structures his story around a logic of doubling and excess — [*Another* was nominated for the Honkaku Mystery Award in 2010](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)), and it has the architecture of a puzzle mystery, with the fundamental puzzle being: which person in the room doesn't belong? Mei's backstory answers that question at a biographical level before the plot even poses it formally. She was the child who didn't belong to the family she was born into. She is the twin who survived when the other died. She is always, in every context, the extra one.

## What the Countermeasure Actually Requires

The class's [countermeasure](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Countermeasures) has always disturbed people more than the deaths it's supposed to prevent. You designate one student as non-existent; the entire class, including teachers, collectively agrees to pretend that person isn't there. It's [a formalization of bullying](https://www.japanpowered.com/anime-articles/another), and fan discussions have long noted its uncomfortable resemblance to [*mura hachibu*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murahachibu_ostracism) — the Japanese village practice of collectively cutting off social ties with anyone who violates community rules, a practice [rooted in the Edo period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murahachibu_ostracism) but whose logic has persisted in school *ijime* culture, where [group exclusion operates through collective participation](https://savvytokyo.com/bullying-japanese-schools/) and the diffusion of individual responsibility. The class does something *mura hachibu* always did: it makes cruelty institutional so that nobody has to feel personally culpable for what happens to the target. The show presents it as a necessary horror. You treat one person as nothing to stop everybody from dying.

What the countermeasure requires — and what people rarely interrogate — is someone who will accept total erasure and keep coming to school. The designated student has to endure the process without breaking — without making a scene, without demanding acknowledgment, without the kind of angry resistance that would force the class to confront what they're doing. They need someone who has already built a tolerance for invisibility.

Mei has been building that tolerance her whole life. She was given away at birth and told nothing about why. Her mother sees her partly as a beautiful object. Her closest bond — with her twin — was hidden from her and then severed by death. By the time Class 3-3 designates her as non-existent, she has years of practice in occupying space that isn't fully acknowledged. She doesn't fight the countermeasure. She retreats to the old school building with her sketchbook and waits for someone to actually see her.

What makes Kōichi's acknowledgment of her so charged — and so disruptive to the curse's mechanics — is precisely this. [He sees her](https://myanimelist.net/anime/11111/Another). Not in the casual sense of noticing someone in a hallway, but in the sense of treating her presence as real and her words as worth hearing. For someone whose entire life has been structured around managed erasure, being seen is an event. It's also, the show makes clear, the thing that breaks the system. The countermeasure works through collective commitment to a fiction. The moment one person refuses the fiction, it starts to unravel.

## The Curse's Perfect Candidate

There's a grim efficiency to who gets selected. The class's collective amnesia (another feature of the curse — [people connected to dead classmates lose their memories of them](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/The_Calamity)) means they can't remember their reasoning. But the logic is present in the text: you need someone who can be made to disappear without the disappearance causing too much friction. Someone socially isolated enough that their designation won't break down under protest. Someone who, at some level, already understands that their presence in the world is negotiable.

Mei fits this description with disturbing precision. She's not the most popular student. She doesn't have a thick web of friendships that would create pressure to include her. Her emotional self-containment — which is legible to a casual viewer as "mysterious girl" characterization — is actually the hard-won coping mechanism of someone who has spent years existing in a family that partially pretended she was someone else.

The [academic hauntological reading of *Another*](https://newvoices.org.au/volume-12/the-past-is-with-us-and-yet-to-come-a-hauntological-analysis-of-tsutomu-mizushimas-anime-series-another/), applying Derrida's framework to the series, argues that the curse operates as an allegory for Japan's post-WWII historical consciousness: deny a death officially, and it comes back to haunt subsequent generations. That reading holds at a societal scale — and it maps cleanly onto Mei's personal history. The hauntological argument identifies Mei as occupying "temporal in-betweenness," positioned between past and present, denied full acknowledgment — a figure structurally analogous to the *hibakusha*, the atomic bomb survivors who were officially unacknowledged, existing in a liminal space the rest of society preferred not to look at directly. Her family's refusal to name what they did when they gave her away — acting as if displacement were neutral — prefigures exactly the institutional amnesia the curse enforces on Class 3-3.

[Director Tsutomu Mizushima](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/people.php?id=13401) and P.A. Works made a clear choice in adapting the source material: the anime amplified the deaths far beyond what [Ayatsuji's novel required](https://www.englishlightnovels.com/2015/04/13/feature-another-look-at-another-the-novel-vs-the-anime/). According to comparative analysis of the two versions, "the novel is a lot tamer in comparison" and "more focused on keeping readers attached to details regarding Misaki's life or the other students' views of her," while the anime "focused extensively on the deaths and even enhanced the intensity of them." The final two episodes include substantial anime-original content — the girls attacking Mei, the mass murder sequence, the collapsing building — none of which appears in the novel. Some of what gets lost in that shift is the interiority around Mei's character. The anime hands you the facts of her backstory but doesn't linger on what those facts accumulate into. You have to do that work yourself.

It's worth doing. Because the most disturbing thing about Mei Misaki isn't the eye patch or the quiet or the ability to see who's about to die. It's the recognition that the curse didn't manufacture her capacity for non-existence. It just found it, recognized it, and put it to use.
