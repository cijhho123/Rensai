When a horror story puts a child at the center, there are two conventions available. The first makes the child the victim — the most vulnerable target, the pure soul the audience must protect. The second makes the child the conduit — the one whose uncanny perception opens a door to something the adults can't understand or control.

[*Dark Gathering*](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=26147) does neither. Yayoi Hozuki, the elementary schooler at the heart of Kenichi Kondo's manga and its [anime adaptation by OLM](https://animecorner.me/kondo-kenichis-dark-gathering-horror-manga-gets-anime-adaptation/), is not the victim. She is the predator. The ghosts are not hunting her. She is hunting them — systematically, ruthlessly, and with the kind of long-term strategic patience that should be impossible for a child and is horrifying precisely because it isn't.

The series' central irony, and the thing that separates it from the crowded horror-shonen field, is that the correct response to Yayoi is fear. Not fear of the ghosts. Fear of her.

## What Horror Requires

Standard horror storytelling is organized around a power asymmetry: the supernatural entity is stronger, faster, more knowledgeable than the human protagonist. What gives horror its texture is the experience of being outmatched — the audience's identification with vulnerability, the slow revelation that the rules they understood don't apply, the inevitability of confrontation with something that does not negotiate.

[Most horror anime maintain this structure](https://gamerant.com/the-creepiest-anime-of-summer-2023/) even when they add combat elements. In *Jujutsu Kaisen*, the sorcerers can fight the curses — but the curses are always portrayed as genuinely dangerous, and loss is always possible. The power asymmetry fluctuates but never disappears entirely. The audience knows what it means to be on the human side.

*Dark Gathering* disrupts this at the premise level. [Yayoi has developed, entirely on her own, a system for capturing dangerous spirits](https://dark-gathering.fandom.com/wiki/Yayoi_Hozuki) by sealing them inside mystic substitute dolls, then subjecting new captures to the aggression of her existing prisoners until they submit — or get devoured. Keitaro, who has a spiritual constitution that makes him supernaturally sensitive and who spent two years as a shut-in after a traumatic ghost encounter, describes this method as "quite cruel" and [visibly shudders at the thought that a child invented it](https://www.cbr.com/viz-media-dark-gathering-vol-1-manga-review/). He is correct to shudder.

The TV Tropes entry for the series captures it well: Yayoi can be seen as a cross between Mathilda Wormwood and Robert Neville, [being regarded as a terrifying monster by those we would normally perceive as terrifying monsters](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Manga/DarkGathering). The entities that, in any other horror story, would be the threat, are here the prey. What Yayoi does to them is not clean.

## The Ghost She's Not Afraid Of

The series begins with Keitaro emerging from two years of isolation. His childhood friend Eiko — whose casual disregard for his trauma is [itself treated as a horror-comedy undercurrent](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2023/summer/dark-gathering/.199288) — has arranged for him to tutor her young cousin as part of his rehabilitation into society. The cousin is Yayoi.

What follows is not a protective adult-and-spooky-child dynamic. It's an inversion of that trope: Keitaro is the audience proxy, experiencing everything the way a competent horror audience should. He is frightened. He is reluctant. He notices when things are wrong and says so. He sees exactly what Yayoi is doing and objects to it on moral grounds that the narrative takes seriously.

[Yayoi, by contrast, is functionally fearless toward the supernatural](https://hero.fandom.com/wiki/Yayoi_Hozuki). Her double-pupil eyes — which the series frames as eerie and which other characters react to as unsettling — see spirits clearly at all times. She has been operating her ghost-capture system, improving it, refining it, since childhood. She speaks about enslaved spirits with the matter-of-fact tone of a researcher discussing experimental subjects. She distinguishes between spirits that "deserve" harsh treatment (the actively malevolent) and innocent ones (to whom she extends mercy), which gives her a moral framework — just not one that looks normal from the outside.

This is the series' sharpest move: Yayoi's methods are understandable. Her mother's soul was stolen by an enormously powerful entity called Kuubo. She has bent her entire childhood toward accumulating enough spiritual force to rescue her. Her cause is sympathetic. But [the way she pursues it is, by any normal measure, monstrous](https://obscuremanga.wordpress.com/2022/10/31/dark-gathering/) — and the series never lets Keitaro (or the audience) forget that the ghosts she tortures and enslaves used to be people.

## Keitaro as Genre Surrogate

This is where the structural intelligence of *Dark Gathering* becomes clear. The series is not actually about Yayoi. It is about Keitaro.

[He is the protagonist in the sense that matters for audience identification](https://harperanimereviews.com/lets-ghost-dark-gathering-spoiler-review/): he is the character processing the experience in real time, the one whose emotional arc tracks what this world costs to inhabit. [Following a traumatic encounter that left him a shut-in for two years](https://dark-gathering.fandom.com/wiki/Keitaro_Gentoga), he has barely rejoined society when Yayoi enters his life. He lacks her offensive capabilities and cannot capture or command ghosts. What he can do is notice, and object, and choose to remain despite his better judgment — and the narrative takes his discomfort seriously rather than treating it as cowardice to be overcome.

This puts Keitaro in the role of what horror criticism sometimes calls the genre-literate character: the one who knows what kind of story they're in and is scared for the right reasons. The problem is that his fear runs in two directions. He fears the ghosts — which is conventional and correct. He also fears what Yayoi is becoming, or may already be.

The Mathilda Wormwood / Robert Neville comparison is apt and I'll own it: Yayoi works because she is simultaneously sympathetic and frightening in the exact proportion that makes you unable to look away. Like Mathilda, she is a brilliant child using extraordinary capabilities in response to adults' failure to protect her. Like Neville in *I Am Legend*, she is [the monster that other monsters fear](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Manga/DarkGathering) — and the audience has to decide whether "monster" is condemnation or description. The [ANN preview noted discomfort](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2023/summer/dark-gathering/.199288) with how Yayoi and Eiko dismiss Keitaro's trauma. That discomfort is correct to have — and it's what the series is deliberately staging. You are supposed to be uneasy. Keitaro's perspective is validated.

That second fear is where *Dark Gathering* does something most horror-shonen doesn't bother with. [The capture mechanic is genuinely R-rated not for gore but for ethics](https://www.comicsbeat.com/manga-review-dark-gathering-oozes-a-creepy-atmosphere/). Imprisoning a vengeful spirit in a doll so it can absorb damage meant for Yayoi, then throwing new captures in to be beaten until they submit — this is not a heroic power system. It is coercion and cruelty, however logically justified by Yayoi's goal. Keitaro sees this clearly. So does the reader. The series does not ask us to look away.

## The Atmosphere Works Because the Monster Is Central

Kondo's art does the structural work at the panel level. [The style — heavy blacks, stark inked shadows, spirit designs built from abstract rather than folkloric templates](https://www.comicsbeat.com/manga-review-dark-gathering-oozes-a-creepy-atmosphere/) — reads as a visual argument for the series' central claim: even the moments of mundane horror, in broad daylight, carry the weight of something fundamentally wrong in the composition. [Reviewers have compared the atmosphere to Junji Ito](https://merlinsmusings.com/2024/10/01/anime-review-dark-gathering/), and the comparison holds not just because of the horror content but because both artists use negative space and shadow to suggest that something is present in the frame that shouldn't be. In *Dark Gathering*, that "something" is increasingly Yayoi herself.

In conventional horror-action, the protagonist defeating the monster is cathartic. You were scared; now you're relieved; the power balance has been restored. In *Dark Gathering*, the power balance was never what you thought it was. What you're watching is a child accumulate an army of enslaved spirits to challenge an entity that stole her mother. The victories don't feel like liberation. They feel like escalation. [The Blu-ray review from Anime UK News](https://animeuknews.net/2025/04/dark-gathering-collection-blu-ray-review/) notes how the anime's sound design amplifies this: scraping and clicking effects create tension not just in the ghost encounters but in the quieter scenes where Yayoi is simply present — as if the atmosphere acknowledges, correctly, that she is the most dangerous thing in the room.

## The Second Season's Stakes

[When Season 2 was announced in October 2025](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2025/10/28/dark-gathering-season-2-anime-announced), the production staff described it as "a second season made due to popularity" — confirmation that the series' particular approach found its audience. The setting shift to Kyoto is the meaningful detail: a city where the spiritual geography is older, more entrenched, and historically more resistant to being captured and enslaved than the suburban haunts of Season 1. If the central argument of the series is about what Yayoi's methodology costs her, Kyoto is where that cost should come due. [The franchise has now crossed 2.65 million copies in circulation](https://www.fandompost.com/2025/01/05/manga-by-the-numbers-dark-gathering-by-kenichi-kondo-crosses-2-million-mark/), suggesting that this inversion — the horror at the center of the protagonist rather than surrounding her — is exactly what readers were looking for.

What drives that growth is exactly what makes *Dark Gathering* distinctive: the audience is not rooting for the protagonist to survive against the monsters. They are rooting for the protagonist to help the other protagonist survive herself. Keitaro's job — and ours — is not to defeat the horror. It is to watch a child become capable of defeating it and try to understand what that cost her.

That's a different story than the one most horror-shonen tells. It requires more from the audience and gives more in return.

Some horror asks: can you survive? *Dark Gathering* asks something more uncomfortable: what do you become, surviving?
