The Eclipse is one of manga's most harrowing sequences. In the span of a few dozen pages of [Berserk](https://myanimelist.net/manga/2/Berserk), Griffith — beautiful, visionary, the man every member of the Band of the Hawk would have died for — activates a demonic ritual that sacrifices every one of them as offerings. He watches them die while becoming Femto, the newest member of the God Hand, a creature that barely resembles the person he was. And then he leaves.

The standard reading is: Griffith betrayed his friends. He was corrupted. He chose power over loyalty.

I want to argue something harder: Griffith never had friends. He had people he cared about in the way someone can care about people when their dream is the only thing that is real to them. The Eclipse is not the moment Griffith changed. It is the moment he stopped pretending he ever could.

## The Dream Before the People

Everything you need to understand Griffith is in the scene where he explains his dream to Princess Charlotte early in the [Golden Age arc](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Golden_Age_Arc). He speaks about it the way religious people speak about God — with absolute certainty, absolute priority, absolute willingness to pay any price. The dream is not "I want to be powerful." The dream is "I want my own kingdom, and I will not accept a version of myself that does not have it." This is the distinction Miura draws with care: it is not ambition in the usual sense, not a desire for wealth or status or recognition. It is a vision of the self that requires the dream to be true. Without the kingdom, Griffith does not exist in any form he recognizes.

[Anime News Network's analysis of Griffith's character](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2017-04-19/berserk-why-griffith-is-the-perfect-villain/.114991) frames him as "the perfect villain" precisely because his ambition is not cartoonishly evil — it emerges from a vision of personal meaning that is, in its own register, coherent and even admirable. He does not want to destroy the world. He wants to build something. The horror is that what he is willing to destroy in order to build it includes the people who loved him.

[Marija Neshkoska's analysis](https://medium.com/@neshkoskam/the-completion-of-a-machiavellian-mind-embracing-traits-from-the-ultimate-manga-antagonist-c52454851160) of Griffith as a "Machiavellian mind" identifies the operative principle: he is capable of genuine attachment, but attachment is always subordinate to the dream. When the two conflict, the dream wins. It has always won. It will always win. The question is just when the conflict finally becomes explicit.

## The Wound Guts Made

Griffith wins. He rises from a mercenary boy to the commander of one of the most celebrated military forces in the kingdom. He is beloved by his people and feared by his enemies. He has done it through genuine skill, genuine charisma, and a complete willingness to use people as instruments when necessary. And he has Guts.

Guts is the problem. Guts is the one person in Griffith's life who functions differently from everyone else — not as an instrument, not as an audience, not as a subordinate who has chosen to be useful to the dream. Guts is a person whose strength Griffith recognizes as equivalent to his own, and this recognition does something to Griffith that he cannot manage: it makes him want something other than the dream. In [Volume 5 of the manga](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Golden_Age_Arc), in the scene that sets the terms for everything that follows, Griffith tells Guts that a true friend would be someone who pursued their own dream, even if it brought them into conflict with Griffith — not someone who followed Griffith's star. A friend who could stand opposite him and still be worth knowing. He is describing the kind of person Guts is. He is also, without knowing it, describing why Guts had to leave — and why Guts leaving would destroy him.

When Guts finally leaves the Band of the Hawk to pursue his own path, Griffith's response is not anger. It is the closest thing to devastation the character is capable of. He duels Guts, loses for the first time, and immediately afterward sleeps with Princess Charlotte — an act of desperate self-destruction that costs him everything. He is captured, tortured for a year in the Tower of Rebirth, and emerges as something broken: the man whose dream was his entire self-conception, now unable to hold a sword.

The prison sequence is Miura being explicit about what Griffith is. Without the dream — without the possibility of the dream — there is nothing there. [The Berserk fandom wiki's analysis of this period](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Griffith#Rescue_and_the_Eclipse) describes him as "a hollow shell of his former self." This is not metaphor. His identity depends entirely on the dream's possibility. Once that possibility seems foreclosed, Griffith does not grieve and rebuild. He ceases.

## The Eclipse Is Not a Choice

What the Egg of the Perfect World shows Griffith in the Invocation of Doom is not a temptation. It is a mirror. It shows him exactly who he is: someone for whom the dream is total, for whom the Band of the Hawk was always conditional, for whom the moment of sacrifice was never a question of whether but of when.

The [TV Tropes YMMV discussion of Berserk](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/YMMV/Berserk) captures the fandom divide over Griffith's agency: some readers believe he was genuinely changed by the torture, that a year of physical destruction in the Tower of Rebirth rewired a person who might otherwise have found another way. This reading has real evidence behind it — the pre-prison Griffith is cautious, calculated, always managing consequences. The post-prison Griffith has nothing to lose. The torture does matter. But what it changed is not who he was. It changed what he could survive losing. Before the Tower, he had the dream's possibility. After, he had nothing — and nothing is what opened the door to Femto. He did not become someone who would sacrifice his friends. He became someone for whom the calculation was finally explicit, the conditional finally tested.

[Guts himself acknowledges this](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Guts-Griffith_relationship) in the aftermath — not in so many words, but in the nature of his fury. His rage at Griffith is not the rage of someone who was lied to. It is the rage of someone who knew, somewhere, what Griffith was and chose to follow him anyway, and who cannot forgive himself for the choice. Guts is not surprised. He is destroyed by the confirmation.

The debate over whether "Griffith did nothing wrong" — popular in certain fandom spaces — is [the wrong frame](https://gamerant.com/berserk-griffith-nothing-wrong/). It implies his actions were justifiable. The more honest reading is that his actions were inevitable given what he was. Justification is beside the point. He was a man whose dream was incompatible with genuine human connection, and who had surrounded himself with people who genuinely loved him, and eventually the incompatibility had to resolve.

## Femto Is Not a Transformation

The mistake is reading Femto as what Griffith became. Femto is what Griffith was, liberated from the constraints of the form that required maintaining the performance of humanity.

The God Hand member who rapes Casca while Guts watches — in what [Berserk volumes 12-13](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Eclipse) render as one of manga's most deliberately unwatchable sequences — this act is not violence for its own sake. It is Griffith, now fully himself, doing to Guts what Guts did to him: taking the thing that mattered most, the relationship that had made him momentarily want something other than his dream, and destroying it in front of him. It is an act of finality. It is Griffith closing the door.

[The feminist analysis of Berserk at Anime Feminist](https://www.animefeminist.com/how-berserks-casca-challenges-the-myth-of-the-strong-black-woman/) focuses on what happens to Casca as a subject — her loss of self, the hundreds of chapters of trauma the manga uses as the foundation for Guts' continuing journey. This critique has weight: Casca is made into an object of Griffith's act rather than an experiencing subject, and her arc is subordinated to Guts' grief. But the act also has a specific target: it is not cruelty for cruelty's sake but the most precise violence imaginable. Griffith, who understood Guts better than anyone, knew exactly what to take.

This precision is Femto. This is the intelligence and understanding that made Griffith so compelling as a leader, turned toward the act of permanent devastation. He did not lose his intelligence or his understanding when he became Femto. He lost the part of himself that had found a reason to hold back.

## Why Miura Refused Moral Closure

What makes Griffith one of manga's most enduring antagonists is not that he is evil. Most manga villains are evil, and evil is easy to dismiss. What makes Griffith impossible to set aside is that his vision was genuinely compelling. [Retrospectives on Miura's work following his 2021 death](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-greatest-villain-griffith/) consistently identify this as the achievement that separates Berserk from its genre peers: Griffith is not a foil for Guts, he is an equal whose failure is a different answer to the same question about what a life should be built around. The kingdom he wanted to build was not a tyrant's fantasized dominion. His leadership of the Band of the Hawk was not a charade. He genuinely inspired people, genuinely led them, and genuinely cared — in the way someone can care when care is always ranked below the dream.

[Kentaro Miura](https://myanimelist.net/people/1780/Kentaro_Miura) never lets the reader off the hook of understanding Griffith. He could have made the pre-Eclipse Griffith a secret monster — shown us the manipulation, the calculation, the contempt for his followers. He does not. He shows us the genuine article: the vision, the charisma, the friendship that was real in its own conditional way. And then he shows us what that vision costs.

This is the horror Berserk is actually constructing beneath its spectacular violence and dark fantasy setting. Not the demons. Not the monsters. Not the grotesque details that have made [the Eclipse one of the most discussed scenes in manga history](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Eclipse). The horror is that someone who was genuinely extraordinary — who could have been one of history's great figures — was always going to do this. The dream was too big. The dream always wins.

Guts survives the Eclipse with the Brand of Sacrifice carved into his neck, pursued by demons for the rest of his life, with the woman he loved broken beyond his reach. He becomes the Black Swordsman, the man with the sword too big to be a sword and the rage too big to be a person. And for all the subsequent volumes of [Berserk](https://anilist.co/manga/30002/Berserk) that trace his journey, the question he is living inside — can a person's life be defined by something other than revenge? — is the mirror image of the question Griffith answered at the Eclipse: can a person's life be defined by something larger than the people they love?

Griffith said yes. He said it with certainty, with finality, with the full weight of everything he had built and everyone who had believed in him.

He was always going to.
