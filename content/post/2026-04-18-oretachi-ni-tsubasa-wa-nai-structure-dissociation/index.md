For the first several hours of *Ore-tachi ni Tsubasa wa Nai*, [the fifth visual novel developed by Navel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navel_(company)), released January 30, 2009 under the tagline "under the innocent sky," you are following three separate young men through what appears to be three separate VN routes. Takashi Haneda is a melancholic high school student with a girlfriend and a younger sister, who daydreams obsessively about a fantasy realm called Gretagard. Shusuke Chitose is a broke twenty-something bar worker with a sharp tongue and an unsteady relationship. Hayato Narita is a hardboiled street-level handyman who seems to be everyone's problem-solver and nobody's friend.

Three protagonists. Three genres. Three different social registers — school romance, working-adult slice of life, rough neighborhood noir. The game switches between them chapter by chapter without explanation. [The anime adaptation by Nomad, which aired in 2011, was explicitly described by reviewers as "avant-garde" in its approach](https://anidb.net/anime/8112) — a word rarely applied to ecchi harem adaptations — because the narrative's deliberate viewer-deception was so unusual for the genre.

If you have read many visual novels, you will spend these hours waiting for the routes to bifurcate — for the moment when you choose which of these men's stories you're following. That moment doesn't come. Eventually, you realize why: [all three are the same person](https://vnerogereview.wordpress.com/2015/06/23/we-have-no-wings-no-hope-and-no-future-review-of-090130oretachi-ni-tsubasa-ha-nai/).

## The Trick Is the Thesis

Dissociative identity disorder is a psychological condition in which a person develops multiple distinct identities as a response to severe trauma. In the game's internal logic, [the protagonist Haneda Youji](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:We_Without_Wings) created four alternate personalities after witnessing [his mother's death as a child — a trauma that sent him into a psychological fugue state](https://vnerogereview.wordpress.com/2015/06/23/we-have-no-wings-no-hope-and-no-future-review-of-090130oretachi-ni-tsubasa-ha-nai/) while his alters stepped in to manage daily life. Each personality — named after major Japanese airports and their associated birds — occupies the body in rotation. Takashi is Hawk (named for Haneda Airport). Shusuke is Eagle (Chitose). Hayato is Falcon (Narita). They refer to whoever is currently controlling the body as being in the "cockpit," [with the others resting in an abstract space they call the Abyss](https://vnerogereview.wordpress.com/2015/06/23/we-have-no-wings-no-hope-and-no-future-review-of-090130oretachi-ni-tsubasa-ha-nai/). When necessary, [they convene in a pocket dimension — imagined as a church — to negotiate who has control](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:We_Without_Wings).

The game's structural decision to conceal this from the reader for hours isn't just a twist. It's an argument. Before you understand what you're reading, you've already demonstrated the problem: three men who are the same person seem completely distinct to an outside observer. You — the reader — cannot tell. You've been spending emotional energy on people who aren't entirely real, building sympathy for fragments of an incomplete whole.

Most VNs give you a single protagonist and ask you to identify with him. *Ore-tachi ni Tsubasa wa Nai* gives you three protagonists and makes identification impossible. Takashi's emotional register is completely different from Shusuke's, which is completely different from Hayato's. [The three personalities are genuinely rendered](https://breadmasterlee.com/2017/04/16/galge-review-oretachi-ni-tsubasa-wa-nai/), not as shading of a single character type but as distinct people with distinct speech patterns, distinct social circles, distinct relationships to their own histories. [The TV Tropes entry on the game](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/VisualNovel/OretachiNiTsubasaWaNai) catalogs the personalities' differences at length, noting that even the heroines who know multiple alters learn to distinguish them by behavioral cues rather than any visible physical difference. And yet they share a face, a body, and a past they collectively don't remember.

The VN medium usually identifies the "you" of the game with the protagonist. Here, the "you" is nobody — or more precisely, it's the original Youji, who has been absent and unconscious this entire time while his alters ran the life he couldn't face.

## Named After Airports That Never Meet

The airport naming scheme is deliberate and worth dwelling on. [Haneda, Chitose, and Narita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_Without_Wings) are Japan's three major domestic and international airports. [All five personality names follow this pattern](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:We_Without_Wings): Youji = Condor, Karura = Garuda (mythological bird), Shusuke = Eagle (Chitose/Sapporo), Hayato = Falcon (Narita), Takashi = Hawk (Haneda). Airports are transit spaces — places you pass through, not places you live. The naming convention tells you something about how these personalities understand themselves: they are not destinations. They are transit points in a journey that hasn't resolved into a location yet.

The [fifth personality, Youji — Condor — has been dormant](https://breadmasterlee.com/2017/04/16/galge-review-oretachi-ni-tsubasa-wa-nai/) since the trauma, deep in the Abyss while the others managed his life in shifts. The condor is the largest flying land bird. It circles at altitude. It survives by watching. That the true self is named for the bird with the greatest wingspan, in a game called "we don't have wings," says everything about what integration would mean: Youji coming back to himself, inheriting the wings the alters have been keeping warm.

![Ore-tachi ni Tsubasa wa Nai VN characters](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a6/We_Without_Wings.jpg)

The "wings" metaphor runs through the entire game as a motif for hope, future, and meaning. [The three protagonists are "wingless"](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:We_Without_Wings) — not because they lack potential, but because a fragmented person cannot fly. The wings belong to the whole self, which has been absent. Each heroine represents a relationship that tethers one personality to the present — to life as it's actually being lived — and the resolution involves recognizing that these relationships are real even for the person who built them from fragments.

## What the Three Routes Actually Share

Because the three narrative strands run through the same city at the same time, careful readers will notice them touching at the edges. Characters from Shusuke's bar vignette appear in Hayato's street stories. Locations from Takashi's school world border Shusuke's working-adult sphere. [The vignettes are constructed to interlock](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:We_Without_Wings), so that what looks like three different slices of Yanagihara City is actually one city experienced through three incomplete perspectives.

This is, again, a formal argument about dissociation. The city is one city. The protagonist is one person. The reader, moving between vignettes, is performing what the alters do: occupying partial perspectives on a world they don't collectively understand. The revelation isn't "these three people are one person" — it's "of course they are. Look at what all three were missing that the others had."

[The galge review at breadmasterlee](https://breadmasterlee.com/2017/04/16/galge-review-oretachi-ni-tsubasa-wa-nai/) clocks the game at over ninety hours — a significant investment, and one the reviewer found padded with repetitive gang subplot material and repetitive comedic beats. The [VNDB community score of 8.57 from 536 votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_Without_Wings) suggests that players who completed it found it genuinely rewarding, even though the anime's [3.95/10 on AniDB](https://anidb.net/anime/8112) and its 49% completion rate indicate that the adaptation failed to convey what the VN structure was doing. This is a fair criticism, and the pacing problem is real. The game's formal intelligence is most visible at its structural hinges — the moments of transition, the Abyss scenes, the revelation — and least visible in the long stretches of genre-conventional material between them. The padding dilutes the psychological impact. You spend so much time with each vignette as a "normal" slice-of-life VN that the structural argument gets buried under genre familiarity.

But the argument is still there. [AniList users who completed the anime have consistently flagged the experience as "confusing initially but rewarding for patient viewers"](https://anilist.co/anime/9922/Oretachi-ni-Tsubasa-wa-Nai-under-the-innocent-sky), which suggests that even the watered-down adaptation communicates something of the original structure's intent. The game's central achievement — using the conventions of the VN route system to simulate what it's like to encounter a person with DID from the outside — remains sharp even when the pacing isn't.

## The Heroine Who Also Had Wings

There is one narrative detail that makes the structural argument most explicit: [Asuka Watarai, Takashi's girlfriend, also had dissociative identity disorder as a child and has recovered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_Without_Wings) — a detail established early in the VN that retroactively frames the entire school vignette as a story about two people navigating the same condition at different stages. [The allthetropes entry on the series](https://allthetropes.org/wiki/We_Without_Wings) notes this explicitly as the "mirror character" dynamic at the heart of Takashi's route. She is the heroine of the route that most directly concerns integration. Her recovery is presented not as a cure but as a process of synthesis — learning to acknowledge the parts of herself that fragmented and allowing them to inform the integrated person she's becoming.

She is, in other words, a mirror character. What she accomplished is what Youji needs to accomplish. The game positions her as a guide and as evidence that integration is possible — that the airport personalities can grow up to be destinations.

The title is a statement of the present condition, not the permanent one. We are without wings *right now*, because we are not yet whole. [Navel's games have consistently explored self-discovery through genre conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navel_(company)) — their breakout hit *Shuffle!* used a harem framework to examine emotional authenticity — but *Oretachi* is the most formally ambitious of their works precisely because it refuses to let you occupy a stable perspective long enough to feel comfortable. The three protagonists — the three airports — are transit points toward a person who hasn't arrived yet but is on his way.
