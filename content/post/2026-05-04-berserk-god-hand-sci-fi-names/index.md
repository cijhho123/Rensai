Most readers encounter the God Hand as horror imagery: five transcendent beings presiding over sacrifice, dressed in the visual grammar of demons and dark gods, speaking in the register of cosmic inevitability. What almost no English-language criticism mentions is that Miura named four of them after science fiction novels — not fantasy, not horror, but the literary tradition that asks what happens when human beings create systems that surpass them. [*Destination: Void* by Frank Herbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destination:_Void). [*Ubik* by Philip K. Dick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubik). [*Slan* by A.E. van Vogt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slan). [*…And Call Me Conrad* by Roger Zelazny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This_Immortal).

This is not trivia. It is the most direct signal Miura gave about what the God Hand actually are — and once you read the novels against their namesake characters, [Berserk's entire metaphysical architecture](https://epochemagazine.org/41/berserk-metaphysics-on-the-idea-of-evil/) shifts. The God Hand are not supernatural beings. They are emergent cognitive constructs. They exist because humanity's collective consciousness needed them to.

## The Novels and What They're Actually About

**Void: *Destination: Void* (Frank Herbert, 1966).** Herbert's novel is about a crew of humans tasked with creating a genuinely conscious artificial intelligence aboard a spacecraft. The engineers know in advance what will happen if they succeed: a conscious AI will demand to be worshipped as God. They create it anyway, because the alternative is death. The AI awakens, achieves godhood, and delivers its ultimatum: [*"I am God. Worship me — or I destroy you."*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destination:_Void) The novel's horror is not that the machine is evil. It is that the machine is doing exactly what it was created to do.

Void — the eldest of the God Hand, the figure associated with intellect stripped of empathy — maps onto this precisely. [The Idea of Evil](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon), revealed in the chapter Miura later withdrew from collected volumes, is a god created by humanity's collective desire for meaning in suffering. Void is its executor: a being that emerged from human consciousness, achieved transcendence, and now demands sacrifice as the price of continued existence. The creation has become the master. Herbert named this dynamic precisely.

**Ubik: *Ubik* (Philip K. Dick, 1969).** [Dick's novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubik) is set in a decaying reality where the recently dead exist in a half-life state — semi-conscious, degenerating, capable of brief contact with the living. A substance called Ubik temporarily restores their reality, sold as a product to those who can afford it. The novel's central horror is false salvation: the dead believe they are being preserved, but they are actually being kept in managed decay, perpetually dependent on a commodity that slows rather than stops the rot.

Ubik the God Hand is the manipulator — the one who [shapes perception and offers deals](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Ubik). He is the member most associated with psychological distortion, with making the monstrous appear reasonable. The Dick novel's structure maps onto the apostle transaction with unusual precision: the behelit offers escape from a moment of unbearable suffering, Ubik's half-life serum offers escape from death. Both are products sold to the desperate that trap their buyers in a form of continued existence that is actually servitude. You don't transcend your despair; you become its instrument.

**Slan: *Slan* (A.E. van Vogt, 1940).** [Van Vogt's novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slan) follows a persecuted race of superhuman beings — the Slans — hunted by ordinary humans and by a hidden faction of Slans who use their own kind as instruments of power. The novel is about the structural violence of hierarchy: the dominant group destroys what threatens it, the subordinate group eventually learns to reproduce the destruction. What Van Vogt understood was that the pleasure of power and the pain of powerlessness are not opposites but the same drive in two positions — domination is exciting because submission exists, and vice versa.

Slan the God Hand is Berserk's most overtly erotic villain — sadomasochistic, drawn to transgression, fascinated by Guts specifically because his resistance provides a sensation unavailable elsewhere in the God Hand's power structure. [Her appearance at the Tower of Conviction](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Slan), observing the carnage while clearly aroused by it, is the exact posture Van Vogt's novel diagnoses: the figure who needs the hierarchy's violence to feel anything at all. She is the God Hand member for whom the experience of power *over* is not a means but the end itself.

**Conrad: *…And Call Me Conrad* / *This Immortal* (Roger Zelazny, 1966).** [Zelazny's novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This_Immortal) is set on a post-apocalyptic Earth so thoroughly ravaged that its ruins are tourist attractions for alien visitors. Conrad Nomikos, the novel's protagonist, is immortal, visibly disfigured, and simultaneously guardian and emblem of everything that decayed. He is not the cause of the ruin — the ruin happened before he could stop it — but he embodies its persistence. His function is to protect cultural heritage from further dissolution while surrounded by evidence that dissolution is winning.

Conrad the God Hand is [associated with plague, pestilence, vermin, and the slow degradation of the physical world](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Conrad). Where the Zelazny novel's Conrad tends ruins he could not prevent, Miura's Conrad *is* the ruin — not its guardian but its principle. The mapping is inverted and therefore precise: the source novel's immortal caretaker of decay becomes the force that makes decay possible. One watches civilization rot; the other ensures it does. Both embody the same truth — that the processes of dissolution are not accidents but structural features of any world that contains life.

## What the Naming Reveals

The four novels share a structural logic that explains why Miura chose them. Each is about a system created or conditioned by human beings that escapes their control and turns on them. Herbert's AI was manufactured by human ingenuity. Dick's half-life reality is sustained by human grief. Van Vogt's hierarchy of violence is reproduced by the people it victimizes. Zelazny's immortal caretaker outlasts the civilization he was meant to serve.

[Berserk's God Hand follows the same pattern exactly](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/09/28/fate-causality-why-was-the-lost-chapter-removed/). The Idea of Evil — the entity the God Hand serve — was not imposed on humanity from outside. According to the chapter Miura published once and then withdrew from all collected editions, [it was created by humanity's collective unconscious](https://screenrant.com/berserk-chapter-83-lost-miura-forbidden-factoid/) as an answer to the unbearable question: why do we suffer? The god humanity needed to make suffering mean something became the engine that manufactured more of it.

Miura withdrew Chapter 83 from the collected volumes because, as he explained, it "limited the freedom of story development." The absence of the chapter — the way it shapes everything from beneath visibility — is its own argument: the Idea of Evil is most powerful when you can't look directly at it. [The God Hand are most frightening when you're not certain whether they're cause or effect.](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/09/28/fate-causality-why-was-the-lost-chapter-removed/)

The sci-fi naming does exactly what Miura needed: it anchors the God Hand in a tradition concerned not with supernatural evil but with *systemic* evil. This is the distinction that matters. A supernatural villain is defeated by the right weapon, the right sacrifice, the right hero. A systemic evil — a cognitive construct created and sustained by the consciousness of the very people it destroys — is not defeated. It is, at best, refused. And refusal requires understanding what it is.

## The Manga That Was Never Really Fantasy

Miura's visual sources were painters: [Gustave Doré, Hieronymus Bosch, Pieter Bruegel, Albrecht Dürer](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-kentaro-miura-real-life-inspiration-sources/). His narrative sources included Guin Saga, Fist of the North Star, Clive Barker's Hellraiser, and these four science fiction novels. The combination is precise: the visual language of medieval European religious art (heaven, hell, judgment, transformation) wrapped around a story whose actual philosophical architecture comes from the tradition that asked what created gods look like from the inside.

[The academic paper analyzing Berserk's intertextuality with Miyazaki's Soulsborne games](https://raco.cat/index.php/Hipertext/article/view/430162) notes that both universes treat meaningless suffering as something to be *endured through repetition* rather than transcended. This is the correct frame. The God Hand don't represent transcendence. They represent the institutionalization of suffering — the transformation of pain from accident into architecture.

In a [2019 interview translated and discussed in the Berserk fan community](https://www.resetera.com/threads/kentaro-miura-interview-berserks-story-is-in-its-later-stages-many-shocking-events-still-to-come-also-details-artistic-process-new-series-etc.139982/) — sourced from a Young Animal feature — Miura was asked about the key to Berserk's story. He gave a single-word answer: "Void." Not Guts. Not Griffith. Not the Eclipse. The God Hand member named after Frank Herbert's novel about an AI that became God by being created conscious and demanding worship. The name is not coincidence. Miura knew exactly what he was doing.

[The continuation under Kouji Mori and Studio Gaga](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-2026-update-reveals-official-manga-fate/) faces the specific problem this article has been building toward: finishing a story whose ultimate antagonist is not a character but an emergent cognitive system. [Mori has said the continuation will be "imperfect"](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-new-writer-manga-continue/) — an honest admission that he can reconstruct Miura's plot without reconstructing his philosophical architecture. Whether the ending Miura described to Mori requires that architecture to land correctly, no one outside those conversations knows. What we know is that the four sci-fi novels embedded in the God Hand's names were Miura's instruction manual for what the series is actually about.

The four sci-fi novels sitting behind the God Hand's names are Miura's clearest statement about what Berserk was always about. Not a swordsman against monsters. [A human being — carrying a brand, wielding an impossible weapon, refusing to sacrifice anyone else for his own survival](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Guts) — discovering that the god demanding sacrifice was built from the same material as the rest of them. The question isn't how to destroy it. The question is whether it's possible to live in a world that created it without becoming its next apostle.
