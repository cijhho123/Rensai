In May 2026, [Whirlpool](https://noisypixel.net/whirlpool-final-visual-novel-relirium-2026/) releases their final visual novel. In July, they hold [a farewell concert at Stellar Ball in Shinagawa](https://x.com/KEVINNGW808/status/2014613219516846453) to mark twenty years as a studio. Pre-orders for the event [exceeded the venue's capacity](https://noisypixel.net/whirlpool-final-visual-novel-relirium-2026/) before it could go fully on sale. A small company known almost entirely to the kemonomimi VN community, folding after two decades with more demand for their goodbye than their going-away party can handle.

It seems like a good moment to talk about what they actually built.

*NEKO-NIN exHeart 2*, released in March 2018 by Whirlpool and localized the same year by [Sekai Project and Denpasoft](https://sekaiproject.com/product/neko-nin-exheart-2/), is the second proper entry in their signature franchise. It isn't the best visual novel Whirlpool made. It probably isn't even the best NEKO-NIN game. But it is a precise and instructive example of what the studio understood that most developers don't: how to make exactly the thing your audience wants and not a single frame more, and how to do that with enough care that the result doesn't feel like a product.

## The Premise Remains the Premise

The first *NEKO-NIN exHeart* established the franchise's mythology with admirable economy: an era when ninja neko formed alliances with humans, with the demi-humans serving as shinobi for powerful patrons. Two catgirls, Yura and Tama, arrive to fulfill an ancient contract with a young man named Haruki — who, as the story notes with deliberate comedy, has absolutely no need for ninjas. The first game offers barely two choices and a single ending. It is, for all practical purposes, a [kinetic novel](https://www.tech-gaming.com/neko-nin-exheart/) about cute catgirls being cute.

This is not a bug. The first game doesn't hide what it is. The [Tech-Gaming review](https://www.tech-gaming.com/neko-nin-exheart/) gave it 75%, noted it appealed squarely to kemonomimi fans, and that "Kemonomimi fans will probably take to it like a spool of yarn." That is exactly the game's audience and exactly the game's pitch. No pretension, no false ambition, no story gesturing toward depth it doesn't have.

*NEKO-NIN exHeart 2* operates from the same foundation, but with expansion. [Ritsu and Maya](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2019-04-21/sekai-project-to-release-whirlpool-neko-nin-exheart-3-visual-novel/.145972) join the cast: Ritsu is a mysterious rival ninja cat who turns up at the village, and Maya is a self-described cat who happens to be a bunny girl — a deliberately absurdist addition that signals the franchise's confidence in its own comedic register. [Steam reviews](https://store.steampowered.com/app/828580/NEKONIN_exHeart_2/) settle at 95% positive from 276 users, a rate that has held consistently since release. The game takes about four and a half hours on first read, contains [23 CGs and seven scenes](https://www.games-t.com/neko-nin-exheart-2-guide), and offers essentially the same structural experience as the first entry, expanded by two characters whose presence makes the ensemble more fun.

The question worth asking is not whether this is good by some universal standard. The question is whether Whirlpool executed their specific intent with craft. They did.

## The Nekomimi Contract

There is an academic case to be made about why the catgirl archetype specifically generates the affective response it does — the combination of human warmth and feline independence, the moe arousal that comes from a character designed to feel both attainable and slightly elusive. [The oldest iterations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catgirl) of the nekomimi concept in Japanese culture trace back to nekomata legends and 18th-century misemono spectacles; the anime design language descended from that through Osamu Tezuka and into the 2000s explosion of kemonomimi characters in manga and games.

The interesting thing about *NEKO-NIN exHeart* is that it doesn't engage with any of this mythology's ambiguity. Yura and Tama are not uncanny or threatening or liminal. They are warm, devoted, energetic, slightly chaotic, and have tails. The series deliberately chooses the comfort end of the nekomimi spectrum — not the archetype's strangeness but its coziness. This is a choice, and it's the right one for what the series is trying to do.

[NEKOPARA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nekopara), the franchise most often cited alongside *NEKO-NIN*, makes similar choices but with a different premise structure: the catgirls in that series live in a domestic arrangement that generates its emotional warmth from the rhythm of everyday life and shared space. NEKO-NIN's catgirls are ninjas who arrive on a mission — the framing is slightly more comic, slightly more action-flavored, and generates its warmth from the comedy of two extraordinarily capable characters deployed in a completely mundane context. Both are valid; they're appealing to overlapping but distinct sensibilities.

What [Whirlpool](https://noisypixel.net/whirlpool-final-visual-novel-relirium-2026/) understood — and what the NEKO-NIN series executes on consistently — is that the nekomimi visual novel audience is not asking for subversion. They know what they want. They want to spend time with characters designed to be appealing, voiced by actors who make those characters feel alive, in a context warm enough that the experience is consistently pleasant. Delivering that reliably is harder than it sounds. Most attempts at this formula produce something that feels factory-assembled. NEKO-NIN doesn't.

## The Craft Underneath the Cute

Voice acting carries the franchise. The [Tech-Gaming review](https://www.tech-gaming.com/neko-nin-exheart/) identifies this precisely: "the whole game is carried by the extremely cute visuals and voiceovers." This is technically a criticism, but it undersells what it describes. Voice direction in moege VNs is frequently mediocre — competent delivery of lines, nothing that generates a specific comedic or emotional register. The NEKO-NIN cast, across both games, is doing more than delivering lines. The timing on the comedy, the warmth in the affection scenes, the specific quality of Tama and Yura's dynamic (which carries from the first game into the second without losing its energy) — these are directorial and performance choices that actively contribute to the experience rather than merely accompanying it.

The writing deserves more credit than it typically receives. The [Drastik Measure review](https://www.thedrastikmeasure.com/2017/08/15/neko-nin-exheart-pc-review/) gave the first game an 8.6 and noted that it "becomes silly, but it is all super fun" — which is exactly right. The humor in NEKO-NIN has a specific flavor: the catgirls' dedication to an ancient contract that is completely out of proportion to any actual need, generating scenes that are aware of their own absurdity and committed to it anyway. This is harder to write than earnest drama. Earnest drama can hide behind sincerity. Comedy has no cover — if the timing is off, nothing works. The timing in NEKO-NIN is almost always right.

The sequel earns its position in the franchise by understanding what made the original work and adding to it rather than substituting for it. Ritsu and Maya don't replace Yura and Tama's dynamic; they interact with it. Maya's entire characterization is built on a single absurdist premise — she is a bunny girl who insists, with complete sincerity, that she is a cat — and the comedy works because the game never blinks on it. She isn't confused; she is committed. That commitment, played with a straight face while everyone else in the room responds normally, is exactly the comedic timing at which NEKO-NIN excels. The [community verdict](https://steamcommunity.com/app/828580/reviews/?browsefilter=toprated) on the sequel is consistent: "a very good follow up to the first game with nice art, great lewd scenes, and cute and fun new characters." That's not a weak review — that's a reader who got what they came for and was satisfied.

## A Studio's Closing Argument

Whirlpool built twenty years on this. The [NEKO-NIN franchise](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2019-04-21/sekai-project-to-release-whirlpool-neko-nin-exheart-3-visual-novel/.145972) running from 2016 through [SPIN! in 2024](https://fuwanovel.moe/2024/07/neko-nin-exheart-spin-has-released-today/); the [Slobbish Dragon Princess series](https://nichegamer.com/slobbish-dragon-princess-confirmed-for-western-release-2020/) that ran to three entries; a catalog of kemonomimi games that served their audience with genuine care. Their final game, [Relirium](https://noisypixel.net/whirlpool-final-visual-novel-relirium-2026/), launches in May 2026. The farewell concert in July. A live event selling beyond capacity for a studio most people outside the niche have never heard of — which is exactly the kind of loyalty you build by spending two decades doing exactly what you promised.

There's a version of this conversation where NEKO-NIN exHeart 2 gets dismissed as shallow fan service for a niche audience. That version misunderstands what "niche" means and what "shallow" costs. Making something deliberately targeted, consistently pleasant, and genuinely enjoyable for the audience it's designed for is not a lesser achievement than making something that aspires to universality. Universality is usually code for "appealing to as many people as possible," which frequently means serving no one in particular especially well.

Whirlpool served their people especially well. The sold-out farewell concert is the evidence.

*NEKO-NIN exHeart 2* is four and a half hours of warm, well-executed catgirl comedy that knows exactly what it is. In 2026, as the studio that made it prepares to close, that turns out to have been enough to last twenty years.
