There is a moment in *The End of Evangelion* when billions of people dissolve into orange liquid and it feels, somehow, like relief. Not horror. Not tragedy. Something close to the comfort you'd feel if you'd been carrying something very heavy for a very long time and someone told you that you could put it down now. That feeling is not accidental. It is engineered. And the engineer is a song.

!["The End of Evangelion" theatrical poster](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/11/End_of_Eva_poster.jpg)

## Anno Wanted "Hey Jude"

According to [EvaWiki's documented production notes](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Komm,_s%C3%BCsser_Tod), Hideaki Anno's original intent for the Third Impact sequence was to score it with The Beatles' "Hey Jude." The rights fell through — copyright costs and tonal fit were both cited as factors — and so he commissioned an equivalent. What he wanted, and what the substitute had to accomplish, is worth sitting with, because it explains everything about why "Komm, Süsser Tod" sounds the way it does.

"Hey Jude" is not primarily a love song. It's a song about dissolving individual pain into collective joy. The verse is intimate, one-to-one, addressed to a specific person in their grief. The na-na-na outro is the opposite: no words, no individuality, just a mass of voices that sounds like one voice. Paul McCartney and 36 other people recorded that fade-out together. The song structurally enacts a movement from the isolated self into the communal whole — and it asks the listener to join. You cannot hear "Hey Jude" without wanting to sing along, without feeling your separate self dissolve a little into the shared sound.

This is exactly what Instrumentality does to the cast of Evangelion. This is what Anno wanted to score it with.

## What He Built Instead

Shiro Sagisu constructed "Komm, Süsser Tod" on a bed of structural inevitability. Its harmonic foundation borrows from [Pachelbel's Canon in D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) — one of the most "already-known" pieces of music in the Western harmonic tradition, a chord progression so deeply embedded in listeners' ears that each resolution feels like arriving somewhere you were always going. Layer on top of that a melody with clear debts to "A Whiter Shade of Pale" (which itself samples Bach), and build toward a call-and-response outro that mirrors the "Hey Jude" structure beat for beat. The result is a song that sounds ancient and familiar the first time you hear it. Its comfort feels pre-installed.

Arianne, the South African-born British teenager who performed it, was introduced to Shiro Sagisu by songwriter Rob Davis. She recorded the vocals without being familiar with the series. She didn't know what the song was for. You can hear that in the performance — there is no ironic distance, no awareness of the weight she's carrying. The voice is pure.

The person who *did* know what the song was for was Hideaki Anno, who wrote the original Japanese lyrics himself under the title "Amaki shi yo, kitare" — "Come, sweet death." The English adaptation by Mike Wyzgowski includes lines like: *"I feel the best thing I could do is end it all and leave forever / What's done is done, it feels so bad."* These are suicide lyrics. Sung over the sound of Pachelbel's Canon. With a call-and-response outro designed to make you sing along.

## The Dissonance Is the Point

Anno's use of tonal dissonance isn't unique to this moment. It's woven into Evangelion's DNA from the start: episodes that end on psychological rupture segue immediately into the upbeat swing of "Fly Me to the Moon." The technique has been compared to [Stanley Kubrick's use of classical and pop music against atrocity](https://www.japantimes.co.jp/culture/2014/10/18/films/hideaki-anno-emotional-deconstructionist/), and the comparison holds. When "Singin' in the Rain" plays over a rape scene in *A Clockwork Orange*, the effect is destabilization — the familiar becomes threatening. Anno's version of this cuts differently. He uses cheerful music not to implicate you in horror but to make horror feel like home.

The effect during the Third Impact sequence is specific and devastating. As Instrumentality expands, as AT-Fields collapse and souls begin to merge, the music wraps itself around you in the way only deeply familiar music can. You find yourself almost anticipating the chord progressions. The call-and-response outro arrives and your body knows where it's going before your mind does. You are, in a measurable neurological sense, dissolving your individual attention into the pattern of the song. The music is doing to you, in miniature, what the Anti-AT-Field is doing to every human being on screen.

## You Are Invited to Want This

This is the film's central perversity and its central achievement: Anno built a sequence that makes the audience genuinely *feel* the appeal of Instrumentality. Not understand it intellectually. Feel it. The option Rei offers Shinji — to give up the pain of individual selfhood, to let the boundary between yourself and everything else go soft and dissolve — is not a horror in this moment. It is a relief. And the music is why.

The lyrics say: I want to die. The melody says: you want this too, you've always wanted this, sing along. And because the song is built on Pachelbel's Canon and Baroque harmonic inevitability, there's a quality of *rightness* to it, a sense that this was always where the progression was heading.

That Anno wrote these lyrics himself is significant. He has spoken openly about his depression, his sense of fundamental aloneness, his investment in Evangelion as a way of working through what he described as years of not wanting to engage with the world. "Komm, Süsser Tod" is not an abstract artistic choice. It is Anno giving the audience his death drive in musical form and asking whether it feels familiar.

## The Song as Thesis

Most film scores *describe* what is happening. They confirm the emotional content of a scene, amplify it, make sure you're feeling the right feeling at the right time. "Komm, Süsser Tod" doesn't do any of that. It *reproduces* the experience of its scene on the audience. By the time Instrumentality is complete on screen, you have just spent several minutes having your individual critical attention softened by a piece of music specifically engineered to be known before you know it, built to make you want to sing along and therefore to briefly be something communal rather than individual.

Anno couldn't get "Hey Jude." What he built in its place does something "Hey Jude" could never have done, because "Hey Jude" already carries too much meaning, too much specific cultural history. "Komm, Süsser Tod" arrives familiar but unspecific — close enough to every song you already know that it feels like memory, not music.

That's the trap. And it's perfect.
