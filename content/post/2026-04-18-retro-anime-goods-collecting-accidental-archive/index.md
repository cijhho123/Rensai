In 2021, [a single animation cel from *My Neighbor Totoro* sold at auction for $238,256](https://kotaku.com/anime-cels-are-starting-to-get-more-valuable-than-ever-1847242159) — the highest price ever paid for an anime cel, by a wide margin at that point. The same frame of Totoro, in 1988, would have been discarded. Not as a difficult disposal problem, not as something to be stored in a warehouse — discarded as literal industrial waste, along with the rest of the production.

That gap is the entire story of retro anime goods collecting.

The hobby is built on objects that the industry that created them did not consider worth keeping. Animation cels were [often buried, burned, or sold off by the pound when studios cleared storage](https://www.kotaku.com/anime-cels-are-starting-to-get-more-valuable-than-ever-1847242159). Shitajiki — plastic pencil boards printed with anime characters, given out as school supplies — were produced in single print runs and treated as functional stationery. Bromide photo cards were sold in convenience stores and arcades as disposable entertainment. None of it was made to last. The rarity that now commands premium prices at Mandarake and Yahoo! Japan Auctions is entirely accidental.

## What Survived and Why

The types of objects that constitute retro anime goods were not created equal in terms of survival probability.

Animation cels occupy the extreme end of precarity. [Each cel is a unique, hand-painted object](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/the-mike-toole-show/2011-03-13) — the actual transparent layer from which a frame was photographed, painted by studio employees who were often paid poverty wages. Studios had material reasons to dispose of them: [the original nitrate celluloid used in early animation was genuinely flammable and chemically unstable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cel), a fire hazard that studios were legally required to manage. Even after the industry-wide shift to cellulose acetate, the sheer volume of cels accumulated per production (an average TV episode might generate thousands of individual frames) made storage impractical. [One studio was forced to bury them in its backyard because disposal was so difficult](https://kotaku.com/anime-cels-are-starting-to-get-more-valuable-than-ever-1847242159).

What saved most surviving cels was informal redistribution. [As ANN's Cellector authentication guide and collector accounts document](https://www.cellector.net/authenticity), cels reached the secondary market in multiple ways: animators taking home stacks they'd been assigned (with or without authorization), fans who waited at studio back entrances on trash collection days, the occasional studio that sold batches to dealers rather than dealing with disposal costs. [Mike Toole's 2011 Anime News Network column on cels](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/the-mike-toole-show/2011-03-13) documented this economy of salvage — a secondary market built entirely on material the industry hadn't planned to release. [Studios like Madhouse reportedly sold vaulted cels to stay financially afloat](https://www.cellector.net/authenticity) in lean periods; others simply let employees take what they wanted when clearing storage.

Shitajiki and bromides survived through different means: distributed in enormous quantities, stored in drawers and closets by fans who never used them, passed down or resold at second-hand shops. The [Wikipedia entry on pencil boards notes that most designs only go through one print run](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pencil_board), making pristine examples scarce today. A Sailor Moon shitajiki that sat unused in a drawer for 30 years is now the collector's ideal — [old school otaku forums like The Old School Otaku Lounge exist specifically to catalog and trade these pieces](https://www.oldschoolotaku.com/forum/viewtopic.php?t=597).

## The Market That Globalization Made

Until the mid-2010s, the retro anime goods market was essentially Japan-local. [Mandarake's Nakano Broadway location](https://www.mandarake.co.jp) was the gravitational center — a multi-story complex of stalls specializing in different eras, franchises, and object types, accessible only to those who could travel to Tokyo and navigate the space. Yahoo! Japan Auctions existed but required proxy services to access from abroad.

The combination of improved proxy services, Twitter's emergence as a collecting community platform, and the global mainstreaming of anime through Netflix, Crunchyroll, and other platforms changed this. Western fans who grew up with 90s anime gained purchasing power in their 30s and 40s and discovered, simultaneously, that the merchandise from their childhood existed, was obtainable, and was rising in price.

For global fans who grew up with 80s and 90s anime, original merchandise functions as a claim on a specific cultural moment — an assertion that you were there, before it became mainstream. This impulse accelerates with every reboot or anniversary. The 2022 *Urusei Yatsura* anime revival brought a surge of searches for vintage Lum figures and original manga goods on [Mandarake's auction platform](https://ekizo.mandarake.co.jp/auction/item/indexEn.html), a pattern that repeats each time a classic franchise returns: new fans discover the vintage market, and existing collectors watch their inventories appreciate in real time.

Every reboot functions as a discovery engine — it's not just new viewers watching the show, it's new buyers entering a market that was previously invisible to them. [Overseas anime merchandise revenue reached ¥156 billion (~$1.1 billion USD) in 2023 according to the Association of Japanese Animations](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/beyond-the-shelf-why-vintage-anime-merch-is-more-than-just-a-throwback/66c3c97e556d9a16ab2cea046c1b4616), with international demand as the primary growth driver. The goods were always there. The audience for them was just discovering they had access.

## The Difference That Scarcity Makes

The retro anime goods market differs meaningfully from the market for new figure releases. Modern limited-edition figures from Alter, Good Smile Company, or Kotobukiya are artificially scarce — produced in defined runs, sold through specific retail channels, with prices that reflect manufacturing costs plus speculative premium.

Vintage goods have a different kind of scarcity. A 1987-era *Dragon Ball* sofubi vinyl figure or a boxed set of original *Sailor Moon S* trading cards isn't scarce because someone decided to limit it — it's scarce because time destroyed most of the production. As [the DHL guide to Japan's secondhand market notes](https://www.dhl.com/discover/en-jp/logistics-advice/import-export-advice/guide-to-japans-secondhand-market), Japan's vintage goods market commands premiums driven by condition, authenticity, and simply the difficulty of finding undamaged examples after 30+ years. The condition premium is extreme: shitajiki that were used as actual pencil boards, carrying the marks of schoolchildren, trade for a fraction of pristine examples. [The Centaku Media shitajiki guide](https://centakumedia.com/interesting-find-the-mini-guide-for-new-shitajiki-collectors/) notes that collectors specifically seek boards that were "never used and barely even handled" — items that defeated the object's entire original function.

There's something philosophically interesting here. The most valuable vintage anime objects are the ones that survived without being consumed — the cel that didn't get photographed into mud by repeated projection passes, the shitajiki that was never slipped under a homework page, the bromide that stayed in its original packaging at the back of a drawer. Existence as artifact rather than object is what creates the value.

## What It Means for Preservation

The retro anime goods market has a preservation dimension that the industry is only beginning to reckon with. [The Kotaku piece on cel values](https://kotaku.com/anime-cels-are-starting-to-get-more-valuable-than-ever-1847242159) quoted writer and Japan expert Matt Alt noting that rising prices are pulling cels out of obscurity — "it's an ongoing story" — but the infrastructure for proper conservation lags behind. [Cel Oasis and similar specialist restorers](https://celoasis.com/index.php/home/conservation/) provide archival guidance, but the bulk of the surviving material is in private hands, stored with varying degrees of care.

The same applies to printed goods: shitajiki, bromides, and similar items are subject to the standard degradation challenges of plastic and paper. A generation of collectors who are reaching middle age is sitting on archives of material that, if not properly stored, will be irreplaceable losses.

There is an argument that this should be institutional — that the same studios whose cels now sell for hundreds of thousands of dollars should have established archives. [Automaton West documented the NGE cel phenomenon](https://automaton-media.com/en/news/obsolete-part-of-anime-history-brought-to-light-through-unusual-detail-on-neon-genesis-evangelions-painstakingly-hand-crafted-cels/) and noted that even for major productions like Evangelion, surviving cels are scattered across private collectors worldwide rather than housed in any systematic archive. The original creation was an industrial process; the preservation is happening entirely through the market.

The irony is that collectors are doing more for anime history than the anime industry ever did. The objects survived because fans valued them before anyone else did. Now that the rest of the world has caught up, the collectors who held onto that shitajiki from 1987 are the custodians of a record that nobody thought to keep.

The studios threw it all away. The fans didn't.
