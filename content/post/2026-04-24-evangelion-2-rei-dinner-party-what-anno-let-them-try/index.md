The dinner party scene in *Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance* exists because Hideaki Anno remembered it. He had planned something like it for the fourth episode of *Neon Genesis Evangelion* in 1995 — Rei reaching out to the people around her, trying to host a meal, trying to be something more than a tool — and shelved it because it didn't fit the story he was telling. Fourteen years later, he put it back in. That choice is the most honest thing *Evangelion 2.0* does, and it tells you more about what the Rebuild project actually is than any amount of spectacle about Near Third Impact.

## What the Original Could Not Afford

The Rei Ayanami of *Neon Genesis Evangelion* was not a character who could want things. This wasn't a failure of writing — it was the point. Anno designed her as a vessel, a projection surface, a blank-slate figure onto whom Shinji (and the audience) could imprint whatever they needed. She was the first moe archetype before the term had fully solidified, [according to the Wikipedia entry on the character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rei_Ayanami), and her influence on the genre is immeasurable precisely because she functioned as a mirror rather than a person. The ambiguity was structural. Give her desires, give her specific wants, and she stops being a mirror. She becomes an individual with a trajectory. NGE needed her to be the former.

More than that: the NGE that aired in 1995-96 was built around the principle that these children should not survive what was being done to them. [Anno has spoken at length](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-06-19/hideaki-anno-after-evangelion/.148019) about the depression he was working through during the original series — the NHK documentary on the Rebuild project makes the same portrait visible from the outside. The series does not believe its characters will be okay. It does not offer the kinds of small human moments — cooking, planning, reaching out — that would imply a future worth having. To give Rei a dinner party in episode 4 would have been to suggest she had a future, which was not the story being told.

So Anno shelved it. [Production records from the Complete Records Collection](https://www.gwern.net/doc/anime/eva/2010-crc) confirm that the episode 4 concept was remembered specifically for inclusion in 2.0 — [Anno described it](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/7294/Dinner/) as "something like Shinji's birthday party" that he replaced with a dinner party this time. The small distinction matters: a birthday party someone else organizes for Shinji. A dinner party that Rei organizes herself.

## What 2.0 Gave Her

The dinner party subplot in *Evangelion 2.0* is not a side element. [It is, structurally, the emotional core of the entire middle section of the film](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/7294/Dinner/) — the production documents describe Shinji's story in Part C as "nothing but looking forward to Rei's dinner party." Rei's story is built around the effort of preparing it: she is shown with bandaged fingers from knife cuts, practicing cooking, writing a letter by hand. [According to the CRC production materials](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Rei_Ayanami/Rebuild), the handwriting on Rei's letter was Anno's own. He asked Gendo for permission to hold the meal for her. She invites the other pilots. She tries.

What she's trying to do is build a family. Not perform one, not obey one — build one. She wants Shinji and Gendo in the same room, sitting down together, because she has been watching their estrangement and decided, on her own initiative, that it should be different. That decision is not coded into her programming. It comes from somewhere inside a character who, in the original series, had nothing inside her that wasn't implanted there by Yui Ikari or SEELE.

This is not a softer Rei. It's a more dangerous one. Because the dinner never happens. The Unit-03 incident intervenes, Unit-01 goes berserk under the Dummy System, and by the time the film reaches its climax, Rei has been absorbed into the core of the Tenth Angel and then into Unit-01 itself. The dinner party becomes the measure of everything she lost. [ComicsAlliance's review](https://comicsalliance.com/evangelion-2-0-review/) noted that 2.0 presents an Eva that brings "people closer to each other" — but the film withholds that closeness the moment you believe in it. That's the structural cruelty. In the original series, Rei couldn't lose the dinner party because she never had it. In 2.0, she planned it, and you watched her plan it, and then she was taken.

## The Parallel with Asuka

What Anno did with Rei in 2.0 is mirrored in what he did with Asuka. [Asuka Soryu and Asuka Shikinami are different characters at the level of backstory and trajectory](https://www.cbr.com/asuka-langley-sohryu-shikinami-similarities-differences/), and the difference is deliberate. Soryu's defining characteristic was her inability to accept help — a psychological fortress built around the trauma of her mother's death, reinforced by her need to be the best so that someone would want her. The original series uses this character to demonstrate what prolonged coercion does to a person who has no resources to process it. [By the time Soryu reaches the Unit-03 activation sequence](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Asuka_Langley_Soryu), she is already destroyed. The series set her up to be destroyed.

Shikinami is not set up to be destroyed. [She is, per the EvaWiki entry on her character](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Asuka_Shikinami_Langley), the last of a series of clones — she has no parents, has never had them, but has also not been shaped by one specific formative catastrophe the way Soryu was. She is more open. She confides in Misato near the end of the film — a brief conversation where she talks genuinely about her feelings — where Soryu in the equivalent moment of the original series was dissociating inside Unit-01, reduced to "[I'm the best, I'm the best, I'm the best]" as her sense of self dissolved. The contrast is precise: one version of Asuka reaches out before the loss; the other was already gone.

[Fan discourse around this change has been divided](https://www.tumblr.com/ritsushinbro/659222675812663296/my-thoughts-on-asuka-langley-shikinami-in-the) — some viewers find Shikinami less interesting precisely because she lacks Soryu's devastating psychological architecture. That's a fair observation. But it misses the point of the change. [Shikinami is interesting for a different reason](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Asuka_Shikinami_Langley): she's the version of Asuka that isn't structurally required to be broken. And she does survive — eventually, in *3.0+1.0*, she gets out. Soryu could not have gotten out. The narrative didn't have that ending available.

## What Anno Changed, and Why

[Anno has said the Rebuild films were intended to be "oriented towards entertainment"](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=21201) rather than metaphysical, and that he himself had changed since the original series. [The NHK documentary on the final film's production](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2021-03-25/nhk-documentary-captures-hideaki-anno-creative-struggles-on-final-evangelion-film/.171027) shows a man still exhausted, still embattled by the work, but oriented differently — not toward illustrating despair but toward finding a way out of it. This is usually interpreted as a tonal shift: more action, more spectacle, more crowd-pleasing structure. That's accurate but incomplete.

What changed most fundamentally is whether Anno believed these characters could be given a chance before the tragedy arrived. In NGE, the answer was no. There was no dinner party because there was no point in showing what could have been. In 2.0, [there's a version of the story where Rei succeeds](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2021/08/27/evangelion-2-0-you-can-not-advance-a-branching-point/) — where the dinner happens, where Gendo and Shinji sit down, where the family is repaired for one evening. The film doesn't deliver that version. But it shows you the attempt. It makes you believe it's possible before taking it away.

That is a different kind of grief than NGE's. NGE's grief was the grief of a system that never offered anything else. 2.0's grief is the grief of something specific that almost was. [The dinner party was the most human scene Anno could imagine for this character in 1995](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-06-19/hideaki-anno-after-evangelion/.148019), and he couldn't include it because his story wasn't about human things. By 2009, he had married [Moyoco Anno in 2002](https://aftermath.site/hideaki-anno-is-animes-ultimate-wife-guy/) and emerged from the decade between NGE and Rebuild as a different person — one for whom the question of whether a character could want a dinner party had become answerable in the affirmative.

He wrote Rei's letter in his own handwriting. He remembered the scene he couldn't use the first time and decided that this time, Rei was allowed to try.

She still doesn't get to have the dinner. But she cooked for it. And Anno let her.
