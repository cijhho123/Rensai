The premise of Kaguya-sama: Love is War is that two extremely intelligent people are in love with each other and refuse to say so. The show frames this as comedy, and it is — watching Kaguya and Shirogane construct elaborate psychological traps to force the other into confessing first is genuinely funny, particularly because the show's narrator delivers every internal monologue with total deadpan sincerity, as if documenting a battle of historic consequence rather than two teenagers being scared of their feelings.

But then there's Ishigami Yu.

Ishigami doesn't play the game. At a certain point, he tells Tsubame Koyasu that he loves her. Not through a scheme, not as part of some strategic maneuver designed to make her confess first. He says it directly, and then he loses. She doesn't love him back. He cries on the stairs going home. The show, which has built three seasons of comedy on two protagonists who won't say three words, uses its most earnest supporting character to demonstrate what happens when someone actually says them.

This is the most important thing Kaguya-sama does, and the one most easily eclipsed by the main romance's more immediately satisfying arc.

## The Character Who Carries Real Shame

Ishigami enters the series as [a problem](https://kaguyasama-wa-kokurasetai.fandom.com/wiki/Yu_Ishigami) — specifically, as a student treasurer with terrible social skills, a gloomy affect, and a reputation that follows him like weather. His middle school classmates ostracized him. He carries this into Shuchiin Academy and into the student council, where he starts as an uncomfortable outlier in a room full of high-achieving overachievers.

The backstory that explains his reputation is deliberately withheld. What the audience sees first is the effect: Ishigami is the kind of person other students at a school for the elite quietly avoid. He is not rich in the way Kaguya is rich; he is not a prodigy in the way Shirogane performs genius. He is [genuinely kind and genuinely isolated](https://www.cbr.com/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-ishigami-unsung-hero/), and the show presents both as facts about the same person.

The truth of what happened in middle school eventually emerges in fragments. Ishigami discovered that a classmate named Ko Ogino was cheating on Kyoko Ootomo with a second girlfriend. He confronted Ogino. Ogino retaliated by [framing Ishigami as a stalker jealous of his relationship with Kyoko](https://kaguyasama-wa-kokurasetai.fandom.com/wiki/Kyoko_Ootomo), and the entire class believed Ogino's version — including Kyoko herself. Ishigami was suspended. The truth was only uncovered later by the student council, and to protect Kyoko from knowing what Ogino had done to her, the full story was kept quiet. Ishigami accepted this.

This is the piece of information that reorganizes everything else about the character: he chose public humiliation, ostracism, and indefinite suspension to protect the privacy of a girl who called him "crazy" to his face. He got nothing from it. The game-playing of the main romance — the elaborate schemes, the strategic retreats — exists in a different moral universe from this. [TV Tropes notes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/KaguyaSamaLoveIsWarYuIshigami) that Ishigami "conceals his good deeds and shows his worst side to others," which makes him the inverse of every other character in the show: where Kaguya and Shirogane perform intelligence and competence to mask vulnerability, Ishigami performs incompetence and gruffness to mask virtue.

## The Shape of His Romantic Arc

When Ishigami develops feelings for Tsubame Koyasu, the show structures it recognizably: he notices her, he decides she's out of his league, he starts trying to be someone she could love. He joins the cheer squad because she's in it. He works to rebuild his reputation. He works on himself, sincerely, in the way that the main romance is always performing the appearance of working on oneself without ever quite committing to the actual vulnerability.

[Kaguya-sama's supporting character arcs have always been richer than they get credit for](https://bookwormsandbluestockings.wordpress.com/2022/10/18/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-but-i-like-the-supporting-character-arcs-more/), and the Ishigami–Tsubame arc is the example that proves the thesis. Tsubame genuinely came to care about Ishigami during the cheer squad arc — not as a romantic partner, but as someone she admired and wanted to support. She'd even worked behind the scenes to help rehabilitate his reputation, spreading counter-rumors about the Ootomo incident. None of this was what Ishigami thought it was.

When [he confesses formally](https://kaguyasama-wa-kokurasetai.fandom.com/wiki/Tsubame_Koyasu/Relationships), Tsubame doesn't reject him immediately. The delay, as the wiki notes, was not cruelty but its own form of conflict: she didn't want to hurt him, and she'd been avoiding the truth rather than face it. This is a small detail that makes the arc more honest than it could have been. The eventual rejection — delivered at a Christmas party, in circumstances that the show makes quietly devastating — tells Ishigami that she's leaving for a distant university and doesn't think they could sustain anything across that distance. He breaks down on the stairs afterward, alone, and Miko Iino — who had rushed to help him and broken her hand catching him — sits with the aftermath.

[Game Rant identified](https://gamerant.com/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-why-yu-ishigami-is-best-boy/) Ishigami as a character whose "kindness and consideration" operate largely invisibly. What that observation doesn't quite reach is why the Tsubame arc matters beyond character development. The rejection is not a sign of his failure. It's the show making explicit something it usually keeps in the background: being sincere, being genuinely vulnerable, and putting down the armor is not a guarantee. The armor exists for a reason.

## What This Reveals About the Main Romance

Kaguya and Shirogane's games are funny because the audience knows they love each other, and watching two brilliant people perform elaborate indifference to their own feelings is a recognizable kind of absurdity. But Ishigami's arc makes explicit what the comedy requires you to not quite name: the reason the games exist is that declaring yourself is dangerous.

The narrator's deadpan voiceover — [what Jonah's Books calls "the spice that makes Kaguya-sama tick"](https://jonahwrites.blog/2022/05/12/the-narrator-the-spice-that-makes-kaguya-sama-tick/) — treats every internal strategizing session as a battle of enormous consequence. This is comedy because we read it as overthinking. But the story is quietly defending the overthinking. Ishigami says what he means, directly, and it costs him. The games that Kaguya and Shirogane play are not just pride — they are a rational response to the evidence that honesty creates exposure that isn't always rewarded.

Ai Hayasaka, who functions as [Kaguya's foil and the show's sole consistent voice of reason](https://jonahwrites.blog/2020/09/24/hayasaka-ai-two-sides-of-the-coin/), understands this better than anyone. She watches Kaguya's elaborately constructed emotional defenses and recognizes them as exactly that — defenses, built against something real that could actually hurt her. Hayasaka is, as Mechanical Anime Reviews noted, [a side character whose small screen time has outsized impact](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2019/04/24/ai-hayasaka-small-screen-time-big-impact/): she spent her entire adolescence in service to the Shinomiya family, watching Kaguya perform invulnerability without once being allowed the same protection for herself. Her perspective is the most honest in the show because she's the one who has to stay in the room after Kaguya's games fail and help her understand what she actually feels. In this way, Hayasaka and Ishigami occupy the same structural position: both are people who already know what it costs to be genuinely invested in someone else's feelings, and both watch the main characters avoid that knowledge for as long as they possibly can.

## The Aftermath and What It Points To

The trajectory after the Tsubame rejection matters. Ishigami doesn't collapse. He doesn't give up on human connection. What changes is that Miko Iino — who has been his foil all series, the rigid rule-enforcer to his deliberate casualness — starts to become something different to him. The relationship that eventually forms is earned differently from the main romance. It's not a game. It's two people who have both been broken by the year, sitting with that, and finding that the other person is still there.

When [ANN's reviewer noted](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=24889) that Season 3 earned the title "Ultra Romantic" by finally delivering on years of emotional setup, the praise was directed primarily at the Kaguya–Shirogane confession arc. That arc is narratively satisfying. But the reason Season 3 carries emotional weight is also Ishigami: he got rejected, he cried, and he got back up. The show demonstrated that you can be Kaguya Shinomiya — brilliant, strategic, armed — and that all of this is also, at some level, because you are afraid of what Ishigami already went through. And he survived it.

The manga's later arcs received [mixed reception](https://www.cbr.com/kaguya-sama-anime-story-too-long-debate/) for pacing and genre drift — the comedy receding as the drama expanded — and [the planned adaptation has faced scrutiny over reportedly skipping major manga arcs](https://screenrant.com/anime-rom-com-kaguya-sama-love-is-war-new-anime-manga-chapters-skip-theory/). But the Ishigami thread is the one that gives the genre shift emotional credibility. By the time the series moves away from the comedic battle format that defined its early identity, the audience has watched someone within the story do the thing the format is designed to delay — and watched it not go perfectly. The Love is War framing is funny because it's also true: love is a risk. Ishigami took it, fully armored, and still got hurt.

The show doesn't moralize about this. It doesn't suggest that Kaguya and Shirogane should stop playing their game. It just makes sure you've seen what they're avoiding.
