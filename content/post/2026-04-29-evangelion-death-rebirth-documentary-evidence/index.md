Most watch guides for Neon Genesis Evangelion tell you to skip [Death & Rebirth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion:_Death_%26_Rebirth). Watch the TV series, go directly to [The End of Evangelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion), treat the 1997 film as a historical footnote for completionists. The advice is cinematically correct. Death & Rebirth is a clip show followed by an unfinished first act. As cinema, it's a rough draft of a building that was later demolished and rebuilt better.

But that framing misses what the film actually is and why it matters. Death & Rebirth is not really a film in the conventional sense. It's a document — the exact trace left by a production in catastrophic crisis, a creator being destroyed by the work that made him famous, and the moment when the relationship between Hideaki Anno and his audience broke down completely and publicly. Understanding what End of Evangelion is actually doing requires understanding this document. And you can't understand it without sitting with the specific wreckage it records.

## What the Film Actually Contains

[Death & Rebirth](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Evangelion:_Death_and_Rebirth) splits into two formally distinct pieces. The first, Evangelion: Death, is a sixty-minute compilation of the TV series — not a chronological recap, but a thematically organized reordering of footage from episodes 1-24, incorporating approximately twenty minutes of new animation originally created for the Director's Cut home video releases. The editing is genuinely unconventional: scenes from different points in the series are placed against each other for thematic resonance rather than narrative sequence, creating something that reads more like an essay on the series than a summary of it.

The second piece, Evangelion: Rebirth, is where the historical significance lives. Twenty-seven minutes of entirely new animation, intended as the opening third of the alternate theatrical ending — the events the TV series' final episodes had abandoned in favor of the psychological inward turn. The JSSDF invasion of Nerv. Rei and Asuka's fates. The beginning of the Human Instrumentality Project as external spectacle rather than interior experience. It ends abruptly, mid-action, with the arrival of the mass-production Evangelion units. That's it. The film just stops.

The reason it stops is that [Gainax ran out of time and money](https://www.evamonkey.com/writings/mdwigs-evangelion-movies.php). What was supposed to be a complete new ending was simply not finished when the theatrical release date arrived. So they released the fragment as a preview — "only the beginning of the new ending," as Gainax announced — with a commitment to deliver the complete version later. [The End of Evangelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion) arrived four months later as that completion.

## What Was Happening to Anno

The production context for Death & Rebirth is documented well enough that it doesn't require reconstruction. [Anno had been in clinical depression](https://opus.ing/posts/how-hideaki-anno-depression-influenced-evangelion) since before Evangelion aired. The TV series' final episodes — episodes 25 and 26 — were the product of that depression: introspective, anti-spectacular, resolving the story's external plot entirely within Shinji's psychology and leaving the physical world of the series unresolved. They were also, as Anno later acknowledged, partly a response to the practical reality that Gainax had run out of the budget needed to produce action sequences.

The fan response was ferocious. [Gainax's offices were vandalized](https://www.cbr.com/end-of-evangelion-production-trivia/). Death threats arrived. One letter, repeated over and over, read "Anno, I'll kill you." Anno himself described the period in an NHK interview [documented by Kotaku](https://kotaku.com/online-hate-took-evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-to-a-d-1846533756): reading hostile comments about how to kill him pushed him into suicidal ideation. He described considering jumping in front of a car and off his studio's roof. His friend Hayao Miyazaki told him to take a break.

This is the man who was then tasked with producing a theatrical film — a new, better ending, the one the fans had demanded — in response to exactly the hostility that had brought him to this point. Death & Rebirth is what he was able to produce in that situation.

The incompleteness of Rebirth is not a footnote about budget. It is the shape of the wound.

## The Death Segment as Evidence

The Death compilation, usually treated as the expendable half of an already expendable film, is actually the more interesting document of the two. Masayuki's editing [makes no attempt to be a conventional recap](https://letterboxd.com/film/neon-genesis-evangelion-death-and-rebirth/). It reorganizes the series around thematic axes — the children's relationships with their parents, the question of identity, the nature of the AT Field as psychological as much as physical barrier — placing scenes in proximity not because they're adjacent in the story but because they rhyme. This is, in miniature, the same editorial logic that the series itself had been applying to its borrowed imagery: the Freudian symbols, the Christian iconography, the references that accumulate into an argument rather than a source document.

The Death segment is not showing you the story again. It's making an argument about what the story was about. That this argument is difficult to follow — [frustrating for newcomers, redundant for veterans](https://letterboxd.com/film/neon-genesis-evangelion-death-and-rebirth/) — is part of why the film gets skipped. But the editorial logic is an extension of Anno's intellectual method rather than a departure from it.

## What End of Evangelion Owes to This Failure

[The End of Evangelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion) is usually discussed as a response to fan demand — the external conclusion the TV series denied. This reading is correct but incomplete. End of Evangelion is also the product of Anno's relationship to that demand: a film that gives fans the spectacle they wanted while simultaneously, deliberately, confronting them with what they were asking for. The JSSDF invasion is brutal in a way that makes the action unwatchable as entertainment. The Human Instrumentality sequence takes the introspective psychology of the TV ending and makes it vast, terrible, and surreal. The film's famous final line — Shinji's response to Asuka's disgust — is one of the most withering expressions of ambivalence in anime.

This is the movie that Rebirth was an early draft of. And the distance between them — the rawness of Rebirth, the calculated hostility of End of Evangelion — tells you something about what four months of finishing the work did to Anno's perspective on his audience.

The [theatrical pamphlets for the films](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) claimed to explore "the relationships between a work and its admirers." The fan mail displayed in End of Evangelion — letters of anger and devotion mixed together, including some fabricated by staff for legal reasons — was that relationship made literal and turned back at the audience. Anno wasn't just finishing the story. He was processing what the story had done to him, and he was doing it in public, in front of the people who had done it.

Death & Rebirth is where that processing was still just production crisis. End of Evangelion is where it became art.

## Why the Skip Recommendation Misses the Point

The recommendation to skip Death & Rebirth treats it as a failed version of something End of Evangelion does better. This is cinematically accurate. But the incompleteness of Rebirth is historically irreplaceable. The raw cut, the abrupt ending, the clip show that tries too hard and doesn't quite cohere — these are not problems that the later film corrected by being better. They are the record of a specific impossible situation: a creator being required to respond to his audience by force, at the worst moment in his career, with insufficient resources and a crumbling team.

[Gainax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gainax) — [already financially embattled](https://www.gosugamers.net/entertainment/features/71680-how-evangelion-studio-gainax-fell-into-bankruptcy) from the failure of Royal Space Force and the budget overruns of Nadia, riding the improbable success of Evangelion through a period of genuine institutional precarity — released this film in March 1997 knowing it was unfinished. Between Death & Rebirth's theatrical run and [End of Evangelion's premiere](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0169880/), Anno completed the work under the specific conditions the article describes: depression, death threats, a studio under financial pressure, and the task of giving a hostile audience what they'd demanded from a position of near-collapse. The [¥1.8 billion gross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion:_Death_%26_Rebirth) of Death & Rebirth funded the completion of the ending. In that sense, the incomplete film paid for the complete one.

But what it also did, in its incompleteness, was record honestly what the situation was. This is the ending we had. This is what we could finish. This is where we stopped.

No amount of revision could produce that document again. Every subsequent version — Death (True), Death (True)², the revisions that refined and polished — moved further from the raw record toward something more presentable. The original theatrical Death & Rebirth remains the unretouched mark of a production that hit its limit in real time.

## The Document You Were Never Supposed to See

The JSSDF sequence in Rebirth and [the same sequence in End of Evangelion](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Evangelion:_Death_and_Rebirth) are technically the same scene — the same event, the same characters, the same broad story beats. But they differ in editing, in specific animation, in the score choices. End of Evangelion's version is controlled. Deliberate. Fully committed to what it's doing. Rebirth's version is rougher, less certain of where it's going, cut shorter because it literally had nowhere to go yet.

![Evangelion: Death & Rebirth — theatrical poster](https://media.themoviedb.org/t/p/original/lXBjqzo6c4NyLuJtqOiH39kswji.jpg)

That roughness is the thing the complete version cannot replicate. End of Evangelion is the answer to what Death & Rebirth couldn't finish. It is also, in a real sense, a deliberate transformation of crisis into argument — the documentation of a process that Death & Rebirth was too incomplete to disguise.

Every subsequent version of Death — Death (True), Death (True)², the revisions that refined and polished — moved further from the raw record toward something more presentable. The original theatrical Death & Rebirth remains the unretouched mark of a production that hit its limit in real time. That document is not available in the version most people encounter: the Death (True)² cut on Netflix and current Blu-ray releases is cleaner, tighter, stripped of some of the rougher decisions. The original theatrical experience of the incomplete Rebirth is now essentially a historical artifact.

Which makes it worth sitting with, even in the form available, before you watch End of Evangelion. Not because Death & Rebirth is better cinema. Because it shows you the crisis before Anno processed it into art. You can see, very clearly, the moment the studio stopped. That's not a failure of the film. That's the film's most honest moment — the closest it gets to showing you the truth of what the Evangelion production actually was at its worst point.

Death & Rebirth is the crisis itself, crystallized and released into theaters because the deadline arrived.
