The first episode of Attack on Titan's final season opens on a battlefield you've never seen before. Not Paradis. Not Shiganshina. No familiar faces. Instead: Marleyan soldier candidates, children in uniform, charging toward a fort defended by artillery that has never encountered Titan power. Among them is Gabi Braun, twelve years old and furious, sprinting through mortar fire to single-handedly destroy an armored train. The episode frames it as heroism. The music swells. She makes it back alive.

You have just spent three seasons watching these people be the enemy.

This is what the Marley arc actually does, and calling it "moral complexity" is underselling the violence of the maneuver. The season doesn't ask you to reconsider your allegiances — it just reassigns them without asking, and then waits to see if you'll notice. By the time Eren Yeager finally appears four episodes later, stepping out of the crowd at Willy Tybur's declaration of war, the camera has been sitting inside Marley's world long enough that his arrival registers as threat. He's watching the ceremony with Reiner's people. He's standing in the country he's about to destroy. When the attack begins, the edit cuts between Eren transforming and children running, between falling buildings and Gabi screaming, between the Survey Corps killing and the Tybur family dying. It's shot exactly the way Season 1, Episode 1 shot the Titans breaking through the wall at Shiganshina — shock, chaos, terror, a monster arriving from nowhere. Except now you know whose side the camera is on.

## Four Episodes Before a Paradis Character Appears

This structural choice deserves more attention than it gets. [Attack on Titan's final season](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Marley_arc) withholds its established protagonist group for four full episodes — roughly ninety minutes of television — while it builds an entirely new emotional infrastructure. We watch [Reiner Braun's suicidal ideation](https://aniviewer.com/reiner-braun-the-hero-who-wished-for-death-anatomy/) in a quiet rooftop scene with Falco, a boy who doesn't know what Reiner actually is. We watch Gabi's entire moral universe, every assumption she has about Eldians and Marleyans and what she deserves to become, laid out with more care than the show gave most of its Season 1 supporting cast. We watch the internment zone in Liberio — the armband-wearing Eldians confined behind fences, their second-class status formalized into law — and the show asks us to inhabit people who are simultaneously victims and weapons, persecuted by the same system they serve.

This isn't a cold open. It's a full transplant. [Analyst Jess Beavers, reviewing the manga arc](https://jessbeavers.wordpress.com/2020/08/22/attack-on-titan-marley-arc/), described it as showing "the view from the other side of the ocean" — but the effect is more destabilizing than that metaphor implies. By the time Willy Tybur delivers his speech explaining the world's history (partly true, strategically incomplete, designed to manufacture consent for Paradis's destruction), the viewer has been positioned to receive it as one reasonable person's account of events. You understand why the Marleyan public believes what they believe. You've been living inside that belief system for four episodes.

The genius of this is that it makes Eren's attack structurally identical to every Titan attack you've previously rooted against. [As CBR's analysis noted](https://www.cbr.com/aot-marley-arc-vs-series-finale/), the Marley arc "showed fans the view from the other side of the ocean, opening their eyes to new perspectives" — but more specifically: it showed them that the experience of losing people to sudden, overwhelming violence looks the same from both sides of a conflict. Gabi watching her friends die in Liberio is Carla Yeager dying in Shiganshina. The formal equivalence is the point.

## Gabi Is the Evidence That It Worked

Fan discourse around Gabi Braun is the most useful artifact the Marley arc produced, because the hatred she attracted reveals exactly what the perspective inversion was designed to do. [Screen Rant documented the intensity of viewer antipathy](https://screenrant.com/attack-titan-gabi-character-hate-sasha-eren-kill/) after she killed Sasha Braus — a beloved character — in the season's seventh episode. Fans wanted Gabi dead. Message boards lit up with demands for her suffering. She became, for a significant portion of the Western fandom, the most hated character in the series.

Consider what this requires you to believe: that a twelve-year-old child raised entirely within a propaganda system, who watched her friends die in a surprise attack, who acted out of grief and indoctrination, was uniquely blameworthy for an act of violence in a show where every major character has committed equivalent or worse violence. [As Game Rant's analysis of the character observed](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-gabi-eren-parallel/), "Eren and Gabi are two sides of the same coin" — both children who grew up defining the other side as subhuman monsters, both acting on that definition with lethal results. The difference is that Gabi's victim had three seasons of attachment, and Eren had the protagonist slot.

The venom directed at Gabi is proof that the show succeeded. If the audience could have simply processed her as a villain — a foreign soldier, an antagonist — there would have been no emotional charge. The hatred came from recognition. The audience had been repositioned into Gabi's world, made to understand her as a subject rather than an obstacle, and then when she did something unforgivable, they had nowhere to put the discomfort except onto her. [The Fandomentals' Jodie Slaughter, writing about the show's complicated politics](https://www.thefandomentals.com/attack-on-titan-fm-plus/), identified this as a signature move of the series: creating emotional stakes that make judgment feel necessary, then structuring the narrative so that judgment implicates the judge.

## The Logical Endpoint: Zeke's Plan

The Marley arc's moral repositioning produces its most explicit philosophical statement in Zeke Yeager's euthanasia plan — and it's worth dwelling on exactly what that plan says about the world the arc has constructed. [As Sportskeeda's breakdown explains](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/understanding-zeke-s-eldian-euthanasia-plan-attack-titan), Zeke proposes using the Founding Titan's power to sterilize all Subjects of Ymir, letting the Eldian people die out naturally within a century. No more Eldians, no more Titans, no more cycle.

This is, by the show's own moral logic, the most rational solution anyone offers — and that fact alone tells you how completely the Marley arc has broken the moral framework. It doesn't require mass slaughter. It ends the conflict permanently, at the cost of one people's future. That it is also, plainly, the forced sterilization of an ethnic group — the exact mechanism the show's own imagery codes as atrocity — is the point. Zeke's plan doesn't escape the logic of the oppressor. It inherits it and calls it liberation. [Screen Rant traced this thinking directly to the abuse Zeke received from his father Grisha](https://screenrant.com/attack-titan-zeke-euthanasia-plan-grisha-jaeger-fault-reason/), who treated him as a political tool rather than a child — a trauma so complete that Zeke cannot conceive of why anyone would want future generations to exist. The plan reads as mercy because it was born from a worldview that cannot distinguish mercy from extinction.

What the Marley arc established, and what Zeke's plan crystallizes, is that the conflict between Marley and Eldia has no clean resolution. Every historical wrong has a counter-wrong behind it. The Eldian Empire oppressed Marley; Marley then oppressed Eldians; Eldians now want to destroy Marley; the world outside will then respond to that destruction with more war. [As a Medium essay on Eren and the cycle of hatred put it](https://ebukauwajeh.medium.com/are-we-free-eren-yeager-hatred-and-the-endless-cycle-in-attack-on-titan-3931a922c37f): "Depending on when you begin telling the story, both the Marleyans and the Eldians can be made to look like total monsters." The Marley arc began telling the story from a different point, and it changed everything about where the monsters were.

## Why No Ending Could Win

[CBR noted](https://www.cbr.com/aot-marley-arc-vs-series-finale/) that the Marley arc "may have hindered the series' ending" — the argument being that it raised expectations too high, created too many character arcs that the final war arc couldn't adequately resolve, and sent the story down a path from which there was no satisfying exit. [FandomWire echoed the assessment](https://fandomwire.com/attack-on-titans-final-arc-may-have-been-disappointing-because-of-the-success-of-another-arc-altogether/), pointing to the gap between the Marley arc's political depth and the War for Paradis arc's rushed pacing.

This is the right observation with the wrong framing. The Marley arc didn't make the ending bad. It made the ending structurally insoluble, which is a different thing. Any resolution that validated Eren would require dismissing the humanity of everyone the Marley arc had just spent four episodes building. Any resolution that condemned Eren would require dismissing the humanity of everyone who spent three seasons following him. [The Tumblr analysis on how the Marley arc set up War for Paradis](https://ackermom.tumblr.com/post/677386907549925376/) identified this as the fundamental trap: "how do we return to the Survey Corps' perspective, catch up, and tie all of this back together in a satisfying way?" The answer was that you couldn't, and being unable to wasn't a failure of execution — it was the honest outcome of what the story had become.

The epilogue's suggestion that war breaks out again after the Rumbling — that 80% of humanity dead didn't stop the cycle — is the only intellectually honest conclusion available. It's also the most unpopular one, because audiences wanted the violence to have earned something. [Isayama, in his Crunchyroll interview after the ending](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/interviews/2023/1/13/interview-attack-on-titan-creator-hajime-isayama-reflects-on-the-series-ending-game-of-thrones-and-more), expressed anxiety about the reception and asked for kindness — suggesting he understood the ending would be punishing to sit with.

## The Ambiguity Problem

There's a coda to all of this, and it matters. [The New Republic's 2021 piece on Attack on Titan](https://newrepublic.com/article/160193/attack-titan-alt-rights-favorite-manga) documented how white nationalist communities on /pol/ had claimed the series as ideological validation — reading the Eldians as white people under persecution, the Rumbling as justified response to historical dispossession. The reading requires inverting the show's own imagery, but it's made possible by the text's structural refusal to pass judgment on anyone. Isayama has stated explicitly that he doesn't think Attack on Titan "passes judgment on what is 'right' or 'wrong'" — that he wanted to explore how violence feels rather than prescribe what it means.

[Anime Feminist's piece](https://www.animefeminist.com/my-fave-is-problematic-attack-on-titan/) identified the cost: a work saturated with historically coded imagery — armbands, internment zones, ethnic propaganda, generational persecution — that declines to adjudicate its own allegory becomes a vacuum. It will be filled. The same structural neutrality that gives the Marley arc its power is what makes it a usable tool for readings the series itself would seem to condemn. That's not an accident of execution. It's the consequence of deliberate artistic choice. You can defend the choice and still name what it permits.

The Marley arc is the most ambitious thing a modern shonen attempted: a final season that opened by making its own hero look like the monster he was becoming. It worked. It worked so thoroughly that no ending could have satisfied everyone, because everyone had been made to feel the loss from a different direction, and there was no position available that didn't implicate somebody.

The question the show leaves you with — whether violence can break a cycle or only continue it, whether understanding your enemy changes your obligation to them — doesn't have an answer inside the text. It was never meant to. The Marley arc built an emotional trap, and the ending is what living inside it feels like.
