[*Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/1121/Evangelion__2_0_You_Can_Not_Advance) runs for approximately 108 minutes. For roughly 106 of them, you could be forgiven for thinking you were watching a more polished retelling of the original series — a Rebuild that justified its name. The Angels are bigger. The digital compositing is decades ahead of the TV production. The dinner party scene finally exists. Shinji is briefly, devastatingly heroic.

Then, in the film's final ninety seconds, something descends from the moon. And two words in Japanese — *kondo koso* (この度こそ), typically translated as "this time" or "at last" — change the meaning of everything you watched.

![Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance — the film that announced the Rebuild was never a retelling](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1948/93408.jpg)

## What "This Time" Contains

Kaworu Nagisa's appearance at the end of 2.0 lasts perhaps ninety seconds. He descends aboard Evangelion Mark.06, speaks one line to the unconscious Shinji, and the film ends on his face. He says: "*Kondo koso wa, kimi wo shiawase ni shite miseru*." Rendered in English: "This time, I'll show you happiness."

The word "this time" is not filler. It is the film's thesis.

*Kondo koso* implies that previous attempts exist. It is the word you use when you have tried before and failed — when you are returning to something with the specific intention of getting it right. Kaworu could have said "I will make you happy." Instead he says "this time I will." The distinction collapses the distance between the Rebuild and the original series. It announces that the original exists — that Kaworu has memory of it, or something equivalent — and that what you have been watching for two films is not an alternate telling but a loop with a passenger who remembers the previous iteration.

Every viewer who watched [*Neon Genesis Evangelion*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/30/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) before the Rebuild already knew, at some level, that Kaworu was going to appear. They knew he would be significant. What they could not have anticipated is that he would arrive with the weight of prior knowledge in his face, positioning himself not as a new character introduced in a new story, but as a survivor of something that already ended badly.

## The Troubled Face

The production commentary on this scene is unusually revealing. According to the [*Evangelion 2.0 Complete Records Collection*](https://gwern.net/doc/anime/eva/2010-crc), Kaworu's line stayed identical from the first storyboard. What changed, at the last minute before post-synchronization, was the expression he wore while saying it. The original direction would have given him the familiar light-hearted smile from the TV series. What appears in the film is something different.

Assistant director Kazuya Tsurumaki, who worked on the original series and followed Anno to Studio Khara, described the decision in production notes. [He characterized Kaworu's final expression](https://www.tumblr.com/17th-angel/11922017113/tsurumaki-kaworus-line-stayed-the-same-from-the) as that of "a man who takes his wife back by force" — possessive, determined, burdened. Not the serene calm of the original Kaworu, who approached love as a gift. This Kaworu approaches it as an obligation he has failed before and will not fail again.

The specific framing of that change — "a man who takes his wife back by force" — is worth sitting with longer than the film has time for. In the original TV series, Kaworu was the only character who offered Shinji unconditional acceptance without agenda: "I like you, Shinji Ikari." It was a gift. It was also taken from Shinji in the cruelest possible way — Shinji himself had to crush Kaworu's soul gem, having just been told by Kaworu that this was what he wanted. The original Kaworu surrendered. The Rebuild Kaworu does not.

What the expression change signals is a fundamental reframing of the Kaworu-Shinji relationship from the TV series to the Rebuild. In the original, Kaworu offers love as something freely given and freely ended. In 2.0, [he arrives with possession already implied](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Kaworu_Nagisa/Rebuild), with an expression that says he is reclaiming something rather than offering it. The shift from "offered love" to "reclaimed obligation" tells you everything about the cost the troubled face is accounting for. He is not descending from the moon to make an offer. He is there because he already made the offer once, it ended in his death, and he is back with less illusion about how this works. The smile is gone because he isn't making an offer this time. He's making a commitment in the full knowledge of what commitment means in this story.

This is what the final ninety seconds of 2.0 actually are: a promise delivered under duress, from a character who remembers the last time.

## What the Loop Reveals

The critical observation here is not simply that the Rebuild involves a time-loop mechanic — that becomes clearer in [3.0 and 3.0+1.0](https://myanimelist.net/anime/1069/Evangelion__3_0_You_Can_Not_Redo) — but that it is *Kaworu* who is positioned as the loop's conscious bearer. Every other character in 2.0 experiences their events as new. [Kaworu alone arrives already weighted](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Kaworu_Nagisa), already grieving, already aware that the next attempt to give Shinji happiness is going to require a different kind of sacrifice than the last.

This transforms what the Rebuild project was for. The loop mechanic — the idea that these events have occurred before, that Kaworu's consciousness spans iterations — is the structural explanation for why the films exist at all. Kaworu's prior knowledge is Anno's prior knowledge. Hideaki Anno made [*Neon Genesis Evangelion*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/30/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) from inside a depressive collapse. He [described the original production](https://opus.ing/posts/how-hideaki-anno-depression-influenced-evangelion) as made from the inside of an illness he did not yet have language for. The ending of the TV series — the infamous instrumental version of "Fly Me to the Moon" over the End of Evangelion credits — is precisely what someone makes when they cannot imagine recovery. When Anno returned to Eva with the Rebuild, he was a recovered person, [married and stable](https://collider.com/hideaki-anno-evangelion-interview/), attempting to tell the same story with a different outcome available to him.

The Rebuild is itself *kondo koso*. It is Anno arriving, like Kaworu, from a position of prior knowledge — knowing how the story went, knowing what it cost, and attempting this time to make it end somewhere other than collapse. The troubled face on Kaworu in the final shot of 2.0 is not just character design. It is the face of the project itself.

## Why 2.0 Had to Be This

The structure of 2.0 needs Kaworu's ending to work because the rest of the film is designed to get the audience to want exactly the thing that causes catastrophe. Shinji's decision to save Rei — to activate Unit-01, to initiate Near Third Impact — is framed as heroic until it isn't. The film spends its middle act building the case that Shinji could be someone who acts, who chooses, who protects the people around him. It gives him the dinner party. It gives him Rei's first gesture of genuine warmth. Then it puts him in the position where the heroic thing he has learned to want to do is the thing that ends the world.

The song playing during Near Third Impact is "[Tsubasa wo Kudasai](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Tsubasa_wo_Kudasai)" — "Please Give Me Wings." It is a 1970s Japanese folk song originally composed for a choir festival, the kind sung by schoolchildren across Japan for decades. [Megumi Hayashibara's recording](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsubasa_o_Kudasai) plays from Rei's cassette player as the Eva ascends, transforms, and begins tearing the sky open. The juxtaposition is precise: a children's song about longing for freedom plays over the moment when one boy's desperate need to save someone he cares about begins destroying civilization. The song asks for wings. Unit-01 grows them. Neither means what you wanted it to mean.

The film needed exactly this level of tonal whiplash before Kaworu arrives, because what Kaworu arrives to do is not save the day in any clean sense. He stops Near Third Impact through force — through the spear, through Mark.06, through the same brutal intervention the troubled expression prepares you for. And then he speaks *kondo koso* and the film ends.

Most first-time viewers in 2009 read this as a tease — Kaworu introduced early for the third film, with an enigmatic line adding intrigue. The phrase "this time" was easy to misread as narrative shorthand for "finally," the way a hero arriving at last might say "at last we meet." The full implication — that this is a loop, that prior iterations exist, that Kaworu carries memory of them — only becomes clear in retrospect, once 3.0 makes the structure explicit. This is why the end of 2.0 works as both a mystery and a thesis simultaneously: you can watch it without reading the loop, and on a second viewing the loop is the only thing you can see. You are left with a promise made by someone who has failed before, under an expression that knows failure is possible again.

## What Anno Is Saying About Saying It Again

There is a document archived on Gwern called the [*Evangelion 2.0 Complete Records Collection*](https://gwern.net/doc/anime/eva/2010-crc), which contains interview material with Anno and Tsurumaki about the production. Anno described the Rebuild in terms that echo Kaworu's position precisely: he didn't want to do the same ending because "the time has changed and the world is now a different place from back then, and he is different, having changed from back then, so he thought he could do something different and interesting from before."

He wanted to "do something different from before." The original series couldn't give Shinji — or Anno, or the audience — a version of happiness that didn't come at catastrophic cost. The Rebuild was the attempt to find what *kondo koso* might produce. Not a promise that it would work. An attempt to discover whether it could.

The troubled face on Kaworu does not undermine this. It earns it. You don't make a promise like that with a light smile. You make it knowing what promises cost in this universe, having watched the story end badly before, and making it anyway.

That is what the final thirty seconds of 2.0 actually are: the Rebuild explaining to you what the Rebuild is. Not a remake. Not a retelling. A second attempt by someone who remembers the first one, and is not certain the second will go better, but needs to try.

*Kondo koso.*
