The horror of *Doki Doki Literature Club* depends entirely on the girls being hollow.

Not hollow in the sense of poorly written — they're written with enough texture to make you care, which is the trap. They're hollow in the sense that Monika eventually reveals: they are scripts, code, characters in a VN running on your computer. [Sayori's depression, Yuri's obsessive self-harm, Natsuki's abuse-sourced defensiveness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doki_Doki_Literature_Club!) — these are not experiences. They are variables. And the horror, the thing the game insists you feel, is that you were treating them as experiences. That you were participating in a genre that structurally requires you to treat fictional girls as romantic objects, and the game has now made you feel the weight of what you were doing.

This is a genuinely powerful idea. Then in 2021, [Team Salvato released *Doki Doki Literature Club Plus!*](https://teamsalvato.com/news/announcing-doki-doki-literature-club-plus/) — a commercial expanded version with six new side stories that gave Sayori, Yuri, Natsuki, and Monika rich inner lives, independent friendships, and histories that have nothing to do with the player character. The side stories are warmly written and deeply effective.

They are also a direct contradiction of everything the original was saying.

![Doki Doki Literature Club! — official Steam art](https://cdn.akamai.steamstatic.com/steam/apps/698780/header.jpg)

## What the Original Actually Did

Let me be clear about what *DDLC* accomplished before examining where it fails.

The game's core innovation, [as the WithATerribleFate analysis identifies](https://withaterriblefate.com/2018/04/13/not-just-monika-sadistic-horror-in-doki-doki-literature-club/), is that it weaponizes its own medium. "The horror of losing control" — Sayori's suicide is unavoidable regardless of how hard you work to reach her — is horror that only functions in an interactive medium. You clicked. You chose. You played. And the game made sure those clicks meant nothing, which is worse than not being given the choice at all. A film can make you watch tragedy happen. A game can make you fail to prevent it, which is a different and crueler thing.

[The game's manipulation of VN conventions is precise](https://www.nymgamer.com/?p=17258): the character sprites that move outside the UI boundaries, the distorted music, the background noise in Act 2, the file system manipulation — these are not random horror elements. They are the visual novel's formal language being turned against the reader. The still images that typically feel contemplative become, in Act 2's forced viewing of Yuri's corpse, traps. You must click through every line of glitched dialogue. The medium that usually respects your pace now holds you captive.

And Monika's awareness is the game's most sophisticated move. [As critics have noted](https://darkskiesfilm.com/how-is-doki-doki-literature-club-a-horror-game/), DDLC reverses the usual fourth-wall dynamic: rather than the character being inside the game and the player outside it, Monika positions herself as the external agent and the player as the one trapped inside the fictional economy. She knows you're there. She knows the others don't. This inversion is precise and unsettling.

[Dan Salvato, developer at Team Salvato, has stated he wanted to subvert the VN stereotype of "cute girls doing cute things,"](https://collider.com/doki-doki-literature-club-characters-fetishization-tropes-explained/) and that the game is intended as a critique of how players relate to dating sim characters — the "love/hate relationship with anime" he describes, particularly around how young girls are written and received. [Salvato has described the original as exploring "the player's relationship with fictional characters and fictional media in general."](https://www.nintendolife.com/features/doki-doki-literature-club-creator-on-revisiting-the-club-members-in-plus) The game achieves this ambition in places.

## The Exploitation That Undermines the Critique

The problem, [as Vrai Kaiser argued at the time of the original's release](https://vraikaiser.com/2017/10/30/doki-doki-literature-club-stumbles-between-deconstruction-and-exploitation/), is that DDLC "wants to convey how awful it is to treat its characters like exploitable objects" while simultaneously exploiting them. Kaiser's critique is worth taking seriously: the game "exploits them and their individual struggles with mental illness relentlessly," using Sayori's depression and Yuri's self-harm not to deepen understanding of those experiences but as horror spectacle. "The rough equivalent of stabbing a cow and then painting 'meat is murder' in its blood."

This lands. Sayori's depression is presented with enough authenticity to feel real, then her death is deployed as a jump scare that recontextualizes everything you thought you were playing. The horror depends on you having cared about her. But the game doesn't give you — or her — anywhere for that care to go. There are no outside resources, no possible intervention, no world beyond the club room. [The characters have no agency independent of their relationship to the player character](https://vraikaiser.com/2017/10/30/doki-doki-literature-club-stumbles-between-deconstruction-and-exploitation/). They cannot seek help, cannot form meaningful relationships with each other, cannot exist as people rather than role-players in a romance.

This is not a critique of the VN genre's treatment of women. This is the genre's treatment of women, reproduced and intensified in the service of shock. The game wants credit for deconstructing something it is actively doing.

[The Fuwanovel extended review](https://fuwanovel.moe/2024/01/review-beyond-the-visual-novel-a-deep-dive-into-doki-doki-literature-club/) and [the Rat's Den analysis](https://laurietom.blogspot.com/2019/02/vn-talk-doki-doki-literature-club.html) both note that the game's horror is so effective that players report genuine distress — not the pleasurable tension of good horror, but actual psychological discomfort. [It received significant attention for its handling of mental health themes and was downloaded over two million times within a month of its free release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doki_Doki_Literature_Club!), with major YouTubers including PewDiePie, Markiplier, and Jacksepticeye producing playthroughs that amplified its reach. But popularity and coherence are different things.

## The Self-Correction That Proves the Point

[*DDLC Plus!*, released June 30, 2021](https://www.destructoid.com/doki-doki-literature-club-plus-reveal/), adds six side stories set before the events of the original game. These stories depict how Sayori, Yuri, Natsuki, and Monika became friends. They have disagreements that don't involve the player. They pursue interests independently. They talk to each other as people talk to each other, with the textures of real friendship — awkwardness, loyalty, misunderstanding, repair.

The side stories are genuinely good. [The DDLC Plus! review on RPGFan](https://www.rpgfan.com/review/doki-doki-literature-club-plus/) notes they provide the characters with depth that the original structurally denied them, making the tragedy of the main game hit harder. Salvato frames this explicitly: [in an interview with Nintendo Life, he said the characters "stick with you because they feel like friends in your mind" and that Plus! honors that investment](https://www.nintendolife.com/features/doki-doki-literature-club-creator-on-revisiting-the-club-members-in-plus). This is exactly the problem.

![Doki Doki Literature Club Plus! — expanded edition key art](https://cdn.akamai.steamstatic.com/steam/apps/1388880/header.jpg)

If Sayori, Yuri, Natsuki, and Monika can be written as full people with real inner lives and authentic friendships — and the Plus! side stories prove they can — then the question becomes: why weren't they in the original? The answer is that the original needed them to be hollow, because the horror depends on their hollowness. But "needed for the horror" is not a neutral design choice. It means the game required these characters to be denied interiority so that their suffering could function as a plot mechanism.

[The Sidequest piece "Being Sayori,"](https://sidequest.zone/2018/04/09/being-sayori-doki-doki-literature-club-and-understanding-my-mental-health/) by a writer who found Sayori's depression personally recognizable, captures why this matters: the game creates genuine emotional identification with Sayori's experience, then destroys her as a horror set piece. The reader's care was real; the game treated it as a resource. Plus! rehabilitates Sayori as a character — but that rehabilitation also means the original's use of her was an expenditure, not a portrait.

## What Deconstruction Actually Requires

*Undertale*, the game *DDLC* is most often compared to, built its deconstruction on a foundational commitment: the characters you might treat as obstacles have inner lives, and the game will show you those inner lives whether you acknowledge them or not. The horror of a pacifist route or a genocide run in *Undertale* is identical — you understand what these beings are, and your choices reflect on you regardless.

*DDLC* inverts this. The horror comes from the revelation of hollowness, which means the characters must remain hollow to sustain it. The deconstruction cannot survive its own completion. When Plus! fills in the hollow spaces, it doesn't extend the original's argument — it undermines it by showing that the characters could have been full people all along.

[A true deconstruction of the VN genre](https://steemit.com/gaming/@elseleth/doki-doki-literature-club-a-three-layer-deconstruction-cupcake-demiboy-vs-backlog-game-9) would require the game to treat its characters as people while simultaneously critiquing the conventions that reduce them to less. *DDLC* instead treats them as props for a critique of prop-treatment, which is a different and more self-serving move.

The game is most interesting not as a successful deconstruction but as a document of what deconstruction costs commercially — and how difficult it is to sustain a thesis that implicates your own audience when that audience has to keep playing for revenue to justify a sequel. [*DDLC Plus!* sold at $14.99](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1388880/Doki_Doki_Literature_Club_Plus/) while the original remained free. In his own words, Salvato acknowledged the commercial tension: ["Are they paying for the privilege of playing DDLC, a free game, on their favorite console?"](https://nintendoeverything.com/interview-doki-doki-literature-club-creator-dan-salvato-on-new-plus-version-journey-to-switch-more/) — a question that reveals how clearly he understood the optics of monetizing what had been, and remained, free. The answer he arrived at was yes. The market logic is legible. The horror game that interrogated parasocial attachment became, four years later, a product designed to deepen it.

This isn't a condemnation of Salvato or of the people who love these characters. The Plus! side stories are warm and worth playing. But that warmth is precisely the problem: it is the warmth the original game spent its entire runtime insisting was a lie, and it turns out the lie was the horror, not the warmth.

Just Monika — until the quarterly earnings report.
