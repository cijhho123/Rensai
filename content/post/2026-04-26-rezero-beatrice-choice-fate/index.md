Beatrice waited 400 years for someone who didn't exist.

That is not a romantic premise. It is a trap — designed with cold specificity by [Echidna, the Witch of Greed](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Echidna), who created Beatrice as an artificial spirit, tasked her with guarding the Forbidden Library, and told her to wait until "that person" arrived. What Echidna never mentioned: [there was no specific person](https://animehunch.com/what-is-beatrices-contract-in-rezero/). "That person" could be anyone Beatrice chose. The entire contract was Echidna satisfying her curiosity about whether Beatrice would ever figure that out — an experiment in autonomous choice designed by a being who understood that she had made the experiment nearly impossible by never providing that information.

Beatrice did not figure it out for 400 years. She waited. The book that was supposed to guide her eventually went silent. Without external direction, she concluded that her purpose had ended — and that she was supposed to die.

This is the character that Re:Zero built its most consequential arc around. And the reason it works is that Beatrice's resolution is not a rescue. It is something the series has been arguing for from the beginning: that the only thing that actually matters is what you choose when you're not required to choose anything at all.

## The Architecture of the Trap

To understand what Echidna did to Beatrice, you need to understand what Echidna is. She is the [Witch of Greed](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Echidna) — and her greed is specifically for knowledge. She does not hoard things or people; she hoards understanding. She runs the Witch's Tea Party as a perpetual information-gathering operation. She offers Subaru a contract not out of benevolence but because she wants to observe how someone with Return by Death navigates the world. Everything Echidna does is research.

The Beatrice contract follows the same logic. Echidna created an artificial spirit, bound her to a specific location, gave her a defined purpose, and then deliberately obscured the one piece of information that would have let Beatrice fulfill that purpose without suffering: anyone can be "that person." You could have left at any point. You could have chosen long ago.

This is presented in the series as evidence of Echidna's essential cruelty — the way her intellectual curiosity operates without regard for the objects of her study. But it also does something more interesting than simply condemn Echidna. It reveals that [the contract's value was not in its terms](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Beatrice/Relationships) but in the space it opened between those terms: 400 years of Beatrice deciding, day by day, who and what she was, without being able to rely on a pre-specified answer. The trap made the choice possible. There is no guarantee that without the trap, Beatrice would have chosen at all.

## What 400 Years of Waiting Actually Looks Like

When Re:Zero introduces Beatrice in season one, she presents as comic relief. She is small, imperious, and condescending — she teleports Subaru out of the Forbidden Library repeatedly with theatrical disdain, speaks with a peculiar verbal tic, and treats his presence as an annoyance beneath her station. The show frames this as endearing eccentricity. It takes until season two to understand that the imperious performance was covering something else.

[As Fortress William's analysis notes](https://fortresswilliam.com/2020/09/18/beatrices-death-wish-rezero/), Beatrice had, by the time of the series' events, developed a functional death wish. The Gospel — her guiding book, the text that was supposed to tell her the future — had gone blank. Without its guidance, she could not identify "that person." Without "that person," she had no reason to continue. The logic was clean and terrible: the contract required her to wait for someone who would end the waiting, and without the contract there was no point, and she could not break the contract, and so the only exit was death.

The imperious exterior, the petty power plays, the constant dismissal of Subaru — all of it was management. She was running out the clock on a life she had decided to end, waiting to be killed rather than simply waiting, because waiting for death required no choice and made no demands.

## The Argument Subaru Makes

In Season 2, during the burning mansion sequence that culminates the Sanctuary arc, Subaru finds Beatrice as the building falls around them. She has made her peace. She is not going to run. She does not want rescue. She wants him to let her die here, in the library, where she has always been.

[Subaru's argument is not heroic in any conventional sense](https://gamerant.com/rezero-how-much-has-beatrice-changed-since-season-1/). He does not tell her she has value or that people need her. He tells her that a blank book and a 400-year-old verbal promise should not be allowed to dictate her life. He tells her that "that person" was never assigned to arrive — she was always free to assign that role herself. And then he tells her that if she needs a reason to choose, she can choose him. Not because of fate. Not because the contract specified him. Because he is here and he is asking.

The scene works not because Subaru is eloquent — he is characteristically messy and direct — but because [what he offers is the one thing the 400 years of waiting could never provide](https://translationchicken.com/2016/10/12/rezero-arc-4-chapter-3-reunion-and-passing-part-22/): a choice with no predetermined answer. Echidna designed a trap where Beatrice couldn't choose because she didn't know choosing was possible. Subaru dismantles the trap by naming it.

Beatrice's response — choosing Subaru, leaving the library, forming a new contract with him — is not submission or rescue. It is the first unscripted act of her existence. She has operated under Echidna's framework for 400 years. She breaks it not because Subaru is destined but because he is chosen.

## Two Kinds of Isolation

Re:Zero is a story about the cost of iteration. Subaru's Return by Death is not a power — it is a mechanism that isolates him completely, forcing him to carry memories of deaths that happened in timelines no one else can access, toward goals that shift every time he resets. He cannot communicate about it. He cannot share the weight. The series has been arguing, from its first arc, that this isolation is the real enemy — not the witch cult, not any political threat, but the fundamental inability to depend on anyone when you alone possess the information that matters.

Beatrice mirrors this structure with eerie precision. [Where Subaru carries memories no one else has, Beatrice carries years no one else experienced](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Beatrice). Both of them are psychologically isolated by knowledge that belongs only to them and cannot be shared. Both of them resolve that isolation not by finding someone who can access that knowledge but by finding someone who accepts them without needing to access it.

It is worth noting that not every critical reading finds the "Choose Me" scene as clean as that thesis implies. [One contemporary critical reading](https://starcrossedanime.com/rezero-starting-life-in-another-world-2-24-choose-me/) characterized the scene as Subaru claiming another female character as a possession — noting that his mode of rescue resembles the very visual novel entitlement the show spent two seasons critiquing. That reading is worth sitting with. It points to the tension in what the series asks: Subaru is supposed to have grown, but the "choose me" framing puts him at the center of a moment that is supposed to be about Beatrice's agency. Whether the show earns that frame or undermines it depends on how much you trust what comes after.

## The Rem Comparison

This is also why Beatrice's arc functions as a direct counterargument to what the series says about Rem. [Rem's love for Subaru is famously unconditional](https://www.crunchyroll.com/series/GRGG9798/re-zero-starting-life-in-another-world) — she loves him even knowing he is broken, even after he pushes her away, even after he tells her he doesn't love her back. This is treated as beautiful and tragic. But it is also a relationship that makes no demands: Rem's love requires nothing from Subaru because it exists regardless of what he does. It is love as a given condition — it does not require him to choose.

[The "white knight complex" that critics identified early in the series](https://frogkun.com/2016/05/27/re-zero-and-the-white-knight-complex/) — Subaru's tendency to position himself as rescuer and provider of meaning — was always most comfortable when the women around him did not require him to be anything specific. Rem's unconditional love is the fantasy version of that arrangement: she loves him regardless. Beatrice breaks it. She does not love Subaru before choosing him. She chooses him in the moment of the choice, with nothing guaranteed on either side, in a burning building, while prepared to die. That is love as decision — contingent, costly, and entirely her own.

Re:Zero has been building toward this distinction all along. The series is not interested in devotion. It is interested in what people do when they are actually free to choose otherwise — and whether, given that freedom, they still choose connection. Beatrice's answer is the clearest one the show offers.

## The Character the Series Built to Wait

There is something worth sitting with in the design of Beatrice's situation. Echidna created an artificial spirit specifically to be left alone with a question she wasn't given the tools to answer. The series treats this as evidence of Echidna's self-serving curiosity — and it is. But it also means that Re:Zero contains, at its center, a character who was [built to wait for someone who might never come](https://popculturelibraries.wordpress.com/2025/04/26/beatrice-i-suppose-examining-this-mysterious-librarian-in-re-zero/), who spent 400 years in a room that was functionally a prison, who developed an elaborate persona to manage the slow dissolution of her reason to exist, and who was saved not by fate fulfilling itself but by a stranger who refused to respect the terms of a contract she'd been following for four centuries.

What Beatrice discovers — and what Re:Zero insists on — is that the scripts don't hold. Echidna's contract couldn't specify the person because the person was always going to be whoever had the will to be chosen. Return by Death can't preserve the future because each reset creates a new one. The Witch Cult can't complete its Gospel prophecies because Subaru keeps breaking them. The whole series is an argument that fate is a structure for people who haven't decided yet, and that deciding — actually deciding, with full awareness of the cost — is the one thing no predetermined arrangement can contain.

Beatrice waited 400 years because she didn't know she could decide. When she finally does, it collapses four centuries of Echidna's experiment into a single moment. That's not a rescue. That's an answer.
