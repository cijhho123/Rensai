When Unit-01 goes berserk at the end of *Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance*, sprouting wings of light and ascending toward Near Third Impact, you are cheering. That is not an accident. It is the entire point. Hideaki Anno spent 112 minutes carefully teaching you to cheer, so that the moment you did, the world ended.

*Evangelion 2.0* (2009) is often discussed as the film where the Rebuild series got interesting — the point where Anno started making genuine creative choices rather than compiling the original series. That reading is right, but it understates what those choices actually are. The film is not just a more action-oriented reimagining. It is a structural trap. Anno built a machine to turn your emotional investment against you, and the machine works exactly as designed.

![Evangelion 2.0 promotional art showing Unit-01 in god mode](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Evangelion_2_poster.jpg/220px-Evangelion_2_poster.jpg)

## The Original Problem

To understand what 2.0 is doing, you have to remember why the Rebuild series existed at all. According to Anno, the original plan was that "the first 80% would function as a compilation" of the TV series, with significant divergences only occurring in the final film — because, as the production team explicitly felt, [*End of Evangelion* could not receive a sequel](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Rebuild_of_Evangelion). They were not going to top the original ending. They were going to work around it.

That plan changed. Major divergences started appearing in 2.0, not the final film. Anno himself explained it straightforwardly in interviews: ["The world is now a different place from back then. I changed from back then. So, I thought that I could do something different and interesting from before."](https://time.com/6090247/neon-genesis-evangelion-thrice-upon-a-time-hideaki-anno/) What "different and interesting" means is not just aesthetic. It means fundamentally restructuring what kind of film this would be — and therefore what kind of trap it would set.

The original *Neon Genesis Evangelion* was a deconstruction machine. It built you up with mecha tropes and then systematically dismantled them: the battles became traumatic rather than exciting, the hero became increasingly unable to function, the adults were corrupt or complicit, and the ideology of human connection was examined until it collapsed under its own weight. It was not designed to make you feel good. Anno later described himself at the time as ["a broken man who could do nothing for four years,"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff_and_Cast) channeling his depression directly into the work — and [an NHK documentary following the Rebuild production](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2021-03-25/nhk-documentary-captures-hideaki-anno-creative-struggles-on-final-evangelion-film/.171027) confirmed the pattern held all the way through the franchise. The result of the original series showed that history on screen.

*Evangelion 2.0* is not that. It is — at least for its first hundred minutes — conspicuously, almost aggressively crowd-pleasing.

## The Bait

The shift is evident in everything. [Critics noted the film "shifts away from the original series' heightened attention on deconstruction and psychology, instead getting heavy with robot action battles"](https://wondersinthedark.wordpress.com/2011/01/29/the-more-things-change-hideaki-annos-evangelion-2-0-you-can-not-advance/) — and this is not a criticism of the film, it is a description of its strategy. The battles are spectacular. The [animation won the Animation of the Year award at the 33rd Japan Academy Film Prize](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Evangelion_2.0:_You_Can_(Not)_Advance), and it deserved it; the CGI Angel designs integrate with hand-drawn animation so cleanly it is difficult to see the seam.

Shinji is different too. In the original series, he was passive to the point of paralysis — the show's central critique of the heroic mecha protagonist. In 2.0, he is proactive, emotionally curious, genuinely reaching out. [The relationship between Shinji and Rei is warmer here, with Rei making genuine gestures of goodwill — inviting Asuka to dinner, attempting human connection in ways her original counterpart never did](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Asuka_Shikinami_Langley). Even Asuka's function changes. In the original series she was isolated, obsessive, and emotionally closed off. [In 2.0, she is "much more open with Shinji"](https://timber-wolf-studios.com/understanding-asuka-in-evangelions-second-act/) — lying beside him not in the original series' sequence of humiliation and disconnection, but in something closer to actual comfort.

The whole film is gentler at the human level, and more explosive at the action level. It is, structurally, what fans of the original series who found it too bleak might have wished the original series had been. The version where Shinji actually tries. The version where the robot fights are sick. The version where Anno gives you what you asked for. The [EvaGeeks forum has a thread that runs for pages](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=6154) titled "Why is Shinji so universally hated?" — it documents exactly the fandom frustration Anno was answering here, the Western-audience consensus that Shinji's passivity disqualified him as a protagonist. *Evangelion 2.0* addresses that complaint directly. The film agrees with it, provisionally, and then shows you what happens when Shinji stops being passive.

![Evangelion 2.0 Rei Ayanami](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e8/Rei_Ayanami_NGE.jpg/220px-Rei_Ayanami_NGE.jpg)

## The Switch

Then Zeruel arrives.

The [Tenth Angel](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Tenth_Angel) is not just a difficult fight — it is a systematic defeat. It tears through the NERV defenses, dismembers Unit-02, and seizes Rei inside its body. Shinji has been told not to pilot. He refuses. He gets in the Eva. He goes to fight, not because he is ordered to, not because he has no choice, but because *Rei is in there* and he cannot stand it. This is exactly what fourteen years of original NGE fans said they wanted. Shinji finally growing up. Shinji finally choosing.

What follows is the film's most deliberately engineered sequence. Unit-01 takes catastrophic damage. Shinji refuses to stop. The Eva reactivates with ["seemingly limitless power, regrows its severed arm out of light, sprouts a halo and goes to town on the Angel."](https://kotaku.com/evangelion-2-22-is-a-film-that-plays-on-your-expectatio-5959963) The music swells. The imagery is explicitly divine — a cross-shaped energy wave, a third eye, wings of light radiating from the Eva's back. It is the most visually overwhelming sequence in the Rebuild series up to that point. And you are cheering.

The [broader thematic analysis of the Rebuild series](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Rebuild_of_Evangelion) identifies this sequence as the moment Shinji's emotional will — his genuine love for Rei — becomes the trigger for Near Third Impact. He is not being manipulated into piloting. He is not acting on orders. He *chooses*, out of genuine human feeling, to do the thing that begins the end of the world.

This is different from *End of Evangelion*, where Third Impact happens through coercion, corruption, and Shinji's own breakdown. In 2.0, it happens through Shinji at his *best* — proactive, caring, resolved. Anno took the criticisms of the original ("why won't Shinji just try harder") and answered them: here, Shinji tries harder. Here is the consequence.

## The Meta-Layer

Anno has never been subtle about his relationship to his audience. The credits sequence of *Evangelion 2.0* includes stills of fake fan mail and graffiti — including, explicitly, ["Anno, I'll kill you!!"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) The official pamphlet explains these "simulated the hypothetical reactions of fans to reflect on the relationships that are established between a work and its admirers." This is a filmmaker putting the audience's rage at him directly into his own film, labeled as a thing to examine.

The [original *End of Evangelion* was made in direct response to the actual death threats Anno received](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats) after the TV ending — fan rage that the show did not give them what they wanted. Anno's response was to make something more disturbing than what they had complained about. The fake death threats in 2.0's credits are him returning to that dynamic in a more controlled, self-aware way. *Here is what your rage looks like. Here is the relationship between us. Watch what happens when I give you what you want.*

What you want is Shinji choosing to fight. What you get, when he does, is Near Third Impact. Anno is not punishing you for wanting the hero to be heroic. He is showing you what heroism costs in his version of this world — and he is doing it in a way that requires your investment to work. A film where Shinji was passive and broken could not have delivered this ending with the same force. The emotional trap requires the emotional attachment, and the emotional attachment requires 112 minutes of a more accessible, action-forward, character-warmer film.

## Mari and the Problem She Reveals

None of this is undermined by Mari Illustrious Makinami, but Mari is worth addressing because she illuminates a tension in 2.0 that the film never fully resolves. [A King Records producer explicitly requested a new female character to "strengthen the film's appeal and expand its audience,"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious) and Anno welcomed the request as something that would "distance the new films from the original work." Her name contains the kanji 破 (*ha*), meaning both "interlude" and "break" — the same character in the film's Japanese title.

Mari is, in other words, a commercial note made into a character. She is explicitly there because someone thought the film needed broader appeal. And Anno knew this and did it anyway — which critics and fans have read as either compromise or a fourth-wall wink at Rebuild's own identity. [She never fully integrates into the thematic core of 2.0](https://gamerant.com/rebuild-evangelion-movies-compared-to-original-anime/), and the film does not pretend otherwise. Anno even acknowledged in the screenplay process that he "did not know what exactly to do" with her as the story evolved.

But here is the position worth taking: Mari's commercial origin is not a flaw in the trap — it is part of the trap's honesty. She is the film's admission that it was built for broader audiences, worn openly on the sleeve. And Anno is a filmmaker who has always been explicit about the relationship between commerce and creation in Evangelion. [Kotaku noted the film "plays on viewer expectations while defying them as the film approaches its climax"](https://kotaku.com/evangelion-2-22-is-a-film-that-plays-on-your-expectatio-5959963) — Mari is a visible marker of those expectations. She represents the version of *Evangelion* that is just fun. When fun curdles into apocalypse in the final act, the gap between her register and the ending's register is itself a statement: this is what Anno thinks of the version of the franchise you wanted. He made it. He gave it a character explicitly designed to make you comfortable. Then he used your comfort against you.

## What the Ending Actually Demands

The final line before Kaworu arrives to stop Third Impact — "The promised time has come, Shinji Ikari. This time, I'll be sure to bring you happiness" — is the film declaring its hand. "This time" implies foreknowledge of a previous attempt that failed. *Evangelion 2.0* is not a remake that went wrong. It is a structure in which multiple iterations of the same story are being consciously managed, and the one you just watched — the good version, the hopeful version, the one where Shinji chooses — ended the world.

That is a thing only this version of the story could do. The original NGE did not have an audience primed to cheer Shinji on when he triggered Third Impact, because the original NGE spent years making sure that cheering felt impossible. The Rebuild films, by choosing to be more accessible, created the conditions for something the original could not: an ending that works as tragedy precisely because the film worked as a crowd-pleaser.

*Evangelion 2.0* [opened at number one at the Japanese box office with over 512 million yen in its opening weekend](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2009-06-29/evangelion-2.0-opens-at-no.1-with-510-million-yen) — nearly double the [280 million yen opening of *Evangelion 1.0*](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2007-09-03/japanese-box-office-august-25-26-september-1-2), and the full theatrical run would end at 4 billion yen, exactly twice 1.0's total. It gave audiences what they came for. Then it showed them what they had asked for. Anno had been here before, with the actual death threats after the TV finale and the making of *End of Evangelion*. He knew how this relationship works. He built it again, more carefully, and put the fake hate mail in the credits as a receipt.

The question *Evangelion 2.0* leaves open — and that *Evangelion 3.0* would answer in the most brutal possible way — is what happens to an audience that gets everything it wanted and still ended the world. But that's a different article. For now, the second film has done its job: it made you care enough to cheer, and then it made you responsible for what that cheering cost.
