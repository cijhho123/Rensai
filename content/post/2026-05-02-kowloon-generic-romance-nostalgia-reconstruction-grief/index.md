The Kowloon Walled City was demolished in 1994. It took two years to tear down — a labyrinthine urban enclave that had grown without planning or law into one of the most densely populated places on earth. After it was cleared, the site became a park. Today it exists primarily as nostalgia: in photographs, in documentary films, in the PlayStation game *Kowloon's Gate* that Jun Mayuzuki played as a teenager, and in the enormous cultural afterlife of a place that has been mourned more thoroughly than it was ever understood.

*Kowloon Generic Romance* is set in a version of Hong Kong where the city was never torn down. The reconstruction happened instead: what stands in the manga's world is a new Kowloon built to preserve the old one, and the residents know the difference. They know they're living in a copy. They know the original is gone. They are, consciously and happily, choosing to inhabit the reconstruction anyway.

This is not a science fiction premise. It is the emotional premise of the entire series.

## Three Reconstructions

Jun Mayuzuki has described the project as drawing ["my ideal Kowloon — a version of Kowloon Walled City that I'd want to live in."](https://mangasplaining.substack.com/p/interview-jun-mayuzuki-creator-of) That framing is more precise than it might initially seem. She is not interested in documentary accuracy. She is interested in what it feels like to love a place that no longer exists — and in what you do with that feeling when you cannot let it go.

The series structures this into three parallel reconstructions, each one a different version of the same problem.

The first is the city itself: Kowloon as it appears in the manga's world, lovingly detailed and recognizably nostalgic. [The UK Anime Network noted](https://www.uk-anime.net/Articles/Kowloon_Generic_Romance:_Mystery_wrapped_in_Nostalgia.html) that within the fiction, "this new Kowloon and its reconstruction" is itself a mystery — the residents know they're living in a rebuilt version of something that was lost, and the implications of that knowledge ripple through the story.

The second reconstruction is Reiko Kujirai. She has no memory prior to the last six months. She lives in an apartment that matches the apartment of her coworker Hajime Kudou's dead fiancée. She has the same name. The same job. She is, as the series gradually reveals, [a near-perfect copy of the woman Kudou lost](https://animewarcrimetribunal.wordpress.com/2025/07/04/review-kowloon-generic-romance/) — created, somehow, from his grief and the city's floating intelligence system, Terra. She is Kowloon's answer to Kudou's inability to let go of what he'd had.

The third reconstruction is the manga itself. [Mayuzuki learned about Kowloon Walled City through a game](https://anitrendz.net/news/2023/08/04/interview-jun-mayuzuki-talks-kowloon-generic-romance-nostalgia-manga-career/) — a mediated, fictional version of a real place — and built her fictional city from research, photography, and longing. [The editor of the series, Hakko Okuma](https://mangasplaining.substack.com/p/interview-jun-mayuzuki-creator-of), identified the themes as: "love and romance; the human question of where we've come from and what we are; concerns about our reputation among others; the absolute nature of our own perceptions; thoughts and views about nostalgia." That list is dense and accurate, but it all collapses into one question: can a reconstruction be real?

## The Wong Kar-wai Question

[Aftermath's piece on the anime](https://aftermath.site/kowloon-generic-romance/) called it "the closest anime has come to a Wong Kar-wai film," and the comparison is not merely aesthetic. Wong Kar-wai's work — *Chungking Express*, *In the Mood for Love*, *2046* — is constituted by Hong Kong spaces holding emotional memory. His characters live in cities that feel porous to the past: every corridor, every food stand, every late-night neon-soaked counter is saturated with the residue of what happened there. The city is not backdrop; it is archive.

*Kowloon Generic Romance* inherits this understanding and literalizes it. Where WKW's cities hold memory implicitly — through atmosphere and light and the way his camera lingers — Mayuzuki makes the conceit explicit: the city *is* a memory, deliberately constructed to preserve what was lost. And Reiko is the same. Where a WKW heroine might carry unrequited feeling in her eyes, Reiko's entire existence is unrequited feeling made flesh.

This is the series at its most genuinely unsettling. What does it mean that Kudou found his grief reconstructed and walking around his workplace? What does it mean for Reiko that her personhood, her autonomy, her developing feelings for Kudou — all of it is conditioned by the fact that she was designed to be someone else? [The conversations the series stages around these questions](https://animeuknews.net/2024/11/kowloon-generic-romance-volumes-6-7-and-8-review/) are among the best in recent manga: slow, emotionally precise, genuinely interested in what "identity" means when your identity has an origin in someone else's trauma.

## The Anime's Problem

The [13-episode anime adaptation by Arvo Animation](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-10-21/kowloon-generic-romance-receives-tv-anime-live-action-film/.216995), which aired Spring 2025 and was [confirmed as a complete adaptation](https://animecorner.me/kowloon-generic-romance-anime-confirmed-to-be-a-complete-adaptation-will-have-13-episodes/) of the entire manga, has a speed problem: it covered approximately eleven manga volumes in thirteen episodes. This is aggressive even for a genre-condensed adaptation, and the cracks show.

[CBR's review described it](https://www.cbr.com/kowloon-generic-romance-season-1-review/) as "Spring 2025's most disappointing adult anime," arguing that "characters' milestones and setbacks don't register the intended emotional response because of how hurriedly they're deployed." The specific failure is in the secondary characters — people like Yaomay and Xiaohei, who in the manga have space to become full presences, are resolved in half-episode arcs in the anime. The pacing works against the series' core strength, which is its slow accumulation of mood and meaning.

The [Anitrendz review](https://www.anitrendz.com/news/2025/07/07/kowloon-generic-romance-review) was more generous but identified the same problem: the series needed "more room to breathe" for the relationships to develop before plot intervened. 74/100 — worth watching, but not the experience the manga provides.

[CBR's critique of the ending](https://www.cbr.com/kowloon-generic-romance-episode-13-finale-review/) is harder to dismiss: the revelation that Kudou was "the main character all along" — that everything was, at root, his memories and his grief made real — risks positioning Reiko as serving his arc rather than having her own. This is the central tension the series has to navigate, and whether it navigates it successfully depends entirely on how you read the ending.

My read: the series does not reduce Reiko to Kudou's wish-fulfillment because it takes seriously the question of whether she becomes real through the act of choosing. Her resolution — [leaving Kowloon, becoming a travel agent, eventually reuniting with Kudou on her own terms](https://www.cbr.com/kowloon-generic-romance-episode-13-finale-review/) — is the argument that a copy, given enough time and genuine experience, stops being a copy. She was designed to be someone else. She chose to be herself. The series insists that the second fact makes the first one secondary.

Whether the anime's pacing gives that arc enough room to land is a different question, and [the honest answer is that it doesn't always](https://countzeroor.com/2025/08/09/anime-review-kowloon-generic-romance/). The [live-action film released August 2025](https://animecorner.me/kowloon-generic-romance-gets-anime-and-live-action-movie-adaptations-in-2025/) may have had more success with the tonal dimensions the anime compressed.

## Why the Setting Matters This Much

[Game Rant noted](https://gamerant.com/kowloon-generic-romance-spring-2025-sci-fi-romance-anime/) that one of KGR's achievements is being a mature romance "not set in high school" — an obvious point but a real one, since the genre's default is to anchor emotional stakes in adolescent firsts. Mayuzuki's protagonists are adults with histories, and the series' emotional texture is adult in a specific way: it is interested in the grief of people who are past the age when grief is supposed to be resolved.

The Kowloon setting is essential to this. [The manga ran from November 2019 to April 2026 in Weekly Young Jump](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2026-02-27/jun-mayuzuki-kowloon-generic-romance-manga-ends-in-april/.234764) — six and a half years in a demolished city that its creator "wanted to live in." That duration, that specific desire, is the most honest thing Mayuzuki has said about what the series is. It is a love letter to a place she never visited, in a city that no longer exists, from a woman who felt pulled toward it by a game she played as a teenager.

What *Kowloon Generic Romance* understands about grief that most manga about grief do not is that the reconstruction can be better than the original. The real Kowloon Walled City was a dangerous, overcrowded, lawless place. Mayuzuki's Kowloon is warm and neighborly and detailed with loving specificity. It is not a documentary. It is what you make when you mourn something you never had, and you make it perfect, and then you populate it with people who also know it's a copy and choose it anyway.

This is also Reiko's situation. She is a copy who didn't choose to be made, living in a copy of a place that didn't choose to be rebuilt, both of them surrounded by people who know the difference and stay anyway. The series' question is not whether a reconstruction can be real. It's whether real is even the right thing to want.
