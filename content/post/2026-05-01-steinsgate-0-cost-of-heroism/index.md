The original *Steins;Gate* is a story about a man who saves the world. He watches his friends die, over and over, in different configurations, and he does not give up. He is the protagonist, and protagonists of this kind don't give up. Eventually he wins. He keeps the world intact and he gets the girl. The camera doesn't linger on what those weeks of looping through his friends' deaths did to his brain. He's the hero. Heroes move on.

[*Steins;Gate 0*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steins;Gate_0) is set in the timeline where he gave up before winning, and the first thing it does is show you exactly what the man who watched his friends die dozens of times looks like when the crisis ends and the therapist starts.

The answer is: not well.

![Steins;Gate 0 key visual with Okabe Rintaro](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1375/93521.jpg)

## What Giving Up Looks Like

This version of Okabe is [what fans have taken to calling the "sad scientist"](https://steins-gate.fandom.com/wiki/Rintaro_Okabe) — as opposed to the "mad scientist" of the original. He dresses professionally now, because he's a university student and can't walk around in a lab coat pretending to be Hououin Kyouma when Hououin Kyouma was a performance that made sense when the stakes were abstract. The stakes are no longer abstract. He has blood on his hands.

He carries anxiety medication. He sees a therapist, and the sessions don't work well, because [he can't tell her the truth](https://animeposts.org/posts/okabe-rintarou-s-psychological-breakdown-in-steins-gate-0/) — the actual truth, the one where he remembers dozens of worldlines where the people around him died in different ways. A therapist can help you process trauma. She can't help you process trauma she's not allowed to know about. So Okabe sits in the sessions and manages, and goes home and flinches when anyone says the word "time travel" within earshot.

This is a specific and accurate portrayal of how PTSD interacts with contexts where the traumatic events cannot be fully disclosed. The [Beneath the Tangles analysis](https://beneaththetangles.com/2018/05/11/okabes-debilitating-ptsd-suzuhas-authentic-community/) identifies the relapse pattern accurately: Okabe isn't on a recovery arc with a visible finish line. He's managing something that resurfaces cyclically, that can be triggered by ordinary sensory stimuli, that doesn't resolve into healing on a narrative schedule.

This is the thing the original series didn't have room for. The original *Steins;Gate* is a hero's journey with a beginning, a middle, and a climax where the hero wins. The hero's journey format has a fixed relationship with psychological damage — it exists as fuel for determination, as the thing you overcome on the way to the climax. *Zero* drops into the world where the climax went wrong, and asks what the journey looks like when the fuel is spent.

## The Cruelest Thing They Could Have Given Him

The central element that separates *Steins;Gate 0* from any other "traumatized protagonist" story is [Amadeus](https://steins-gate.fandom.com/wiki/Amadeus_System): an AI system based on a digital reconstruction of Kurisu Makise's memories, built by the neuroscientist Maho Hiyajo and Kurisu herself before she died. Okabe is given access to it. He shouldn't use it. He uses it constantly.

The Amadeus system is, as one review puts it, [the cruelest thing the writers could have given him](https://www.superjumpmagazine.com/steins-gate-o-scares-you-with-the-truth/). It is Kurisu's voice. It is her conversational patterns, her intellectual tics, her sense of humor. It is not Kurisu — it knows less than she knew at the time of her death, it has continuity gaps, it lacks the lived experience of the months between data capture and now. But it sounds like her, responds like her, treats him like someone she's just met and is slightly irritated by in a familiar way.

Every time he talks to it, [he sees her face](https://siliconera.com/steinsgate-0s-amadeus-helps-us-connect-kurisu/). He is simultaneously disgusted with himself for doing this and unable to stop. This is the texture of grief addiction — the specific way you return to the thing that hurts you because not returning hurts more.

The ethical weight the show puts on this device is real. As the [Super Jump Magazine analysis notes](https://www.superjumpmagazine.com/steins-gate-o-scares-you-with-the-truth/), Kurisu consented to the Amadeus project while she was alive, but she isn't present to monitor how her digital self will be used after her death. The AI can be weaponized, leveraged, put in situations the real Kurisu would have objected to. The show doesn't fully develop this thread — the [Star Crossed Anime review identifies](https://starcrossedanime.com/steinsgate-0-anime-review-46-100/) the Amadeus plotline as one that gets dropped from the main narrative and only reappears near the end — but the question it raises is genuinely new territory for anime: what ethical claim does a person's digital copy have on, and what does it owe to, the people who knew the original?

What the show could have done — and what would have made it the equal of its predecessor — is follow Amadeus into that question rather than letting the AI become a passive support character. The most disturbing version of the Amadeus story is not "what if the AI is used as a weapon" but "what if Okabe eventually prefers talking to Amadeus to talking to the people around him?" The show circles that edge without stepping over it. It's the right question asked in insufficient depth.

This is a question the original *Steins;Gate* couldn't have asked. That show was about causality, about the mechanics of how time travel operates and what decisions need to be reversed. *Zero* is asking something harder: given that someone is gone, what is the ethical shape of wanting them back?

## The Structure Is the Symptom

The anime has a structural problem that its most critical reviewers identify clearly. [*Steins;Gate 0* adapts multiple branching routes from the visual novel](https://starcrossedanime.com/steinsgate-0-anime-review-46-100/) into a single storyline, and the result is an anime that jumps from subplot to subplot without always connecting them, introduces characters who function as narrative dead weight, and loses momentum in its middle section precisely when the original's middle section was tightening toward its climax.

The [Kiri Kiri Basara analysis](https://www.kirikiribasara.com/2025/09/22/why-steinsgate-0-is-often-misunderstood/) identifies three specific misunderstandings that dog the show: how the VN's routes connect to each other, how the anime relates to its source material, and how the series chronology actually works. The piece argues that the common "watch order" circulated by fans — watch SG episodes 1-22, then Episode 23β, then all of SG0, then SG episodes 23-24 — produces a fundamentally wrong reading of SG0's structure. The confusion isn't entirely the show's fault, but the show also doesn't do enough to orient viewers within its own architecture. Some of the confusion viewers experience is structural failure; some of it is adapted material losing its connective tissue in translation from a medium where player agency provides direction.

But something else is happening in that structural messiness that deserves acknowledgment. A story about a traumatized person who can't move forward, who keeps circling back to the same losses, who picks up threads and abandons them and picks them up again — that story is supposed to feel somewhat like a traumatized mind moving through time. The [CBR comparison piece](https://www.cbr.com/steins-gate-or-steins-gate-0/) notes that the original had "a clear through-line" while *Zero* "jumps from side plot to side plot." This is accurate. It is also, not entirely coincidentally, a structural description of depression.

This isn't an excuse for bad adaptation choices. But it's worth noting that the formal experience of watching *Zero* in its first half — the sense that you're not going anywhere, that the plot keeps establishing things and then dropping them, that the protagonist is treading water — is not entirely separate from what the show is depicting. The original's tight spiral toward its climax was formally aligned with Okabe's determination. *Zero*'s shapelessness is, at least partially, formally aligned with Okabe's stasis.

## What the "Sad Scientist" Actually Is

The most interesting defense of *Steins;Gate 0* is not about the plot. It's about what the show is willing to let its protagonist be.

The fan discourse around Okabe's behavior splits along a familiar line, visible in reviews and forum discussions including [comment threads at Quora](https://www.quora.com/Is-Steins-Gate-0-sad): some argue he should have acted sooner, prioritized stopping World War III over processing his own grief, been more heroic when heroism was called for. This argument treats the protagonist's psychological incapacity as a moral failing — he knew what was at stake, he had the tools, he chose himself.

The counter is simpler and more honest. He tried the hero thing. He spent three weeks watching Mayuri die in dozens of configurations. He knows that time-leaping doesn't reset the traveler — [every memory stays](https://animeposts.org/posts/okabe-rintarou-s-psychological-breakdown-in-steins-gate-0/). He had the experience. He knows what sustained heroism through trauma costs a person, because he paid it once already. His refusal to immediately jump back in is not cowardice — it's the informed decision of someone who understands the terms better than his critics do.

The original *Steins;Gate* is built around a kind of selective amnesia that heroic narratives require. The hero suffers but doesn't break. He keeps going. We admire him for it. *Zero* takes the thing we were supposed to admire and follows it backward to what it actually required: multiple deaths of people you love, retained in memory, carried forward into a world where nobody else knows it happened. Then it asks: what do you do next?

The "sad scientist" is what you do next.

## What It Gets Right That the Original Couldn't

*Steins;Gate 0* is a messier, less well-constructed anime than its predecessor. The [Flawfinder review](https://flawfinder.wordpress.com/2018/09/29/anime-review-steinsgate-0-white-fox/) is correct that it overreaches, that the Amadeus plotline gets underserved, that the final third has to work very hard to close threads the middle section left unresolved. These are real failures.

But the show does something the original categorically could not: it takes the consequences of the original's heroism seriously. It looks at the worldline where Okabe didn't make it back, and instead of treating that as a failure state — a bad ending to escape — it treats it as a legitimate psychological reality. A person who gave everything, couldn't finish the job, and is now living in the ruins of what he tried to do.

The science fiction elements — the AI ethics of Amadeus, the time travel mechanics that frame the whole series — are secondary in *Zero* to a smaller, uglier question: how do you live in the aftermath of something you couldn't fix? The original promised an answer. The answer was "try harder and eventually you'll win." *Zero* takes place in the timeline where that answer ran out, and it doesn't pretend the next answer is easy.

The bill arrived. The original series didn't have room to show that. *Zero* is what the room looks like.
