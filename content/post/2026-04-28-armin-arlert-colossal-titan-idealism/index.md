There is a design detail about Armin Arlert's Colossal Titan that most viewers either miss or choose not to sit with. When Bertholdt Hoover transformed — when the Titan who destroyed Wall Maria and kicked off the entire series rose above Shiganshina — his form had ears. When [Armin transforms into the same Titan after consuming Bertholdt](https://screenrant.com/attack-titan-armin-never-colossal-transform-reason/), the earless skull that erupts from the steam has none. Fan discourse has long circulated a specific explanation: Armin could not bear to hear his victims' screams. He made himself deaf to it. Whether that's confirmed canon or the most accurate fan reading anyone has ever produced, it doesn't matter — it is true to what the character *is*.

That detail is the article. Everything else in Armin Arlert's arc is an elaboration of that image: the idealist who dreamed of a free world, who engineered himself to be partially unable to experience the full consequences of building it.

![Armin transforms into the Colossal Titan at Liberio](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Armin-becomes-the-colossal-titan.jpg)

## The Boy with the Book

[The book Armin kept as a child](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Armin_Arlert) — the illegal one about the outside world, the one that got his grandfather in trouble — is not a prop. It is the series' ur-text. Every character who ever pointed at the horizon is downstream of Armin's obsession with that book. He gave Eren the dream of freedom before Eren understood what freedom cost. And when they finally reach the ocean in Season 3, standing at the exact shoreline Armin spent his whole childhood imagining, Eren picks up a rock and asks whether killing everyone across that water is the price of keeping this.

[Armin drops the seashell.](https://collider.com/attack-on-titan-series-finale-ending-explained/) He understands immediately what the dream was always going to cost. He always understood — which is the tragedy. The [psychological analysis](https://www.zimbardo.com/the-psychology-behind-armin-arlert/) of his character points to the tension at his core: "the tension between vulnerability and resilience, intellect and emotion, idealism and pragmatism." That tension is not resolved over the course of the series. It is lived all the way through, to the end.

## The Inheritance

There is a loaded irony in Armin becoming the Colossal Titan. He inherited the power of the being who [destroyed Wall Maria in 845](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Armin_Arlert), who killed hundreds of thousands and started everything — and he inherited it by consuming Bertholdt alive. That is how the power transfers: the new holder must eat the previous one in Titan form. Armin literally digested the person who almost killed him, absorbed his guilt and his memories along with his Titan.

What the series does with this, through the [Paths dimension](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Armin_Arlelt_(Anime)) in Season 4, is give Armin direct empathic access to Bertholdt's experience. He starts to feel, from the inside, what Bertholdt felt — what it is like to be someone who grew up as a weapon, who was raised by a system that pointed him at a wall and told him it was necessary. The Marleyan Warriors were child soldiers recruited before they could consent to what they were agreeing to. Bertholdt was not a monster. He was a person the system manufactured into one. Armin understands this. He understands it because he can feel it. This is what makes the Liberio port attack so devastating when it comes.

## What He Saw After

During the [Raid on Liberio](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Raid_on_Liberio) in Season 4, Armin sails alone to Marley's port, climbs into a small boat, slips past the harbor, and transforms. The resulting explosion [destroys the port and Marley's naval fleet](https://moral-ranking.fandom.com/wiki/Armin_Arlert), kills hundreds of people, and buys the Survey Corps exactly the tactical window they need to escape. It is a textbook successful military operation. Armin climbs back out of his Titan form and looks down at what he has done.

What he says, standing in the wreckage he created, is that Bertholdt must have looked at the same scene. The same view. The same consequence. He thought about the man he consumed and realized: I just did what was done to us. I understood it from the inside.

This is the moment Armin's arc turns. Not when he receives the serum. Not when Erwin dies. Here, standing in the port, recognizing that [his guilt mirrors Bertholdt's](https://amritbirdi.substack.com/p/attack-on-titan-an-inherited-pain), he understands that being a person of conscience in a war does not exempt you from committing atrocities. It just means you know what you're doing while you do it.

And he still doesn't use the Titan again until he has to. [The earless form stays locked away](https://screenrant.com/attack-titan-armin-never-colossal-transform-reason/) for as long as possible, because using it means he has to become temporarily deaf to what he's causing.

## The Paths Conversation and the Thank You

![Armin confronting Eren in the Paths dimension](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/attack-on-titan-armin-yelling-at-himself-in-the-paths.jpg)

[Chapter 139](https://www.postapocalypticmedia.com/attack-on-titan-manga-chapter-139-review-final/) is where the fandom erupted. In the Paths dimension — the metaphysical space connecting all Titans — Armin has one final conversation with Eren, who reveals the full scope of what he planned and why. And Armin, the pacifist, the idealist, the one who has spent the series arguing for diplomacy and peaceful resolution, says something that broke a significant portion of the readership: he thanks Eren for becoming a mass murderer for their sake. He swears the transgression won't be wasted.

The backlash was immediate and understandable. It looked like the series canonizing Eren's genocide, using the moral center of the cast as the vehicle. It looked like Armin forgiving the unforgivable.

Speaking to [the New York Times in November 2023](https://www.dexerto.com/anime/attack-on-titan-hajime-isayama-breaks-silence-on-controversial-scene-2371657/), Isayama acknowledged that he failed to execute the scene clearly and explained what he was actually going for: "It was more that he wanted to, in a sense, take joint responsibility. He wanted to become an accomplice." The distinction matters. Armin wasn't endorsing the Rumbling. He was refusing to stand at a safe moral distance from it — refusing to be the person who watched the terrible thing happen and then shook his head and said *I never approved of this.* He had approved of it, by staying. By using his brilliance to plan the wars that made it possible. By being present in Eren's life as the friend who never quite stopped him. "He wanted to become an accomplice" means: *I am going down with you, because pretending I'm not implicated would be a lie.*

That is actually a darker reading than the misinterpretation. Forgiving your friend's genocide is at least a recognizable emotional response. Declaring yourself an accomplice to it — accepting joint liability because the outcome benefited you — is something stranger and more honest, and more philosophically corrosive.

The anime [changed the line entirely](https://www.dexerto.com/anime/attack-on-titan-hajime-isayama-breaks-silence-on-controversial-scene-2371657/) with Isayama's direct involvement: in *The Final Chapters Part 2*, Armin says "Let's go to hell together" rather than thanking Eren. Isayama acknowledged this version better captured his intent. It is cleaner and emotionally legible. It is also, arguably, a retreat from the most unsettling version of what the manga was reaching for — the idea that solidarity in damnation looks indistinguishable from complicity, because it is.

## The Bitter Function

The series ends with Armin as humanity's peace ambassador, heading back to the continent with the surviving Survey Corps members to negotiate after [80% of humanity has been killed](https://collider.com/attack-on-titan-series-finale-ending-explained/). His intelligence, his emotional acuity, his ability to understand people on multiple sides — all the qualities that made him valuable as a tactician now repurposed for diplomacy. He gets to be the man of peace he always wanted to be.

This is the series' most pointed irony, and I don't think it's accidental. The [extra pages Isayama added to the volume release](https://www.postapocalypticmedia.com/attack-on-titan-manga-chapter-139-review-final/) show Paradis Island, generations later, bombed into rubble during another war. The cycle continues. Armin's diplomacy, however sincere, however successful in the short term, does not end anything permanently. Nothing does.

![Armin and Eren in their early Survey Corps days](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/armin-arlert-and-eren-yeager-leap-to-action-in-odm-gear-in-front-of-attack-on-titan-s-shiganshina.jpg)

What [Attack on Titan](https://myanimelist.net/anime/16498/Shingeki_no_Kyojin) is actually saying about idealism — not about fascism, not about cycles of violence in the abstract, but about the *specific cost of being the idealist* — comes through most clearly in Armin. He is the character who thinks his way through every impossible situation and finds a path. Finding the path doesn't make you innocent of where the path leads.

A [fan essay on Armin's martyr complex](https://ackermom.tumblr.com/post/659357541664505857/an-analysis-of-armins-martyr-complex) argues that he ends the series as someone who commits arson and then gets called a hero for helping put the fire out — that his arc concludes in sophisticated self-deception rather than genuine moral reckoning. It is a sharp reading, and it is wrong about one thing: Armin is not deceiving himself. He knows what he did. The Liberio scene makes that explicit — he stands in the ashes and thinks about Bertholdt and does not comfort himself. The problem in his arc is not self-deception; it is that knowing does not change anything. The port is still burned. The Rumbling still happened. He is still alive because of it. You can be fully conscious of your complicity and still have nowhere to put that knowledge.

Intelligence, [in the world of Attack on Titan](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-eren-armin-conversation/), is not a moral shield. It's a map to the place where the worst choices are clearest. Armin saw the ocean. He saw what was on the other side of it. He saw everything, and he burned the port anyway, and he couldn't let himself hear what the burning sounded like. That's the earless Titan. That's the whole arc.

The boy who gave everyone the dream didn't betray it. He just learned what it cost, up close, in the most intimate way possible — by being the one who paid.
