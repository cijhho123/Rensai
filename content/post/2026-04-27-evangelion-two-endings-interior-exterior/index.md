Every conversation about Neon Genesis Evangelion eventually reaches the same impasse: was the TV ending (*Episodes 25-26*) an artistic choice or a production failure? Was *The End of Evangelion* the "real" ending or a capitulation to fan pressure? These are the wrong questions. Both endings are true simultaneously, neither cancels the other, and the "budget cuts" explanation that has governed this debate for thirty years gets the actual story wrong.

![Neon Genesis Evangelion — the series Anno described as "burning my feelings into film"](https://s4.anilist.co/file/anilistcdn/media/anime/cover/medium/bx30-AI1zr74Dh4ye.jpg)

## The Myth and What Actually Happened

The received narrative: Gainax ran out of money. They couldn't afford animation. They cobbled together Episodes 25 and 26 from recycled footage, rough sketches, and inner monologue — the "congratulations" sequence at the end was cheap filler passing as art. End of Evangelion was then made as the real ending, the one the fans deserved.

Almost every part of this is wrong.

Gainax's serious financial trouble came [years after Evangelion](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/3389/The-production-of-episodes-25-and-26/) finished airing, primarily from unpaid taxes on the show's profits. The immediate production problem was scheduling collapse and creative indecision, not bankruptcy. [Toshio Okada](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion), Gainax's co-founder, stated the real issue clearly: it "was not only a problem of schedule or budget," because Anno "couldn't decide the ending until the time came." At the moment of voice recording for Episode 25, the script for the final episode didn't exist yet. The remaining animation was completed in three days.

But crucially: this is not the same as "the ending was a mistake." Assistant director Kazuya Tsurumaki described the deliberate choice embedded in that crisis: when the production system was "completely falling apart," some staff wanted to stop entirely. [Tsurumaki disagreed](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff): "My opinion was, 'Why don't we show them the entire process including our breakdown. You know — make it a work that shows everything including our inability to create a satisfactory product.'"

That statement, from inside the production, is more illuminating than any external critical framework. The decision to let the breakdown show — in the rough drawings, the recycled footage, the sudden pivot to pure interiority — was itself a formal position. And Anno later confirmed it. [He stated](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/3389/The-production-of-episodes-25-and-26/): "The episodes 25 and 26 that aired on TV were a direct reflection of how I was feeling at that point in time, so I'm happy with them. I don't regret it."

## What Episodes 25 and 26 Actually Do

Strip away the circumstances and Episodes 25/26 are one of the most formally audacious finales in television anime history. The show abandons external events entirely. No Angels. No Eva units. No NERV. The Human Instrumentality Process that has been the series' apocalyptic endpoint for 24 episodes turns out to occur entirely inside Shinji's consciousness — and what we get is a sustained psychological interrogation of every anxiety that has driven the series.

The structure is [deliberate therapy](https://culturedvultures.com/evangelion-depression-hideaki-anno-lars-von-trier-anime/). The [EvaGeeks Commentary Project](https://wiki.evageeks.org/FGC:Episode_25_Scene_01) notes that Episode 25 opens by framing its central question in French — "raison d'être," the reason one is allowed to exist — and then spends its entire runtime making Shinji prove he has one. Characters present alternative versions of himself: Shinji who is confident, Shinji who is aggressive, Shinji who doesn't need other people. The episodes systematically dismantle each version until what remains is the undefended question: does he have a right to be here at all?

What Anno was doing, as he himself acknowledged discovering mid-production, was working through his own psychological architecture in public. He had spent four years after *Nadia: The Secret of Blue Water* in what he described as simply "not dying" — [a broken man who ran away from everything](https://www.evamonkey.com/writings/panchan-life-is-an-unfamiliar-ceiling-a-look-at-director-hideaki-anno.php) for four years, unable to create. By the time he began Evangelion, he had been struggling with depression his entire adult life but lacked clinical language to name it. He told [ComicBook.com](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-interview-mental-health/): "I tried to include everything of myself in Neon Genesis Evangelion — myself, a broken man who could do nothing for four years."

The celebrated rough-sketch sequence in Episode 26 — the incomplete drawings, the live-action footage of empty chairs, the sudden drop in visual register — functions as a formal argument. Shinji's internal world is not rendered with polish because polish would suggest his psychological state is knowable, stable, resolved. The incompleteness *is* the content. When the episode arrives at the "congratulations" sequence — every character from the series applauding Shinji for choosing to continue existing as an individual — the validation is Anno's own. He described [in the Wong Amos interview](https://www.evamonkey.com/writings/wong-amos-interview-with-hideaki-anno-director-of-neon-genesis-evangelion.php): "Shinji does reflect my character, both the conscious and unconscious parts."

## The Fan Response and What It Did

While the series was airing, Anno was reading [online message boards](https://kotaku.com/online-hate-took-evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-to-a-d-1846533756) where people discussed methods of killing him. He described the effect directly: "I stopped caring about everything — like, making anime. I'd had enough. One time, I thought about jumping in front of a car. Another time, I thought about jumping from the company's roof." When asked what stopped him, he said only that he feared physical pain.

After Episodes 25/26 aired, [Gainax's offices were vandalized](https://wiki.evageeks.org/End_of_Evangelion_Death_Threats). Fans who wanted action-sequence resolution, a conventional climax, a "real" ending, directed their anger physically at the people who had made it.

This is the context in which *The End of Evangelion* was made. And this context matters enormously for understanding what the film actually is.

## What End of Evangelion Actually Is

*The End of Evangelion* was [not made in response to fan demands](https://wiki.evageeks.org/The_End_of_Evangelion). The staff were explicit: this was "a way for the team to give the series a satisfactory ending for themselves, as the working conditions by the end of the series had become so grueling that they gave up trying to save the finale." They didn't owe the audience an action ending. They wanted to make something complete for their own purposes.

What they made contains both: the graphic external version of Third Impact that Episodes 25/26 implied but didn't show, AND the psychological culmination where Shinji's choice determines whether Instrumentality succeeds. Rei appears as a goddess. Asuka and Shinji are the last two people on a beach strewn with debris. He chokes her. She calls him "disgusting." He cries.

The film is [explicitly called a "remake" by the staff](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:List_of_Common_Misconceptions) — not a supplement, not a director's cut, but a parallel version of the same climactic moment. What it does differently from the TV ending is render the external consequences of the internal event. Episodes 25/26 show Shinji accepting himself inside the Instrumentality process. EoE shows what that acceptance costs, and what it requires him to reject.

The famous final scene — Shinji strangling Asuka, then stopping — is his choice in physical space. Understanding why requires the series' own logic: AT Fields are not just supernatural shields; they are [the psychological boundaries between individuals](https://wiki.evageeks.org/A.T._Field), the barriers that make both love and violence possible. Instrumentality dissolves AT Fields — the price of union is the end of the individual self. Shinji's act of choking Asuka is the reverse: to hurt someone, you have to exist as a separate entity from them, and they have to exist separately from you. The fact that she responds — "disgusting" — confirms they have remained two distinct people. She is not absorbed into him. Her contempt is the evidence of her separateness, and his decision to stop is the decision to let her remain that way. The words that close the TV ending ("I feel I can be here") become in EoE a choice made against apocalypse with blood on his hands.

## The Same Moment, Two Registers

The mistake in the critical discourse around these endings is treating them as competing answers to the same question. They're not. They're answers to different questions about the same moment.

Episodes 25/26 ask: *Can Shinji accept himself?* The answer is yes, and the evidence is interior, psychological, almost wordless. The congratulations sequence is the internal breakthrough validated.

End of Evangelion asks: *What does that acceptance require?* The answer is the rejection of Instrumentality — the choice to be separate, individual, alone-but-real — and the evidence is written in blood across a destroyed world. [Anno encoded his own rage](https://kotaku.com/online-hate-took-evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-to-a-d-1846533756) at the fan violence into the film's apocalyptic imagery. The mass extinction sequence is Anno working something out. The beach at the end is what remains after he has.

Both versions acknowledge the same central thesis: that choosing to exist as an individual, with all the pain and rejection that entails, is worth it. One shows Shinji arriving at that realization. The other shows him living it.

[Anno himself stated](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-interview-mental-health/) about the series as a whole: "I tried to include everything of myself in Neon Genesis Evangelion... It is a production where my only thought was to burn my feelings into film." The two endings are both instances of that burning. Episodes 25/26 are what it looked like when the production collapsed and he let the collapse speak. End of Evangelion is what it looked like when he rebuilt enough to answer the violence with more violence, and at the end found something quieter.

The question is not which ending is real. Both are real. The question is which part of the answer you need.
