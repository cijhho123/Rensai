Every time a major idol group changes labels, Western observers treat it like it matters: press releases are written, analysts weigh in, fans speculate about creative control. When [AKB48 left King Records for Universal Music Japan in 2023](https://neotokyo2099.com/2023/02/28/after-15-years-with-king-records-akb48-has-moved-to-universal-music/), after fifteen years with the same label, it generated significant coverage. But if you're watching Japanese idol culture specifically for label moves, you're watching the wrong thing. The label was never really in charge.

Understanding the history of idol labels in Japan requires first understanding that the word "label" means something different here than it does in Western pop. In the Western music industry, the label is the power center: it controls recording budgets, release strategy, promotion, and often owns the masters. Artists negotiate against labels for their careers. The label is the antagonist in a thousand music business cautionary tales.

[In Japan's idol industry, the talent agency (jimusho) is the power center](https://jmpc-utokyo.com/keyword/talent-agencies/). The label is the distribution infrastructure. This is not a subtle distinction. It reshapes who holds leverage at every stage of an idol's career, who profits from their work, and — critically — who can destroy them.

## The 1970s: When Labels Had More Say

The Japanese idol as a concept emerged in the early 1970s through television audition shows. [Momoe Yamaguchi, Candies, and Pink Lady were recruited through programs like Star Tanjo!](https://japaniche.org/articles/the-history-of-japanese-idols-by-decade), and their label relationships looked somewhat more like the Western model — individual stars managed by agencies but with label deals that shaped their sound and release cadence. [Seiko Matsuda's 1980 debut came through CBS/Sony Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seiko_Matsuda), a major international label, after years of auditions and deliberation. In this era, a major label deal was genuinely significant, a gatekeeping function that controlled access to the production and distribution infrastructure idols needed.

But the seeds of the inversion were already present. [Johnny Kitagawa founded Johnny & Associates in 1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_&_Associates) and immediately built a vertically integrated model: his company controlled scouting, training, performance, and increasingly the revenue from all of it. Idols under Johnny's weren't primarily label assets. They were agency assets, with the label serving as a necessary partner rather than a controlling one.

## The 1980s: Onyanko Club and the Joint-Label Compromise

The 1980s idol boom — the period [the era's observers called the "Golden Age of Idols"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_idol) — tested both models. Television drove the industry. [Onyanko Club, formed in 1985 from a Fuji Television audition contest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onyanko_Club), became the decade's most significant collective and pioneered the graduation system that AKB48 would later scale to unprecedented size.

Their label arrangement was telling: [Kirigirisu Records, the label created specifically for Onyanko Club, was a joint venture among Canyon Records, For Life Records, CBS/Sony, and EPIC/Sony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onyanko_Club). Four labels, one group. This was not conventional. It was a power-sharing arrangement designed to manage the commercial scale of a phenomenon that no single label wanted to let a competitor own exclusively. The group's producer — not the label — was the real center of power, and the multi-label structure reflected that.

That producer was [Yasushi Akimoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yasushi_Akimoto) — who would go on to create AKB48 two decades later, explicitly using Onyanko Club as his blueprint. [As one fan history documents, Akimoto used "the blueprints of Onyanko Club to form... the super successful group of the new millennium"](https://aisasami.wordpress.com/2015/04/12/the-club-that-started-it-all/) — including the amateur-idol premise, the rotating membership, and the concept of graduated familiarity between fans and performers. The label structure was part of what he carried forward: AKB48 would be produced independently and distributed through a label deal, exactly as Onyanko Club had been.

## The Johnny's Monopoly: When Labels Became Irrelevant to the Equation

Through the 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s, [Johnny Kitagawa progressively established a monopoly over male idol culture in Japan](https://www.japansubculture.com/the-pioneer-and-the-serial-predator-of-the-japanese-idol-industry-johnny-kitagawas-legacy-of-exploitation-and-control/) that made the label question almost moot for his artists. His mechanism was media leverage: if networks and magazines weren't cooperative, they'd be blacklisted from working with his idols. Since his idols dominated television ratings, he had leverage over every media outlet in Japan. [As Variety documented in 2019](https://variety.com/2019/film/asia/johnny-kitagawa-power-abuse-japanese-media-omerta-1203271575/), this gave him effective veto power over critical coverage of himself and his company — a power that suppressed reporting on his serial sexual abuse of young male performers for decades. [Nippon.com's investigation confirmed that television networks, which should have been news organizations, "paid no attention to Kitagawa's sexual abuse in their programming, including news shows"](https://www.nippon.com/en/in-depth/d00945/), with flagship networks silent for years after initial allegations.

From a label perspective, the consequence was that Johnny's artists operated on his terms regardless of which label they were nominally signed to. [Until his death in 2019, Johnny's blocked its artists from streaming platforms and social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_&_Associates), not because the labels wanted this — streaming revenue was clearly valuable — but because Kitagawa's control over the physical distribution ecosystem and media access made labels cooperate with his preferences. The label was a captive partner. In 2019, the [Fair Trade Commission investigated Johnny & Associates for potentially violating the Anti-Monopoly Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_&_Associates) — specifically for threatening television companies that aired competing male idol groups.

[J Storm, the in-house label created for Arashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J_Storm), was the natural endpoint of this logic: why share revenue with an external label when you control enough of the industry to handle distribution yourself?

![AKB48, the group that inherited the Onyanko Club template and built it to industrial scale](https://i2.wp.com/www.kawaiikakkoiisugoi.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/AKB48lineup.jpg)

## AKB48 and the King Records Model

[AKB48's fifteen-year relationship with King Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AKB48) is the most instructive case study in what idol label relationships actually look like at scale. Yasushi Akimoto — AKB48's creator and primary architect — operated AKS (the management company) with near-total control over the group's creative direction, member selection, promotional strategy, and the senbatsu election system that was the group's defining commercial mechanism. [King Records provided You Be Cool, the sub-label under which releases were distributed](https://neotokyo2099.com/2023/02/28/after-15-years-with-king-records-akb48-has-moved-to-universal-music/), and presumably financing and production infrastructure.

But the strategic decisions were Akimoto's. The election system, the handshake events, the theater model, the sister groups — none of these were label innovations. King Records handled the physical distribution of records that were breaking first-week sales records annually. The relationship was stable for fifteen years precisely because King Records understood its role: the label was the infrastructure, not the idea.

[AKB48's 2023 transfer to Universal Music Japan's EMI Records](https://neotokyo2099.com/2023/02/28/after-15-years-with-king-records-akb48-has-moved-to-universal-music/) wasn't a creative pivot. It was a logistical one, consolidating AKB48 under the same distributor as its sister groups (HKT48, NGT48, NMB48), which had already moved to Universal. The label change changed almost nothing about how the group actually operates.

## The Chika Idol Endpoint: No Label at All

The logical conclusion of the agency-first model is the [chika (underground) idol](https://rawjpn.com/articles/idol250724) scene, which barely involves labels at all. Underground idols perform in small basement live houses, self-release CDs, and generate revenue primarily through [cheki (instant photo) sales and direct fan interaction](https://rawjpn.com/articles/idol250724) rather than through the recorded music distribution system that labels exist to service.

[The economics of chika idol management are brutal](https://www.nippon.com/en/japan-topics/c06003/the-idol-management-business-no-room-for-small-fry.html) — one Nippon.com report documented a manager running twenty performances a month and still ending the year ¥3 million in the hole. [Japan Today has documented widespread exploitation in this sector](https://japantoday.com/category/entertainment/focus-exploitation-overwork-a-scourge-in-seedy-world-of-chika-idols): overwork, unpaid wages, ambiguous contracts that bind performers without compensating them. But the model is philosophically coherent if you accept the agency-first premise: if the label was always junior to the agency, and the agency can support an idol career through direct fan economy, why involve a label at all?

The rise of [chika idols as an industrial category](https://rawjpn.com/articles/idol250724) in the 2000s and 2010s didn't just expand the idol market. It exposed the architecture. The major idol industry had always been agency-first; the underground made this explicit by removing the label from the picture entirely. [By the mid-2010s, over 3,000 female idol groups were active in Japan with roughly 10,000 performers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_idol) — the vast majority of them without label deals, living on cheki sales and live house bookings.

## The Johnny's Collapse and What Comes After

[The 2023 rebranding of Johnny & Associates into Smile-Up and Starto Entertainment](https://variety.com/2023/biz/news/johnny-and-associates-renamed-astarto-entertainment-1235829272/), following the BBC documentary on Johnny Kitagawa's decades of sexual abuse, represents the most significant structural rupture in the history of the male idol industry. The scale of what collapsed — the media monopoly, the streaming blockade, the image control so total that [no idol thumbnails existed on the company's own website until 2011](https://cooljapan.pressbooks.com/chapter/johnny-and-associates-japanese-pop-idol-producer-faces-a-changing-world/) — reveals in retrospect how much of the industry had been built around a single agency's power, not around any label's.

The labels that had worked with Johnny's over the decades were never the story. They were the pipes. The agency was the water pressure. When the agency finally broke, labels were not the ones scrambling to adapt. It was broadcasters, management companies, and the performers themselves who had to figure out what came next.

Idol label history is real. It tells you where distribution went, who collected physical sales revenue, which companies had financing relationships. But if you want to understand the power — who built the idols, who owned them, and who could destroy them — you need to look at the agencies. They were always the center. The labels just pressed the records.
