In [Re:Zero Season 3 Episode 14](https://gamerant.com/rezero-season-3-episode-14-a-saint-in-name/), Reinhard van Astrea draws his Dragon Sword and kills his grandmother.

She is already dead — technically. Theresia van Astrea, the previous Sword Saint, has been reanimated by the Witch Cult's Archbishops as part of an assault on the Water Gate City of Priestella. What stands before Reinhard is a corpse wearing her face, moving with her skill, carrying none of her love. His grandfather Wilhelm tried to handle it himself. The old swordsman's self-imposed mission — the one he chased for years after the Great Expedition cost him Theresia — was to be the one to release her. He failed. Heinkel's voice, Reinhard's own cowardly father, cut through Wilhelm's focus at the wrong moment. Theresia's blade found his leg.

And then Reinhard stepped in. He cut her down. When Heinkel collapsed in grief and rage, blaming him — the familiar accusation Reinhard has been hearing since he was five years old — Reinhard told him, without visible emotion, that what he had killed was a reanimated corpse. Not his grandmother. The words were accurate. They were also exactly the wrong thing to say.

That moment is the most honest portrait of Reinhard van Astrea in the series. Not the stunning fight against Regulus. Not the [effortless obliteration of threats](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Reinhard_van_Astrea) that other characters spend entire arcs building toward. The most telling frame is the one where he does everything right and everyone around him still walks away hating him a little more than before.

## The Hero Who Was Never the Protagonist

Re:Zero's foundational joke — or premise, depending on how charitable you're feeling — is that Natsuki Subaru is [not the hero the isekai template promised him](https://gamerant.com/re-zero-reasons-subaru-great-isekai-protagonist/). He arrives in another world with no magic, no combat ability, no cheat power. He is arrogant, self-destructive, and categorically unequipped. The series spends its first arc establishing exactly how badly the conventional isekai protagonist lens distorts everything he encounters.

And then it introduces Reinhard.

Reinhard is what Subaru thought he was going to be. He is physically perfect — tall, red-haired, the [archetypal knight design](https://otakumode.com/otapedia/anime/re_zero/rezero_reinhard_van_astrea) made flesh. He is morally impeccable: courteous, humble, and possessed of a sense of justice that operates without internal conflict. He has more Divine Protections than any other person alive; [Nagatsuki himself has stated in author commentary](https://re-zero.com/news/2517/) that no stronger character will appear in the series. He cannot permanently die — the Divine Protection of the Phoenix grants resurrection, and if that runs out there is a Phoenix Next, and then a Phoenix Next Next. The world, in some fundamental sense, will not let Reinhard van Astrea be killed.

This is what Subaru's cultural framework — shaped entirely by isekai conventions — said the chosen hero looked like. Reinhard has the title, the power, the appearance, the blessing. And [Tappei Nagatsuki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tappei_Nagatsuki) built Re:Zero around the deliberate exclusion of Reinhard from the role of protagonist.

The exclusion is not incidental. It is the point.

## An Inheritance Nobody Asked For

The [Divine Protection of the Sword Saint](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Divine_Protection) does not pass from parent to child. It is not willed or bequeathed. The Sword God decides, according to criteria no one fully understands, which member of the Astrea bloodline is most suited. It transferred from Theresia's uncle to her when she was twelve. It transferred from Theresia to Reinhard when he was five.

The second transfer happened during the Great Expedition against the White Whale. Theresia was in the field because her son Heinkel — her own son — had been too frightened to participate. She went in his place. The transfer came in the middle of the battle, stripping her of the extraordinary power she had built her identity around, leaving her exposed in a fight calibrated for someone with her former abilities. [She did not survive](https://animehunch.com/rezero-theresia-van-astrea-death-wilhelm-remember/).

Reinhard's desire to become the Sword Saint — to be the hero, to be worthy — may have been what summoned the transfer. He was five. He wanted to be good. The mechanism responded to that want, and the consequence was his grandmother's death.

He did not cause her death. That much is important to be precise about: Pandora's intervention, Heinkel's cowardice, the nature of the White Whale itself, these were all causal factors well outside a child's control. But [Reinhard internalized the blame anyway](https://animehunch.com/wilhelm-reinhard-heinkel-understanding-the-astrea-family-dynamics-in-rezero/), and the people who raised him gave him no reason to set it down. Wilhelm, hollowed out by grief, saw Reinhard as a living reminder of what he lost. Heinkel, unable to look at his own failure, redirected his self-loathing onto the son who had inadvertently exposed it.

The strongest person in the world grew up being told his existence was the reason for the family's tragedy.

## What Suppression Looks Like When It's Adaptive

Reinhard did not break. He also did not emerge unscathed. What he developed instead was a capacity for emotional compartmentalization so thorough that it reads, in moments of crisis, as coldness.

The mechanism is legible once you examine the inputs. Wilhelm's grief expressed itself as stoicism, which became the model Reinhard absorbed — not because anyone formally taught him to suppress his feelings, but because suppression was what survival looked like in the Astrea household. [Reinhard grew up being blamed for a tragedy he had no control over](https://animehunch.com/wilhelm-reinhard-heinkel-understanding-the-astrea-family-dynamics-in-rezero/), and if the world consistently responds to your displays of feeling with renewed accusation, you learn not to display feelings. That is not trauma in the dramatic cinematic sense. It is something quieter and more stubborn: a person who learned that the expression of emotion was a liability and never found reason to unlearn it.

This is not the same thing as not having them.

The Season 3 scene cuts sharper when you understand it through this lens. When Reinhard tells Heinkel that what he killed was a reanimated corpse, he is not being dismissive of Heinkel's grief. He is, from inside his own framework, trying to offer the only comfort he knows how to provide: factual accuracy that separates the horror of what just happened from the deeper truth that Theresia herself is not subject to the horror. He gets this wrong. He gets it wrong because his training in how to manage overwhelming emotional situations is not "speak to what the other person needs" but "be precise and controlled." The error is not cruelty. It is a person doing their best with tools that were never adequate for the moment.

## The Narrative Instrument

Reinhard's [appearances in the main arcs](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Reinhard_van_Astrea) follow a consistent structure: he arrives, deploys overwhelming force, and resolves or substantially advances the crisis. In Arc 1, he kills Elsa Granhiert and retrieves Felt, then exits the story. In Arc 5 (Season 3), he participates in the assault on Priestella, fights Regulus Corneas after the Archbishop tries to exploit a duel structure that Reinhard honors because it is his code, is temporarily killed, and resurrects via Phoenix. [Despite the desperate cries of his grandfather not to kill "my Theresia," the Sword Saint attacked](https://animerants.net/2025/03/17/re-zero-s3-episodes-63-and-64-one-hit-and-one-miss/). The mission came first. It always does.

He has no subplot he is struggling to complete. He has no relationship that is failing him in ways he needs to fix. Even the family tragedy, which is clearly the emotional core of his character, is not something Reinhard is actively trying to resolve — he is largely passive in relation to it, enduring rather than working through.

Subaru, by contrast, [cannot stop working through things](https://screenrant.com/re-zero-subaru-most-frustrating-isekai-protagonist-season-3/). Return by Death means every failure is an opportunity to try something different. He accumulates understanding through suffering, and that accumulation is the story. His power is awful and it makes him the only person who can solve the problems the series puts in front of him, but solving them requires becoming someone different than who he started as. Subaru changes. Reinhard is, as far as the series allows us to see, largely unchanged by the events he participates in.

This is not a criticism of Nagatsuki's construction. It is the argument.

A person who cannot fail cannot be changed by failure. Reinhard's divine protections do not simply make him powerful — they functionally remove the mechanism by which characters in this series develop. [Return by Death is a power built from consequence](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2025/12/rezero-starting-life-in-another-world-season-3/); Reinhard's arsenal is built precisely to preclude consequence. The series cannot be told from his perspective because the genre requires a protagonist who can be genuinely threatened, and Reinhard cannot be.

## The One Person Who Treats Him Like a Person

There is one exception to Reinhard's consistent experience of being perceived as an instrument or a burden: Felt.

Felt has no investment in Reinhard's reputation. She was a thief from the slums of Lugunica when he found her, and she has spent the time since being dragged into [a Royal Selection she did not ask for](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Royal_Selection) and does not want to win. She has no reason to treat Reinhard as the Sword Saint or as a walking symbol of the Astrea family's tragedy. She treats him as a person, which means she argues with him and [refuses to treat his legendary status](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Felt/Relationships) as a reason to defer to him.

This is the closest thing Reinhard has in the series to being seen as a person rather than a force. The distinction matters. Everyone else around Reinhard relates to his power first — they depend on it, resent it, or are awed by it, and the person behind it is secondary at best. [Felt's relationship with Reinhard](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Reinhard_van_Astrea) is the one case in the series where someone genuinely starts from him as a person and relates to his power as an incidental fact rather than the defining one.

That the person best equipped to see past Reinhard's power is someone with no social investment in it is not an accident. Power structures perception. Every character who has something to gain or lose from the Sword Saint's status relates to Reinhard through that lens first. Felt's irreverence is not a personality quirk — it is what happens when someone has nothing at stake in the hierarchy that built Reinhard into a symbol.

## Being Chosen Is Not the Same as Being Free

There is a reading of Reinhard that is purely functional: he is the narrative mechanism by which the series maintains stakes for Subaru without making the world unconquerable. If Reinhard can be in a scene, the scene resolves. Nagatsuki keeps him offscreen as often as possible precisely because his presence collapses dramatic tension.

But this functional reading doesn't fully account for why the series spends as much time as it does on the Astrea family dynamics, on Reinhard's guilt over Theresia, on the specificity of his inability to comfort his family even when he does exactly what was needed. There is something being argued about what omnipotence costs, beyond its narrative inconvenience.

Re:Zero is, at its core, a series about the relationship between power and connection. Subaru's power is total isolation — [he cannot share Return by Death with anyone](https://animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2016-09-21/which-re-zero-girl-should-win-the-election/.106462), carries every death alone, cannot be known in the way he most needs to be known. Reinhard's power is total adequacy — he can handle everything, and as a result everyone around him either depends on that handling, resents it, or cannot conceive of him as someone who might need something from them.

Both conditions produce the same result. The most powerless protagonist in isekai and the most powerful character in his world arrive at the same fundamental isolation through opposite paths. Subaru cannot be helped because no one knows what he carries. Reinhard cannot be helped because no one can conceive of him as needing help.

![Re:Zero Season 3](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1706/144725l.jpg)

Nagatsuki has said he [originally planned Re:Zero for around nine or ten arcs](https://x.com/LoremIpsumVerb/status/1999536118274969612), with each Royal Selection candidate receiving an arc of focus. Whether Reinhard's full arc ever arrives, whether there is a version of events in which the Sword Saint gets to experience failure on his own terms and find out what he is without the guarantee of victory — that question hangs over the series the way it hangs over Reinhard himself.

He killed his grandmother's corpse with precision and restraint. He said the right true thing to the wrong person in the wrong moment. He walked away, because there was nothing left to do and no power in his possession to make it better.

That is what it looks like when the chosen one wins.
