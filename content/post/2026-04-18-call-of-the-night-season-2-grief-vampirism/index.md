[Lost in Anime's series review](https://lostinanime.com/2025/09/yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night-season-2-12-end-and-series-review/) called Season 1 "mainly about psychology" and Season 2 "mainly about plot." That's accurate as far as it goes, but it explains why someone who loved Season 1 might find Season 2 a step down without explaining why someone else might give it [a 10/10 and call it "criminally underrated."](https://theglobalraider.substack.com/p/call-of-the-night-season-2-a-review) The gap between those two readings is real. Understanding it requires asking what the show was actually doing in its second season — not what it was doing *instead* of Season 1, but what Season 1's dreamy nocturnal premise was always set up to reveal.

The answer: vampirism in this world is what happens to people whose grief, longing, or desire has nowhere to go. Season 2 is an excavation of that thesis, conducted through the backstories of every vampire introduced in its twelve episodes. The Kou-Nazuna romance is the frame. The ensemble tragedy is the content. And the audience split — [9.5/10 from But Why Tho](https://butwhytho.net/2025/09/call-of-the-night-season-2-review/), ["disappointing"](https://lostinanime.com/2025/09/yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night-season-2-12-end-and-series-review/) from Lost in Anime, ["more depth, more danger, less Kou"](https://nerdynook.co.uk/call-of-the-night-season-2-review/) from Nerdy Nook — maps cleanly onto which premise viewers came for.

## What Season 1 Set Up

Kou Yamori leaves school and starts walking at night because he cannot sleep — but more precisely because he cannot feel. His peer relationships are performative, his emotions flattened, his connection to the daytime world of homework and social obligations effectively severed. He meets Nazuna by accident, and the deal she offers (let me drink your blood, fall in love with me, become a vampire) functions as a specific proposal: here is a way to want something badly enough to transform for it.

Season 1 is about whether Kou can learn to feel. It is also, deliberately, [slow to the point of formlessness](https://beneaththetangles.com/2025/07/04/first-impression-call-of-the-night-season-2/) — two people wandering Tokyo at night, talking, not quite connecting, not quite failing to connect. The aesthetic (synthwave neon, purple skies, empty convenience stores at 2am) is the show's actual argument: the nighttime is a space where normal social metrics don't apply, and that space might be the only place Kou can figure out what he actually wants.

What Season 1 does not tell you is *why* anyone becomes a vampire. It presents Nazuna's vampirism as given. It presents the vampire world as a backdrop. It doesn't ask what made these people opt out of daylight in the first place.

Season 2 asks that question and answers it the same way every time.

## The Grief Anatomy

[Anko Uguisu](https://villains.fandom.com/wiki/Anko_Uguisu) — born Kyouko Mejiro, now a vampire who hunts vampires — is Season 2's breakout character. [Her origin episode](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/call-night-season-2-episode-8-nazuna-makes-offer-tragedy-strikes-meijiro-family-anko-uguisu-born) reveals the full collapse: her father was turned vampire and killed her mother; teenage Kyouko killed him in self-defense. She later became a vampire herself, took on the name Anko, and has spent every year since trying to destroy the thing she is. The death-wish disguised as crusade, the name changed to mark the split — even her identity is a grief response, a severing of who she was from who she couldn't stay being. Her plan to expose vampires publicly would also expose and kill her. [Miyuki Sawashiro's performance in Episode 8](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/call-night-season-2-episode-8-nazuna-makes-offer-tragedy-strikes-meijiro-family-anko-uguisu-born) was called the season's defining performance across nearly every review that covered it — which makes sense: the episode is essentially a forty-minute grief monologue delivered through action.

Kabura Honda is a vampire in love with a dead woman. [As the DoubleSama review reconstructs it](https://doublesama.com/call-of-the-night-season-2/): Haru Nanakusa (Nazuna's mother) was human, had a child with a vampire, asked Kabura to raise the daughter. Haru died. Kabura has been raising Nazuna — who looks exactly like her mother — ever since, with the tenderness of grief that has found its only permitted object. She cannot move forward from an unrequited love that completed itself in death, so she tends its offspring instead. Her vampirism is not explained as a condition; it functions as a description of precisely this state: someone whose love has nowhere to go and so has been redirected indefinitely sideways.

The pattern is consistent. [As CountZeroOr's review identified](https://countzeroor.com/2025/10/05/anime-review-call-of-the-night-season-2/), the season argues that "our humanity and our connections to each other can sometimes make us monstrous, but also how those connections can keep the monstrous parts of ourselves away." Vampirism in this world is not predation or power fantasy — it is what forms when human longing reaches a threshold and the normal structures for processing it fail.

## Kou as the Negative Space

This reframes Kou's premise in a way Season 1 didn't make explicit. Kou started the series unable to feel — emotionally flat, disconnected, drawn to the night because the night has no demands. Every vampire he meets in Season 2 is the opposite: someone who feels too much, so intensely that normal human structures couldn't hold it. Kou is the absence; the vampires are the excess. His journey toward feeling enough to fall in love and complete the transformation is a journey toward the condition that made them all undead in the first place.

When [Kou becomes a half-vampire in Episode 11](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori) — triggered while preventing Anko's suicide attempt, his transformation making him briefly "bloodthirsty and unhinged" — [some reviewers called it a genre betrayal](https://pinnedupink.com/blogs/the-cel-block-anime-reviews-analysis-for-the-gen-x-fan/call-of-the-night-season-2-review-story-characters-and-what-s-next), a shift too far from the show's romance register. That reaction was missing this context. His transformation isn't a betrayal of the romance premise. It's the romance premise completing its first step: Kou has now felt something strongly enough to change. The question Season 3 would inherit is whether what he feels is love specifically, or just the capacity for feeling that he'd been missing.

[Nazuna's English voice actress Natalie Rial](https://thenaturalaristocrat.com/2025/09/04/call-of-the-night-season-2-english-dub-voice-cast-interview-nyc-video/) read the relationship more clearly than most critics: Nazuna has already crossed into love well before Kou has consciously reciprocated, and her emotional walls are a defense against the implications of that fact. The vampire mechanic literalizes it — Nazuna cannot drink the blood of someone she loves without risking her life. The more she loves Kou, the more dangerous completing the transformation becomes. The mechanism that should fulfill their deal is the one that forecloses it as her feelings deepen. Vampirism as the shape of unresolvable longing.

## The Finale as Authorial Statement

[The manga completed at Chapter 200 in January 2024](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-11-15/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-200th-chapter/.204474). Season 2 ends at Chapter 97, with 103 chapters unadapted. Kotoyama — the manga's creator — [wrote Episode 12 himself](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night-season-2/episode-12/.229086), producing a mostly anime-original closing episode that functions as a thesis statement rather than a cliffhanger into the next arc.

The episode concludes with Kou recognizing that everyone — vampires, insomniacs, queer characters, social misfits — is weird. Normalcy is not a real category; the night is full of people for whom daytime's social architecture never fit. [The AniDB score of 8.02 from 488 votes](https://anidb.net/anime/18531) and a drop rate of just 0.4% suggest the fanbase that stayed found something meaningful in it.

What Kotoyama chose to end on, rather than adapting forward, is the show's real thesis: the night belongs to people the day couldn't hold. Grief is one way to become such a person. Kou's flatness is another. The vampire world isn't an escape from human emotion — it's what human emotion looks like when it has no exit.

Season 2 spent twelve episodes making that argument through backstories the show's romance framing wouldn't initially suggest were the point. [The split audience](https://doublesama.com/call-of-the-night-season-2/) — those who wanted the vibe, those who wanted the characters — reflects genuine aesthetic preferences rather than quality disagreement. Both seasons delivered on something real. Season 2 just delivered on the something that Season 1's premise was always implying, and not everyone came to Call of the Night to hear it said out loud.

The [manga's ending](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained) leaves Kou and Nazuna separated for years, then reuniting — her love still not fully expressed, his still in process. The conclusion Kotoyama actually wrote for them is itself grief wearing the shape of a romance. Season 2 was the announcement that this was the genre the show was in all along.
