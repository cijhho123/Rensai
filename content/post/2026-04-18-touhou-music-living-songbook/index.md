On October 27, 2009, an anonymous animator known as Anira uploaded a music video to Nico Nico Douga. The video was two minutes and twenty-two seconds long, rendered in black and white silhouettes, and set to a vocal cover of a song from a Windows bullet hell game most of the world had never heard of. [Exactly one year later, it became the first video on Nico Nico Douga to reach ten million views](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bad_Apple!!). As of 2025, it has 31 million on the platform and [over 110 million on YouTube](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/bad-apple).

The song is "Bad Apple!!" The original was composed by ZUN — one person — for the 1998 PC-98 shooter *Lotus Land Story*. The version in the shadow art video is an arrangement by Alstroemeria Records from their 2007 doujin album *Lovelight*, featuring vocalist nomico. So the video that defined a generation of Nico Nico Douga is an animation of an arrangement of a game track from 1998, reworked by a fan circle, set against silhouettes drawn by an amateur animator, about a game franchise created entirely by one man.

This is Touhou Project's music culture in miniature.

![Touhou Project logo and ZUN artwork](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Touhou_logo.svg/400px-Touhou_logo.svg.png)

## The One-Man Franchise

[Jun'ya "ZUN" Ōta has single-handedly handled the music composition, programming, story, and character art for Touhou Project across more than 20 mainline games since 1997](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZUN_(video_game_developer)). No major franchise of comparable scale has a single person composing its entire musical output. [ZUN has released twelve dedicated music albums alongside the games](https://en.touhouwiki.net/wiki/ZUN%27s_Music_Collection), each containing rearranged versions of game tracks — meaning ZUN himself arranges his own music. The act of revisiting and reinterpreting is built into how Touhou's catalogue is maintained.

The games shifted from PC-98 to Windows in 2002 with *Embodiment of Scarlet Devil*, and [the Windows era crystallized the sound that would define Touhou music for the next two decades](https://www.melodigging.com/genre/touhou-music): brisk tempos (often 140-175 BPM in their electronic and club arrangements), melody-forward structures with instantly memorable hooks, dramatic key changes, and a signature timbre that the fan community eventually named after its creator.

## The ZUNpet and the Harmonic Fingerprint

The most immediately recognizable feature of ZUN's music is a sound the community calls the ZUNpet. [The instrument's formal name is "Romantic Tp," a trumpet sample from the Edirol/Roland SD-90 Studio Canvas synthesizer](https://www.shrinemaiden.org/forum/index.php?topic=18968.0) — a bright, brassy lead tone that ZUN deployed as his primary melodic voice across dozens of games. By the time the fan community had a name for it, the ZUNpet had become so inseparable from the franchise's identity that it sounds like a deliberate aesthetic choice rather than a hardware limitation. It became one by design: ZUN kept using it because it worked.

More structurally important is ZUN's harmonic vocabulary. [He writes exclusively in minor keys](https://www.shrinemaiden.org/forum/index.php?topic=13847.0), and his foundational chord progression — the VI-VII-i movement native to natural and harmonic minor — appears across the catalogue with enough consistency that it functions as a kind of signature. Songs like "Reach for the Moon, Immortal Smoke" deploy VI-VII-i-VI-VII-V. "Lunatic Eyes ~ Invisible Full Moon" runs VI-VII-i as its primary cadential motion. The harmonic minor's raised seventh degree creates a V chord with a leading tone pull toward the tonic, giving ZUN's music a constant sense of directed tension that resolves cleanly — [a feature that makes the melodies feel inevitable even on first listen](https://www.hooktheory.com/theorytab/artists/z/zun), and therefore easy to remember and return to.

This matters for arrangement culture in a specific way. Music that is melody-forward and harmonically legible provides a stable platform for genre experimentation. The arranger does not need to work around harmonic ambiguity or rhythmic opacity — the original provides a clear melodic statement that survives transplantation into trance, eurobeat, metal, jazz, orchestral, or denpa contexts without losing recognition. [Touhou arrangements span all of these styles and more](https://www.melodigging.com/genre/touhou-music). The song is still identifiably the same song in all of them.

## The Licensing Decision That Changed Everything

ZUN's compositional choices would have been interesting but contained if not for a policy decision that unlocked them. [For non-commercial doujin activities — creating and selling fan works at conventions or online — creators do not need to notify ZUN or request permission](https://en.touhouwiki.net/wiki/Touhou_Wiki:Copyrights). The copyright of derivative works belongs to the creators of those derivatives. You do not need to ask. You simply make the thing.

This is structurally different from how nearly every other game franchise handles fan creation. Most IP holders tolerate doujin culture rather than explicitly enabling it; most music IP holders actively pursue unlicensed arrangements. ZUN's policy, articulated in the early 2000s before Touhou's Windows-era expansion, said: if you are making something non-commercial for the doujin community, it is yours. The music is the material. Use it.

The fan community treated this as exactly what it was: a standing invitation. Within two years of *Embodiment of Scarlet Devil*'s 2002 release, [Touhou circles were appearing at Comiket](https://gwern.net/touhou). By Comiket 65 in 2003, the arrangement scene was established enough to have its own presence. By 2011, [Comiket's Touhou circles had grown to 2,808 participating groups at their peak](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2015-06-13/kantai-collection-comiket-circles-edge-ahead-of-touhou-project-again/.89189) — the largest the franchise's presence at the convention had ever been.

## How IOSYS Made Touhou Foreign-Readable

[IOSYS, a doujin circle formed in Sapporo in 1998](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOSYS), became the arrangement group that carried Touhou beyond Japan's doujin event circuit. In November 2006, they uploaded a promotional video for their vocal arrangement "Marisa Stole the Precious Thing" to Nico Nico Douga — a remix of Alice Margatroid's theme from *Perfect Cherry Blossom*, sung from Alice's perspective about her complicated feelings toward series protagonist Marisa Kirisame. The video became one of the first big Touhou hits on NND, [spreading to 4chan and introducing the franchise to English-speaking audiences who had never played the games](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/marisa-stole-the-precious-thing).

This pattern — a fan arrangement introducing a non-player to the franchise's musical vocabulary — has been repeated so many times that it functions as Touhou's primary distribution mechanism. The games are in Japanese, moderately obscure in their base form, and require significant investment to access. The music arrangements are on YouTube and Spotify, genre-diverse, and accessible without any prior knowledge. For much of Touhou's international audience, [the path into the franchise runs not through the shooter games but through the NND video or the Spotify playlist](https://aniplaylist.com/Touhou).

## The Scale of the Corpus

[A 2012 analysis of the Touhou music corpus by the researcher Gwern Branwen found 44,421 tracks across 4,952 albums from approximately 1,264 music circles](https://gwern.net/touhou). [Production peaked in 2009 with 10,278 tracks released in a single year](https://gwern.net/touhou). The cumulative total from the scene's beginning through 2012 exceeded 41,000 tracks — and this is an undercount, reflecting only what had been archived in the dataset used for the analysis.

For comparison: the entire catalogued output of the Beatles, Rolling Stones, and Bob Dylan combined is a few hundred tracks. The Touhou arrangement corpus by 2012 was equivalent to all of those catalogues combined, multiplied by roughly seventy, and made by fans over a decade rather than professionals over fifty years.

The infrastructure for this volume is the Touhou-specific convention system. [Reitaisai — the Hakurei Shrine Grand Festival, a Touhou-only doujin event — began in 2004 and had grown to approximately 5,000 participating circles by 2012-2013](https://en.touhouwiki.net/wiki/Reitaisai). Comiket included Touhou among its general program. Multiple smaller regional conventions added to the output. [The franchise earned a Guinness World Record as the most prolific fan-based shooter franchise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touhou_Project), but the record misnames what it is actually measuring: not just a shooter franchise, but a music licensing model that happened to be attached to one.

## Bad Apple!! and the Proof of Concept

The shadow art video's ten-million-view milestone in 2010 is worth dwelling on because of what it represents technically. The video is not composed of official Touhou assets. It is not produced by ZUN, by Alstroemeria Records, or by any entity with a formal stake in the franchise. It is an animator's fan work set to a fan arrangement of a twelve-year-old game track. [The original game, *Lotus Land Story*, is the fourth Touhou Project title, released for PC-98 in 1998](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bad_Apple!!). Almost nothing about its creation involved any of the usual infrastructure of music production, promotion, or distribution.

What allowed it to exist was simple: ZUN's policy said the music could be used. Alstroemeria Records used it. Anira used Alstroemeria's version. Ten million people watched it. The arrangement ecosystem — not the game, not the studio, not the publisher — is what produced the first 10M-view video in Nico Nico Douga's history.

[Gwern's analysis of Touhou production patterns found no correlation between track output and external economic factors like unemployment rates](https://gwern.net/touhou). The production curve was driven by the franchise's internal logic: an accessible, arrangement-friendly catalogue, free to work with, distributed through a convention system that rewarded prolific output. The arrangement scene grew because the conditions for arrangement were optimal, not because external pressure pushed people toward creative outlets.

## Why This Didn't Happen to Any Other Franchise

Plenty of video game franchises have beloved music. The melodies of Koji Kondo's Mario and Zelda themes are as widely recognized as anything ZUN wrote. [Final Fantasy's orchestral compositions have been performed at sold-out concert halls globally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distant_Worlds:_music_from_FINAL_FANTASY). Mega Man's NES soundtracks have their own deep arrangement culture.

None of them produced forty-four thousand tracks from twelve hundred circles in a decade.

The difference is structural. Nintendo does not permit the kind of unlicensed commercial doujin creation that Touhou's policy enables. Square Enix manages Final Fantasy arrangements through licensing. These are rational IP management decisions. They are also, as the Touhou case demonstrates, decisive for scale. A fan who arranges a Zelda track and sells it at a convention is operating in legally uncertain territory; a fan who arranges "Night of Nights" and sells it at Comiket is operating inside an established framework with the creator's explicit, if informal, endorsement.

ZUN's policy did not just permit fan creativity. It gave it a floor. [The doujin community understood the policy and built an entire economy on it](https://studenttheses.uu.nl/handle/20.500.12932/40208) — the convention circuit, the distribution networks, the specialized labels, the streaming pipelines. [Touhou Doujin Music Distribution now channels arrangements to Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, and Amazon Music](https://en.touhouwiki.net/wiki/Touhou_Doujin_Music_Distribution). The shadow art video has 110 million YouTube views. The original PC-98 game that supplied the source material turns 28 this year.

The living songbook is still being written.
