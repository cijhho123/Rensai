Go Nagai's *Devilman* manga ran in *Weekly Shōnen Magazine* from 1972 to 1973. By the final chapter, humanity was dead. Satan stood over the corpse of the only person he had ever loved. God prepared to reset the world so the cycle could begin again.

That story has been adapted several times. Each time, it failed to be that story.

The [1972 Toei TV anime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman) — the first and most famous of the prior adaptations — aired in an early-evening timeslot aimed at elementary schoolchildren. The producers wanted something compatible with *Mazinger Z*, which was airing around the same time. They got it. The TV *Devilman* has fart jokes. It has Scooby-Doo endings. [Satan is not in it at all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman) — Ryo Asuka was conceived too late in the production cycle for him to be incorporated, which means the story has no heart, no tragedy, and no ending. It runs for 39 episodes and stops rather than concludes. It is, by any measure, a different story wearing Devilman's character designs.

[*Devilman Crybaby* (2018)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby) is the first adaptation that ends at God resetting the world. It is the first adaptation where Ryo has always been Satan. It is the first animated version where Miki dies in the way Nagai wrote her death and where the final image is Satan weeping for the human he destroyed. [Critic Daryl Surat described it as "perhaps the most faithful animated adaptation" of the manga](https://www.newsweek.com/devilman-crybaby-netflix-anime-akira-season-2-779002). He is right. And the reason it could finally be what the manga actually is comes down, almost entirely, to the platform it aired on.

## What Broadcast TV Made Impossible

The [1972 TV anime's fundamental problem was its timeslot and audience](https://devilman.fandom.com/wiki/Devilman_(Anime)). Toei Animation had approached Nagai about adapting *Demon Lord Dante*, his previous dark work, and Nagai instead proposed something even more extreme. The network wanted a superhero show for children. What resulted was a negotiated product: Devilman the brand, stripped of Devilman the content. The violence was toned down. The horror was stripped out. The story that ends with the death of the human race was converted into a Tokusatsu-adjacent monster-of-the-week show.

This is not a failure of the creators involved — it is an accurate reflection of what broadcast TV for children in 1972 Japan could contain. The platform determined the product. You cannot put the Miki death scene in a 7 PM children's anime. You cannot write Satan's tragedy into a show whose audience has homework in the morning. The 1972 anime is exactly what its medium demanded it be.

The [1987–1990 OVA series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman) moved the needle substantially. The two installments — *Devilman: The Birth* and *Devilman: Demon Bird Sirène* — were made for an older home video audience and were more willing to include violence and mature content. [They are genuinely dark pieces of work with real fidelity to the manga's look and tone](https://dailydead.com/crypt-of-curiosities-devilman-ovas/). But they covered only the first half of the story, stopping before the demons were revealed to humanity, before Miki's death, before the apocalypse. A 2000 OVA titled *Amon: Apocalypse of Devilman* covered a different arc from the franchise and also failed to reach Nagai's actual ending. The format was right — uncut OVAs aimed at adults — but the scope, across all three productions, was never enough to deliver the complete narrative. [What might have been the definitive pre-streaming Devilman adaptation remains a half-finished artifact](https://www.animusensei.com/the-ultimate-devilman-watch-order-guide-tv-series-ovas-onas-movies/).

Nagai himself, when approached about the 2018 adaptation, reportedly told Yuasa: ["I'll leave it up to you. Do whatever you want."](https://gigazine.net/gsc_news/en/20180116-devilman-crybaby-go-nagai-masaaki-yuasa-interview/) This was not indifference. Nagai understood what every prior adaptation had proven: that the real Devilman had never successfully been animated. The story he wrote could not exist in broadcast television.

## What Netflix Actually Changed

The critical question about *Devilman Crybaby* is not whether it's good — it's extraordinary — but whether it could exist under any prior media arrangement. Yuasa has been explicit about this. In interviews, he noted that he could make Crybaby because [people "who think 'I shouldn't see it' don't have to watch it, and people who want to watch it can watch it"](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2018/01/08/devilman-crybaby-interview-with-director-masaaki-yuasa/), adding: "I think it would have been a work that couldn't have been made with so many restrictions."

The streaming model changed four specific things.

First, content. There are no broadcast standards to comply with on Netflix. The orgy scene in Episode 1, Miki's death, the explicit violence and sexuality throughout — none of this was available to broadcast TV production. Yuasa's stated approach was: ["If Nagai-sensei could have done as he pleased, he'd have gone even more extreme."](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-04-24/interview-devilman-crybaby-director-masaaki-yuasa/.146067) He treated Crybaby as the adaptation the original author would have made if shōnen magazine publication hadn't constrained him.

Second, structure. The 10-episode format, released all at once globally, is not a broadcast format. It is not a weekly TV rhythm. The binge structure means the story can build without worrying about cliffhangers per episode or whether casual viewers will return next week. Crybaby is a single continuous narrative designed to be consumed as a unit — something broadcast episodic TV structurally resists.

Third, completion. Unlike the OVAs, which ran out of resources before the story ended, Netflix's investment covered the full narrative. Crybaby is [the first animated version of Devilman to actually finish the story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby) — which means it is the first adaptation where the tragedy can land with its full weight. An ending where humanity dies and Satan mourns requires the audience to have been with Akira and Ryo for ten episodes. You cannot reach that ending in two OVAs and then stop.

Fourth, audience. Yuasa noted the unprecedented nature of being able to reach a global audience simultaneously: ["such a story could be provided in an environment where people all over the world could see it if they wanted to."](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2018/01/08/devilman-crybaby-interview-with-director-masaaki-yuasa/) [Approximately 90% of Crybaby's viewers were outside Japan](https://www.cbr.com/masaaki-yuasa-anime-devilman-crybaby/) — an unthinkable figure for any broadcast anime. The story that is, at its core, a tragedy about violence done to the marginalized and the destruction of human connection found an international audience with no pre-existing Devilman familiarity, reading it fresh against the political climate of 2018.

## What Yuasa Added (and Why It Works)

It would be wrong to suggest Crybaby is simply the manga in animation form. Yuasa made significant adaptational choices — the [delinquents became rappers](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-04-24/interview-devilman-crybaby-director-masaaki-yuasa/.146067), the contemporary setting introduces social media as a mechanism for the human violence that follows the demon revelation, and Yuasa's visual language (the distorting animation style, the deliberately non-photorealistic character design) is specifically his own rather than Nagai's.

But these changes are in service of the original material, not departures from it. [Yuasa chose rappers as the moral witnesses because "rappers are the people who speak what's on their minds today"](https://gigazine.net/gsc_news/en/20180116-devilman-crybaby-go-nagai-masaaki-yuasa-interview/) — a direct translation of Nagai's delinquents as social outsiders who tell uncomfortable truths. The social media layer amplifies Nagai's original thesis rather than contradicting it: when humans discover the demons, the violence that follows isn't a response to an external threat — it's existing hatred finding a cover story. [Crybaby depicts this through the mechanism Twitter in 2018 actually used](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby): doxxing, mob harassment, live-streaming violence. Nagai in 1972 had to imagine how a society tears itself apart. Yuasa in 2018 could just describe what was already happening. Nagai told Yuasa his ingredients were available; how they were cooked was up to him.

The Ryo/Satan adaptation is where Crybaby is most distinctly its own work. In the manga, Satan's emotional complexity emerges slowly; in Crybaby, [Ryo's coldness is foregrounded from childhood, making the final revelation not a twist but a confirmation](https://devilman.fandom.com/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby) — the entire show is retrospectively his tragedy. Nagai described entering a trance state while writing Miki's death scene and being surprised by it on re-reading. Yuasa designed the structure so that moment is inevitable and visible in advance. The horror is not the surprise. The horror is that you can see it coming and cannot stop it.

[The Crunchyroll Anime Awards gave Crybaby Anime of the Year for 2018](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby), and Yuasa won Best Director. [ANN's review called it "one of the rare graphic action anime that has an incredible story to back up its fantastic animation"](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/devilman-crybaby/episodes-1-10/.126524). [The show created the "biggest buzz"](https://www.newsweek.com/devilman-crybaby-netflix-anime-akira-season-2-779002) at its release — "the first big talking point of the year in the anime community" and one of the most discussed anime of the decade.

What was most discussed was the violence and explicitness. But the violence was the point — or more precisely, the ability to include it was what finally allowed the story to be what it was. A Devilman where Miki survives is not Devilman. A Devilman where the human race makes it through is not Devilman. The darkness of Nagai's manga is not shock value — it is the thesis. Humanity destroys what it fears; love arrives too late; God resets and the cycle continues. Every adaptation that softened this — by removing Satan, stopping before the ending, toning down the violence, or redesigning it for children — produced something that could be broadcast but could not be the story.

## The Platform as Argument

Forty-six years passed between Nagai's manga ending and the first animated version of that ending reaching audiences. That gap is not incidental. It is a record of what every prior distribution model could and could not carry. The 1972 broadcast model could carry a children's superhero show. The 1987 OVA market could carry the beginning of a mature horror story. Netflix in 2018 could carry the whole thing.

[Yuasa received Japan's Medal with Purple Ribbon in 2021](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2021-11-02/japan-sinks-eizouken-devilman-crybaby-director-masaaki-yuasa-receives-japan-medal-with-purple-ribbon/.179111), in part for the cultural impact of Crybaby. It is a strange honor for a show about the end of the world — but appropriate for a work that proved the medium had finally caught up with the material. The story had been waiting. The platform was the last piece.
