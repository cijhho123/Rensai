There is a moment near the end of The End of Evangelion where Misato Katsuragi, bleeding out in a corridor, presses a cross pendant into Shinji Ikari's hand. She kisses him — a gesture that has been analyzed to death, for reasons that are beside the point — and shoves him into an elevator before the explosion takes her. The doors close. The pendant is bloodstained.

If you know the series, you know where that cross came from. Dr. Katsuragi pressed it into young Misato's hand as he put her in an escape pod before Second Impact swallowed him. That was the first transfer. EoE is the second. And between those two moments, roughly eighteen years apart, is the entire arc of who Misato Katsuragi is and what she never managed to resolve.

## The Cross as Inherited Weight

The pendant is not a symbol of Christianity. [Anno himself has said](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/List_of_religious_and_philosophical_terms_in_Evangelion) that the religious imagery in Evangelion was chosen because "it looked cool" and seemed mysterious to a Japanese audience. Take him at his word — not because he's always reliable about his own intentions, but because the cross works better as a secular object anyway. It is not salvation. It is inheritance.

Misato wore her father's cross for eighteen years without ever deciding how she felt about him. She resented him. He was absent, obsessed with his work, a stranger who chose his research over his family. And then he died for her — gave her the only thing he had left, his own survival, and pushed her into a capsule before Second Impact turned the Antarctic sky red. She couldn't forgive him and couldn't stop wearing his pendant. Both things were true for the entire run of the series.

That irresolution isn't incidental. It's the psychological engine under everything Misato does. Her relationship with Kaji, her relationship with Shinji, her role at NERV — she orbits men who need saving or men she needs to save, and she can never entirely separate the two drives. The [Evangelion fan community has long recognized](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=20696) the parallels between Misato and her father, but most readings frame it as character symmetry. It's more than that. It's entrapment.

## The Structure of the Repetition

The [visual parallel in EoE](https://discover.hubpages.com/entertainment/The-Graphic-Details-of-Misatos-Death-in-The-End-of-Evangelion) is deliberate and exact. Dr. Katsuragi: places young Misato in a capsule, seals her in, dies in the disaster he created. Misato: places Shinji at the elevator to Unit-01, pushes him through, dies in the conflict she helped create. The film draws this so precisely that it can't be accidental composition — it's the point. She has not escaped her father's story. She has become its next chapter.

What makes this genuinely painful, rather than just structurally elegant, is that Misato knows something is wrong in her final moments. She questions whether she has "been on the right side." Not whether she chose correctly tactically, but whether her whole life's orientation — her choices, her allegiances, her reasons for doing any of this — was justified. It's a moment of radical uncertainty, and the film doesn't answer it. It can't. The elevator doors close.

She dies not knowing. That's the inheritance too: her father died doing what he believed was necessary, and whether his life's work was right or monstrous is a question the Second Impact never let anyone ask. Misato gets a few seconds and no resolution. The cross goes forward.

## What the Kiss Actually Is

The kiss gets read as many things: maternal, erotic, inappropriate, calculated. It is probably all of these and none of them cleanly. What it functionally does in the scene is break Shinji's paralysis — she uses the one register she has access to, intimacy, to reach him when nothing else is working. It is not tender. It is desperate.

That desperation is consistent with who Misato has been throughout the series: someone who substitutes intensity for stability, who reaches for connection through provocation. She and Shinji have had this dynamic all along — she pushes him, sometimes too hard, and it sometimes works. In her final moment she does the most extreme version of that, and it works, and then she dies.

There is an argument that the kiss is exploitative, and that argument is not wrong. But EoE is not a film interested in moral clarity about its characters. Misato is flawed, and she is dying, and she is doing what she has always done. The film lets her be all of those things simultaneously.

## The Cycle and What Shinji Does With It

Shinji takes the pendant. He goes into Unit-01. He does not save the world — he triggers the Third Impact, initiates Instrumentality, and it is only much later, on the other side of that horror, that he makes his choice. He chooses to return. To reject the dissolution of self. To come back to a world where separation and pain are the price of existing as an individual.

He comes back without Misato. He comes back to a beach and Asuka and rubble. We don't know what he does with the pendant after. The film doesn't tell us.

But the cycle, at minimum, interrupted. Misato passed forward a trauma object that had been transmitting the same loss for nearly two decades. Whether Shinji can hold it differently — whether anyone can — the film wisely refuses to say. What it does say, with crystalline structural precision, is that carrying your parents' suffering forward isn't heroism. It's just what happens when no one ever gets to put it down.

The cross is still bloodstained. That's the last thing we know about it.
