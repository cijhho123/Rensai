When Seitaro Higuchi first appears in *The Tatami Galaxy*, he is a warning. Eight years at university, perpetually in a yukata, presiding over a black cat café of dubious purpose, accepting devotion from acolytes without apparent reciprocation, a man who has reached an age where his presence in an undergraduate dormitory has become a small philosophical problem. He is the answer to the question "what happens if you fail to find your rose-colored campus life?" He is what Watashi might become. The show positions him this way — and then, over nine more episodes, quietly demonstrates that Higuchi has solved the problem Watashi cannot.

## The Failure Type

[The Tatami Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tatami_Galaxy) is structured around Watashi's search for a "rose-colored campus life" — an idea of the perfect undergraduate experience, populated by "fair-haired maidens" and meaningful friendships and the discovery of his true calling. [In nine parallel loops](https://www.otakujournalist.com/tatami-galaxy-and-the-quarter-life-crisis/), he joins different university clubs and fails, each time, to arrive at the life he wanted. The premise is comic because it's recognizable: the specific disappointment of finding that the club/major/friend group you were certain would transform you is just more of the same.

Higuchi is the character who makes explicit that this disappointment has a structural cause. [According to the show's characterization of him](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/TheTatamiGalaxy), he has somehow accrued eight years at university without completing his degree, and appears to be in no hurry. Dressed in a yukata. Running a café. Sticking pins into a globe according to a scheme that becomes clear slowly, over several episodes.

At first reading, he is the figure Watashi doesn't want to become: the eccentric permanent student, the person who stopped trying and found a comfortable niche in the margins of academic life. [Fan analysis from the Tatami Galaxy's initial run](https://bewilderedgalaxy.wordpress.com/2019/04/21/romanticization-of-an-ideal-college-life-tatami-galaxy/) described him as occupying the "outcast and failure" archetype — proof that not everyone gets the rose-colored life, proof that some people fall out of the competition entirely. The show wants you to read him this way, at first.

## Two Years Under the Sea

The detail that unlocks Higuchi is a small one that gets planted without fanfare in the early episodes: he has spent the past two years reading Jules Verne's *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*, and he has been using a globe to map every destination the submarine traveled. [FungaFuFu's episode analysis](https://fungafufu.wordpress.com/2010/05/14/tatami-galaxy-3-and-4-cycling-club-soleil-disciples-wanted/) notes that the globe borrowed from Watashi is therefore a symbol — Higuchi is not just marking the book's route, he is undertaking a mental journey with genuine investment. He has been "completely invested in the mental journey that the book provides him."

This is not the behavior of a man who gave up. It is the behavior of a man who found something that interested him and followed it with complete commitment, regardless of whether it was the kind of activity a third-year student is supposed to be doing. While Watashi is looping through clubs hunting for his calling, Higuchi has been, for two years, doing exactly what he wanted to do: reading carefully, thinking about what he read, tracing it on a map, and preparing for the journey he intends to take when he's ready.

["Nemo" derives from the Latin for "no one" or "no man"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_Nemo) — probably an allusion to the name Odysseus used after blinding the Cyclops. Captain Nemo removed himself from humanity and chose to live underwater, isolated, as a condition of his freedom. [The contrast with Higuchi is intentional](https://fungafufu.wordpress.com/2010/06/24/the-tatami-galaxy-9-secret-society-lucky-cat-chinese-restaurant/): Higuchi read a book about a man who chose isolation as freedom and concluded that the opposite move was correct — he would go into the world, physically, toward the places Nemo fled through. He is not a Nemo. He is the person who read Nemo and understood what not to become.

## The Speech

[Episode 9, "Secret Society Lucky Cat Chinese Restaurant,"](https://matthigh.wordpress.com/2010/07/02/anime-review-the-tatami-galaxy-episodes-7-8-and-9/) contains Higuchi's direct statement of the show's thesis. He tells Watashi: "There is no such thing as that rose-colored campus life. Why? Because there is nothing rose-colored in this world. Everything is all a bunch of colours mixed up." And then, more specifically: "The root of all your evil is in always relying on one of your other possibilities to get your wish. You must accept that you are the person here, now, and that you cannot become anyone else other than that person."

This is not bitterness. Higuchi is not a man who wanted the rose-colored campus life and failed to achieve it. He is a man who understood, probably early, that the premise was wrong — that chasing an idealized version of your life is what prevents you from living the actual version. [The Chicago Review of Books analysis of the novel](https://chireviewofbooks.com/2023/02/20/multiversal-revelations-in-the-tatami-galaxy/) identifies the central problem of the series as the protagonist's belief that "any attempt to change himself is pointless" — what he can do is change his choices. Higuchi's counter-argument is that the self must change, not the choices, and that you change the self by accepting what it currently is.

"Your life has not yet begun," Higuchi tells Watashi. "You are spending overtime inside of your mother's belly." This is not cruelty. It's the diagnosis. Watashi has been gestating inside the fantasy of who he might become if the circumstances were different. Higuchi has already been born.

## The Departure

[In the same episode, Higuchi announces his decision](https://afterimag3.tumblr.com/post/776191196/the-tatami-galaxy-episode-9-higuchi-ive): "I've thought about this for quite a while, but now my mind is made up. It will soon be the day for me to go forth, out into the world. I will circle the globe." He is going to trace the submarine's actual route, physically. The two years of reading are ending. The journey is beginning.

Watashi has been looping — internal, hypothetical, paralyzed by the existence of unchosen alternatives. Higuchi undertook an internal journey and converted it into an actual one. He is not fleeing the university because it failed him. He is leaving because he has finished what he came to do, which turned out to be something no one else was expecting him to do. [The ANN DVD review of the series](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/the-tatami-galaxy) describes the show as ultimately concerned with whether the protagonist can stop "restarting from zero" — and Higuchi is the counter-example to that entire impulse.

[The starkfyre analysis of the series](https://starkfyre.wordpress.com/2015/11/06/the-tatami-galaxy-life-does-not-have-to-sparkle-to-be-beautiful/) notes that the show's argument is "life does not have to sparkle to be beautiful" — that the lived life, the specific and imperfect one, is the one worth having. Higuchi has been living that argument for eight years. He found a book he loved, he thought about it deeply, he runs a café that functions as a space for honest conversation, he mentors a protagonist who resists being mentored, and he leaves when it's time. None of this sparkles. All of it is his.

## The Reread

[In every loop the show offers](https://www.animepedialove.com/2025/05/the-tatami-galaxy-surreal-journey.html), Higuchi is essentially the same. He doesn't have an arc. He doesn't learn something he didn't know. This is usually a trait of supporting characters who exist to serve the protagonist's development — but in Higuchi's case, the lack of change is itself the argument. He is the same because he has already done the work Watashi is still refusing to do. His consistency across timelines is not a narrative limitation; it is a character trait, and the character trait is: I know who I am and I don't need to become someone else to have a meaningful life.

Watashi's nine loops are frantic, anxious, and ultimately interchangeable because the variable he refuses to change is himself. [The ending of the series](https://animedepth.com/the-tatami-galaxy-ending-explained/) resolves only when he stops believing in the better timeline and accepts the timeline he's in. He stops, in other words, operating like someone who needs to become Higuchi — and starts operating like someone who can be who he actually is.

Higuchi is not the joke. He never was. He is the person who understood the show's thesis before the show started. The eight years at university are not the failure. They are the proof.
