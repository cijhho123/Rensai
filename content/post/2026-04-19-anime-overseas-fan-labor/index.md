There is a version of this story where anime conquered the world because it was good. Where the global fanbase formed organically, drawn by the quality of the storytelling, the emotional depth that Western animation had abandoned, the sheer range of what the medium could do. In this version, Crunchyroll built a streaming service that fans wanted, Funimation produced dubbing that competed with the source, and Sony's $1.175 billion acquisition of Crunchyroll in 2021 was simply capital recognizing value.

That version is wrong — not completely, but in the ways that matter.

![Toonami broadcast block promotional image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Toonami_logo.svg/1280px-Toonami_logo.svg.png)

The global anime market was built by fans working illegally, at personal cost, with no promise of return. The infrastructure they created was then captured by the companies that had initially ignored the demand signal those fans represented. Understanding that sequence is essential to understanding why the current moment — consolidation, subscription fatigue, content library wars — looks the way it does.

## The Blueprint Italy Built

The conventional American narrative places anime's international breakthrough in the late 1990s: Toonami, Dragon Ball Z, Pokémon. But that narrative is 20 years late. [Italy was the first major international anime market](https://discover.hubpages.com/entertainment/10-most-popular-cartoons-in-italy-in-the-80s-the-anime-invasion), and it got there through a specific combination of economics and policy that had nothing to do with cultural osmosis.

In the late 1970s, Italy had just undergone dramatic media deregulation, suddenly expanding television content needs. [UFO Robo Grendizer arrived in 1978](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/264089012_The_Italian_anime_boom_The_outstanding_success_of_Japanese_animation_in_Italy_1978-1984) to massive ratings. The reason Italian broadcasters could afford it: the post-oil-shock yen was weak, making Japanese animation licensing extraordinarily cheap. Italy didn't discover anime because of quality — it discovered anime because it needed content, Japanese studios needed revenue, and the currency math worked.

What Italy proved was a distribution template: buy cheap Japanese content, hire local singers for Italian opening themes (not just subtitles — actual Italian-language performances), apply lighter censorship standards than neighboring European markets, and let the merchandise boom follow. [France, Spain, and Latin America replicated this model](https://animepapa.com/how-france-became-one-of-the-biggest-anime-hubs-outside-japan/) through the 1980s. France is now [the second-largest manga market in the world](https://www.ypulse.com/newsfeed/2023/01/04/french-readers-are-now-the-second-biggest-consumer-of-manga-only-behind-japan/) — larger than the United States — and the foundation was laid not by streaming platforms but by those early broadcast deals.

Meanwhile, Southeast Asia had been receiving anime continuously since the early 1980s — Thailand's Channel 9 had a [weekly three-and-a-half-hour anime block from 1981 onwards](https://www.scmp.com/week-asia/lifestyle-culture/article/3339415/soft-power-hard-cash-southeast-asias-billion-dollar-anime-obsession). By the 2000s, Indonesia, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Malaysia had [larger relative anime fanbases than the United States by virtually every metric](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2024-05-20/bringing-anime-to-southeast-asia-with-medialink-ani-one-co-founder-lovinia-chiu/.210560). This is nearly invisible in Western anime history, which has an embarrassing tendency to treat "international" as a synonym for "American."

## Fansubs Created the Demand That Companies Then Licensed

The American anime market didn't get its Italian-style infrastructure in the 1970s or 1980s. What it got instead was fansubs — fan-made subtitled translations distributed first on VHS cassettes traded through convention networks, then via early internet channels in the late 1990s. These were copyright violations. The people who made and distributed them knew they were.

They did it anyway, and the market they created was real. By the time [Toonami launched in March 1997](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toonami), substantial American anime fandom already existed — built on fansub distribution of Dragon Ball Z, Sailor Moon, and dozens of titles that hadn't yet received official English releases. Toonami didn't create that audience; it [canonized it](https://www.complex.com/pop-culture/a/elijah-watson/oral-history-of-toonami). It gave broadcast legitimacy to demand that fans had already demonstrated through piracy.

A [2011 Japanese government study](https://torrentfreak.com/internet-piracy-boosts-anime-sales-study-concludes-110203/) reached the obvious conclusion that the industry spent years denying: online piracy of anime boosted DVD sales rather than cannibalizing them. The mechanism was simple. Fansubs functioned as marketing. They put titles in front of audiences who then bought merchandise, attended screenings, and purchased official releases when those became available. The pirates were doing market research that the companies hadn't bothered to commission.

This doesn't mean piracy is neutral — it creates genuine licensing complications, and the quality of fan translations varied wildly. But the morality of the fan act is separate from its economic function. What fansubbers built, through years of unpaid labor, was the demonstrated demand that made the licensing deals viable.

## Crunchyroll Started as a Pirate Site. That's Not Incidental.

[Crunchyroll was founded in 2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crunchyroll,_LLC) as an illegal streaming aggregator — a site where users could upload unlicensed anime content for others to watch for free. It operated this way for three years. In 2009, [Venrock investment came with a condition](https://medium.com/@misfitotaku/from-piracy-to-powerhouse-the-business-evolution-of-crunchyroll-95345262eff0): go legal. The company pivoted to licensed simulcasting — same-day subtitled streaming in multiple languages — and the model worked precisely because the audience that piracy had created was ready for a legal, convenient alternative.

The progression is not incidental. It's the template. Fan communities build demand through informal and often illegal distribution. That demand creates a market signal. Venture capital or corporate acquisition validates the signal and monetizes the infrastructure. The fans who built the ecosystem end up as paying subscribers.

By 2021, [Sony acquired Crunchyroll for $1.175 billion](https://www.cbr.com/crunchyroll-anime-monopoly/). In March 2022, Crunchyroll absorbed Funimation, concentrating approximately [92% of Western anime licensing](https://www.japanpowered.com/anime-articles/crunchyroll-monopoly) under a single Sony subsidiary. The Department of Justice reviewed the acquisition. Voice actor and union advocate Marin Miller, representing the [Coalition of Dubbing Actors (CODA)](https://inthesetimes.com/article/anime-dubbing-voice-actors-union-crunchyroll-funication-jjk-aot-coda), described the resulting entity as "a chokepoint of power" — sign one union contract with Crunchyroll, she argued, and hundreds of shows instantly shift from non-union to union, which is how much control the merger had concentrated.

## The Irony of the Current Moment

The [global anime market reached $28.8 billion in 2023](https://www.cognitivemarketresearch.com/anime-market-report), with streaming accounting for a major and growing share. The COVID-19 lockdowns accelerated the overseas shift: [Japan's domestic anime market contracted 9.7% in 2020](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2021-11-03/what-is-happening-in-the-anime-industry-in-2020-2021-an-analysis-of-the-animation-industry-report-/.179153), while streaming was the only domestic sector to grow, jumping 35.8% to 93 billion yen. The overseas market [surpassed domestic for the first time ever in 2021](https://stock-daily.com/news/streaming-and-covid-19-have-entrenched-animes-global-popularity/).

The merger's concrete effects were immediate. When Funimation's app shut down in April 2024, [over 100 titles became digitally unavailable to Western audiences](https://boundingintocomics.com/anime/completion-of-funimation-crunchyroll-merger-results-in-over-100-anime-titles-being-made-digitally-unavailable-to-western-audiences-including-astro-boy-slayers-and-mobile-suit-gundam-seed/) — Astro Boy, Slayers, Mobile Suit Gundam SEED among them. [Digital copies purchased through Funimation did not transfer to Crunchyroll](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-02-07/funimation-app-shuts-down-on-april-2-as-its-accounts-merge-with-crunchyroll/.207344). The fans who had been buying legal copies for years found their libraries evaporated. The [editing controversies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Editing_of_anime_in_distribution) that plagued early Western distribution — 4Kids turning Sailor Moon's Uranus and Neptune from lovers to cousins, replacing rice balls with "donuts" — are largely gone, but what replaced them is a single corporate gatekeeper deciding which titles survive and which disappear into licensing limbo.

The audience exists because fans refused to wait for studios to get around to licensing what they wanted. They built their own distribution network, did their own translations, hosted their own servers. Then those same fans — or their successors — watched Sony spend $1.175 billion to buy the company that replaced the infrastructure they built, and now pay a monthly subscription fee for the privilege.

The anime market is global because of fan labor. The profits from that market flow almost entirely to capital. That's the actual history.
