In February 2022, episode director Teruyuki Omine posted a tweet that said he had gone home "for the first time in three days." He had just finished his work on *Attack on Titan: The Final Season Part 2*, episode 78 — "Two Brothers," one of the most technically complex episodes in the run. Then he [deleted the tweet](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-director-reignites-anime-crunch-debate-after-working-72-hours-straight/). The discourse it sparked was predictably focused on whether MAPPA was a bad studio. The more uncomfortable question — why the largest anime franchise in the world was being finished under conditions that required one of its directors to sleep in his office — went largely unanswered.

That's the question this piece is about.

![Attack on Titan Season 1 promotional visual by Wit Studio](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/10/47347l.jpg)

## How Wit Studio Built the Monster

When *Shingeki no Kyojin* premiered in April 2013, [Wit Studio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wit_Studio) was barely two years old. It had been spun off from Production I.G in 2012 with around 35 employees and no major title to its name. Attack on Titan was its first real bet — and the bet paid out spectacularly. The show became a global phenomenon almost immediately, propelling Wit into a tier of relevance most anime studios never reach.

But the consequences of that success were structural, not just financial. Wit's business model at the time [relied on DVD sales data](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-vinland-saga-anime-studio-explains-reason/) to determine whether a sequel would be greenlit and when. The data took time. By the time the numbers came in for Season 1, Wit had already filled its production calendar with other projects. The second season of *Attack on Titan* didn't air until 2017 — [four years after the first](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/anime-attack-on-titan-vinland-saga-wit-mappa/). Season 3 came in two parts: 2018 and 2019. By the time the production cycle for a final season was being discussed, the show had been running for six years.

Those six years had cost something. According to Wit Studio president [George Wada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Wada), the production had placed [immense strain on the studio's staff](https://animehunch.com/wit-studio-reveal-why-they-dropped-attack-on-titan-vinland-saga-sequels/) over that entire period. Wit was still a relatively small operation — roughly 130 employees by 2021, tiny relative to the scale of the IP they were servicing. *Attack on Titan* was a show that demanded resources at a scale that was, frankly, incompatible with the studio's size. They had made it work for three seasons through extraordinary effort. A fourth — which would cover the longest and most technically demanding arc of the manga — was a different proposition.

So Wit made a decision. They let it go.

## The Industry Structure Nobody Wanted to Discuss

Wada's explanation was direct: by the time they received a sequel offer, their schedule was already committed to other projects. There was [no malice, no falling out](https://animehunch.com/wit-studio-reveal-why-they-dropped-attack-on-titan-vinland-saga-sequels/), no crisis. Just a structural impossibility — a small studio, a tight calendar, and an IP that had grown too large for the company that built it.

This is a more interesting story than "Wit abandoned AoT." The anime production pipeline is built around short-cycle commitments, physical media sales as success metrics (only recently displaced by streaming data), and a labor ecosystem that runs on freelance animators paid per-cut rather than per-hour. [Wada himself admitted](https://animecorner.me/interview-production-i-g-wit-studio-on-new-projects-and-the-evolving-anime-industry/) that his management practices at the time weren't sophisticated enough to hold a multi-season slot open for a series while waiting on sales data. The system wasn't designed for long-running commitments to a single franchise at that scale.

The studios that were approached to replace Wit mostly said no. Not because *Attack on Titan* was a bad job, but because the ask was enormous: finish the most popular anime in the world, on a timeline set by the publisher and broadcaster, under conditions inherited from whoever came before you. Most studios looked at that brief and passed. [Only MAPPA agreed](https://fictionhorizon.com/here-is-why-wit-studio-isnt-making-attack-on-titan-season-4/) — and even their acceptance was characterized as "We'll think about it" before becoming a yes.

## What MAPPA Actually Signed Up For

MAPPA took the job under conditions that should have been a warning sign for anyone paying attention. Season 4 Part 1 was given [roughly eight months of production time](https://www.cavsconnect.com/opinion/2021/01/29/attack-on-titan-or-on-its-animators/). For a major finale to one of the highest-profile properties in the medium, that is not a generous timeline. It is an aggressive one.

To manage the volume, MAPPA leaned heavily on CG for titan designs and combat sequences — a decision that drew immediate criticism from portions of the fanbase. What that criticism tended to overlook was that CG is frequently faster to produce than hand-drawn animation at equivalent complexity. [CBR's breakdown of the aesthetic differences](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-season-4-premiere-maapa-wit-animation-differences/) notes that MAPPA's approach — more rotoscoping, more realistic facial rendering, less emphasis on exaggerated eye expression — was also a deliberate tonal choice suited to the Marley arc's more grounded, war-documentary register. But the CG Titans were also a concession to the clock.

Meanwhile, MAPPA was simultaneously producing *Jujutsu Kaisen*, *Dance Dance Danseur*, *Kakegurui Twin*, and preparing *Chainsaw Man*. The studio was running [multiple major productions in parallel](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-director-reignites-anime-crunch-debate-after-working-72-hours-straight/). An anonymous animator described working conditions as "factory-like," with crews working "until the sun came up." Veteran animator Ippei Ichii went on the record to allege that Netflix was [paying only the bare minimum per animation cut](https://wegotthiscovered.com/tv/attack-on-titan-producer-comes-under-fire-for-extreme-workplace-crunch/), compressing margins further down to the individual craftspeople doing the actual work.

MAPPA denied the worst allegations and pointed to above-industry pay rates. But the trajectory of events — the Omine tweet, the anonymous accounts, the hospitalized animator documented during the *Jujutsu Kaisen* season — suggests that whatever MAPPA's intentions were, the conditions the final season was produced under were not healthy ones.

## The Fan Debate That Wasn't a Debate

The online discourse about this transition mostly played out as an aesthetic argument: Was MAPPA's *Attack on Titan* as good as Wit's? Did the CGI ruin it? Was the rotoscoping soulless or more naturalistic? [Game Rant ran a "who did it better" comparison piece](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-who-did-it-better-wit-studio-vs-mappa/). YouTube is full of side-by-side comparisons. Subreddits debated character design fidelity.

What almost none of that discourse addressed: the workers.

The comparison between Wit's early seasons and MAPPA's final season is genuinely interesting as an aesthetic question — [Wit's kinetic, almost baroque approach to ODM action](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-mappa-wit-studio-comparison/) versus MAPPA's more grounded, psychologically intense visual grammar. Wit's emotional shorthand was in the eyes; MAPPA's is in the rest of the face. These are real differences that reflect different directorial visions and studio cultures. They are worth discussing.

But treating this as just a style debate — as if both studios were simply making artistic choices under comparable conditions — ignores everything we know about how Season 4 was produced. The initial fan reaction to the studio change was fear. [That fear transformed almost overnight](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-studio-change-wit-mappa/) when the Season 4 trailer dropped and showed animation that was, in many ways, more faithful to Isayama's actual manga designs than Wit's version had been. (Mikasa's scarf, always black in the manga, had been rendered red by Wit for three seasons.) The fear became celebration.

That pivot was real and understandable. MAPPA delivered a technically strong final season under conditions that should not have been the conditions. The problem is that celebrating the output while ignoring the conditions is precisely the mode of consumption that makes those conditions persist.

## What the Streaming Era Changed (and Didn't)

George Wada, reflecting on the transition years later, noted that [streaming platforms had fundamentally altered the calculus](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2022-12-05/production-i.g-and-studio-wit-president-george-wada/.192199). Under the old model, Wit had to wait for DVD sales data before it could commit to a sequel slot. Under the streaming era — where Netflix and Crunchyroll measure success in concurrent viewers, not physical units — the turnaround on sequel announcements is faster, and studios can maintain longer-term commitments to a single property.

This is genuinely a structural improvement. It is also incomplete.

The reason for that incompleteness is structural. *Attack on Titan: The Final Season* was controlled by a production committee that included [Pony Canyon, Kodansha, Production I.G, MAPPA, Dentsu, MBS, and Pony Canyon Enterprises](https://x.com/aotwiki/status/1335786706260246530). Under [Japan's production committee system](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/05/02/what-is-an-animes-production-committee/), those entities pooled investment and shared rights — which means they share revenue from streaming licenses, merchandise, and international sales. The animation studio is effectively a vendor: paid a fixed production fee to deliver the show, and [excluded from the upside](https://fullfrontal.moe/production-committees/) if it becomes a global phenomenon. Kodansha [credited Attack on Titan for its first revenue increase in eighteen years](https://licensing.kodansha.com/title/attack-on-titan/). None of that windfall was contractually obligated to flow to the people who animated it.

Streaming money flows to committee members before it reaches studios, and studios pass what's left to animators last. The per-cut payment structure for freelance animators remained largely unchanged through the streaming boom. The [MAPPA crunch episodes](https://gizmodo.com/mappa-jujutsu-kaisen-crunch-allegations-1851034576) that followed *Attack on Titan* — most visibly during *Jujutsu Kaisen Season 2* — show that the money being generated by global anime consumption is not being translated into sustainable working conditions at the labor level.

Isayama, in his [2023 interview with Crunchyroll](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/interviews/2023/1/13/interview-attack-on-titan-creator-hajime-isayama-reflects-on-the-series-ending-game-of-thrones-and-more), acknowledged that completing the series under MAPPA's stewardship represented something he couldn't have done alone — that without a studio willing to take on the final season under those conditions, the story might never have been told. That's a meaningful acknowledgment. It is also quietly a statement about what the system required to finish his work.

## What's Actually Being Argued

The legacy of *Attack on Titan*'s production history is this: the most globally successful anime of the 2010s outgrew the studio that built it, was handed to a larger studio under an aggressive timeline, and was completed by workers who were, by multiple accounts, operating at or beyond their limits. The result was good — sometimes very good. The critical acclaim for the Marley arc, the visual clarity of the Rumbling sequences, the genuine formal strength of the "Two Brothers" episode that Omine worked 72 hours straight to finish — all of that is real. It was also produced in conditions that should embarrass an industry generating the kind of global revenue *Attack on Titan* now represents.

But the industry that produced it is still structured around the assumption that passion is a substitute for adequate compensation and reasonable hours. The Wit-to-MAPPA switch is routinely discussed as a logistical footnote or an aesthetic choice. It was neither. It was a case study in what happens when a franchise becomes larger than the industrial structure around it — and who pays for the gap.

That question doesn't resolve with "MAPPA did a good job." It asks: what would it have looked like if they'd had the time?

The answer is probably that it would have looked the same, because the timeline wasn't set by MAPPA. It was set by a production committee with a release schedule. And that's the part of the story that never makes it into the side-by-side YouTube comparisons.
