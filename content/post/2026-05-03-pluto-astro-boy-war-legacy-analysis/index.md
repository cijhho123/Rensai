Gesicht is a robot detective at the top of his field — internationally respected, emotionally stable, partnered and content. In the first episode of Pluto, he stands over the body of another of the world's greatest robots, torn apart in a way that should have been impossible, and the camera lingers on his face long enough to let you read what it registers. Not calculation. Grief.

That is the series in a single image. A robot capable of grief, living in a world sophisticated enough to protect that capacity legally, standing inside the aftermath of violence so senseless it could only have come from the same capacity turned inside out.

![Pluto key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1021/138568l.jpg)

[Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto_(Japanese_TV_series)) — the eight-episode Netflix ONA produced by Genco with animation by Studio M2, released in full on October 26, 2023 — is an adaptation of Naoki Urasawa and Takashi Nagasaki's acclaimed manga reimagining of Osamu Tezuka's "The Greatest Robot on Earth" story arc from *Astro Boy*. That lineage matters more than it might seem. Urasawa did not take the Tezuka story and darken it as a deconstruction. He took it at its word, and that sincerity is what makes Pluto devastating.

## The World Tezuka Promised

Osamu Tezuka's *Astro Boy* imagined a future where robots had achieved genuine intelligence, genuine emotion, and — crucially — genuine legal standing. In Tezuka's world, robots had rights. They could form families, hold jobs, feel love. [The original "Greatest Robot on Earth" arc](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-02-14/netflix-confirms-pluto-anime-series-in-2023-with-sneak-peek-video/.194919) was a tournament story: a villain named Pluto was dispatched to destroy the seven most powerful robots on Earth one by one, with Astro Boy ultimately prevailing. It was action-adventure in Tezuka's round-eyed, essentially optimistic mode. Even its darkness was resolved.

Urasawa inherited that world without irony. [As critics noted when the anime premiered](https://kotaku.com/pluto-netflix-anime-review-1850975057), Pluto takes place in a society that has genuinely achieved many of the things Tezuka hoped for: robot rights legislation exists, international law governs their treatment, humans and robots can form genuine families. This is not a dystopia wearing a utopian mask. Tezuka's dream came partially true.

And then it went to war.

The 39th Central Asian War — a direct, [unambiguous allegory for the 2003 US invasion of Iraq](https://nathangoldwag.wordpress.com/2024/01/01/pluto-the-emptiness-of-war/), launched on the false pretext of "robots of mass destruction" with UN investigators finding no evidence — happened in this world where robots and humans had coexisted peacefully. [The manga began serialization in September 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto:_Urasawa_x_Tezuka), the same year the US invaded Iraq; Urasawa was writing in real time as the consequences of that decision were still unfolding. The United States of Thracia mobilized its most advanced robotic soldiers. Nations sent their greatest mechanical warriors into combat. The war was brief, decisive, and apparently victorious.

Pluto takes place in the aftermath. The veterans — robot and human — are trying to live with what they did.

## Gesicht's Erased Memory

![Gesicht character](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/3/527725.jpg?s=e7a5043b5c01dba0ca88aa25fb0be00d)

Gesicht is the series' primary viewpoint character, and the architecture of his character arc is precise. He is assigned to investigate the murders of the world's greatest robots — the same seven from Tezuka's original arc, now translated by Urasawa into fully realized, grieving adults. As he investigates, he discovers something about himself: he committed a murder. He killed a human being. He had the memory suppressed because living with it was impossible.

[As Cinema Holic explains the scene](https://thecinemaholic.com/pluto-what-happened-to-gesichts-memory/), the act was not random — a robot kidnapper killed Gesicht's adopted son, and Gesicht obliterated him in response. The tragedy is not that he killed. It is that the capacity for that killing was identical to the capacity that made him love his son in the first place. The grief and the violence came from the same place.

[When he finally recovers the memory and understands the full chain of events](https://lostinanime.com/2023/11/pluto-06/), Gesicht has a moment of perfect clarity. "Nothing will be born of this," he realizes. Nothing comes from killing except more killing. His death, which comes immediately after this realization, is one of the most meticulously constructed deaths in recent anime: he dies understanding, which is the only thing worse than dying without understanding.

## The Robot Who Was Not Built to Fight

The character the series names itself after is not the villain of Tezuka's story in any simple sense. In Urasawa's version, Pluto was a gentle gardener — a robot created by a scientist whose family was destroyed in the 39th Central Asian War. [The scientist weaponized his own creation](https://animewarcrimetribunal.wordpress.com/2023/12/16/review-pluto/), pouring the entire apparatus of his grief and hatred into a robot that had not asked to carry it. Pluto did not choose to become a weapon. He was turned into one by someone else's inability to process loss.

This is Urasawa's most structurally precise move. Pluto — the character, the creature — is the embodiment of [what war does to civilians](https://medium.com/@Rahul.CK/pluto-hate-to-accept-or-to-overcome-8123894343ec): it conscripts them. It takes beings built for peace and fills them with the concentrated hatred of people who lost something. The robot who tears apart the world's greatest robots is not malicious. He is a vessel. He cannot help what he has become.

## The Question the Series Is Actually Asking

Pluto is structured as a mystery — who is killing the seven greatest robots, and why — but [as the Kotaku review identifies](https://kotaku.com/pluto-netflix-anime-review-1850975057), the mystery is really the container for a philosophical question Urasawa keeps circling: can you build beings capable of genuine love without also building the capacity for genuine hatred? The answer, assembled across eight hours of increasingly bleak evidence, is no.

[Atom/Astro Boy's arc in the final episodes](https://lostinanime.com/2023/12/pluto-08-end-and-series-review/) makes this explicit in the most uncomfortable way possible. When Tenma revives Atom using Gesicht's memory card — the one that contained the suppressed hatred — [Atom awakens full of violence, barely recognizable](https://animerants.net/2023/12/16/pluto-thoughts-on-episodes-7-and-8/). Then, when Gesicht's final memories reach him — memories not of hatred but of love for the child in front of him, for Helena back home — Atom processes all of it. He absorbs the inherited hatred, understands it, and chooses to put it down. He almost crushes a snail in his hand. He stops.

This is not a triumphant moment. It is barely an escape. The character who is supposed to represent Tezuka's hope — Astro Boy, the mechanical child who embodies humanity's best aspirations for artificial intelligence — only survives morally intact by the thinnest margin, because someone who understood hatred was able to tell him the truth about it before dying.

## Why the Iraq Allegory Works Here

[Several critics flagged the political parallel immediately](https://joysauce.com/netflixs-new-pluto-borrows-the-politics-of-astro-boy/), and Urasawa never tried to hide it — the manga was written during the Iraq War, and the 39th Central Asian War's "robots of mass destruction" is not a metaphor that requires decoding. But the allegory works *better* in the Tezuka frame than it would in realistic fiction, for a specific reason.

In a realistic war story, the presence of violence confirms what everyone already suspects: human beings are capable of this. In Tezuka's world, we believed otherwise. The robots have rights. The international community has institutions. Things are better. And still: the war happened. The justification was fabricated. The deaths were real. The grief in the aftermath is identical.

This is the structural advantage of setting the allegory inside Tezuka's utopia rather than a realistic future: the war in Pluto is more condemnable, more obscene, more specifically indicting than it would be in a grittier frame. [In a world of robot rights and international law](https://artreview.com/robot-rights-and-android-dreams/), the war had no excuse. Nobody could claim the system wasn't good enough. The system was good. People went to war anyway. Which means the problem was never the system.

[As the ANN retrospective captures](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/this-week-in-anime/2023-11-02/.204013), Urasawa is adapting a world that was already, in Tezuka's hands, a comment on the anxieties of its era. [Tezuka was a survivor of the firebombing of Osaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osamu_Tezuka), and *Astro Boy* — the robot boy who transformed the terror of atomic power into something hopeful — grew directly from that trauma. His pacifism was not abstract. It came from having watched modern technology obliterate his city. Urasawa's Pluto picks up that thread and continues it for a new era of catastrophic, unjustified violence: what does the optimist's heir do with the knowledge that the optimist was right about what was possible, and the world still chose war?

The implication is not that progress is impossible. It is that progress does not remove the risk. A society of beings capable of deep feeling — of the attachment that generates rights, families, genuine grief — is also a society of beings capable of the wars that shatter them. You cannot legislate the capacity for hatred out of beings who are genuinely alive. You can only ask them, again and again, to choose otherwise.

Gesicht does not choose otherwise. He cannot. He is too human for that.

That is the tragedy. That is also [why Pluto is the best argument Urasawa ever made](https://gundamnews.org/pluto-naoki-urasawas-scifi-thriller-is-a-masterpiece-on-the-futility-of-hatred/) — not because it offers a solution, but because it shows exactly how the problem works, from the inside, in a world that had every reason to have solved it already.

Tezuka built a world that deserved better. Urasawa took that world seriously enough to show what "better" still couldn't prevent.
