The most devastating thing Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time does happens quietly, in a rice paddy, before any of the apocalyptic machinery even gets going.

A new Rei — no memories, no programming, no sense of self beyond the fact that she was recently decanted from a container — stands in the sun and figures out what soil feels like. She learns the names of vegetables. She gets tired from labor and discovers that this is somehow satisfying. She smiles at old women who teach her to cook. She experiences, for the first time in her existence, the texture of ordinary life. And then she dissolves, her constituent matter returning to wherever Reis come from, because she was always a vessel and the film was never going to let her keep what she found.

This is not a subplot. This is the question the entire Rebuild tetralogy was building toward.

## The Character Built Wrong for Every Previous Version of Evangelion

Rei Ayanami has always been the franchise's most instrumentalized figure — in every sense. In the TV series, she is a vessel for Yui Ikari's soul, engineered for compliance, and her key moment of agency (choosing to merge with Lilith rather than execute Gendo's plan) arrives only because she'd absorbed enough of Shinji to have something like a preference. In The End of Evangelion, she becomes briefly cosmic and then briefly nothing — her defiance of Gendo is reactive, not chosen; she doesn't want something for herself so much as she refuses something for someone else. The distinction matters. Refusing a command isn't the same thing as wanting a life. She never gets to be simply a person who wanted ordinary things.

Rei-Q is different from every previous incarnation not because she's less capable, but because she's earlier. She's a Rei who hasn't been trained yet, who has no purpose assigned, who arrives in a world she was never prepped for. In [EvaGeeks' wiki entry on the character](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Rei_(Provisional_Name)), she's formally listed as having no memories of her predecessor — which is the point. She's the philosophical thought experiment the franchise had been avoiding: what would Rei be if she weren't being used for something?

The answer, and the film's thesis, is that she would learn to want things. Small things. Food that tastes good. The satisfaction of physical work. The warmth of being around people who aren't afraid of her. She'd find out that being alive — the mundane, bodily, unglamorous version of being alive — is worth having. And she would not get to keep it.

## Village-3 as a Register the Franchise Had Never Used Before

The village sequences in Thrice Upon a Time are aesthetically unprecedented in Evangelion. Where the franchise built itself on claustrophobic interiors, red oceans, and the lurid palette of existential crisis, Village-3 is rendered in greens, golds, and clear blues. Rice paddies. Communal meals. Chickens. The color temperature shifts so radically from everything else in the film that it reads almost like a different work — which is exactly the point.

Anno, in a [TIME magazine interview](https://time.com/6090247/neon-genesis-evangelion-thrice-upon-a-time-hideaki-anno/) after the film's 2021 release, described his evolution as a filmmaker in terms that illuminate these sequences directly: "25 years ago, when I first created Evangelion, the world was a different place, and I was a different person. The way I described things was more focused on the inner self, and describing the inner world. Right now, I think the time is right to focus on how I describe the outer world."

Village-3 is the outer world. Rei-Q experiencing it — tentatively, completely, with the full attention of someone for whom nothing is routine — is the formal embodiment of this shift. Anno spent 25 years mapping interiority with terrifying precision. The village sequences are him asking whether that same precision can be applied to the simple fact of being somewhere, doing something physical, being glad you did.

The editing tempo makes the argument for him. Where the rest of the film cuts with urgency — the WILLE battle sequences are aggressive and dense — the village scenes breathe. Long holds on Rei's hands in the soil. Cuts that linger on faces rather than moving away. This isn't just tonal contrast; it's the film teaching the viewer how to watch differently, slowing down the eye the same way Rei is slowing down for the first time.

## What Her Death Actually Costs

There's a reading of Rei-Q's dissolution as tragedy-at-a-distance: sad, certainly, but she was always going to dissolve, she knew it, and the film moves past it fairly quickly into its climactic machinery. This reading underestimates what the film is doing.

Rei-Q's death is not an interruption of her arc. It is her arc. The entire point is that she found out life was worth wanting, in full knowledge that she couldn't keep it. This is the specific problem Evangelion has always circled — the question of whether existence has value when it's fragile, uncertain, and ends — but where the original series and EoE answered it through grand philosophical apparatus (Instrumentality, AT Fields, the Human Instrumentality Project), Rei-Q answers it through farming. Through learning someone's name. Through being tired in a good way.

Her dissolution costs something precisely because she earned it. A Rei who never learned anything would dissolve as a tragedy of waste. A Rei who spent her final weeks discovering that ordinary life is actually livable dissolves as a statement: she proved it, she demonstrated it, and now it's Shinji's job to build the world where that can be permanent.

## The Question It's Answering About Anno

The original Neon Genesis Evangelion was made by a man in active depression who was, by many accounts, not sure he wanted to continue existing. That context doesn't diminish it — it's part of what makes the series as precise as it is about the phenomenology of withdrawal and self-destruction. But it means the series could only honestly go as far as its creator could see: to the threshold, not past it.

Thrice Upon a Time was made by a man who had done substantial recovery work. NHK spent four years following Anno with exclusive access during the film's production, resulting in *Professional: Work Style — Anno Hideaki Special* and an extended cut titled ["Sayonara Subete no Evangelion: Anno Hideaki no 1214-Nichi"](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2021-08-20/amazon-prime-video-streams-hideaki-anno-documentaries-with-english-subtitles/.176472) (Goodbye, All of Evangelion: Hideaki Anno's 1,214 Days) — both now available on Amazon Prime with English subtitles. The documentaries feature Miyazaki and Ghibli producer Toshio Suzuki observing a filmmaker who, per Miyazaki's description, sheds blood for his films; Moyoco Anno's interviews document the domestic cost. In between, Anno directed Shin Godzilla (2016), which he credited as having genuinely restored his ability to work.

Rei-Q's arc is the narrative embodiment of what Anno could see in 2021 that he couldn't see in 1997: that learning to want ordinary life is not a capitulation, not a betrayal of suffering, not a lie about how hard it is. It's just — possible. The village sequences are visually the most un-Evangelion thing Anno has ever put on screen, and they are also, for the first time, shots of something that looks genuinely livable.

## Why the Ending Works When It Shouldn't

The film's finale — Shinji creating a new world without Evangelions, one where the people he cared about get to exist without the freight of apocalyptic instrumentalization — should read as wish fulfillment. By any normal critical standard, an ending this clean, this explicitly hopeful, would be a betrayal of everything the franchise established.

It doesn't feel like a betrayal, and Rei-Q is the reason why. The film earned the right to a clean ending by first demonstrating, through her arc, exactly what it would mean for a world without EVAs to be better. A world where a person built to be a vessel could keep the things she found in a rice paddy. Where being tired from labor is allowed to just be satisfying, without the weight of being useful for something. The loop breaks because we've already seen, in precise detail, what breaking it would mean — not as abstraction but as a girl learning to cook rice in a survivor village.

The final image of the franchise is blue water, not red. It's a choice that would have been dishonest in 1997, when Anno was still in the red water and could only write from there. In 2021, it's the most honest thing he could offer: here is what it looks like when the loop breaks. Here is what Rei-Q was pointing at. It was worth it.
