The premise of Tokyo Babel is, on paper, the most theologically ambitious setup in visual novel history. Not Fate/stay night-level mythology-borrowing, where Arthurian legend and Norse gods are costumed into a mages' war. Not Evangelion-level Christian symbolism, where angels are geometry and sefirot are stage dressing. Tokyo Babel, the [2012 action VN by Propeller](https://mangagamer.org/tokyobabel/) and writer Yuuichirou Higashide, posits something genuinely radical: that every major world scripture is simultaneously, literally true. The Bible. The Quran. The Talmud. Dante's Divine Comedy. Milton's Paradise Lost. The Gnostic texts. The Kabbalah. The Epic of Gilgamesh. All of it, true at the same time. And now something has gone wrong with God, the gates of Heaven are sealed, Hell has overflowed, and the last surviving humans, angels, and demons have to climb a floating purgatory to fix it.

That is not a premise. That is a theological detonation. The implications alone could sustain a dozen articles.

Tokyo Babel does not write those articles. It writes a chuunige.

## The Thought Experiment the Game Refuses to Run

What actually happens if every world scripture is true simultaneously? This isn't a trivial question. The Bible's God is singular and jealous. The Gnostic tradition proposes a demiurge — a lesser creator distinct from the true unknowable God — which maps uncomfortably onto the patriarchal Yahweh. The Talmud posits a Lilith preceding Eve, born from the same dirt as Adam, who fled rather than submit. Islam's Allah is the same Abrahamic God, but with a radically different jurisprudence and eschatology. Dante's cosmology layers these into a structured afterlife that has almost nothing to do with the spare biblical text. Milton's Satan is a Romantic figure of tragic grandeur, which conflicts with every orthodox reading.

[Tokyo Babel throws all of these into a single blender](https://vnrw.wordpress.com/2016/03/28/vn-tokyo-babel/) and the result is not theological chaos — it is, impressively, a coherent world. The game's setting acknowledges that its "all texts true" premise would produce exactly this kind of internal contradiction, and it uses those contradictions structurally. Lilith's mythology is drawn from the competing Elohist and Yahwist strands of Genesis — the game explicitly acknowledges these two textual traditions as two distinct historical realities that the character embodies. Raziel, the angel who in Jewish mysticism was the keeper of divine secrets and allegedly dictated the *Sefer Raziel HaMalach* to Adam after the Fall, appears with her canonical role intact. [The roster of antagonists](https://operationrainfall.com/2016/04/20/review-tokyo-babel/) — Astaroth, Belial, Dantalion, Camael, Leviathan, Samael — all carry their specific mythological functions drawn from demonological texts and grimoires.

Higashide did the research. On that, there is no argument. [The sources he drew from](https://fuwanovel.moe/2016/04/tokyo-babel/) — the Bible, the Quran, the Divine Comedy, Paradise Lost, the Gilgamesh epic, the Talmud, the Timaeus, Gnostic and Kabbalistic apocrypha — form one of the most comprehensive mythological frameworks ever attempted in a VN. This is not window dressing. The characters exist within their traditions and argue from them. The theological debates between immortals before battle are, when they occur, genuinely interesting. The game grasps that a world where every scripture is true would be a world in profound crisis not just physically but metaphysically — because the scriptures do not agree on what salvation means, who is saved, or what God wants.

That is remarkable. The problem is what happens after the argument ends.

## The Genre That Eats Its Premises

[Chuunige](https://sweetmonia.com/The-Lost-Konpeitos/a-guide-for-visual-novel-terms-genres-charage-moege-plotege-chuunige-ichaicha-kamige-kusoge-nakige-utsuge-eroge-nukige-lolige/) — the subgenre built on power hierarchies, named techniques, escalating supernatural conflict, and melodramatic stakes — has a specific metabolic requirement: it must keep moving. Debate slows things down. Introspection slows things down. The genre operates on the same principle as a shonen battle arc: establish the philosophical opposition, then resolve it through combat. The stronger fighter wins; the argument is settled. This is why [Tsukihime and Fate/stay night](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/VisualNovel/TokyoBabel), the ancestral chuunige, structure their routes around fights as thesis statements rather than as genuine exchanges.

The problem for Tokyo Babel is that its premise cannot be resolved through a fight. You cannot punch your way to a coherent theory of divine sovereignty. Whether God's absence is a moral failure, a test, a collapse, or simply the end of a contract — these questions require dwell time. They require characters sitting with uncertainty. They require the narrative to model what it feels like to believe something about the divine and then watch the architecture of that belief become a pile of rubble. Dostoevsky spent 900 pages on this. Dante needed three canticles. Milton needed twelve books of blank verse.

Tokyo Babel has 50 hours, and it spends most of them staging [elaborate supernatural combat sequences](https://www.rpgfan.com/review/tokyo-babel/).

![Tokyo Babel battle CG](https://mangagamer.org/tokyobabel/images/gal/TokyoBabel_cg01.jpg)

The pattern is consistent across all three routes: the pilgrimage through Babel's seven layers brings the protagonists into contact with a "Master" — a former pilgrim who lost their mind trying to reach Heaven — and each Master is a walking theological position. Their madness is their theology taken to its extreme. This is smart. This is structurally sophisticated. And every time it is about to become genuinely interesting, someone activates a named technique and the debate ends in violence.

Fan criticism has consistently identified this gap. [One of the most thorough community analyses](https://deluscar.wordpress.com/2016/06/28/tokyo-babel-review/) notes that "plot points get introduced, teased and tossed aside like nothing" — the theological material surfaces, generates momentum, and is abandoned in favor of the next fight. Another reviewer [writing for RPGFan](https://www.rpgfan.com/review/tokyo-babel/) put it bluntly: the game "tries so hard to be epic only to result in an overlong and boring narrative," a verdict that lands precisely because the gap between ambition and execution is so visibly wide. When your premise demands theological patience and your genre demands combat escalation, you get a 50-hour tug of war that neither wins.

## What Higashide Does Well, and Where It Matters

Higashide Yuuichirou — [who went on to write Fate/Apocrypha and contribute to Fate/Grand Order](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Yuuuichirou_Higashide) — is exceptional at one specific thing: building archetypes from mythological raw material and then making them feel like people rather than symbols. Lilith in Tokyo Babel is not the generic demon-seductress the name usually produces in fiction. Her characterization emerges from the actual tension in her source material — the competing patriarchal and egalitarian strands of early Judaism that the two Genesis creation accounts represent. She is not evil. She is a being who refused a hierarchy she found unjust, who has spent centuries being redefined by the people who feared her, and who wants, at the end of the world, to be seen on her own terms.

That is a real character built from real scholarship. [The oprainfall review](https://operationrainfall.com/2016/04/20/review-tokyo-babel/) correctly notes that Higashide "did a truly amazing job at researching his themes, capturing the essence of some very complicated beings like Astaroth perfectly." This is Propeller's [distinctive style as a studio](https://glitchwave.com/game-company/propeller/) — their heroines are never purely romantic fixtures; they carry their own ideological weight. In Ayakashibito, this meant figures whose positions were genuinely opposed to the protagonist's. In Tokyo Babel, it means every major character is a walking doctrinal argument.

The Sorami route, [consistently praised as the strongest](https://vengilikes.wordpress.com/2016/04/28/tokyo-babel-visual-novel-review/) across community discussion, works precisely because it foregrounds this. Sorami is the only human heroine in a cast of immortals, which forces every conversation she has with Lilith or Raziel to become implicitly about what salvation means when you are finite and they are not. She can die. The others cannot. In a game about reaching Heaven, mortality is the only actually urgent stake — and [her route delivers what the others mostly promise](https://deluscar.wordpress.com/2016/06/28/tokyo-babel-review/): a romance that develops with enough room to feel real before the final confrontation, and theological questions that land because the person asking them has something to lose. [One reviewer noted the game](https://vnrecs.github.io/TokyoBabel.html) "gave my tear ducts a serious workout with every route" — but that emotional investment concentrates in Sorami precisely because the human perspective is the only one that feels genuinely mortal.

But Sorami's route is one of three, and requires playing the others first. The larger architecture keeps burying its most interesting moments in setup and combat.

## The Protagonist Problem

Tendou Setsuna is the only character in Tokyo Babel who does not benefit from the game's mythological research. He is an original creation — a human with supernatural attributes, denied humanity since birth, navigating the end of the world — and [the community has noted consistently](https://forums.fuwanovel.moe/blogs/entry/1117-tokyo-babel/) that he is "emotionally muted" and difficult to connect with. This is not a flaw in the writing so much as it is a structural trap that chuunige frequently falls into: the protagonist must be strong enough to matter in supernatural conflict, which means giving him power, which means distancing him from ordinary human stakes, which means the reader loses the ground-level perspective that would make the theological questions feel urgent.

There is an instructive contrast with Evangelion here, which also borrows heavily from Jewish and Christian mysticism to build a world at the edge of annihilation. Anno made Shinji demonstrably weak, and made his psychological collapse the point — the theological architecture works because a broken boy is moving through it. Setsuna cannot collapse; he has to keep climbing the tower. So when the game asks what it means to be human in a cosmos designed by and for immortals, we are hearing the question from someone who barely feels human himself. [Steam reviews calling the game the "BEST visual novel"](https://store.steampowered.com/app/443380/Tokyo_Babel/) tend to come from action enthusiasts for whom this is a feature, not a bug. For readers who want the theological material to land emotionally, Setsuna is the hole in the floor.

## A Premise That Deserved More

Tokyo Babel [holds a 7.69 user rating on VNDB](https://fuwanovel.moe/2016/04/tokyo-babel/) and "Very Positive" on Steam, which tells you it works on its own terms. As a chuunige — as a vehicle for sustained supernatural action sequences anchored in deep mythological research — it is among the most literate examples of the genre that the English market received in [the 2016 MangaGamer translation window](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2015-07-25/mangagamer-adds-propeller-tokyo-babel-the-shadows-of-pygmalion-visual-novels/.90885). The art is dynamic. [The 336GameReviews critique](https://www.336gamereviews.com/tokyo-babel-review/) rightly notes that replaying the routes delivers genuinely fresh content each time rather than recycled scenes — a structural achievement that most VNs fumble. The cast, stripped of Setsuna, is genuinely excellent.

But the premise keeps haunting you. A world where every scripture is simultaneously true is a world that makes every act of faith retroactively correct and simultaneously insufficient — because every tradition promised something different, and all of them are now staring at the same locked gate. That is one of the most interesting apocalypse framings in fiction. What does prayer mean when you can see the angels in physical form and the angels cannot get home? What does salvation mean when the infrastructure of salvation has broken down? What does it mean to believe in a God who demonstrably exists but is no longer answering?

Tokyo Babel touches these questions repeatedly. It just cannot stop long enough to answer them. The fights keep starting. The genre keeps winning. And somewhere in the wreckage of seven layers of Babel, the most interesting VN that Propeller never quite made is still waiting to be written.
