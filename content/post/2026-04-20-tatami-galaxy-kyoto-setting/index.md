Tomihiko Morimi did not grow up wanting to live in Kyoto. He was from Nara Prefecture, went to Kyoto University because his father had, and initially didn't much care for the city. It wasn't until [his first novel was published](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomihiko_Morimi) that he understood what he had. "Fiction is basically a world of untruths," he [later told an interviewer](https://www.studykyoto.jp/en/magazine/2022/10/12/kyoto-through-novels/). "But I feel like in Kyoto, the untruths don't get in the way."

That sentence is the key to everything *The Tatami Galaxy* does with its setting. The show's parallel timelines — the same student, the same room, the same faces, the same failed attempts at a rose-colored campus life — require a city that can hold multiple simultaneous versions of itself without strain. Kyoto is that city. Its ancient temples, its prewar boardinghouses, its forest that is older than the city itself: Kyoto doesn't experience its past as memory. It experiences it as infrastructure. The loops feel natural here because the city has always been a place where time folds in on itself.

## The Boardinghouse That Time Forgot

The Shimogamo Yūsuisō — the dilapidated student boarding house where Watashi lives in his 4.5-tatami room — is [described in the novel](https://archive.org/stream/manga_The_Tatami_Galaxy/The%20Tatami%20Galaxy_djvu.txt) with precise historical coordinates. The place burned down in the chaos at the end of the Tokugawa shogunate, was rebuilt in exactly the same fashion, and had not been renovated since. Watashi thinks, on first seeing it during his orientation tour, that he has wandered into Kowloon Walled City.

This is not comic exaggeration. This is Kyoto as architectural principle. The city is full of structures whose continuity with their pre-Meiji incarnations is literal — rebuilt to the same specifications, unchanged by modernization, still in use. [Kyoto's preservation culture](https://www.japan-guide.com/e/e3941.html) is unlike anything in Tokyo, which rebuilt itself after earthquake and firebombing with no nostalgia for its previous forms. Kyoto's relationship to its own past is something stranger: a city that keeps choosing to stay what it was.

The boarding house's function in the show is more than color. It's the physical embodiment of the show's central problem. Watashi lives in a building that hasn't moved since the Tokugawa shogunate — a structure that is itself a kind of loop, a thing that has been continuously rebuilt to remain what it was. He wants to escape his stagnation and find a new life, but he returns to his 4.5-tatami room every time. The room is the thesis. The building that never changes is the condition he is trying to break free from.

The title's literal translation — ["4½ Tatami Mythological Chronicles" (Yojōhan Shinwa Taikei)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tatami_Galaxy) — makes the room the organizing cosmological principle. The tatami mat is a pre-modern unit of space: a measurement in terms of human bodies rather than abstract dimensions, calibrated to a person lying down. Japanese housing still uses it, but it is a survival from an earlier era of living, and naming your novel after it is a statement about the kind of space being described — intimate, archaic, continuous.

## The Forest of Truth

The Tadasu-no-mori, the sacred grove of [Shimogamo Shrine](https://www.kyoto-np.com/en/articles/-/756), is described by the Kyoto Shimbun as "the only patch of virgin, primordial forest in the city" — older than Kyoto itself, with zelkova and enoki trees that have stood for two to six centuries. Every summer, used book dealers set up under those trees for the [Shimogamo Noryo Furuhon Matsuri](https://openkyoto.com/kyoto-used-book-festival/), one of Japan's largest outdoor secondhand book markets: 800,000 volumes available under an ancient canopy, the Izumigawa stream running along one side. The forest's name means "the forest of truth" — *tadasu* refers to a clearing-away of deception.

This is where Watashi meets Akashi. Every loop. Every timeline. He runs into her at the book fair in the Forest of Truth, and every time he fails to do anything about it.

Morimi chose this location with full awareness of what it contains. [His novels are full of real Kyoto locations](https://jamesofwalsh.com/2015/05/17/tatami-galaxy-and-kyoto/) — the Kamo River bridge where key scenes resolve, the Pontocho alley that runs alongside the water, the Kyoto University buildings whose clocktower is visible in multiple episodes. But the Shimogamo book fair is the show's emotional center, and it is a real place with a real history that runs entirely counter to Watashi's self-deception. He keeps returning to the Forest of Truth and refusing to see it clearly. The irony is not subtle. It is embedded in the geography.

## The University and Its Weight

[Kyoto University](https://www.kyoto-u.ac.jp/en/current/campus-life) is not incidental to the show's social texture. It is Japan's second most prestigious national university, the one historically associated with independent, eccentric, anti-establishment thought — whose student body [ran some of Japan's most intense campus protests in 1968-69](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1968%E2%80%931969_Japanese_university_protests), whose [Yoshida Dorm](https://theface.com/culture/inside-kyotos-radical-self-governed-student-accommodation-yoshida-dorm-japan-students-photography) has been a self-governed student residence since 1913. This is the institution where a generation of students decided the correct response to inherited systems was to set them on fire.

Watashi fails to do anything that dramatic. He joins clubs, makes schemes, and returns to his 4.5-tatami room unchanged. The irony is compressed by the setting: he is at the university whose mythology says that being here means something, failing to make it mean anything, in a building whose continued existence proves that refusing to change is always easier than the alternative. The weight of Kyoto University's history of student radicalism is the backdrop against which Watashi's small-scale cowardice is measured — and found more typical than anyone would like to admit.

[Morimi himself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomihiko_Morimi) went to Kyoto University from Nara, slightly outside the city, slightly outside the main current of urban life, and wrote his first novel — *Tower of the Sun* — while he was a student. That novel won the Japan Fantasy Novel Award while he was at the university. *The Tatami Galaxy* is autobiographically adjacent in ways that matter: the clubs, the tatami boarding houses, the book fair, the feeling of those years as something you will later be required to account for. Morimi is writing from inside the experience he's depicting.

## What Kyoto Lets Morimi Do

"Fiction is basically a world of untruths. But I feel like in Kyoto, the untruths don't get in the way."

This is a specific claim about why Kyoto works as a literary setting, and it goes beyond "ancient city = interesting atmosphere." What Morimi is describing is a quality of the city's relationship to narrative: Kyoto doesn't resist fictional elaboration. It has so many layers of history, so many periods of construction and destruction and reconstruction, so many sacred and secular functions coexisting in the same physical space, that adding a fictional frame doesn't feel like an imposition. The Tatami Galaxy's parallel realities are another set of layers over a city already stratified by centuries.

[The Night Is Short, Walk On Girl](https://myanimelist.net/movie/11769/Yoru_wa_Mijikashi_Aruke_yo_Otome) — Morimi's companion work to The Tatami Galaxy, the female-perspective picaresque set in the same Kyoto — confirms this. Both works require a city that can absorb magical realism without buckling. Kyoto absorbs it because it is already, in some sense, a magical-realist city: a place that has maintained the physical form of a premodern capital inside a modern industrial nation, that has [protected its historic structures](https://www.japan-guide.com/e/e3941.html) while surrounding them with convenience stores and vending machines, that is simultaneously an ancient sacred site and a tourist destination and a functioning city of a million and a half people. Tokyo was rebuilt from scratch after the 1923 earthquake and the wartime firebombing; it has the forward-facing relationship to its own history that comes from having little choice. Kyoto had to be argued out of becoming a nuclear target — it survived the war physically intact, and that survival is part of its character.

The loops of The Tatami Galaxy feel natural here because Kyoto itself is structured as a loop — the same festivals every year, the same streets in the same configurations, the same shrine forests offering the same shade to different generations of students. The city that doesn't renovate its student boarding houses is the right city for a show about a protagonist who can't renovate himself.

## The Pilgrimage and What It Confirms

The writer James Walsh, visiting Kyoto after watching the show, describes finding that ["all I see when I go to Kyoto is Tatami Galaxy."](https://jamesofwalsh.com/2015/05/17/tatami-galaxy-and-kyoto/) The Kamo River bridge, the Shimogamo grove, the Pontocho alley — he sees them overlaid with the show's imagery. That's the specific effect good literary geography produces. It doesn't just describe a place; it creates a second way of perceiving the real one that coexists with the first.

[The Shimogamo book fair](https://kogeiart.kyoto.jp/articles/post-2673/) is now, for many viewers of the show, inseparable from Watashi's repeated failure to pursue Akashi under the ancient trees. The Kamo River is inseparable from Higuchi's drunken singing. The 4.5-tatami room is not any specific room in any specific building — but it refers to real rooms that still exist in the streets around the university, and any Kyoto University student who has lived in them knows exactly what Watashi's claustrophobia means.

![The Tatami Galaxy Shimogamo neighborhood still](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1790/112316.jpg)

Morimi wrote Kyoto into existence as a fictional space by noticing that Kyoto was already partly fictional — that a city committed to preserving its untruths, its historically impossible continuities, its insistence on remaining what it was, was perfectly suited to a story about the impossibility of escaping your own patterns. The protagonist cannot renovate the building he lives in. The city cannot renovate itself. They are, the show quietly argues, the same problem.
