The original Nagatoro — the one posted on Pixiv between 2011 and 2015, before Kodansha came calling — was a genuine sadist. [She broke Senpai until he declared his love in desperation](https://nagatoro.fandom.com/wiki/Webcomic), then walked away satisfied. She didn't apologize. She didn't show tenderness. The pleasure was in the destruction.

The character we have in the 2023 second season, *Don't Toy with Me, Miss Nagatoro 2nd Attack*, is almost unrecognizable from that original. She's still called Nagatoro, still teases, still occupies the same structural role in the same plot. But she flinches when she thinks she's gone too far. She bridles at anyone else messing with Senpai. In the final episode, she goes on a date with him. The trajectory, tracked over two seasons of anime and [fourteen volumes of manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_Toy_with_Me,_Miss_Nagatoro), is from cruelty to courtship.

What's interesting is that the change isn't just cosmetic. The *function* of Nagatoro's attention has transformed, and the 2nd Attack is where that transformation reaches its logical conclusion. This season, finally, is about what the series was always pretending to be about: the boy, not the girl. Senpai. The person being seen.

## What the Webcomic Was, and What the Manga Became

Understanding why the 2nd Attack lands requires understanding how far the source material has traveled. [Nanashi (774)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_Toy_with_Me,_Miss_Nagatoro) came to the series from a doujin career defined by power dynamics that didn't resolve wholesomely. The original webcomic wasn't satire; it was the thing itself — domination as entertainment, suffering as spectacle.

When Kodansha serialized the property in Magazine Pocket starting in 2017, [the transformation was deliberate and extensive](https://nagatoro.fandom.com/wiki/Webcomic). Nagatoro's sadism was walked back chapter by chapter until what remained was tsundere energy: a girl who teases because she can't say the direct thing, who expresses affection through a vocabulary of mild torment. The manga leaned into the ijirimono subgenre — *ijirimono* referring to stories structured around teasing as a relationship dynamic — which [had been gaining commercial traction](https://medium.com/anitay-official/of-course-you-can-step-on-i-mean-tease-me-the-age-of-tease-is-upon-us-22ae0ba9d679) alongside titles like *Uzaki-chan Wants to Hang Out!* and *Takagi-san*.

The mainstream iteration of Nagatoro works as that — a reliably enjoyable ijirimono romcom, one that helped define the subgenre's early-2020s commercial moment alongside *Uzaki-chan Wants to Hang Out!* and [*My Teasing Master Takagi-san*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/My_Teasing_Master_Takagi-san), all serialized in the same era and all built around the same basic tension — but it also works as something slightly different: a story about a specific kind of healing. Naoto Hachioji, Senpai, is introduced as a boy who has preemptively withdrawn from the world. He hides in the library. He draws manga that he keeps to himself. He cannot handle any form of scrutiny without dissolving into embarrassed compliance. The opening of the first season frames him less as shy and more as someone who has decided, quietly, that he doesn't matter enough to defend.

![Hayase Nagatoro](https://myanimelist.net/images/characters/3/522251.jpg)

Nagatoro's function in this frame isn't cruelty. It's refusal. She refuses to let him be invisible. Her teasing is intrusive because it requires a response, requires him to be present, requires him to register as a person who can be affected. She's relentlessly, exhaustingly interested in him — his reactions, his limits, his actual opinion — in a way nobody else in the series is.

## Why the 2nd Attack Earns Its Reputation

The first season established the dynamic; the [2nd Attack](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro-2nd-attack/episodes-1-12/.196632) is where the thesis comes due.

Senpai spends this season actively growing. He learns that Nagatoro has feelings. He starts defending himself, and then defending her. The season's central arc — the culture festival art competition, in which Senpai's work is challenged by Sunomiya, a former art club president who views him as unworthy of the space he's occupying — forces the question the first season circled endlessly: does Senpai actually believe in himself?

[He does, in this arc, for the first time.](https://www.anime-planet.com/forum/threads/dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro-2nd-attack.339822/) He defends the work publicly. He gets angry — specifically, pointedly angry — when someone laughs at art someone poured effort into. Nagatoro witnesses this and responds not with more teasing but with genuine warmth, telling him, against her own embarrassed instincts, that what he did was good. The scene is small by the standard of anime catharsis, but it carries weight precisely because it's been two seasons in building.

The [Honey's Anime review](https://honeysanime.com/ijiranaide-nagatoro-san-2nd-attack-dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro-2nd-attack-review-actual-progression-in-a-romcom-anime/) headlined the season as "actual progression in a romcom anime," and that's not wrong — the date that closes the season, awkward and sincere and gently comic, is the endpoint of a character arc for Senpai that the series has been threading through its entire run.

The progression matters because the genre usually refuses it. [Teasing romcoms are structurally incentivized to freeze their protagonists](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2021/06/27/a-review-of-dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro/) in the status quo — any real advancement deflates the tension that drives the weekly engagement. *Takagi-san* is the cleanest example: Nishikata never gets better at not being teased, never admits his feelings, because the format requires the gap. Nagatoro makes the bet that watching Senpai actually change is more interesting than watching him stay stuck, and the 2nd Attack is the season that redeems that bet.

## The Ijirimono Appeal, Honestly Examined

The fan discourse around Nagatoro season 1 was, notoriously, messy. [Critics debated](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=24896) whether the teasing crossed the line into bullying, whether Senpai's compliance was wish fulfillment for masochistic impulses, whether the show was encoding an unhealthy relationship dynamic as romantic. These concerns weren't baseless — the first few chapters of both the manga and anime carry genuine discomfort, and the webcomic's history casts a long shadow.

The 2nd Attack largely sidesteps this because the dynamic has shifted. Nagatoro's teasing in this season [is more playful than penetrating](https://readysteadycut.com/2023/01/22/dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro-season-2-review/), calibrated to the current shape of their relationship rather than to the earlier power gap. More importantly, the show has started to show her emotional exposure as well as his. When Nagatoro is embarrassed, when she overreaches and catches herself, when she's caught genuinely admiring something Senpai did — these moments distribute the vulnerability more evenly and make the relationship feel less like a dynamic and more like two people figuring something out.

![Nagatoro 2nd Attack key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1902/129579l.jpg)

This is the ijirimono subgenre doing what it does best when it works: using the asymmetry of teasing as a metaphor for the more general asymmetry of early romantic interest, where one person always sees the other more clearly than they're seen in return. [The appeal isn't masochism](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2023/winter/dont-toy-with-me-miss-nagatoro-2nd-attack/.193151) — it's the fantasy of being known. What Nagatoro knows about Senpai is specific and accumulated and real. The teasing is the expression of that knowledge. When it works — when *Nagatoro* works — it's because that knowing-of-someone comes through.

## What Gets Lost in the Sanitization

The transformation from webcomic to 2nd Attack isn't without cost. The original Nagatoro carried something the serialized version doesn't: an acknowledgment that the person doing the tormenting might not be acting from love. The webcomic's fifth chapter — the final installment before serialization — ends with the webcomic Nagatoro walking away after Senpai breaks down, satisfied, with no resolution. There is no reassurance. There is no softening. [The webcomic's Nagatoro](https://nagatoro.fandom.com/wiki/Webcomic) doesn't have a hidden heart of gold; she just enjoys it. That version was asking something genuine: why do we seek the attention of people who hurt us? What does it say about us that being noticed by someone who hurts us still feels like being noticed?

That reading is not available in the mainstream Nagatoro. The series committed, with serialization, to the interpretation that Nagatoro's interest was always affection in disguise, and the 2nd Attack doubles down on that commitment. It's a sweeter story. It's probably a healthier fantasy. But [something was shed in the process](https://medium.com/anitay-official/ive-had-enough-miss-nagatoro-6cdfb8b2ba) — a darker self-awareness the original had by accident if not by design.

The 2nd Attack is not a dark show. It's a warm, funny, sincerely felt slow-burn romance that delivers on its character arc in ways the genre routinely refuses to. The final scene — Senpai asking Nagatoro on a date in his halting, still-awkward way, and her saying yes while deliberately not looking at him — is the culmination of two seasons of watching someone rebuild their own sense of worth. That's what Nanashi built here, through the long path from sadistic Pixiv posts to Kodansha serialization to two cours of anime. In the 2nd Attack, it arrives.

The loss of the darkness is a real loss. The gain is a show that actually lets its protagonist change.
