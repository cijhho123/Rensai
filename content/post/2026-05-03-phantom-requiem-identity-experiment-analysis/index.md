Scythe Master — psychologist, handler, architect of the Inferno syndicate's assassination program — is not a simple villain. He is an empiricist. He has a research question: how do you take a person and make them into a perfect weapon? And across the twenty-six episodes of [Phantom: Requiem for the Phantom](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=10541), Bee Train's 2009 adaptation of [Gen Urobuchi's Nitroplus visual novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phantom_of_Inferno), he runs three separate experiments.

All three fail.

That is the show's thesis. Not that assassins are tragic, not that love conquers all, but something more specific and more troubling: every method of erasing a person fails in a different way, and each failure reveals something different about what personhood actually is.

![Phantom: Requiem for the Phantom key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/8/22470l.jpg)

## Experiment One: Brainwashing

Ein is Scythe Master's first and most complete attempt. [She was a girl before she was a weapon](https://phantom-of-inferno.fandom.com/wiki/Scythe_Master) — but he removed her memories, overwritten her personality with conditioning, and produced the most efficient assassin Inferno had seen. She is not cruel. She is not driven by ideology. She simply executes. Scythe Master considers her a masterpiece.

The flaw in the masterpiece: she is incomplete. Without an identity, there is nothing to anchor her to her function beyond the function itself — [and function alone, as the AniDB review notes](https://anidb.net/anime/6256/review), cannot sustain a being capable of genuine interaction indefinitely. She can perform killing without understanding why she performs it, which means she can also, when given sufficient exposure to someone who does understand why — Zwei — begin to ask the question. The emptiness Scythe Master treated as an asset is actually the crack in the architecture.

[As the character analysis at Anime Planet captures](https://www.anime-planet.com/characters/ein-phantom-requiem-for-the-phantom), Ein's journey across the series is a process of reconstruction — moving from a state of total psychological conditioning to a person discovering her own identity. The name she eventually adopts, Elen, is not just a new alias. It is evidence that brainwashing did not destroy what was there before. It only buried it. Give the buried thing enough time, enough contact with something genuine, and it surfaces.

Scythe Master's first experiment tests the hypothesis: *you can make a weapon by removing the person*. The result: incomplete. Removal is not destruction. Identity is not stored in a single location that conditioning can overwrite. It distributes, persists, and reasserts.

## Experiment Two: Natural Instinct

Zwei is the control case. [The Japanese tourist who witnesses an assassination](https://phantom-of-inferno.fandom.com/wiki/Phantom:_Requiem_for_the_Phantom) and is captured by Inferno is not brainwashed into a killer. [He survives the test where Ein is sent to kill him](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/phantom-requiem-for-the-phantom), overpowering her through reflexive instinct alone — which tells Scythe Master everything he needs to know. He is identified as someone with a natural aptitude — a reflexive survival instinct that, under pressure, produces lethality without instruction. Scythe Master's insight, watching Zwei survive the initial test where Ein hunts him, is that authentic killing instinct is more dangerous than manufactured killing reflex.

He is right about that. Zwei is more dangerous than Ein. But the reason he is more dangerous is also the reason he cannot be controlled: his instincts are his. They are not a program installed by conditioning. They are expressions of who he actually is. And a person, even a person with lethal reflexes, forms attachments. Makes choices. Develops loyalties that do not answer to Inferno's organizational chart.

The clearest demonstration of this is when Zwei starts making decisions that Inferno did not authorize — specifically when he begins protecting Ein rather than treating her as an asset or a rival. He is not defecting out of ideology. He is not following a competing set of orders. He is simply acting on what he has become. Scythe Master engineered a weapon and got a person instead, which is the same result as the Ein experiment, just reached from the other direction.

[As the Mechanical Anime Reviews analysis notes](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2021/12/03/urobuchi-december-phantom-requiem-for-a-phantom/), Urobuchi's writing circles constantly around whether identity is your own or what someone else decides for you. Zwei's answer to this question is delivered through action rather than philosophy: his instincts are tools, but they are his tools. He uses them for his own purposes, including the purpose of extracting himself and Ein from Inferno's world.

Scythe Master's second experiment tests the hypothesis: *you can make a weapon by channeling genuine capacity*. The result: also failed, for the opposite reason from the first. Genuine capacity belongs to the person who has it. It cannot be redirected by the organization that identified it without that person's ongoing consent — and ongoing consent means ongoing agency.

## Experiment Three: Hatred

Cal Devens is the third experiment, and the cruelest.

![Cal Devens character](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/6/202729.jpg?s=e124494db9f565e446f0e2acbf0685d8)

When Scythe Master observed that Zwei, an unbrainwashed natural killer, had produced better results than Ein's conditioning, he revised his hypothesis. The problem with Ein was not that she had been a person — it was that she had been emptied. The problem with Zwei was that he retained attachment. What if you created a Phantom who had everything: memory, identity, emotional capacity — but replaced love with hatred? What if you took someone who had a reason to hate, and made that hatred the engine?

[Cal was a girl who depended on Zwei](https://shinmaru.wordpress.com/2009/09/30/in-defense-of-phantoms-end/) — taken in by him, given safety, given something like a family for the first time. [The anime developed this relationship across eight full episodes](https://jonspencerreviews.wordpress.com/2019/07/26/phantom-requiem-for-the-phantom-a-near-perfect-adaptation/), considerably longer than the visual novel, because the writers understood that Cal's tragedy required the foundation to be built first. After a series of events that she experienced as abandonment — though the reality was more complicated — Scythe Master found her and offered her something simple: the skill to act on what she felt. No conditioning. No memory erasure. Just training, and a target.

The resulting assassin, Drei, is the most complete person of the three Phantoms. She has a self. She has a past. She has genuine feeling. She is also, by the time she and Zwei meet again, entirely defined by that feeling turned toward destruction.

[As the fan analysis of Cal's arc makes clear](https://canime.wordpress.com/2009/11/05/phantom-requiem-for-the-phantom-anime-review/), the tragedy of Cal is not that she was broken. It is that she was whole, and her wholeness was the weapon. Scythe Master's third hypothesis — *hatred is cleaner than emptiness, because hatred is authentic* — is in one sense correct. Cal is the most authentic Phantom. She is also the most completely destroyed, because when the hatred that organized her entire self finally encounters its object and fails to resolve anything, there is nothing else left to be.

[Scythe Master's third experiment](https://phantom-of-inferno.fandom.com/wiki/Scythe_Master) fails not because it didn't work, but because it worked too well. A person weaponized through authentic feeling is fully present for the destruction that follows.

## The Requiem Is Not for the Assassins

The title of the series is a memorial. [As the in-defense-of-the-ending essay argues](https://shinmaru.wordpress.com/2009/09/30/in-defense-of-phantoms-end/), every major death in the series happens at the moment when a killer can no longer sustain the killing — either because they have found something worth living for, or because the hatred that sustained them has consumed its object and left nothing. The requiem is for these moments. For the transitions.

But more precisely: the requiem is for the people who existed before Scythe Master's experiments began. Ein before brainwashing. Zwei before Inferno took his name. Cal before the misunderstanding that Scythe Master exploited. [All three of those people are gone.](https://gamefaqs.gamespot.com/boards/2000121-anime-and-manga-other-titles/63675474) Two of them were replaced by weapons. One — Ein, gradually, through her time with Zwei — managed to find her way back to something close to herself, close enough to remember what her name had been before it was taken.

[Gen Urobuchi's work across his career](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gen_Urobuchi) — Fate/Zero, Madoka Magica, Psycho-Pass — returns obsessively to this question of what systems of control do to people who operate within them. Phantom of Inferno is where those obsessions first appear in coherent form. Scythe Master is the prototype for every Urobuchi antagonist who follows: not a sadist but an architect, running a program that produces desired outcomes and calling the casualties experimental data. The Sibyl System in Psycho-Pass is Scythe Master with legal authority and a government contract. [Urobuchi wrote scripts for episodes 6, 18, and 25 of the anime](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/people.php?id=68860), and the moments he contributed are identifiable — they are the ones where the show stops gesturing at its themes and states them directly.

What the three-Phantom structure adds to this concern is methodological specificity. It is not enough for Phantom to say that Inferno is evil and identity-erasure is wrong. That would be a sermon. The show instead constructs three controlled tests, lets each one run to completion, and collects the results. The results, consistently, point to the same finding: personhood is not a layer you can strip away or a drive you can redirect. It is not, as Scythe Master assumes, a problem to be solved.

[The ending's ambiguity](https://randomc.net/2009/09/25/phantom-requiem-for-the-phantom-26-end/) — whether Elen and Reiji die, whether they survive, whether what awaits them is peace or just another form of the world they tried to escape — is legible within this framework. The experiments are over. The results are in. What happens to the subjects afterward is not the point of the study.

The requiem plays for what was lost at the beginning. Everything after that is what happens to people who were used as data.
