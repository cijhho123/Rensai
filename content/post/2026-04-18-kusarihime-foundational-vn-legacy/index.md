You know *Saya no Uta*. You've probably played *Cross Channel*. You know the Heaven's Feel route of *Fate/stay night* is where Kinoko Nasu gets serious. These three works, made by three different writers, define what "intellectual visual novel" means in practice — the genre that proved VNs could sustain real thematic weight, real formal ambition, real darkness that wasn't just shock value.

None of them would exist without a game most English-speaking VN fans have never played.

![Kusarihime ~Euthanasia~ cover art](https://t.vndb.org/cv/91/88791.jpg)

*Kusarihime ~Euthanasia~* was [published by Liar-soft on February 8, 2002](https://vndb.org/v37), written by Hoshizora Meteor. The title translates to "the Rotting Princess." It never received an official English release. For twenty years, it remained inaccessible to the Western fandom that was consuming everything it had inspired.

The story puts you in the position of Hinokawa Itsuki, who returns to his hometown suffering from amnesia after the deaths of his father and younger sister Juri. There he meets Kurame — a girl in a crimson kimono who bears an uncanny resemblance to the sister he lost. Over four August days, his memories surface against his will, the town around him reveals it is built on buried secrets, and the cycle ends when the world goes silent under [a red sheet of snow](https://kotaku.com/games/kusarihime-euthanasia). Then the loop begins again.

The four days repeat approximately ten times, with [each loop centering on a different heroine](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/) in Itsuki's circle. The mystery is layered in three tiers: who is Kurame, what happened to Itsuki's family, and what is the loop itself. These layers [move at their own pace and cross-reference each other](https://forums.fuwanovel.moe/blogs/entry/2697-kusarihime-%E8%85%90%E3%82%8A%E5%A7%AB-~euthanasia~-liar-soft/) until a resolution that hits obliquely rather than directly. The game doesn't explain itself fully, and that refusal to explain is part of the inheritance it passed down.

## The Fanbook That Started Everything

After *Kusarihime* was complete, Hoshizora Meteor wanted to produce a fanbook. To do this, he brought together three other writers: [Urobuchi Gen, Romeo (Tanaka Romeo), and Kinoko Nasu](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/). The interviews in that fanbook constitute one of the more consequential documents in VN history — a conversation between the people who would define the medium's next decade, gathered around a work that had already shown them what was possible.

[Urobuchi took from *Kusarihime* the concept of a love that can only be confirmed through mutual insanity](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/) — two people whose connection exists outside the terms of a sane world. *Saya no Uta* is that concept distilled to its most brutal form. [Romeo took the time loop structure, the refusal to give clean answers, and the portrait of relationships corroded by proximity and guilt](https://thisgonerrightthere.tumblr.com/post/57352964874) — all of which became the spine of *Cross Channel*. [Nasu took the intertwined treatment of love, family bonds, and insanity](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/) that became the Heaven's Feel path.

The parallels with *Higurashi* are worth noting even without a documented direct lineage. Both works use a small closed town where buried family secrets drive the time loop. Both reveal that the supernatural machinery of the local legend is actually technology. Both locate their horror in the corrosive intimacy of people who have known each other too long. [The structural homology is striking enough to suggest at minimum a common inheritance from a shared cultural moment](https://7nightstranslations.wordpress.com/2011/10/11/kusarihime-euthanasia/), even if Ryukishi07 and Hoshizora Meteor arrived at it independently.

## What Kusarihime Does

The game's formal innovations are specific. [The title screen changes with each loop](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/) — a mechanical tell that the reader is not simply repeating content but moving through versions of the same reality. Recollections from previous loops are displayed on in-game TV screens within the narrative, so the player's access to past information is thematized rather than just mechanical. The denpa aesthetic — that particular register of [unsettling psychological deterioration where reality's coherence frays without any single dramatic rupture](https://www.grimoireofhorror.com/the-yurei/denpa-visual-novels-the-big-three-denpa-games/) — runs through the game at a pitch that predates most of what would later get labeled denpa.

The [1000th Summer review](https://the1000thsummer.wordpress.com/2017/07/15/kusarihime-euthanasia/) describes the game as one where "the most critical questions are answered in perhaps the most otherworldly manner conceivable," and that phrase captures something important. *Kusarihime* is not interested in clean resolution. It is interested in the sensation of understanding arriving alongside grief, the way revelation and loss are sometimes the same event. This is also the structural premise of *Saya no Uta*, the emotional engine of *Cross Channel*, and the thematic center of Heaven's Feel. Three different writers, three different genres, same underlying understanding.

![Kusarihime ~Euthanasia~ gameplay](https://t.vndb.org/sf/46/1346.jpg)

The [Fuwanovel community review](https://forums.fuwanovel.moe/blogs/entry/2697-kusarihime-%E8%85%90%E3%82%8A%E5%A7%AB-~euthanasia~-liar-soft/) goes as far as to call it "the first intellectual visual novel in history" — a claim that is probably overstated but points at something real. Before Kusarihime, VNs with serious thematic ambition existed but not in this mode: the non-linear fragmented structure, the mythology-as-technology reveal, the love story predicated on shared damage rather than shared aspiration. This mode was new, and the games that followed were working from it.

## The Translation Problem

The irony of *Kusarihime*'s position is clean. For two decades, the English-language VN fandom played the games it created — Saya, Cross Channel, Heaven's Feel, Higurashi — while the source remained inaccessible behind its original Japanese. The fanbase knew the children but not the parent.

[In September 2022, Daybreak Translations announced a fan translation project](https://daybreaktl.wordpress.com/2022/09/09/big-ol-announcement/), with translator KKHimawari noting she had been passionate about the work since 2018. [By November 2022, all 255 scripts were translated](https://daybreaktl.wordpress.com/2022/11/12/we-finally-made-it/). The project moved to Melancholy Translations for the editing and QA phase, and [by early 2024 the translation and editing were reported as substantially complete](https://fuwanovel.moe/2023/12/visual-novel-translation-status-03-12-23/), though a full public patch had not yet been confirmed released.

When it does arrive, it will ask Western readers to do something unusual: play a 2002 eroge with uneven art, a partially voiced cast, and a [soundtrack described as "AWESOME"](https://calamitousintents.wordpress.com/2010/12/04/kusarihime-euthanasia/) in capital letters by people who worked through the raw Japanese — and to receive it as the thing it actually was, not through the lens of the polished works that came after.

That may be difficult. *Saya no Uta* has a crisper delivery system. *Cross Channel* has a more developed formal language. Heaven's Feel benefits from twenty years of franchise infrastructure. *Kusarihime* is rougher, stranger, and less concerned with the reader's comfort. It is, in some ways, less than the games it inspired.

But it was there first. And the intellectual seriousness that those later games demonstrated — the willingness to let love be insane, to let structure carry meaning, to leave questions open that other games would have sutured shut — traces back to a [Liar-soft release from February 2002](https://vndb.org/v37) that almost nobody outside Japan ever got to read. The three writers who would define a decade sat down with Hoshizora Meteor and his book of red snow, and they built their careers on what they found there.
