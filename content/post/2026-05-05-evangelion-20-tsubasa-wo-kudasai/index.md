Near Third Impact, the sequence that ends *Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance*, is one of the most constructed pieces of emotional engineering in anime film history. The film's first two acts have been organized around making the audience want Shinji to succeed — to act, to be the hero, to save Rei at any cost. The action sequences that gave Mari her showcase were spectacle for its own sake. The dinner party, Rei learning to want something, the sacrifice that initiates the crisis — all of it has been building toward the moment Shinji climbs into Unit-01 and decides, for once, not to stop.

And then he saves Rei. And it destroys the world. And the song playing over this sequence is "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" — "Give Me Wings."

"Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is a 1971 folk song composed by Yasuaki Miyagawa with lyrics by Michio Yamagami. {{< citation >}}"Tsubasa wo Kudasai" (翼をください): music by Yasuaki Miyagawa, lyrics by Michio Yamagami. First performed by the group Akatsuki, it became a standard of the Japanese school music curriculum and remains one of the most recognized songs in Japan. It is sung by Megumi Hayashibara (the voice actress of Rei Ayanami) in *Evangelion: 2.0*'s Near Third Impact sequence — a casting choice that adds a further layer: the character being saved is the voice performing the song of rescue.{{< /citation >}} It has been standard in the Japanese school music curriculum for decades. Every Japanese adult who watched the film in 2009 had probably sung it in elementary school. The song is about wanting to fly, about the dream of freedom and lightness, about the desire to leave the ground. It is a children's song about hope.

Anno used it to score the end of the world.

## What the Song Is

The question of why this song matters requires understanding what it carries for its audience. "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is not just a piece of music; it is a communal memory object. Songs embedded in school curricula across decades become something different from ordinary songs — they attach to a collective past, to a specific period of innocence or uncomplicated aspiration, to a time before the adult complications that make hope feel dangerous.

The song asks for wings to fly above the clouds, to be free from earthly weight, to be carried somewhere light. It is the purest possible expression of the feeling the audience has been engineered to have in the Near Third Impact sequence: the feeling that Shinji is finally getting what he deserves, that the weight is being lifted, that the film is going to give us the ending we wanted.

This is the trap. The song is the audience's feeling made audible. Everything we have been engineered to want — Shinji's agency, Rei's survival, love that refuses to accept loss — is expressed in the longing for wings that the song voices. And it is playing over the sequence in which that longing is shown to be catastrophic.

## The Engineering

*Evangelion: 2.0* was designed to create this moment. The film deliberately structured itself as a crowd-pleasing version of NGE — the rebuilt Evangelion that satisfies the audience requests for a more active Shinji, more spectacular combat, more emotional payoff. Anno and his team at Khara spent the first two acts building an audience that wanted Shinji to succeed, wanted the love story to work, wanted the heroic Shinji who refuses to stand aside.

The film delivered all of this. Shinji climbs into Unit-01. He transforms it into something divine and terrible. He reaches into the sky and tears open the barrier between Rei and the world. He does the loving, active, heroic thing.

"Tsubasa wo Kudasai" plays. The Eva sprouts wings. Near Third Impact begins.

The song's function in this sequence is not ironic in the way that using, say, a martial anthem over a massacre would be ironic — a comment from outside the emotional register. "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is inside the emotional register. It is exactly the right music for the feeling the film has produced. The longing is real. The love is real. The destruction that the longing produces is also real. The song does not comment on the catastrophe; it is the catastrophe's emotional truth.

## Why Childhood

The specific choice of a children's song — not just any folk song but one with deep curriculum embeddedness — is Anno making a precise statement about where the destructive longing comes from. The desire for wings, for lightness, for the world to accommodate love without loss — these are not adult feelings. They are the feelings that adults learn to suppress or complicate because the world turns out not to accommodate them. The child who sang "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" in elementary school believed, at some level, that wanting hard enough was sufficient. The adult knows it isn't.

The Near Third Impact sequence is the moment when the child's belief takes adult power and enacts itself literally. Shinji, who in NGE was precisely the portrait of someone broken by the adult world's refusal to accommodate the child's need for unconditional love, is given a moment in 2.0 when he can force that accommodation. He can make the world give him what he needs. The song is playing because the scene is about the child's wish fulfillment, and "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is what that looks like in musical memory — the song of innocent, uncomplicated desire.

The result is apocalypse. The adult world cannot accommodate the child's wish at this scale. The music tells you the wish was real. The film shows you what happens when the wish succeeds.

## The Audience's Complicity

The Rebuild films have been read, including by critics familiar with Anno's stated aims for the series, as a different relationship to his audience than the original NGE had. The original's ending was, in part, his rage at the audience that had projected onto his work and wanted things from it he had not intended to give. The Rebuild films reverse this: rather than withholding what the audience wants, they give it, in the specific moment that shows what wanting it costs.

"Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is where this structure is most audible. The song plays because we are all in it — not just Shinji, not just the film's emotional logic, but the audience that wanted Shinji to succeed and got exactly what we wanted and cannot now pretend we didn't know what we were wanting. The children's song we all know is the record of our participation. We gave it wings. We also started the impact.
