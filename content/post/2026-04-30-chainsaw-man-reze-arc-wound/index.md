*Chainsaw Man – The Movie: Reze Arc* begins streaming on Crunchyroll today after a [record-breaking theatrical run](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chainsaw_Man_%E2%80%93_The_Movie:_Reze_Arc) that grossed over $174 million worldwide, with [96% on Rotten Tomatoes](https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/chainsaw_man_the_movie_reze_arc) and near-unanimous critical praise. Before you watch it, there's one thing you should know. Tatsuki Fujimoto, speaking in the movie's theatrical pamphlet distributed in Japan, said: "I like it when the protagonist and heroine don't end up together and I want them to carry that weight forever. That's why I [absolutely didn't want Denji and Reze to get together](https://x.com/AniTVOfficial/status/1992950162415640594), or for Reze to become an ally."

He is not expressing regret about how the arc turned out. He is telling you what it was designed to do.

The Reze Arc is not a love story that ends in tragedy. It's an act of deliberate damage — a story structured to permanently alter the protagonist's relationship with hope, trust, and the possibility of normal life. The love is real (eventually, on Reze's part). The damage is the point. Understanding the difference between these two things is what separates people who found the movie's ending sad from people who found it necessary.

## What Reze Is

[Reze is a Soviet human-devil hybrid](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Reze) — specifically, a military-engineered weapon containing the Bomb Devil, deployed by the Soviet Union to extract Chainsaw Man's heart. She is not an assassin who developed romantic feelings for a mark in spite of her mission. She is someone trained to develop those feelings instrumentally: to simulate intimacy, earn trust, and then kill. The blushing, the nervous smiles, the apparently genuine interest in Denji — [all of these were products of military conditioning](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-movie-reze-denji-love-relationship-analysis/). She was designed to be appealing to exactly the kind of lonely boy Denji is.

And here is where the arc becomes interesting rather than simply dark: she falls for him anyway. Or something approaches that. She plans an escape with him to another country. She imagines a different life. For a moment, the weapon almost defects.

[The essay "Reze and the Wound of Attachment"](https://medium.com/@midnightmoth303/reze-and-the-wound-of-attachment-love-as-a-weapon-in-chainsaw-man-f05e217e4784) argues that what Reze demonstrates is attachment trauma operating as survival strategy — someone who can simulate intimacy without fully trusting it, whose oscillation between tenderness and brutality reflects a nervous system that has never learned the difference between safety and danger. She doesn't fake the feelings she develops for Denji. She simply can't act on them without the fear that vulnerability will be used against her. The system that built her as a weapon also made it impossible for her to accept love as anything other than one.

This is what the arc argues with its title, "Bomb Girl": the metaphor isn't about what Reze does, it's about what she is. She was made to go off. The question is who gets hurt.

## What Denji Was Before Reze

To understand why this wound is permanent, you have to understand the Denji the arc opens with. He is a teenager who has been working off his dead father's debt to the yakuza since childhood, who contracted with a devil to survive, who now works for Public Safety's devil extermination squad in exchange for food, shelter, and Makima's conditional attention. His entire understanding of what it means to be cared for is conditioned: [the IndiWire review](https://www.indiewire.com/criticism/movies/chainsaw-man-the-movie-reze-arc-review-1235156950/) identifies the film's central interrogation as "whether a teenager should really be put in the position of risking his life for the bare minimum of food and shelter." He doesn't ask that question. He's grateful.

Before Reze, Denji's experience of affection is entirely transactional. Makima feeds him, gives him purpose, exists as an authority figure whose regard he desperately wants. The series establishes from its opening pages that Denji has been conditioned by deprivation to confuse being used with being loved. He is, in every meaningful sense, vulnerable to exactly the kind of intervention Reze represents.

She offers him a café where he's welcome. She remembers what he likes. She treats him like a person with preferences rather than a weapon with a handler. [The film's expanded pool scene](https://screenrant.com/chainsaw-man-the-movie-reze-arc-anime-changes-manga/) — one of MAPPA's additions that goes beyond the manga — is not fan service. It's the film's visual articulation of something Denji has never experienced: someone spending leisure time with him for no transactional reason. It reads as profound to him because it is. This might be the first thing anyone has done for Denji purely for his enjoyment.

This is what the arc is building when it's not showing you fight sequences: the complete picture of what Reze is taking from Denji when she takes it.

## The Visual Argument MAPPA Made

The movie looks different from the Season 1 anime, and [director Tatsuya Yoshihara has been explicit about why](https://fandomwire.com/god-tier-animation-formula-chainsaw-man-reze-arc-made-a-conscious-effort-to-make-the-artstyle-just-like-the-manga/). MAPPA created an internal design bible with directives including "remove eye highlights," "reduce shading," and "keep wrinkles natural" — specifically to make the animation feel like ink on paper rather than polished digital production. The team wanted every frame to resemble Fujimoto's drawings rather than the sleek, European-cinematography-influenced aesthetic of Season 1.

This is not just an aesthetic preference. Fujimoto's manga style is deliberately scrappy, expressive, and emotionally direct — characters in extreme states look genuinely distorted, joy looks physically excessive, grief looks like it's tearing the page. [Yoshihara said his team made "a conscious effort to reflect the original manga's expression"](https://www.slashfilm.com/2016560/why-chainsaw-man-the-movie-reze-arcs-looks-different-show/) because that expressiveness is what makes the emotional beats land. Season 1 was technically sophisticated but emotionally composed. The movie is technically extraordinary — $174M at the box office suggests it found its audience — and emotionally raw.

The fight sequences are specifically praised for their visceral quality, [the Grimdark Magazine review noting](https://www.grimdarkmagazine.com/review-chainsaw-man-the-reze-arc/) that MAPPA placed "as much attention on the staging of dialogue scenes where they banter in Reze's café as it does on the action bits." The climactic battle — two people trying to kill each other in a typhoon — is visually spectacular in a way that never lets you forget what it is: one person doing what she was built to do, and the other discovering what she really was.

## Why Fujimoto Wanted the Weight

When Fujimoto says "I want them to carry that weight forever," he means two different theys. He means Denji and Reze, obviously. But he also means the readers and viewers.

The [Bomb Girl arc](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Bomb_Girl_arc) is specifically designed to generate a longing that the series refuses to resolve. Fans who want Reze to come back are responding correctly to the story: she is genuinely the most warmly drawn female character Denji encounters in the manga's first part, the one whose interest in him feels least contaminated by agenda. [Fujimoto's refusal to resolve the longing](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-movie-reze-denji-love-relationship-analysis/) is the whole point. Happy endings for people like Denji — people who were shaped by exploitation, who have been turned into weapons, who live within systems that need them broken — are not available. Denji will spend the rest of Part 1 and beyond trying to understand why every woman he connects with wants to use him. The answer is structural: the world he lives in converts everything, including affection, into a resource to be extracted.

Reze is not the exception. She is the example that proves the rule, made more devastating because she was genuinely trying to become the exception by the end. The tragedy is not that they were prevented from being together by circumstance. The tragedy is that the same systems that manufactured her as a weapon made it impossible for her defection to save either of them.

A [fan-written analysis of Reze's psychology](https://medium.com/@midnightmoth303/reze-and-the-wound-of-attachment-love-as-a-weapon-in-chainsaw-man-f05e217e4784) argues that her real tragedy is that she "almost doesn't betray him" — that she genuinely imagines escape before survival instinct overrides it. The machinery of her conditioning, and the machinery of the world, did not allow "almost" to become "actually." And Denji, who felt the "almost" but didn't understand what stopped it, walks away believing the failure was about him.

The Reze Arc's permanent damage to Denji is not that it taught him love ends in pain. It's that it taught him love always had a mission. [The arc seeds doubt in him](https://superherotoystore.com/blogs/news/how-reze-changed-denji-forever-in-chainsaw-man-s-reze-arc-explained) that the rest of Part 1 systematically grows into something much darker: a protagonist asking whether anyone could love Denji-the-person rather than Denji-the-weapon.

## What Makes the Film Worth Watching Now

The movie is not just adaptation fidelity, though it's exceptional on that count. [MAPPA's decision to chase Fujimoto's visual language](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2025-10-31/chainsaw-man-the-movie-reze-arc-director-tatsuya-yoshihara-assistant-director-masato-nakazono/.230134) rather than the cinematic language that Season 1 imported produces an experience that feels closer to what the manga actually is: expressive, unpolished, unsparing. The fights are better. The quiet moments are better. The film expands on the manga's romantic sequences because the cinema format gives it room to breathe and Yoshihara understood that the action only means something if you've been given time to care about who's fighting.

Kenshi Yonezu's ending theme "IRIS OUT" is the best piece of music the franchise has produced — [composed specifically for the film](https://anitrendz.net/news/2025/09/15/chainsaw-man-the-movie-reze-arc-unveils-new-poster-visual-theme-song-music-video-for-kenshi-yonezus-iris-out/) and doing the work that the arc's ending requires: not providing catharsis, but sitting inside the weight that Fujimoto said he wanted carried forever.

The ending doesn't end. It continues, through every relationship Denji attempts, every time he asks himself whether he's wanted or just useful.

Fujimoto wanted that weight. The movie delivers it.
