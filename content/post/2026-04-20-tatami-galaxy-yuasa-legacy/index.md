In February 2025, [Masaaki Yuasa announced the founding of ame pippin](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-02-17/former-science-saru-head-masaaki-yuasa-establishes-new-studio-ame-pippin/.221332) — his second studio, built after stepping back from Science SARU, positioned with the same explicit mission of making anime production humane. "A place for new opportunities and a fresh creative environment," he called it. A collective that values "joy in creativity."

The language was familiar. It was the same language he'd used when Science SARU launched in 2013, and to some degree the same language embedded in everything he'd been building since he finished *The Tatami Galaxy* in 2010. If you want to understand what Yuasa has been doing for fifteen years — why he builds studios, why he argues for labor reform in an industry that doesn't want to hear it, why his filmography looks the way it does — the thread runs back to a [late-night Noitamina anime about a self-deceiving student in Kyoto](https://myanimelist.net/anime/7088/Yojouhan_Shinwa_Taikei) that won a prize no television show had ever won before.

*The Tatami Galaxy* is not Yuasa's most ambitious work. *Mind Game* (2004) probably is. It's not his most formally radical work — [*Kaiba* (2008)](https://myanimelist.net/anime/3534/Kaiba) may be that. But it is the show that transformed him from a cult director into a recognized force in Japanese animation, and that transformation had consequences that are still unfolding.

## The Madhouse Trilogy and What It Built

Between 2006 and 2010, Yuasa directed three television series at Madhouse — [*Kemonozume* (2006)](https://myanimelist.net/anime/971/Kemonozume), [*Kaiba* (2008)](https://myanimelist.net/anime/3534/Kaiba), and *The Tatami Galaxy* (2010). Looking at these in sequence is instructive, because each one refined a different aspect of his approach while remaining critically marginal by commercial anime standards.

*Kemonozume* is chaotic in a way that still feels unresolved — a monster-romance story that shifts visual registers episode by episode, formally adventurous but unstable. *Kaiba* goes in the opposite direction: soft, round, lyrical, emotionally precise, building a dystopian sci-fi world out of the gentlest possible visual vocabulary. Both won Japan Media Arts Festival jury prizes. Neither crossed over.

*The Tatami Galaxy* was different. It had Tomihiko Morimi's novel as its foundation — source material with an existing readership and a genuinely literary reputation — and [Noitamina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noitamina) as its broadcast slot, which by 2010 had established itself as the prestige lane of late-night anime. It also had [scriptwriter Makoto Ueda](https://myanimelist.net/anime/7088/Yojouhan_Shinwa_Taikei/staff), who helped Yuasa expand Morimi's four-chapter structure into eleven episodes, and in doing so made the loop structure dramatically more rigorous and legible. [As Morimi noted in an interview](https://blog.alltheanime.com/interview-tomihiko-morimi/), he trusted Yuasa's vision and didn't interfere — a decision that gave the show the space to fully commit to its structural argument.

The result was a show that did everything Kemonozume and Kaiba did in terms of formal ambition, but wrapped it in something accessible: a relatable premise (what if you kept redoing your college years), a clear narrative arc, genuine comedy, and a love story. You could follow *The Tatami Galaxy* without training in experimental animation. You just also had to keep up.

## The Prize That Changed the Terms

On December 8, 2010, [*The Tatami Galaxy* won the Grand Prize in the Animation Division at the 14th Japan Media Arts Festival](http://archive.j-mediaarts.jp/en/festival/2010/animation/works/14an_The_Tatami_Galaxy/). The jury described it as "a richly expressive work that turns the limitations of TV on its head" — and specifically noted it was "befitting of the very first Grand Prize given to a TV animation."

That qualifier — *the first* — matters enormously. The Japan Media Arts Festival is not the Oscars, and anime discourse tends to treat institutional awards with appropriate skepticism. But in Japan's cultural establishment, JMAF recognition carries weight, and the Grand Prize had previously gone only to theatrical features. The jury's decision to give it to a late-night TV anime directed by someone who was still largely unknown outside serious animation circles was an institutional statement: this kind of work deserves the same recognition as theatrical animation.

[The Tokyo Anime Awards followed with its own Television Category prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tatami_Galaxy), and Funimation's [simultaneous simulcast deal with Fuji TV](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2010-04-15/funimation-adds-house-of-the-five-leaves-the-tatami-galaxy) made *The Tatami Galaxy* one of Yuasa's first works to reach North American audiences legally in real time. For the first time, Yuasa's name was being discussed outside the narrow community of people who sought out experimental anime specifically.

## Noitamina and the Institutional Context

The institutional context matters here. [Noitamina launched in 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noitamina) with an explicit mandate to reach audiences beyond young male otaku — adapting works with literary prestige, targeting adult women, commissioning aesthetically distinctive productions. By the time *The Tatami Galaxy* aired, [Noitamina had expanded to an hour-long block in spring 2010](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/convention/2010/experiments-in-the-anime-industry-noitamina), doubling its capacity and signaling ambitions that went well beyond late-night drama adaptations.

What *The Tatami Galaxy* demonstrated was that Noitamina's thesis could extend much further than josei-adjacent prestige. It proved that formally radical, psychologically demanding, visually distinctive anime could sit in that slot and win the highest animation prize in Japan. The broadcasting deal with producer Kōji Yamamoto, according to [Masaaki Yuasa's Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masaaki_Yuasa), "marked the start of an ongoing relationship" — Yamamoto would later leave Fuji TV to found Twin Engine, bringing that relationship with him into a new configuration. The significance is that Noitamina provided Yuasa not just a broadcast slot but a co-production network: the kind of institutional backing that makes independent studio-founding plausible.

That infrastructure was, indirectly, part of what created the conditions for Science SARU.

## Science SARU as Tatami Galaxy's Institutional Afterlife

[Science SARU was founded in February 2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_Saru), co-founded by Yuasa and producer Eunyoung Choi. The circumstances of their meeting matter: they connected during the production of *Kick-Heart*, a short film that Yuasa crowdfunded on Kickstarter in 2012. The campaign raised over $150,000 from an international fanbase — a concrete, early measurement of the international appetite for Yuasa's work that had been building since *The Tatami Galaxy*'s overseas streaming deal. It was proof of audience before the studio even existed.

The studio's founding philosophy was explicit: make anime with labor conditions that didn't destroy the people doing the work. Japanese animation production has been [notorious](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/convention/2010/experiments-in-the-anime-industry-noitamina) for grueling hours, exploitative entry-level pay, and a culture that treats overwork as evidence of dedication. [Science SARU modeled itself on practices from American and European animation studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masaaki_Yuasa) — regular hours, time off, rest as default rather than exception. Yuasa stated directly that he believed sustainable production was a precondition for Japan's animation industry achieving genuine global success, not just a nice-to-have for the people inside it.

What made this plausible as a business proposition, rather than an idealistic gesture that would fail commercially, was *The Tatami Galaxy*'s legacy. By 2013, Yuasa had a JMAF Grand Prize, international distribution history, and a body of work recognizable to the festival circuit and streaming services that were increasingly acquiring anime. He had institutional credibility. Without *The Tatami Galaxy*'s validation, the Science SARU pitch is much harder to make.

## The Decade That Followed

Science SARU's output under Yuasa is the direct continuation of what *The Tatami Galaxy* made possible. [*Ping Pong the Animation* (2014)](https://myanimelist.net/anime/20787/Ping_Pong_the_Animation) refined the scratchy, flash-influenced visual language into something even more kinetically exciting than anything Yuasa had done in television. It won its own JMAF prize. [*The Night Is Short, Walk On Girl* (2017)](https://myanimelist.net/movie/11769/Yoru_wa_Mijikashi_Aruke_yo_Otome) — the companion piece to *The Tatami Galaxy*, using Morimi's characters in a Kyoto-set picaresque — won the Grand Prize for Best Animated Feature at the Ottawa International Animation Festival.

[*Devilman Crybaby* (2018)](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=20630) on Netflix brought Yuasa to a genuinely global mainstream audience. Netflix's global simultaneous release put him in front of viewers who had no prior relationship with anime — a different kind of exposure than streaming catalog acquisitions, and one that carried real risk given *Devilman Crybaby*'s graphic content and structural density. That it landed as decisively as it did built on the international streaming infrastructure that *The Tatami Galaxy*'s early simulcast had been part of creating — but it also demonstrated that Yuasa's work could operate in that context without compromising its formal identity. [*Keep Your Hands Off Eizouken!* (2020)](https://myanimelist.net/anime/39792/Eizouken_ni_wa_Te_wo_Dasu_na) is, among other things, a love letter to the act of making animation — to imagination, craft, and the specific joy of getting something on screen through sheer will. It earns comparison to the best of the work it's celebrating.

Each of these exists in a lineage. [*The Tatami Galaxy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tatami_Galaxy) is where Yuasa's career stopped being a series of critically admired experiments and became a recognized creative position — where the question changed from "who is this Yuasa person doing weird stuff at Madhouse" to "what is Yuasa going to do next."

## Ame Pippin and the Next Chapter

In 2025, [with ame pippin](https://variety.com/2025/film/news/masaaki-yuasa-ame-pippin-anime-studio-tokyo-1236311058/), Yuasa is doing the same thing he did in 2013: building a structure that reflects his creative values, this time with the additional leverage of having spent a decade proving those values produce both critical and commercial results. The new studio is backed by ASMIK Ace, Aniplex, and CoMix Wave Films — major institutional partners that would not have been available to someone without a filmography like Yuasa's. His first announced project is *Daisy's Life* (2026), another feature.

None of this happened because Yuasa simply kept making excellent work. Plenty of excellent directors in anime remain cult figures, known to enthusiasts and ignored by institutions. What *The Tatami Galaxy* provided was the specific inflection point where craft met institutional recognition — where one panel of judges decided that formal ambition in late-night TV animation deserved the same prize as theatrical features, and that decision cascaded outward.

The Tatami Galaxy is, finally, a show about what happens when you stop pretending your situation would be different if only you'd made different choices. There is something appropriate about the fact that its legacy is also a story about a director who stopped waiting for conditions to change and started creating the conditions he wanted — studio by studio, show by show, across fifteen years of work that has genuinely changed what experimental animation in Japan can be.
