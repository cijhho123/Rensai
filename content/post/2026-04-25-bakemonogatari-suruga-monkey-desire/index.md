Every oddity in Bakemonogatari is a symptom. Senjougahara lost her weight when she gave up her feelings. Hachikuji can't reach home because she died before she could. Sengoku's snakes found her because she silently wished ill on someone happy. The pattern is consistent: something unprocessed, usually a wound or a shame, coheres into a supernatural affliction that externalizes what the character cannot bring themselves to say. Oshino's therapeutic axiom — "only you can save yourself" — applies uniformly. You are the source of your own haunting.

And then there is Kanbaru Suruga.

Kanbaru's oddity is not a wound inflicted on her. It's a deal she made. Before the events of the series, she found a cursed item — which she believed was the Monkey's Paw of W.W. Jacobs' story, though it is actually a [Rainy Devil](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Suruga_Kanbaru), a more particular kind of entity — and used two of her three wishes. She knew what she was doing. She contracted for her own haunting. When the arm attacks Araragi in the alley near the beginning of her arc, it isn't the Rainy Devil overriding Kanbaru's will. It's the Rainy Devil doing exactly what she asked for.

This is the distinction that the Suruga Monkey arc is built around, and it makes Kanbaru categorically different from every other afflicted character in the series.

## What the Rainy Devil Actually Does

The [Rainy Devil](https://nisioisin.fandom.com/wiki/Suruga_Kanbaru), per NisiOisin's text as adapted by SHAFT, is distinguished from the monkey's paw precisely because it doesn't grant wishes — it fulfills them. "It doesn't grant just any wish," [the Animetropolis thematic analysis explains](https://animetropolisblog.wordpress.com/2017/06/25/thematic-analysis-of-monogatari-series-part-1/), "it grants wishes borne from negative emotions and desires." More specifically: it sees through the human to the dark side, provokes and pulls it out, and fulfills the wish as stated — with the implied negative desire fully honored. It listens to what you say. It also hears what you mean.

This is the nightmare of the arc. Kanbaru made a wish for herself to be able to date Senjougahara. But she made it while feeling jealous, possessive, and desperate. What she actually wanted, at the level she wouldn't acknowledge, was for the person standing between her and Senjougahara to disappear. [As the We Remember Love essay on episode 7 argues](https://ghostlightning.wordpress.com/2009/08/23/bake07/), the arm attacked because Kanbaru's "strong feeling" — deliberately suppressed during her conversation with Araragi — expressed itself through the only channel available to it. The arm didn't make her do anything. It made her do everything she refused to admit she wanted done.

[The Minute Art analysis of SHAFT's visual style in this arc](https://minuteart.wordpress.com/2017/06/16/em-style-in-bakemonogatari-suruga-monkey/) notes that the "violence, speed, and overwhelming colors" of the alley fight sequence exist precisely to externalize Kanbaru's internal state. Where earlier arcs — Senjougahara's weightlessness, Hachikuji's endless wandering — were visualized through stillness and absence, Kanbaru's affliction is kinetic and saturated. The Rainy Devil is an entity "born from jealousy and hatred," and SHAFT renders it accordingly: the arm doesn't drift or linger, it lunges. The visual language is accurate. This is what that feeling looks like when it finally gets out.

## The Three Wishes and the Cost of Outsourcing

Kanbaru's deal with the Rainy Devil is three wishes at the cost of her soul. By the time Araragi encounters her, she has already spent two. The first, [per the arc's exposition](https://umaiyomu.wordpress.com/2020/04/06/bakemonogatari-episode-7-suruga-monkey-part-two/), was used to make herself an athletic prodigy — a wish granted as intended, producing the star basketball player Shuchiin Academy knows. The second was the wish involving Senjougahara, which produced the arm.

The distinction between these wishes matters. The first was straightforward desire, stated cleanly and granted cleanly. The second was compromised desire — what Kanbaru consciously wanted (to be close to Senjougahara) was inseparable from what she wouldn't let herself want (for Senjougahara's new boyfriend to be gone). The Rainy Devil couldn't fulfill the first without acting on the second, because the second was the emotional truth beneath the first.

This is the arc's thesis: desire that is divided against itself doesn't become neutral or benign. It becomes the arm. When you outsource your wishes to a supernatural entity, the entity doesn't take the charitable interpretation. It takes the true one.

[The Issuu analysis of the arc from Konshuu's critical journal](https://issuu.com/konshuu-caa/docs/50-5/s/11282952) frames Kanbaru's unrequited love for Senjougahara in the context of the yuri tradition she subverts. The [TV Tropes yuri genre entry](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/YuriGenre) notes that a "disproportionate amount of yuri stories ended tragically," with a recurring pattern of a character stepping aside to let her beloved be happy with someone else — accepting the situation, vowing to support the couple, moving on gracefully. What Kanbaru could not do was exactly this. She tried to accept Senjougahara's rejection in middle school. She withdrew. She redirected her energy into basketball. She told herself she was over it. The arm is the evidence that none of this was true.

## What Araragi Does With This Information

The resolution of the Suruga Monkey arc has always bothered me a little — not because it fails, but because of what it asks Araragi to do. He learns the truth of Kanbaru's wish. He chooses not to tell Senjougahara. He takes Kanbaru's beating and her secret and carries both, becoming her confidant in the way the series will establish as his pattern: the person who absorbs the truth so others don't have to.

[The Umaiyomu episode analysis](https://umaiyomu.wordpress.com/2020/04/11/bakemonogatari-episode-8-suruga-monkey-part-three/) notes the irony: Araragi, who demanded honesty from Senjougahara in her arc, immediately lies by omission to protect Kanbaru. He recognizes that Kanbaru wasn't consciously trying to kill him — that the arm acted without her awareness — and he uses this to construct a version of events that spares her having to own what the arm did. But the arm only acts on what she actually wants. Excusing the arm is excusing the desire.

This is Bakemonogatari being honest about the limits of its own therapeutic model. The series is committed to the idea that characters can only save themselves — but Araragi keeps intervening, keeps absorbing consequences, keeps deciding on others' behalf what they need to know about themselves. [The Cedwick Tumblr analysis of episodes 6-8](https://cedwickcapati.tumblr.com/post/94991583915/bakemonogatari-the-things-we-do-for-love/amp) names this as "love making us blind" — but it's something more specific: Araragi's savior complex creating a situation where Kanbaru's self-knowledge is filtered through his decisions about what she can handle.

Oshino, when he explains the Rainy Devil to Araragi, doesn't moralize. This is consistent with his function in the series: he explains the mechanics, describes what the afflicted character must do for themselves, and declines to manage the emotional aftermath. His detachment here is pointed. He knows what Kanbaru's arm actually did, and he says nothing that would require her to fully reckon with it. Senjougahara eventually reaches out to Kanbaru directly — what [the Animetropolis analysis describes](https://animetropolisblog.wordpress.com/2017/06/25/thematic-analysis-of-monogatari-series-part-1/) as the character having been "trying to escape the thought that she actually wished for those" — and the arc ends while that escape is still partly in progress. Kanbaru is absorbed into the main cast as a friend. The truth of what she wished for becomes a secret Araragi keeps.

## The Wish That Gets No Resolution

What distinguishes the Suruga Monkey arc from every other arc in Bakemonogatari is that it refuses to fully close. Senjougahara doesn't know the truth of Kanbaru's wish. Kanbaru still loves her. The third wish is still pending. Nothing about the central emotional reality — that Kanbaru wanted Araragi gone so she could have Senjougahara — is actually resolved by the arc. What is resolved is the physical emergency. The arm stops attacking. The Rainy Devil recedes. The desire doesn't.

[The Konshuu essay identifies](https://issuu.com/konshuu-caa/docs/50-5/s/11282952) this as something rare in the yuri tradition the series engages with: an "unfulfillable love" that the narrative doesn't rescue with convenient plot contrivance. In the later Monogatari series — specifically in Nisemonogatari and subsequent entries — [Kanbaru tells Araragi directly](https://nisioisin.fandom.com/wiki/Suruga_Kanbaru) that if Senjougahara ever breaks up with him, she will be first in line to confess. She manages her feelings by managing their container — keeping them declared, acknowledged, waiting. This is not moving on. It is the most honest version of not moving on that is available to her given the circumstances.

The series ends its first season with "Kimi no Shiranai Monogatari" — "The Story You Don't Know" — [Supercell's ending theme built around unrequited love and the Summer Triangle constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimi_no_Shiranai_Monogatari). The song's narrator cannot find Altair — the star that represents her beloved — and concludes that if it's like this, Altair is all alone. The song is written about the Araragi–Senjougahara relationship from outside: the perspective of someone watching two people complete each other while standing apart. That is Kanbaru's position, and [Anime Instrumentality's review of the soundtrack](https://blog.animeinstrumentality.net/soundtrack-reviews/bakemonogataricencoroll-kimi-no-shiranai-monogatari-review/) notes how the song's sense of "nostalgia for a moment that's passing and can't be recovered" suits an ending that is genuinely about loss rather than resolution.

The wish Kanbaru made is still alive. The Rainy Devil made it visible. The series understood that making it visible was not the same as making it go away.
