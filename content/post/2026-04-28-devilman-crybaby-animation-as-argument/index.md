Every adaptation of Devilman before 2018 made the same mistake: they treated transformation as event. The moment a human becomes a demon is a rupture — a visual and narrative shock meant to jolt the audience. Masaaki Yuasa looked at that approach and discarded it entirely. In *Devilman Crybaby*, transformation isn't something that happens. It's the condition everything already exists in.

This distinction sounds subtle until you actually watch the show. [*Devilman Crybaby*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/36038/Devilman_Crybaby) opens with human bodies that are already slightly wrong. The animation is loose, the linework is restless, and character proportions shift moment to moment — not dramatically, just enough that nothing ever fully settles. Before a single demon appears on screen, the visual grammar has already told you: there is no stable state of being human. There is only the degree to which you're becoming something else.

That isn't an accident. It's the thesis.

## Yuasa's Visual Philosophy: Emotion Over Accuracy

[Masaaki Yuasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masaaki_Yuasa) has been explicit about his priorities. His stated goal as a director is to achieve "realism in depicting emotions" while allowing the visuals through which those emotions are expressed to be fully expressionistic. He believes that "characters who move freely and unrestrained by strict realism can express a greater and more faithful range of emotionality" — a philosophy that sounds like an aesthetic preference until you see what it means in practice in Crybaby.

Where other anime directors treat their character models as physical templates to be animated faithfully, Yuasa treats them as starting suggestions. Bodies balloon. Shoulders broaden without warning. A character's excitement might manifest as their coat literally inflating around them, as happens repeatedly with [Ryo Asuka](https://devilman.fandom.com/wiki/Ryo_Asuka). The [BFI notes](https://www.bfi.org.uk/features/where-begin-with-masaaki-yuasa) that Yuasa deliberately prioritizes "explicit cartoonishness" over photorealism, but that framing undersells what's happening: this isn't cartoon silliness, it's a claim that human emotional life doesn't respect the boundaries of the human body.

[Science SARU](https://sciencesaru.com/en/works/DEVILMANcrybaby_en), the studio Yuasa co-founded with Eunyoung Choi, enabled this approach through a hybrid technique that blends traditional hand-drawn keyframes with [digital animation tools](https://blog.adobe.com/en/publish/2020/05/21/how-science-saru-animation-studio-is-redefining-the-japanese-animation-industry). The result is animation that could never emerge from the traditional Japanese studio pipeline — bodies move with a fluidity that suggests they were drawn by someone who genuinely doesn't believe in fixed anatomy. An early American animation influence shows in the squash-and-stretch physics, but the emotional register is completely different. This isn't playfulness. It's instability as worldview.

## The Sabbath Scene and the Body as Battleground

The show's most talked-about sequence — the Sabbath party in the first episode where human revelers begin transforming mid-orgy — demonstrates exactly how this visual philosophy generates meaning. A [34th Street Magazine analysis](https://www.34st.com/article/2024/11/devilman-crybaby-pornography-body-horror-human-nature) argues that both body horror and pornography exploit the body to evoke extreme emotion — disgust and arousal — but operate in opposite directions. Crybaby forces those directions to occupy the same frame: what reads as erotic one moment becomes viscerally horrifying the next, not because something new is added to the image but because the same visual grammar that depicts desire now depicts monstrosity.

This only works because Yuasa has established from the opening frames that human bodies are already unstable. When a dancing woman's breasts stretch into monstrous appendages and her spine curves backward through impossible angles, it registers as acceleration rather than rupture. The body was always doing something. Now it's doing more of it. The horror comes from continuity, not contrast.

The [Hana Ga Saita Yo analysis](https://hanagasaitayo.wordpress.com/2020/08/09/analysis-devilman-crybaby/) correctly identifies the show's argument: nudity in Crybaby functions differently than in the original Nagai manga. Where Nagai used sexuality to signal demonic depravity, Yuasa uses it to argue that human sexual desire is itself a form of instability — not moral corruption, but proof that the self is always in motion. Demons don't introduce chaos to the human body. They just make visible what was already there.

## What Nagai Could Never Show, and What Yuasa Chose to Do With It

[Go Nagai's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_Nagai) original *Devilman* manga ran in Shōnen Magazine from 1972 to 1973. Yuasa was [explicit in interviews](https://gigazine.net/gsc_news/en/20180116-devilman-crybaby-go-nagai-masaaki-yuasa-interview/) that he was aware Nagai had been "restrained in the depiction of sexual and violent content" by the shōnen format. His creative approach was informed by a specific question: "If Nagai-sensei could have done as he pleased, what would he have made?" Nagai, for his part, gave Yuasa total freedom: "I'll leave it up to you. Do whatever you want."

But this isn't really a story about Yuasa filling in content that censorship cut. It's more interesting than that. In Nagai's manga, transformation sequences are rendered as dramatic page-breaking events — the body ruptures, the demon emerges, the reader is meant to be shocked. That shock depends on the surrounding bodies being depicted as stable. Yuasa removes that stability entirely. What Yuasa actually did was find a visual register in which the things Nagai was restrained from showing could be made *meaningful* rather than merely extreme. The violence and sexuality in Crybaby don't read as transgression for its own sake — they read as the only possible expression of what the show's animation style has already established. If you build a world where bodies are constitutionally unstable, then extreme physical events become logical outcomes rather than shock tactics.

The show's approach to puberty as metaphor works the same way. [One reading](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2018/01/22/owls-post-devilman-crybaby-a-little-change-in-character/) frames Akira's transformation as "the devil inside him represents growing up or puberty" — strength and appetite arriving uninvited, the world becoming "not as black and white as it used to be." This is a standard reading of the show's content, but it gains its force from the animation. Yuasa makes *everyone's* body look like it's going through something. Akira's transformation into Devilman is just the most visible instance of a universal condition — and it only reads that way because the visual grammar was already making that argument before Akira ever merged with a demon.

## Specific Episodes, Specific Choices

The show's visual approach wasn't uniform across all ten episodes — it was [deliberately assigned](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2018/01/08/devilman-crybaby-interview-with-director-masaaki-yuasa/) to animators based on thematic function. Kiyotaka Oshiyama's work on episode 5 delivered the most abstract and distorted imagery of the whole run — the dream and hallucination sequences that mirror psychological descent through intensified bodily distortion. Tomohisa Shimoyama handled episode 4's transformation sequences with their own solo key animation approach.

This isn't a production detail. It's evidence of how seriously Yuasa takes animation as argument. Different emotional registers require different visual grammars. The episode where Akira experiences the first full integration of his demonic power gets animation that communicates something different from the episode where Ryo stands among the ruins of everything. You can read the show's emotional arc through how the bodies move, even with the dialogue muted.

The [CBR retrospective on Yuasa's breakout decade](https://www.cbr.com/masaaki-yuasa-anime-devilman-crybaby/) noted that Crybaby marked the point where his style stopped being niche and became legible to a global audience. What's interesting is why: the show arrived on [Netflix simultaneously in 90 countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby), with [90% of its viewership outside Japan](https://devilman.fandom.com/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby). Audiences who had no context for Go Nagai's original manga, no exposure to the franchise's history in Japan, encountered the show's visual language without a frame of reference and read it anyway. Yuasa's animation is communicating something that doesn't require cultural literacy to receive — it operates at the level of physical intuition.

## The Lynch Mob as Abstraction

The approach reaches its logical conclusion in the human persecution sequences. Where most anime depicting mob violence would commit to the blood and the faces and the specific victims, Yuasa renders the mob as [distorted human-shaped shadows against a wall of orange](https://honeysanime.com/masaaki-yuasa-and-the-animation-of-devilman-crybaby/) — featureless, dark-outlined, their individual humanity deliberately dissolved. The color palette shifts: the warm social tones of the earlier episodes drain out, and the fire that illuminates the mob makes the shadows look less like people than like the idea of people becoming something.

The choice works because of what it removes. Specific faces give the viewer something to evaluate — a person who chose badly, a situation with context. Shadows give you nothing to evaluate except the shape. And the shape, after ten episodes of bodies that distort under emotional pressure, reads immediately as: *this is what humans look like when fear gets large enough*. Abstraction isn't Yuasa softening the horror. It's him intensifying it by removing the escape hatch of particularity.

[GameRant's analysis of the show's "shock value"](https://gamerant.com/devilman-crybaby-shocking-themes/) argues that the disturbing content functions as artistic commentary rather than exploitation. That's right but incomplete. The content isn't just commenting on something — it's the mechanism through which the show argues that the line between human and demon was always thinner than we told ourselves. The mob doesn't look monstrous in spite of being human. It looks monstrous *as* the fullest expression of what humans already were.

## The Inheritance

What makes Yuasa's animation approach the real argument in Crybaby rather than the show's subject matter — its anti-bigotry themes, its queer subtext, its anti-war core — is that those arguments exist in the original Nagai manga too. The [comparative analysis of the Devilman body across adaptations](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/390488762_Neither_Human_Nor_Demon_A_Comparative_Analysis_of_the_Devilman_Body_in_Go_Nagai%27s_Devilman_and_Masaaki_Yuasa%27s_Devilman_Crybaby) finds that what Yuasa adds isn't new content — it's a new way of making the content felt. The shift from Nagai's relatively stable body horror to Yuasa's constitutionally unstable visual grammar changes the audience's relationship to the transformation from shock to recognition.

That recognition is the thing. You don't watch Akira become Devilman and think "that couldn't happen to me." You watch the show's animation for ten minutes and you've already absorbed the idea that it could. Bodies are already distorting. Identities are already unstable. The demons aren't coming — they're already the shape that human things can take when pushed hard enough.

This is the achievement the other Devilman adaptations couldn't reach. [The 1987 OVA](https://myanimelist.net/anime/1706/Devilman) and [the 2000 OVA sequel](https://myanimelist.net/anime/1707/Amon__Apocalypse_of_Devilman) depicted transformation faithfully; they couldn't make you feel like it was already happening to you. Yuasa built that feeling into the visual grammar before a single demon appeared, and then let the story confirm what the animation had already established.

The title says it plainly: *crybaby*. Akira cries all the way through because he feels everything. The show's visual language cries too — bodies never quite holding their shape, lines never quite holding still, the world constantly on the verge of becoming something else. It's the only animation style that could have carried this particular argument. Everything else would have just been a retelling.
