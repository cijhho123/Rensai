Everyone knows the last scene. Shinji, alone on a barren shore beside an unconscious Asuka, hands around her throat. She raises her bandaged hand to his cheek. He recoils. She says it: *kimochi warui* — "how disgusting" — and the film cuts to black.

Most critical accounts treat those two words as the film's thesis. Anno, driven to exhaustion and depression, surrounded by fans who had reportedly sent him death threats after the TV series finale, finally turns and says what he thinks of them. The contempt is total. The audience goes home having been called disgusting by their creator.

It's a compelling story. It's also not quite true — and the actual story is stranger and more uncomfortable in ways the myth obscures.

## How Anno Got That Line

The *kimochi warui* you hear in [the film](https://myanimelist.net/anime/32/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion_The_End_of_Evangelion) was not scripted. The original screenplay had Asuka deliver a cold, declarative line: "I can't stand the idea of being killed by someone like you." That's not nothing, but it's clearly authored. It has the shape of a writer's intention.

What actually happened in the recording session was different. Anno, dissatisfied with both the scripted line and voice actress Yuko Miyamura's performance, stopped the session and described a scenario to her. Imagine, he said, that a stranger has been secretly entering your home. He could do whatever he wanted to you while you slept, but instead he masturbates while watching. He has been doing this for a while. You just found out. Now say something.

Miyamura's response — her genuine, unfiltered revulsion — was what Anno captured. Those are the words at the end of the film.

This is not a small production detail. The final statement of *End of Evangelion*, the thing that has been analyzed, quoted, and used as evidence for the "Anno hates his fans" thesis for nearly three decades, was not a statement at all. It was a document. Anno did not write the film's conclusion; he extracted it, through psychological manipulation of a performer, from something real. The authorship of the film's most famous moment is genuinely ambiguous.

## The Hate Mail That Mostly Wasn't

The broader mythology around the film has a similar problem. The standard account runs like this: fans who were furious about the abstract, interior TV finale (episodes 25 and 26) sent [Anno death threats and hate mail](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:List_of_Common_Misconceptions). *End of Evangelion* was his response.

The English-language commentary track on the Manga Entertainment home release helped cement this reading for Western audiences — but the documented record of actual fan correspondence tells a different story. Of the letters and messages that have been examined and translated, the overwhelming majority expressed support, encouragement, or anticipation for whatever Anno would make next. The picture of a director drowning in rage from an ungrateful fandom, while emotionally resonant, does not match what was actually being sent to Gainax.

The real flash point was earlier. When the TV finale aired in March 1996, certain fans did respond with hostility severe enough that the Gainax offices were vandalized. That is documented, and it was genuinely ugly. But that was a response to *Neon Genesis Evangelion*. *End of Evangelion* came out in July 1997 — more than a year after the TV finale, and long after the immediate fury had dissipated. The film was partly a response to that earlier controversy, not evidence that the controversy had continued.

Conflating these two moments — the 1996 TV backlash and the 1997 film — has allowed critics to project the emotional logic of the first event onto the creative decisions behind the second.

## What Anno Actually Said He Was Doing

Anno's own stated framework for *End of Evangelion* cuts against the contempt reading in another way. He described the film as a *shishōsetsu* — confessional literature, a Japanese form that deliberately blurs the line between the author's psychology and the narrative. He called his approach something like masturbation in the original sense: self-exposure, not self-defense. The intent was not to wound but to purge.

Co-director Kazuya Tsurumaki gave an interview where he pushed back explicitly on the idea that the film's darkness was born from bitterness: "A lot of people think anime should always have happy endings, but that's not always the case. We wanted to educate the fans that anime can have bitter endings." That's didactic, maybe condescending — but it's not contemptuous. There's a meaningful difference between a teacher who believes in hard lessons and a director who has decided his audience deserves to suffer.

Anno also wrote: "Evangelion is like a puzzle, you know. Any person can see it and give his/her own answer... We will never offer the answers, even in the theatrical version." The *End of Evangelion* he describes is not a statement but an invitation — deliberately incomplete, deliberately open, asking the viewer to supply what the film withholds. This is antithetical to the "definitive fuck-you" reading.

## The Authorial Dissolution at the Film's End

What the *kimochi warui* production story reveals is something more interesting than "Anno hated his fans" and something more unsettling than "Anno loved them." It reveals a director who, at the film's climax, stopped trying to author meaning and started trying to capture it.

Everything else in *End of Evangelion* is aggressively, almost violently authored: the live-action sequences, the intercut photography of the Shinjuku Milano-za cinema and Tokyo streets, the use of "Komm, süsser Tod" — a saccharine pop song about welcoming death — over the apocalypse sequence. These are choices made by a person with a specific and difficult vision. The JSDF massacre in Episode 25 ("Air") is arguably the most ruthlessly directed sequence in Anno's career — slow, patient, almost ceremonial in its depiction of Unit-01 being overwhelmed.

Then, at the very end, Anno stops directing and starts provoking. He removes the script, removes his own intention, and creates a situation where authentic human disgust will manifest. He captures it and puts it in the film's final second.

If you want to find Anno's actual statement to the audience, the *kimochi warui* scene may be the worst place to look — because that moment, uniquely, belongs to someone else.

## What This Leaves Us With

None of this makes *End of Evangelion* a warmer film. The hospital scene is still there. The violence is still there. Shinji still makes the choices he makes. The film still ends with two people on a shore of orange LCL that was, three minutes ago, the rest of the human race.

But the interpretive frame that has governed the film's critical reception — *Anno was punishing his audience; the film is an act of contempt* — rests on a mythology that doesn't hold up. The hate mail was mostly praise. The co-director described a pedagogical intent. Anno himself described confession and catharsis. And the final line was spoken by an actress who didn't know it was going to be the end of the film.

The more accurate reading might be this: Anno made a film about the unbearable difficulty of existing as a separate self in a world full of other people who can see and judge you. He made it when he was depressed, inside a studio that was committing financial fraud around him, and he used the only tool he trusted — his own psychology, and the psychologies he could reach. The result is not a statement about the audience. It is, as he said, a confession.

The question it leaves open — whether Shinji's choice of painful reality over comfortable dissolution was the right one, whether individuality is worth the cost — that one Anno did leave unanswered. On purpose.
