![Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone theatrical poster](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/8d/Evangelion1_0poster.jpg)

When *Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone* opened on September 1, 2007, Anno's [official statement shown as a poster in fifty Japanese cinemas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebuild_of_Evangelion) announced that "Evangelion has been controversial for 11 years. This is the true final chapter!" Hideaki Anno had walked away from Gainax, founded a new studio called [Khara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khara_(studio)), and brought the Eva cast back to theaters on the promise that this time, things would make sense. No more metaphysical meltdowns. No more ambiguous hand-drawn slides. Just the story, told properly, with better animation and a clear destination.

He was lying. But the brilliant part is that in 2007, he might not have known it yet.

## The Promise of Competence

1.0 delivers. That has to be acknowledged before anything else. Covering the first six TV episodes — Shinji arrives in Tokyo-3, is drafted into Unit-01, saves Rei, defeats Sachiel, watches Rei bleed and not flinch — the film compresses that material with remarkable efficiency and genuine visual upgrade. The Sachiel fight hits with a kinetic weight the TV version never achieved. Tokyo-3 looks like an actual city instead of a sparse production budget. The [third Angel's attack](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Differences_between_Evangelion_1.0_and_Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) is rendered in fluid theatrical widescreen, all hydraulics and impact. Studio Khara had money and time that Gainax never did, and the film earns its [¥1.468 billion box office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_1.0_You_Are_(Not)_Alone) on visual terms alone.

More importantly, the emotional architecture is intact. Shinji's refusal and capitulation, the unbearable gap between wanting to please a father who cannot be pleased, Rei's unsettling blankness that reads as both damage and transcendence — 1.0 preserves the essential Eva psychology. It does not simplify the characters into action-movie archetypes. In a [December 2006 Newtype interview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebuild_of_Evangelion), Anno stated he was finally happy to recreate Eva "as he wanted it to be" from the beginning, freed from the technological limitations of the mid-1990s. His official statement promised to bring back original creators alongside new collaborators; the result genuinely feels like Eva with more oxygen, Eva unencumbered by the weekly production chaos that left TV episodes 25 and 26 as therapy sessions rendered in pencil.

This is what makes the film so strategically effective. It earns your trust.

## The Seeds Nobody Noticed

But watch 1.0 again knowing where the Rebuild ends up, and the divergences reveal themselves even in this supposedly faithful installment. They are small, but they are deliberate.

In the original TV series, the mass beneath Central Dogma — the crucified giant impaled by the Lance of Longinus — is never identified as Lilith until Episode 24. The audience spends most of the show assuming it is Adam, the first Angel. 1.0 shows you Lilith much earlier, the Lance already in position during Operation Yashima. It is a tiny change. It does not affect the film's plot. But it signals something: Anno is not simply transcribing. He is reordering what you know and when.

More pointed: 1.0 includes a shot of Kaworu Nagisa standing on the moon, looking down at Earth. [Kaworu does not appear until Episode 24 of the TV series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_1.0_You_Are_(Not)_Alone), never mind the movie equivalent. He has no narrative function in 1.0. He is there as a tease — a signal that the story's architecture is different even when the surface events match. A character who was a devastating late revelation in the original is being positioned as someone who has been watching all along.

There is also a line of new dialogue about "a contract with Lilith" that does not exist in the TV series. It plants mythology that 1.0 never explains. These are not the edits of a director making a cleaner version of the same story. These are the moves of someone building a longer game while pretending to play a shorter one.

## Anno at Forty-Seven

The context for all of this matters. When Anno made the original *Neon Genesis Evangelion*, he was in the grip of a [clinical depression he had not yet identified as such](https://opus.ing/posts/how-hideaki-anno-depression-influenced-evangelion). He has said he fell into depression after *Nadia: The Secret of Blue Water* and did not emerge until sometime after the original series concluded. *The End of Evangelion* — brutal, audience-hostile, visually extraordinary — was made by a man burning something. You can feel it. The film wants something from you that it refuses to define.

By 2007, Anno was five years into his marriage to manga artist Moyoco Anno. People who knew him described a visible transformation: he lost weight, became less erratic, present in ways he had not been before. He had done the work. Not all of it — the Rebuild would eventually make clear that Evangelion was still unfinished business — but enough that 1.0 feels made by a different person than *The End of Evangelion*. The rawness is gone. The film is confident in a way the original could not afford to be.

This is worth sitting with. Anno in 2007 was healed *enough*. Enough to return to Eva from a position of relative stability. Enough to trust an audience again, to give them something they could follow and enjoy rather than something designed to repel or destabilize. The film's comparative accessibility is not commercial compromise — it is what Anno actually wanted to make, at that moment in his life.

But healing enough to make a clean film is not the same as being done with Evangelion. The announcement of the Rebuild stated plainly that Anno still needed to settle something. He told [Newtype magazine in December 2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebuild_of_Evangelion) that the technological constraints of the mid-1990s had prevented him from making Eva "as he always wanted it to be." 1.0 corrected the surface. The substance remained open.

## What Anno Thought He Was Doing

He had a plan, he said. The structure was announced clearly: Film 1 (1.0) would be faithful, a faithful introduction. Film 2 (2.0) would begin to diverge. Films 3 and 4 would be "totally different." Both 2.0 and 3.0 would be released by 2008.

That did not happen. 2.0 arrived in 2009. 3.0 in 2012. 3.0+1.0 — the concluding film — in 2021. The Rebuild took fourteen years, encompassing Anno's work on *Shin Godzilla* (2016), his ongoing mental health journey later documented in [interviews around *Shin Godzilla* and the final Rebuild film](https://time.com/6090247/neon-genesis-evangelion-thrice-upon-a-time-hideaki-anno/), and whatever continued excavation of himself the Eva material demanded.

The plan changed because Anno changed. The artist who made 1.0 in a state of relative stability, who intended a reasonably structured four-film project, encountered himself again in the making and found more work remaining. 3.0 arrived radically unlike anything 1.0 implied — a time-skip of fourteen years, Shinji rendered powerless and accused, the familiar cast fractured into enemies, the entire emotional register of the franchise inverted. Audiences who had used 1.0 as an on-ramp found themselves lost. The "true final chapter" had opened into something far less comfortable.

In retrospect, 1.0 was the only moment in the Rebuild where Anno could make the film he promised. The stability that produced it was real but not total. The seeds he planted — Kaworu on the moon, Lilith revealed early, the unexplained contract — were probably honest premonitions of a story he had not yet fully articulated to himself. 1.0 is the film of a man who believed he had resolved enough to rebuild cleanly. 3.0 is the film of a man discovering he was wrong.

## The Ending Anno Chose

Consider where 1.0 ends and what that choice reveals. The film closes on Shinji visiting Rei in the hospital after Operation Yashima, in an exchange lifted from the original series — but Rei's smile here lands differently. In 2007, Anno ends his Eva film on that image: two isolated, damaged people; one of them, haltingly, trying to reach the other. It is the most hopeful note the franchise had struck since the original's gentler episodes, and Anno chose it deliberately as the place to stop.

Compare that with where *The End of Evangelion* stops: Shinji strangling Asuka, Asuka saying *kimochi warui* — disgusting — into a ruined world. That was Anno at the bottom, Anno encoding his own inability to connect. 1.0's ending is the same creator, same characters, same genre of broken relationship — and the difference is five years of marriage and whatever work happened in between. Rei's smile is small, tentative, uncertain. But it is there. It is real. Anno in 2007 believed it was possible.

The marketing was genuinely misleading — "true final chapter" aged about as well as a milk carton left in the sun. But the film itself is more honest than its promotional materials. Anno planted exactly enough ambiguity to make the deception visible to anyone willing to look, while leaving it invisible to anyone who just wanted a clean version of the first arc. He served both audiences simultaneously and fully committed to neither.

That is a particular kind of artistic intelligence. Not duplicity exactly, but something more interesting: the honesty of an artist who does not yet know where he is going, encoding his uncertainty in a film designed to look like certainty. 1.0 is the moment Anno convinced you — and possibly himself — that Evangelion was finally over.

It was the beginning.
