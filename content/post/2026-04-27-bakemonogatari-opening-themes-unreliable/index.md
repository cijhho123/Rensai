"[Renai Circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renai_Circulation)" currently has over four thousand fan-made covers on Niconico. It went viral on TikTok in 2021 via a Lizz Robinett English-language version that hit [number one on the Billboard Japan TikTok chart](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/renai-circulation). A single mashup called "Everybody's Circulation" has accumulated [over 24 million YouTube views](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/renai-circulation). It is the most popular piece of music to emerge from *Bakemonogatari*, a series known for its musicality, and it is the most ironic.

"Renai Circulation" introduces the Nadeko Snake arc. Nadeko Sengoku is a shy, cute girl with bangs that curtain her eyes. She sings cheerfully about romance and the simple desire to be with someone. The arc she introduces is about curse magic: she was cursed by a girl whose boyfriend she attracted, and she herself placed a curse in return. Underneath the fuwa-fuwari melody is a story about violent jealousy, obsessive attachment, and the terrible things people do when they want someone and cannot have them.

The gap between the song and the arc is not an accident. It is the entire point of how *Bakemonogatari* uses its opening themes — and understanding that format explains why "Renai Circulation" became the series' most resonant cultural artifact by also becoming its most dangerous.

## The Format

*Bakemonogatari* made [an unusual and expensive decision](https://boosmahn.wordpress.com/2021/06/22/creator-styles-in-the-openings-of-the-monogatari-series/): each arc would get its own opening theme, composed by [Satoru Kōsaki](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/04/monogatari-opening-analysis-1-staple-stable/) and performed by the voice actress of the arc's heroine. "[Staple Stable](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Staple_Stable)" (Chiwa Saito) for Hitagi Crab. "[Kaerimichi](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Kaerimichi)" (Emiri Katō) for Mayoi Snail. "[Ambivalent World](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Ambivalent_World)" (Miyuki Sawashiro) for Suruga Monkey. "[Renai Circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renai_Circulation)" (Kana Hanazawa) for Nadeko Snake. "[Sugar Sweet Nightmare](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Sugar_Sweet_Nightmare)" (Yui Horie) for Tsubasa Cat. Each one is the character singing about herself, in the first person, from the inside — and [each was performed by the heroine's actual voice actress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Monogatari_episodes), a decision that collapsed the usual distance between performer and character.

This is the formal trap. Opening themes are generally understood as a show's self-presentation: here is what we are, here is the mood, here is what to expect. SHAFT inverted that. These openings are character self-presentations — not what the show thinks of these women, but what they think of themselves. And the *Monogatari* series is, architecturally, a study in the gap between self-perception and psychological reality. The openings are the first layer of unreliable narration, appearing before the episode has even begun.

Each song functions as the character's self-image. Each arc is what lives underneath it.

## "Staple Stable" and the Constructed Self

Hitagi's opening is the most honest in the series, in a particular sense: it shows its artifice openly. The title — "[Staple Stable](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/04/monogatari-opening-analysis-1-staple-stable/)" — encodes her condition directly. She has stapled herself together like patchwork to maintain stability. The visual world is greyscale. A giant expressionless Hitagi floats in a place with no gravity. The school supplies that appear — the weapons she deploys against anyone who gets too close — are scattered throughout.

The opening does not present Hitagi as she wants to be seen. It presents Hitagi as she has constructed herself: defended, suspended, stable only by the violence of the effort to stay that way. Unlike the later openings, "Staple Stable" is symbolically transparent about the work of self-construction. It can afford to be, because the arc is about dismantling that construction — the opening is not concealing the truth but displaying the architecture.

The contrast with "Renai Circulation" is precise. Hitagi knows she is constructed. The opening is her acknowledging the construction. Nadeko's opening presents a girl who does not know, or will not look at, what she is built from.

## "Kaerimichi": The Road She Cannot Find

Mayoi Hachikuji cannot find her way to her mother's house. She has been wandering the same neighborhood for years. Her arc is resolved — in a very different way from the series' usual pattern — by Araragi accepting her choice to keep wandering rather than insisting she move on.

"Kaerimichi" (The Road Home) is sung from her perspective and it is exactly what it says: a song about wanting to find the way home. But Mayoi cannot find the way home. That is her condition. The song is her longing, not her reality — the gap between what she wants and what she can access, encoded in the title, audible in the sweetness of the melody which has no resolution because the journey has no destination.

The opening is [accurate as a portrait of Mayoi's inner life](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Kaerimichi) and misleading as a description of her arc. She wants to find the road home. She cannot. The song is her expressing that want, which is different from the show promising she will fulfill it.

## "Renai Circulation": The Perfect Performance

Nadeko Sengoku has perfected the performance of innocence. Her bangs fall across her face — [a physical barrier between herself and direct vision](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/15/monogatari-opening-analysis-4-renai-circulation/), described in fan analysis as representing a girl who has deliberately shifted her gaze from the truth. She is shy, deferential, adorable. She wants only to be loved by Araragi.

The opening makes this performance permanent and musical. "Renai Circulation" is the perfect kawaii anime song — "[fuwa-fuwari](https://full2fungama.com/renai-circulation/)" floating and cheerful, Kana Hanazawa's voice hitting every cute-girl register, the instrumentation sugar-soft. It presents Nadeko exactly as she presents herself: innocent, sweet, wanting only simple things.

In the opening visuals, [Araragi's footsteps create larger ripples than Nadeko's](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/15/monogatari-opening-analysis-4-renai-circulation/). This is the single honest element in the sequence: it shows the power imbalance. She has much less influence over him than he has over her. But the visual is subtle enough to miss, and the song's sonic presentation overwhelms it.

The actual arc is about a girl who was cursed by someone she angered by attracting their boyfriend, and who placed a return curse. There is nothing accidental about this situation. Nadeko's obliviousness — her insistence that she doesn't understand why these things happen to her — is itself a performance. The opening encodes that performance. It is not ironic about Nadeko's self-image; it fully inhabits it. This is what makes it so effective as a trap: the song believes Nadeko.

That is why millions of people loved it. "Renai Circulation" works so well as a standalone pop song because it performs innocence without any visible cracks. The audience absorbs the self-portrait and then meets the character, and by then the portrait is already installed. Nadeko succeeds for two full seasons at presenting as the series' most harmless girl. The song is doing real cultural work, not just aesthetic work.

When she [transforms into a god in *Otorimonogatari*](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/15/monogatari-opening-analysis-4-renai-circulation/), the song becomes audibly retroactive — the fuwa-fuwari hook acquires new valence. The performance was always there. The audience was always watching the wrong thing.

## "Ambivalent World" and "Sugar Sweet Nightmare": Names That Confess

The remaining two openings are honest in different registers — their honesty lives in their titles.

"Ambivalent World" is exactly right about Suruga. She is ambitious, confident, athletic — the girl who seems to have everything figured out. The [heavy rock guitar in Miyuki Sawashiro's smoky delivery](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Ambivalent_World) matches her outward persona completely. But ambivalent is in the title, and the arc is about a girl who wished for things she won't admit wanting, including violence against a rival. The opening presents her confidence accurately and its title admits the split she is living.

"Sugar Sweet Nightmare" is the most explicit self-diagnosis in the series. Tsubasa Hanekawa is the honors student, the perfect girl, the classmate everyone admires. [The opening does a remarkable job of presenting her self-image](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/06/22/monogatari-opening-analysis-5-sugar-sweet-nightmare/): graceful, kind, softly romantic. And then it names what lives under that image: nightmare. The song is Tsubasa presenting herself as sugar-sweet while the title acknowledges the dream she is trapped in, the repressed animal that surfaces when she sleeps. She is the character the series is most honest about because she cannot be saved in this arc — her wound has become her entire self-construction, and the opening reflects both the surface and the shadow built into its title.

## What SHAFT Built

The format choice — [expensive, unusual for 2009](https://boosmahn.wordpress.com/2021/06/22/creator-styles-in-the-openings-of-the-monogatari-series/) — did something specific that is easy to miss in retrospect. By giving each heroine a first-person song performed by her own voice actress, SHAFT created five separate acts of self-narration that precede and frame the arcs. The viewer is primed to hear each character's internal voice before seeing her in the episode. The song is not the show's evaluation — it is the character's.

This matters because *Bakemonogatari* is formally committed to the unreliable narrator. Araragi perceives everything. His perception is not neutral — [the series is built around the gap between what its narrator sees and what is actually true](https://ajantakivandra.medium.com/learning-the-concept-of-unreliable-narrator-from-kaiki-deishuu-d04b6f75d311), a formal commitment that later seasons make explicit through characters like Kaiki who function as correction mechanisms. The openings extend the unreliability before his narration even begins: here is the character's version of herself, which you should also not entirely trust.

Five songs, five self-portraits, five instances of a character telling you who she is. Each arc is then about the distance between that account and what she actually is. The gap is different for each heroine — Hitagi knows her construction and is honest about it; Tsubasa names her darkness in her title while remaining unable to face it; Nadeko performs hers so perfectly it became a global hit.

![SHAFT studio logo — the studio whose decision to give each arc its own voice-actress-performed opening created the series' first layer of unreliable narration](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/SHAFT_logo.png)

The viral success of "Renai Circulation" is not external to the text. It is the text's argument, demonstrated. The song asked you to hear Nadeko's self-presentation as truth. Millions of people did. That is exactly what Araragi does. That is exactly what the arc is about.
