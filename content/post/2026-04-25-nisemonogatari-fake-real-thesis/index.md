There's a moment in Nisemonogatari that gets cited more than any other — an extended scene in which Koyomi Araragi brushes his little sister Karen's teeth. No clothes come off. Nothing explicitly sexual happens. And yet the scene became arguably the most notorious piece of content in an already transgressive franchise, generating years of debate about whether it's brilliant meta-commentary, pure exploitation, or something uncomfortably in between.

The right answer is that this framing is the wrong question. The toothbrush scene isn't about fanservice. It's a philosophical argument rendered in Shaft's visual language — and it's the most precise demonstration of what Nisemonogatari is actually about.

![Nisemonogatari key visual](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1/42302.jpg)

## Kaiki's Thesis

The series' central argument is delivered not by Araragi or his sisters, but by Kaiki Deishuu — the con man introduced in Nisemonogatari as the apparent villain, a fraud who sold cursed charms and damaged the people around him. Near the end of the series, he articulates what has been the show's organizing principle all along: "The more valuable is the fake, that in its own deliberate attempt to become real, becomes even more real than the real thing."

[Fan analysis has long identified this quote](http://surprisinglycomplex.blogspot.com/2013/06/nisemonogatari-to-be-fake.html) as Nisemonogatari's thesis statement. But it's worth dwelling on how strange the claim actually is. Kaiki isn't saying that fakes are as good as originals. He's saying they can be *more* real — that the effort required to be an imposter, the performance of an identity that isn't innate, can exceed the thing being imitated. The original doesn't have to work. The fake has to work constantly. And that work is what makes something real.

This is not a comfortable idea. Applied to the Araragi sisters, it means that Karen's performative heroism — her obsessive, exhausting insistence on acting as a "hero of justice" with neither superpowers nor institutional backing — might be more genuinely heroic than actual heroes, because she has to manufacture every ounce of it from nothing. Applied to Tsukihi, it means that being a literal impostor at the species level (she is a phoenix inhabiting a human life, not actually human at all) doesn't make her relationship with her family false. [As one analysis puts it](https://animetropolisblog.wordpress.com/2017/06/25/thematic-analysis-of-monogatari-series-part-1/): "she may be a fake, but that's what makes her the real Tsukihi Araragi."

## Karen: The Fake Hero

Karen Araragi's arc — the "Karen Bee" half of Nisemonogatari — is structured around the humiliation of her heroism. She's infected by a curse because she tried to help someone who didn't want help, couldn't fight off the supernatural entity because she has no supernatural abilities, and spends most of the arc bedridden. Her brother, who actually has powers, has to save her. By conventional shonen standards, she fails.

But the show doesn't treat her failure as proof that her heroism was empty. [The Bakemonogatari wiki's character notes](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Karen_Araragi) document Karen's defining attribute as an insistence on acting "just" regardless of outcome — a commitment to the *performance* of justice rather than its results. What the arc actually demonstrates is that this commitment, even when it produces catastrophic results, is real in a way that a more calculated heroism would not be. Karen's heroism is fake in the sense that she has no special abilities to back it up. It is real in the sense that she never stops performing it, even when it costs her.

Araragi, who has genuine supernatural power and repeatedly bails people out, is by contrast constantly ambivalent about his heroism. He helps people but needs to be convinced. He has what Karen lacks — the power to make heroism real — and he treats it as a burden. [A contemporary defense of the show](https://ghostlightning.wordpress.com/2012/01/08/nisemonogatari-01-and-the-courage-to-be-authentic/) put this precisely: Nisemonogatari is about "the courage to be authentic" — the will to keep performing an identity that has no inherent support. The show's thesis implies that Karen, despite being the "fake" hero, is the more heroically inclined of the two.

## Tsukihi: The Fake Human

The "Tsukihi Phoenix" arc takes the same thesis to a more extreme place. Tsukihi is revealed as a phoenix — an immortal supernatural entity that has taken the form of a human girl and lived inside the Araragi family without knowing what she is. Two supernatural assassins, the Gaen sisters, arrive to eliminate her precisely because she is not real. She is a parasite masquerading as a family member.

The show refuses to accept their framing. Araragi defends Tsukihi not by proving she is real — she demonstrably isn't, in the literal sense — but by insisting that the fourteen years of family life were not invalidated by her being a phoenix. [As the Bakemonogatari wiki notes](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihi_Araragi), Tsukihi has no memory of being a phoenix and no desire to be one. She is as invested in her human identity as any human would be — more invested, because she has to construct it moment by moment rather than inheriting it. By Kaiki's thesis, her fakeness is precisely what makes her authentically herself.

This is where Nisemonogatari gets philosophically uncomfortable in ways that go beyond the fanservice controversy. If fakeness becomes real through effort and investment, then authenticity is a practice rather than a property. You are not your essential nature — you are what you keep doing. Tsukihi keeps being a Tsukihi. That is enough.

## The Toothbrush Scene Is the Argument

Which brings us to episode 8.

The scene in which Araragi brushes Karen's teeth has been analyzed extensively as a piece of controversial fanservice — [ANN's retrospective feature](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2022-08-03/the-skeptic-guide-to-monogatari/.187165) describes it as portrayed "in excruciating detail, framed romantically, overtly sexually, with groaning noises." [The Terminal Anime blog](https://terminalanime.wordpress.com/2012/03/04/show-and-tell-the-use-of-fan-service-in-nisemonogatari/) examined whether the scene functions as meta-commentary or pure exploitation, and found the debate genuinely unresolvable from a fanservice-ethics framework.

The scene isn't really operating in that framework. It is the show's central thesis embodied in eleven minutes of animation.

Araragi and Karen are not having sex. The scene knows this. The audience knows this. The show knows the audience knows this. And yet the scene is constructed — eleven minutes of extreme close-ups on Karen's mouth and expression, reaction shots that frame the toothbrush as charged with intimacy, sound design full of breathing and groaning, all deployed in a setting (Araragi's bedroom, broad daylight) designed to feel incongruous — to be more erotically charged than a depiction of actual sex would be. Shaft's visual technique here is deliberate estrangement: the camera insists on treating the mundane act as intimate while the dialogue insists it is not intimate, and the gap between those two registers is where the heat comes from. It is a fake erotic scene that becomes more real as erotic experience than a real one could be. The act of working hard to not-be-sex while being entirely about sex produces exactly the effect Kaiki described: the fake, through deliberate effort, exceeds the original.

Whether this is sophisticated or reprehensible depends on how you feel about using incest-adjacent framing as a vehicle for philosophical demonstration. [The "5 Reasons Not to Drop Nisemonogatari"](https://ghostlightning.wordpress.com/2012/03/04/5-reasons-not-to-drop-nisemonogatari-after-9-episodes/) defense, written during the 2012 broadcast, argued that the scene's extremity was what gave the series its teeth — that Nisioisin was willing to go somewhere genuinely uncomfortable to prove a point. [Medieval Otaku's blog](https://medievalotaku.wordpress.com/2014/08/29/the-cuckoo-bird-christ-or-how-the-toothbrush-scenes-not-the-worst-part-of-nisemonogatari/) took the opposite view — not that the scene fails as commentary, but that focusing on it as the controversy distracts from the show's more structurally troubling elements elsewhere. This is the strongest version of the critique: the problem isn't that the toothbrush scene is philosophically empty, it's that the philosophy it demonstrates requires accepting premises the show hasn't earned your consent to.

What is undeniable is that the scene is doing something specific. It is not incidental. Nisemonogatari is a show about things that try so hard to be real that they exceed reality, and the most famous scene in the show is a depiction of something trying so hard to be erotic that it exceeds eroticism. Whether that constitutes a justification is a different question. That it constitutes a coherent artistic choice seems clear.

## Kaiki as the Series' Secret Protagonist

The other thing Nisemonogatari does that only becomes obvious in retrospect is introduce Kaiki Deishuu. [CBR's examination of Kaiki's character arc](https://www.cbr.com/monogatari-deishuu-kaiki-most-perplexing-characters-not-araragi/) and [EAT Monogatari's comprehensive character analysis](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/07/21/a-comprehensive-analysis-of-kaiki-deishuu/) both document how Kaiki functions in Nisemonogatari as the articulator of the thesis — the character who names what the narrative is demonstrating. He is a con man who believes in the value of cons. He sells fake charms, which produce real effects in the people who believe in them. He is the thesis walking around in a brown suit.

By the time Monogatari reaches Second Season, Kaiki is narrating his own arc and has been voted by Japanese fans as the series' most beloved male character — ahead of Araragi, who is nominally the protagonist. A character introduced as a villain became the franchise's unlikely center of gravity because his philosophy is the most interesting thing in it. [Game Rant's analysis of why Nisemonogatari was initially divisive](https://gamerant.com/nisemonogatari-divisive-criticism/) notes that this reassessment has been gradual — as the series continued and Kaiki's thesis proved out across arc after arc, Nisemonogatari came to look less like a weaker Bakemonogatari and more like the place where Monogatari found what it was actually about.

The con man was never lying. He was just early.

## The Weakest Entry That Holds Everything Together

Nisemonogatari has a reputation as the Monogatari entry you watch because you have to, not because you want to. [A 2020 retrospective](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2020/09/12/a-review-of-nisemonogatari/) calls it "one of the most visually pleasing entries in the franchise" while still conceding it's divisive on thematic grounds. [Star Crossed Anime's series ranking](https://starcrossedanime.com/ranking-all-monogatari-series-arcs/) places its arcs below Bakemonogatari, Second Season, and several other entries. [ANN's original review](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/nisemonogatari/episodes-1) was notably harsh. The critical consensus, even among fans, tends to position it as a transitional entry with too much fanservice and not enough of Bakemonogatari's emotional heft.

What this framing misses is that Nisemonogatari is the only entry in the series doing philosophy rather than horror. Bakemonogatari is an aberration-of-the-week show with a great Hitagi arc. Nisemonogatari is a sustained argument about what authenticity is and whether it can be manufactured. That argument runs through every scene — Karen's failed heroism, Tsukihi's non-human humanity, the explicit-that-isn't toothbrush episode — and lands, finally, in Kaiki's formulation of the thesis.

[Nisemonogatari sold 57,000 copies in its first tracked half and 64,000 in the second, ranking first among all 2012 anime releases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogatari_(series)). Commercially it was unambiguous. Critically it was dismissed — because critics were evaluating it as a successor to Bakemonogatari rather than as something doing a different thing entirely. What was missing was the critical vocabulary for what the show was doing, which required knowing how the rest of the series would develop before the pieces could click into place.

A fake that tries harder than the real thing eventually becomes the real thing. Nisemonogatari spent twelve years becoming the Monogatari entry people underestimated.
