There is a version of Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time that could have come out in 2015. Khara had been in production since [around 2009](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Evangelion:_3.0_+_1.0). The third film existed. The franchise was at peak cultural capital. By every external metric, this was the moment.

Instead, after finishing *Evangelion: 3.0 You Can (Not) Redo* in 2012, [Hideaki Anno entered a severe depressive episode](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2016-07-19/hideaki-anno-apologizes-for-delay-on-final-evangelion-film/.104479) that halted the project entirely. By 2015, he stated publicly that he could not work. He did not return to animation until after completing *Shin Godzilla* in 2016. COVID delays pushed the eventual release to March 8, 2021. Nine years between the third film and the fourth.

Almost everything written about this gap frames it as a failure of production — a symptom of Anno's notoriously difficult relationship with Evangelion, the franchise that has functioned as both his greatest achievement and his personal haunting. But that framing gets the causality backwards. The nine-year gap is not a problem the film has to overcome. It is why the film is what it is.

## What *End of Evangelion* Could Not Do

*End of Evangelion* (1997) is a film made from inside a breakdown. Anno has been direct about this: in the 2021 NHK documentary *Professional: The Way of Work*, [he described reading online threads about how best to kill him](https://kotaku.com/online-hate-took-evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-to-a-d-1846533756) and told the interviewer, "One time, I thought about jumping in front of a car. Another time, I thought about jumping from the company's roof." That is what *End of Evangelion* was made inside. The result is a work of genuine ferocity — formally radical, emotionally honest to the point of assault, ending with Shinji choosing individual consciousness over dissolution and the camera staring at him in the ruins of his decision while Asuka says *kimochi warui*.

It is not a hopeful ending. It can't be. Anno, at that moment, did not have hope available to him. The film is brilliant precisely because it doesn't fake it. There is no resolution in *End of Evangelion* because Anno was not in a position to write one. Shinji rejects Instrumentality, yes — but the film's emotional register is exhaustion and damage, not healing. He chose to be a self. Whether that self can live in the world is not a question the film can answer.

The Rebuild tetralogy was announced in 2006 as a "new interpretation" of the original story. The first two films — *1.0* and *2.0* — function more or less as this: engaging remakes that introduce new elements (primarily Mari) while excavating the core relationship between Shinji and his father with renewed emotional directness. Then *3.0* pivots. It is deliberately alienating: Shinji wakes up to a world fourteen years older that treats him as enemy, Rei is not Rei, Kaworu dies again, and the film offers no foothold. It was aggressively anti-cathartic, and fandom reacted with fury.

The nine years that followed *3.0* are, in retrospect, Anno processing what it cost to make that film — and, more importantly, doing the work that the fourth film would need him to have done.

## The Film That Required a Decade

Anno has been explicit about what Shin Godzilla was for him. "I actually needed some time off to work on something else to restart myself, to be able to face Evangelion again," he told [Collider in 2021](https://collider.com/hideaki-anno-evangelion-interview/). "Without it, I don't think I'd have been able to finish Evangelion yet."

Read that carefully. He is not saying Shin Godzilla was a detour. He is saying it was the mechanism of his return. The ability to finish *Thrice Upon a Time* — to write its specific kind of ending — required Anno to have become someone different than the person who finished *3.0*.

Talking to [TIME about the film's release](https://time.com/6090247/neon-genesis-evangelion-thrice-upon-a-time-hideaki-anno/), Anno described the shift in explicit terms: "25 years ago, when I first created Evangelion, the world was a different place, and I was a different person. The way I described things was more focused on the inner self, and describing the inner world. Right now, I think the time is right to focus on how I describe the outer world." And about Shinji's arc in the final film: "In the TV series, Ikari Shinji was still only able to talk about his own story and basically only save himself, but in this movie he's matured a little bit more and he is able to save people who are around him as well."

Anno is describing his own trajectory. The shift from inner world to outer world is not a thematic choice he made at a writing desk. It's the record of psychological change across years — arriving, eventually, at a place where connection is survivable, where the AT Field that protects you doesn't have to dissolve for you to touch another person.

## The Anti-Universe as Confession

The film's final act is the most formally audacious sequence in the Rebuild series. Shinji and his father confront each other in the "Anti-Universe" — a space where the logic of fiction breaks down visibly. The background fails. Scaffolding appears. Evangelion unit costumes hang on the walls of what is unmistakably a motion-capture soundstage. Shinji, in one vertiginous shot, falls through the surface of the film itself.

This is Anno doing something *End of Evangelion* also did — breaking the fourth wall, showing you the machinery — but for entirely different purposes. In 1997, the live-action sequence was confrontational: you, the viewer, are complicit in Shinji's existence; your need for him is part of what destroys him. The message was an accusation.

In *Thrice Upon a Time*, the exposed soundstage says something different. It says: this was always a movie. I made it. I am stepping out of it now. The artifice is visible not to indict the audience but to let them — and Anno — off the hook. There is a way out, and it doesn't require Instrumentality.

The film ends with Shinji and Mari leaving the Anti-Universe for live-action footage of real Ube, Yamaguchi Prefecture — [Hideaki Anno's actual hometown](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Ube-Shinkawa_Station). Specifically, the animated characters walk out of a train station based on Ube-Shinkawa station, reconstructed as it appeared during Anno's youth, the main station in the city where he was born. They are adults. They have left anime behind.

This is as personal as filmmaking gets. Anno literally reconstructed his childhood home on screen and walked his characters out of fiction into it. Not into ambiguity, not into ruin. Into ordinary daylight.

It is worth noting that some fans — and publicly, Gainax co-founder Toshio Okada — have read Mari as a stand-in for Moyoco Anno, Hideaki's wife, who pulled him back from the edge during his worst years. Anno has firmly rejected this, calling it "baseless assumptions." But the fact that the reading persists, and that the film ends with Shinji choosing to be with her in the real world, tells you something about how the Rebuild reads as an autobiography even when its author refuses the label.

## What This Means for the Rebuild Read

The Rebuild tetralogy has always invited the interpretation that it's Anno's autobiography mapped onto Shinji. *1.0* and *2.0* are re-engagement: Anno, recovered enough to revisit the material, finding new energy in it. *3.0* is the cost of that re-engagement: it broke him again. The nine years following are the actual work. And *Thrice Upon a Time* is the record of having come out the other side.

The film earned [¥10.23 billion at the Japanese box office](https://www.siliconera.com/evangelion-3-0-1-0-almost-10-billion-yen-japanese-box-office/) — becoming the highest-grossing entry in the franchise and the first film produced by Khara and distributed by Toei to hit that threshold. Anno's closing statement on Evangelion drew the largest audience any of these films had ever seen.

His reaction, after it was done: "The story of Evangelion has ended. I am not going to continue on, but now that it's ended, it's actually very peaceful for me."

*End of Evangelion* couldn't give Shinji that. It couldn't even give Anno that. The 1997 film is an act of enormous artistic courage — making something honest when honest meant making something bleak. *Thrice Upon a Time* is a different act of courage: the willingness, after everything, to mean it when you say things are okay.

You can only write that ending if you've arrived at it. Not as theme. As experience. That is what the nine years were for.
