Koyomi Araragi graduates from high school and disappears from the story. Off & Monster Season opens with that absence already established, no fanfare, no mourning sequence. The girls are just — there. Living. Dealing with supernatural incidents, personal failures, existential questions. Getting on with it.

And the series immediately becomes more interesting.

That's the honest provocation buried in [Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=31635), which premiered on ABEMA in July 2024 under [first-time series director Midori Yoshizawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midori_Yoshizawa). For fifteen-odd years and a dozen-plus adaptations, Koyomi Araragi was Monogatari's nominal center — the self-deprecating high school student turned half-vampire who kept stumbling into supernatural crises and solving them, usually by placing himself between a girl and whatever was hurting her. He was also the narrator through whom nearly every female character in the series was introduced, framed, and understood. [According to the Monogatari wiki](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Monogatari_Series_Off_%26_Monster_Season), Araragi is the narrator for all First Season and Final Season arcs as well as several from Second Season. The series wore his perspective like a second skin.

Off Season removes that skin. What's underneath is something Nisio Isin has arguably been building toward since Bakemonogatari: a story that was never really about Araragi at all.

## The Structural Problem Araragi Created

To understand what Off Season fixes, you need to sit with what Araragi's narrative dominance actually cost the series.

Araragi is, by design, an [unreliable narrator whose objectives are frequently at odds with the girls he's ostensibly helping](https://anilist.co/forum/thread/72812). He's an enabler, a fixer, a man who cannot stop inserting himself into other people's crises. In Bakemonogatari he literally carries Senjougahara to school on his back rather than letting her walk — and the show frames this as romantic devotion rather than control. In Nadeko Medusa, Araragi's inability to properly intervene or even honestly communicate with Nadeko is one of the structural causes of her spiral into becoming a snake god. His heroism creates dependency. His presence shapes the girls' choices even when he's not actively making them.

The series is not unconscious of this. Senjougahara tells Araragi explicitly that he enables bad behavior; Shinobu's entire relationship with him is structured around a trauma bond the show never entirely resolves. [A 2017 fan analysis of Araragi's character argues](https://animetropolisblog.wordpress.com/2017/10/12/character-reanalysis-of-araragi-koyomi-part-2/) that he's a character whose flaws the series consistently codes as virtues — and that the girls he saves are persistently denied the space to save themselves. That reading has accumulated weight over the years because the evidence for it only multiplies. What Araragi does — repeatedly, across every season — is prevent the central question of each arc from being answered by the character it belongs to.

The question that Off Season poses — one that animates every arc from Orokamonogatari through Shinobumonogatari — is the one Araragi's presence kept foreclosing: *Are you still in love with your choices?* [As one review put it](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/12/anime-analysis-monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-2024-by-midori-yoshizawa/), the season's deepest inquiry is "have you changed, and how do you maintain your balance between dreams and reality?" That question is only meaningful when it's asked of someone who has to answer for themselves. Araragi's structural role in the earlier series was to answer it on everyone's behalf.

## Yotsugi Ononoki Learns to Need Things

The first arc of Off Season, Orokamonogatari's "Tsukihi Undo" segment, opens not with a mystery but with a premise: Yotsugi Ononoki, a [corpse puppet shikigami who is not supposed to have emotions](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Yotsugi_Ononoki), is living in the Araragi household pretending to be a doll. She gets caught moving. Then she has to fight a supernatural threat without any backup from Araragi, who is gone.

The arc is structured as comedy, but its philosophical argument is serious. [Wrong Every Time's analysis of the episode](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/11/25/monogatari-off-monster-season-episode-1/) identifies the central tension correctly: what does it mean to be an entity — a corpse puppet, a constructed thing — that was never supposed to have needs? The answer the arc delivers is that meaningful existence requires difficulty and connection, that without challenge "we collapse into listless, shapeless complacency." But what matters as much as the conclusion is who delivers it. Yotsugi doesn't receive this insight from Araragi explaining it to her. She earns it by getting into trouble, humiliating herself, and having to ask Tsukihi and Nadeko for help — characters who are her peers, not her patron.

That shift in how insight is delivered maps directly onto the series' changed structure. In the Araragi-centered seasons, characters received their growth moments when Araragi validated, rescued, or sacrificed for them. Here, Yotsugi's revelation comes laterally — from other women she underestimated, through embarrassment rather than heroism.

## Nadeko Sengoku Finishes Her Own Arc

The Nademonogatari adaptation is where Off Season makes its strongest case. [Nadeko Sengoku's arc across the original series](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Nadeko_Sengoku) is one of the most sophisticated and underappreciated in Monogatari: she presents as a timid, passive girl while concealing a raging need for recognition, love, and escape from responsibility. Her transformation into a snake god in Second Season's Otorimonogatari is the series at its most caustic — Nadeko weaponizes the passive-victim archetype she's been playing and turns it into genuine menace. Araragi's inability to see her clearly until she's already gone too far is, partly, an indictment of the narrative's own limitations.

Off Season picks Nadeko up years later, living in isolation, trying to become a manga artist. Her past is not erased; it's present in the split personalities she confronts within her own mind. [What the arc delivers is a confrontation with those alternate selves](https://crushingonthemoon.wordpress.com/2019/01/06/sengoku-nadeko-part-8-nadeko-is-dead-long-live-nadeko/) — the Nadeko who performed helplessness, the Nadeko who became divine and terrifying, the Nadeko who just wanted to disappear. She fights them. She wins. And crucially, she does all of this without Araragi showing up to either enable her or rescue her. The lesson she learns — [that you can embrace being alive through uncertainty, that identity is something you actively choose rather than inherit](https://medium.com/anitay-official/monogatari-off-season-nademonogatari-anime-review-doctorkev-and-lia-9ceb7309d102) — could not have been delivered while Araragi was around, because his presence would have made it conditional on his approval.

The arc's structure is also formally interesting. It doesn't resolve neatly. Nadeko becomes a manga artist, yes, but the process is shown as genuinely hard, full of false starts and self-sabotage. [The series uses manga-panel room compositions and mixed-media imagery](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/12/anime-analysis-monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-2024-by-midori-yoshizawa/) to reflect Nadeko's fragmented interiority — her attempts to narrativize herself onto paper while simultaneously living inside stories she can't control. It's Monogatari's long-standing interest in the relationship between storytelling and self-deception, applied here with a character who was previously only ever seen from the outside.

## What Midori Yoshizawa Changes

[Midori Yoshizawa is the first female series director](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midori_Yoshizawa) in the Monogatari franchise. [Akiyuki Shinbo, who had directed every major entry since Bakemonogatari, stepped back to "chief director"](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-03-24/monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-anime-teaser-confirms-staff-2024-launch-with-/.209164) with series composition duties while Yoshizawa took the director's chair. She had been doing episode direction and storyboarding for the series since the Owarimonogatari era, [described by the Sakuga Blog](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/08/23/monogataris-past-present-and-future-midori-yoshizawa-oms/) as an agreed-upon source of hope for the studio entering a new chapter — "little to no experience at that level of responsibility" as a first-time series director, but already deeply embedded in Shaft's aesthetic DNA.

The timing is not incidental. A series that spent over a decade filtering its female characters through a male narrator's perspective now has a woman deciding what to frame, and how. [As the ANN Preview Guide noted](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2024/summer/monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season/.212230), the visual language of Off Season feels "just a bit more active than the static, framed look that defined so much of Monogatari for so long" — the camera moves closer, less like a voyeur and more like a witness. Shinbo's characteristic tableaux — characters posed against abstract backgrounds while monologuing — were always deeply cinematic, but they also kept the viewer at a deliberate remove, encouraging a kind of aesthetic appreciation that could tip toward objectification.

Yoshizawa doesn't abandon the Shaft visual identity. The typography, the head tilts, the mixed-media inserts, the bold color work — [all of that is present and recognizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaft_%28company%29). What changes is the emotional temperature. [The Sakuga Blog's retrospective on Yoshizawa's work](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/08/23/monogataris-past-present-and-future-midori-yoshizawa-oms/) notes that she brings a synergistic approach to the series' themes of time, identity, and creation — and that her prior background as an animator, rather than a pure director, gives her layouts an "ambitious" quality that prioritizes psychological accuracy over composition for its own sake. [Asian Movie Pulse describes her pacing](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/12/anime-analysis-monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-2024-by-midori-yoshizawa/) as "rich in modulations — dynamic openings that slowly accumulate tension before explosive climaxes," with bold close-ups on characters' eyes privileged over the grand symbolic tableaux that characterized Shinbo's era. When you're telling stories about women asking whether they're still the person they wanted to be, that emphasis on face and interiority over spectacle is a directorial choice with real consequences.

## Shinobumonogatari and the 600-Year Debt

The first Monster Season book adapted in this cour is [Shinobumonogatari](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobumonogatari), and it represents Off Season's structural capstone — the moment where the series finally pays the debt it owes to its most consequential character.

[Shinobu Oshino / Kiss-Shot Acerola-Orion Heart-Under-Blade](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobu_Oshino) is, on paper, one of the most powerful entities in the Monogatari universe — a 600-year-old vampire queen reduced to a child form and tethered to Araragi as a consequence of events in Kizumonogatari. She has been his companion, his shadow, his most intimate relationship, and also the character the series has most consistently used as a prop. Her interiority, when glimpsed, is extraordinary — a being who has watched civilizations rise and fall, who has loved and destroyed and been betrayed across centuries, whose relationship with Araragi is built on a shared trauma so specific that neither of them can fully explain it to outsiders.

The Shinobumonogatari arc in Off & Monster Season centers on a specific mystery: five members of the women's basketball club at Naoetsu High School go missing, and [Suruga Kanbaru and a newcomer named Seiu Higasa become entangled in something that connects to Shinobu's past](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-09-07/monogatari-off-and-monster-season-anime-previews-shinobumonogatari-story-in-video/.215218). The supernatural threat here — teenage girls transformed, vampire lineages and their consequences — forces Shinobu to reckon with what her centuries of existence have left behind. The mystery is hers to solve because the damage is hers to own.

![Shinobumonogatari key visual from Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season](https://i0.wp.com/anitrendz.net/news/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/shinobumonogatari-key-visual-1.jpg?resize=350%2C495&ssl=1)

Araragi appears briefly — he was never going to disappear completely from a story about his permanent co-protagonist — but the arc belongs to Shinobu and Kanbaru. Not filtered through his narration. Not dependent on his rescue. [Her character's arc in this season explores how 600 years of loss and loneliness confront a present that her past has shaped without her consent](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/kissshot-shinobu-the-enigmatic-heart-of-the-monogatari-series/7409d5edb2f88466ba36130178fa4fdc) — and it gives her the space, for the first time, to answer for that history herself.

This matters for the series in the same way Nadeko's arc matters: it reveals that the character always had this depth, that the earlier series' filtering of her through Araragi's gaze was a narrative choice rather than a creative limit.

## The Proof of a Theory

[Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season received a second cour announcement in July 2025](https://anitrendz.net/news/2025/07/03/more-monogatari-off-and-monster-season-is-on-the-way/), confirming that Nisio Isin and Shaft have more of these stories to tell. The remaining arcs — Musubimonogatari, Yoimonogatari, Amarimonogatari, Ōgimonogatari, and the two-volume Shinomonogatari — presumably continue the pattern established in the first cour: characters who were peripheral or mediated in earlier seasons now occupying the full space of their own stories.

What first cour already proves is the theory embedded in the series from the beginning. Monogatari was never actually about Araragi's heroism. It was always about the girls he kept almost-but-not-quite letting off the hook — the ones who solved their own problems while he took notes, the ones who were becoming who they needed to be despite his well-meaning interference. The harem structure, the self-deprecating narrator, the elaborate word-game dialogues where Araragi positions himself as a straight man to everyone else's complexity — these were always the tell. The series was clearly more interested in its female characters than in its protagonist. It just needed him to graduate before it could say so openly.

Off Season is what happens when the structure finally matches the actual subject. Yotsugi finds out she needs things. Nadeko finishes her own arc. Shinobu gets to inhabit her own history. And the series becomes, retroactively, what it always was trying to be: a genuine study of identity, change, and what it means to live with the person you've become — without anyone to solve that question for you.

The man left. The story got better.
