In 1996, Kentaro Miura published Chapter 83 of Berserk — an episode revealing the "Idea of Evil," the entity underpinning the entire cosmology of the world. It was published once, [in Young Animal issue #14](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon), and then Miura blocked it from every subsequent collection. His explanation: "The appearance of god in the manga conclusively determines its range. I thought that might limit the freedom of the story development. I myself don't know if the Idea of Evil will show up again in the manga or not."

A creator withdrawing chapter 83 of a story that would eventually run to 374 chapters before his death, because he felt it closed off too many possibilities too early. Wanting to remain free to be uncertain. Willing to remove content that millions of readers had already consumed, simply because it didn't match his relationship to his own work in progress.

This is the specific thing the Berserk continuation cannot inherit. Not the ending — [Kouji Mori knows the ending](https://fandomwire.com/i-thought-miura-would-be-angry-kouji-moris-decision-to-continue-berserk-after-original-mangakas-death-will-break-your-heart/). Not the art — Studio Gaga's key artist Kurosaki has been described by Mori as producing work that occasionally [gives him goosebumps](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-manga-supervisor-difficult-continuing/). What they cannot inherit is Miura's freedom to not know where he was going, and to refuse to go there until he was ready.

## The Promise Mori Made

When [Hakusensha and Kouji Mori announced the continuation in June 2022](https://www.comicsbeat.com/berserk-manga-to-continue-serialization-under-kouji-mori-and-studio-gaga/), nine months after Miura's death, the terms were specific: Mori was the only person Miura had told the complete story of Berserk to, "from the major plot arcs all the way to the final panel." He made a solemn promise to stick strictly to those plot points and not add storylines or details he didn't hear directly from Miura.

This framing is meant to be reassuring. [It reads like fidelity](https://gizmodo.com/berserk-manga-continuation-kentaro-miura-kouji-mori-1849028153). Mori isn't making up a new Berserk; he's reconstructing what Miura already created internally and never got to put on paper. The story already exists — it just needs to be drawn.

The problem is that this framing treats a manga as equivalent to its plot outline. And Berserk, specifically, is the franchise that most aggressively disproves that equation.

Miura spent decades on individual chapters. The hiatuses were not interruptions; they were part of the texture of reading the work. A generation of fans [grew up waiting for Berserk](https://www.tcj.com/memento-miuri/), and that waiting was not separate from the experience — it was the experience. You don't just read Berserk; you live alongside it. The real-time passage of years created a relationship between reader and text that no other franchise quite replicates. When the 1997 Eclipse happened in publication time, readers had been following Guts for years. The betrayal hit differently than if they'd read to it in a collected edition. Time was the medium.

The continuation's chapters, since resuming in June 2022, are faster than Miura's late-period work by a measurable margin. [Studio Gaga has released ten chapters](https://mangashed.com/is-the-berserk-manga-finished/) through Chapter 375, roughly 6-7 chapters per year. Miura, [according to the Wikipedia chapter list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Berserk_chapters), published approximately 2 to 3 chapters per year in his last five years of life — a single chapter in all of 2021, three in 2020, two in 2019. Studio Gaga is publishing at more than twice Miura's late-period rate.

This is not necessarily a criticism of quality — it may reflect the difference between one person agonizing over every line and a studio with multiple skilled artists working in coordination. But faster pacing has real consequences. [Screen Rant's analysis of Chapter 368](https://screenrant.com/berserk-page-proves-end-with-miura/) notes a scene where the Skull Knight suddenly appears, saves a group from attacking spirits, and delivers a major exposition dump about Griffith's invasion — all on a single page. A character even comments: "He is an ally of justice! Probably!" The rhythm is wrong. In Miura's late-period Berserk, the Skull Knight's appearances were events separated by years of publication; his sudden appearance and vanishing was part of an atmospheric logic that required the reader to have been waiting. Giving him an exposition speech and a comedy response line on a single page collapses the atmospheric distance that made him work.

## What Mori Knows and What He Can't

Mori has been admirably honest about the constraints he's operating under. ["It's going to be imperfect"](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-new-writer-manga-continue/) is not something most franchise supervisors say publicly. He's acknowledged that [recreating Miura's distinctive linework and shading](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-manga-supervisor-difficult-continuing/) — the cross-hatching in particular, the Berserker Armor's elaborately interlocking plates — is a challenge no manga artist finds easy.

What he hasn't fully reckoned with, at least publicly, is that pacing is narrative content, not stylistic preference. When Miura spent months on a single chapter, he wasn't being inefficient. He was making decisions about how long each moment deserved to exist in the reader's present tense. Berserk's famous pages — the Eclipse, Guts' childhood flashbacks, the fight with Mozgus — derive their force not only from the draftsmanship but from the weight of the wait. Readers arrived at those scenes having anticipated them across months or years of narrative distance. The page hits because the page earned it. Quickening that tempo doesn't just change the rhythm; it retroactively changes what those moments will be for the readers who arrive at them cold.

The Skull Knight's sudden appearance in Chapter 368 would have been built differently by Miura, not because Mori lacks Miura's narrative intelligence, but because Mori is working from a plot summary of what happens. What Miura had — and what died with him — was the knowledge of how long the approach needed to be.

## The Chapter 83 Problem

Miura's decision to withdraw Chapter 83 from collections illuminates something essential about his relationship to his own work. He wasn't simply controlling the narrative; he was preserving his own creative uncertainty, his freedom to not know what the story was definitively going to be. [A manga whose creator explicitly stated "I myself don't know if the Idea of Evil will show up again"](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon) is not a franchise with a clearly plotted destination. It is a franchise where the creator's active uncertainty was part of the generative mechanism.

Mori's role eliminates this. He must know what the Idea of Evil's ultimate significance is. He must know how the story ends. He cannot not know — that's the entire basis of his legitimacy as a continuation author. But Miura's specific genius was partly constituted by not knowing yet: by following the logic of his narrative into territory he was exploring as much as constructing. [The Comics Journal's memorial essay](https://www.tcj.com/memento-miuri/) frames Berserk as a work that succeeds precisely because it "embraces contradiction rather than resolving" it, making the unfinished state philosophically coherent rather than merely incomplete.

A continuation that knows how it ends cannot make that claim. It can faithfully render the plot points that Mori memorized. What it cannot do is replicate the creative conditions under which those plot points would have been generated by Miura — the conditions of genuine uncertainty, genuine exploration, genuine freedom to be wrong and to pull content from collections when it limits you.

## Artifact Versus Process

This is not an argument that [the continuation should not have happened](https://gamerant.com/why-berserk-should-never-have-returned/). The ethical and artistic cases are genuinely complicated, and the fans who are grateful that Berserk will have an ending are not wrong to be grateful. The question is whether what they'll receive is an ending for Berserk or an ending for the plot of Berserk — and whether that distinction matters.

Serialized manga occupies an unusual position among art forms: it is simultaneously an artifact and a process. The bound volumes are objects you can hold; the reading of it in publication was an experience spread over decades, in which the gaps between chapters were as much a part of the work as the chapters themselves. When readers waited months between chapters during the Fantasia arc, sitting with Guts' journey to Elfhelm across seasons of their own life, the eventual arrival had a weight that no collected-edition binge reader can replicate. The continuation is producing objects — chapters, volumes, plot resolutions — that are continuous with Miura's objects. [Whether it is continuous with Miura's process](https://studybreaks.com/culture/reads/berserk-kentaro-miura-posthumous-continuations/) is a different question.

The [fan community at SkullKnight.net](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/discussing-studio-gagas-art.16716/) has engaged carefully with the question of what's different, often charitably: the art is different but sincere, the pacing is different but not disrespectful. These are good-faith readers being fair. But fair doesn't close the gap between what Mori received — Miura's story, in outline — and what Miura had that couldn't be outlined. The pace. The uncertainty. The freedom to pull Chapter 83 from circulation because it closed off something he wasn't ready to close.

Kouji Mori knows how Berserk ends. That's what makes him the right person to finish it. What he can't reproduce is the 37 years it took Miura to not know yet.
