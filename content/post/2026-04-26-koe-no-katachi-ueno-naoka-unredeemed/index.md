At the end of *Koe no Katachi*, Shoya Ishida has been transformed. He has crossed from the world of the shuttered and self-loathing into something that resembles presence — hearing sounds, seeing faces, standing at the school festival and being flooded with the weight of being alive. It is earned, genuinely moving, and the central emotional event the manga has been building toward. The story that Yoshitoki Oima tells is, in its deepest structure, a story about one person's extraordinary transformation through guilt.

And then there is Ueno Naoka, working in a shop. Largely the same. Her feelings for Shoya calmed. Not redeemed. Not destroyed. Just: living her life.

This is the most accurate thing *Koe no Katachi* does, and the thing the 2016 film could not bring itself to keep.

![A Silent Voice manga and film side by side — different moral stories](https://static0.gamerantimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/asilentvoice-filmandmangacover.jpg)

## Who Ueno Is

Naoka Ueno was Shoya's friend and co-participant in the bullying of Shoko Nishimiya in elementary school. According to the [A Silent Voice manga Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)), she was part of the peer group that systematically excluded and humiliated Shoko through middle school and into her teens. When the bullying is eventually traced back to Shoya alone, Ueno participates in that reassignment of blame — she lets him be the scapegoat. She does not correct the record.

Her motivation throughout the series is an unrequited love for Shoya that began in elementary school. Shoko attracted Shoya's attention — first as a target, then in ways Ueno found threatening. The bullying was jealousy wearing the mask of peer behavior. This does not excuse it. But it makes Ueno more specific and more comprehensible than a simple antagonist: she is someone who participated in cruelty for reasons she probably never examined clearly, and who has been carrying that unexamined weight ever since. In an [ICv2 creator interview](https://icv2.com/articles/columns/view/34645/creator-interview-yoshitoki-oima-a-silent-voice), Oima noted that Naoka reminds her of a real girl who "was high up the social ladder" of her class — confirming that Ueno is not a character invented for narrative convenience but a portrait drawn from a type Oima recognized.

What distinguishes Ueno from Shoya is not the depth of her involvement but the depth of her reckoning with it. Shoya's transformation is the central event of the manga. He reconstructs himself through guilt, through the effort of acknowledging what he did and what it cost. Ueno does not do this. She cannot, or will not, or has not yet found the structure that Shoya found. She feels something — the manga makes this clear — but she does not process it into anything that changes her fundamental behavior.

## The Hospital and What It Reveals

The scene outside the hospital, after Shoko's suicide attempt, is Ueno at her worst and her most revealing. She physically assaults Shoko — slamming her against a fence while Shoko is still in a cast from the fall, as [the manga's character analyses consistently document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)). The violence comes from somewhere very specific: Ueno cannot tolerate Shoko's continued existence as someone Shoya cares about, even in a crisis, even at the hospital where his life may still be at risk.

The assault is not random cruelty. It is a precise expression of what Ueno is: someone whose emotional architecture never fully evolved past the jealousy that drove her bullying at ten years old. She is not a cartoon villain. She has genuine feelings. She sat at Shoya's bedside while he was comatose. She was present, concerned, afraid. And when that fear and love and guilt crystallized into a moment of confrontation with Shoko, what came out was violence.

The [MangaShed analysis of the manga's ending](https://mangashed.com/a-silent-voice-manga-ending/) observes that Ueno's arc ultimately suggests "redemption varies individually, and not everyone undergoes profound change despite being part of the same story." This framing is diplomatic. What the manga is actually showing is something harder: Ueno is the version of this story that happens most often. Most bullies do not have Shoya's extraordinary guilt, his capacity for sustained self-examination, his ability to see the person he hurt as fully human. Most bullies have something closer to Ueno's experience: awareness that something was wrong, a complicated tangle of feelings about it, and no particular transformation.

## The Rain Scene and the Ferris Wheel

After the hospital, Shoko follows Ueno into the rain and offers her an umbrella. This scene — the target of persistent cruelty offering shelter to the person who just assaulted her — is [documented in fan analyses](https://diaryofcas.wordpress.com/2018/05/04/naoki-uenos-unrequited-love-in-koe-no-katachi/) as the moment that begins to crack Ueno's certainty. Shoko's refusal to meet hostility with hostility, her continued reaching toward connection even with someone who has caused her harm, starts to destabilize the emotional logic Ueno has been operating on.

But this does not produce transformation. The ferris wheel scene makes this explicit: Ueno tells Shoko that she hates her, asks her to agree to hate each other, and when Shoko refuses, slaps her. There is no catharsis here. Shoko's kindness does not unlock Ueno's good self. What it does is confuse her, destabilize her, leave her without the organizing principle of clear resentment. She is not redeemed. She is disturbed.

The [Game Rant comparison of the manga and film](https://gamerant.com/a-silent-voice-the-anime-manga-differences/) notes that the manga's Ueno has "romantic feelings for Shoya that become clearer" and that she and Shoko "come to a better understanding of one another." This is technically accurate but overstates what the manga actually delivers. The "better understanding" between Ueno and Shoko is less a resolution than a cease-fire — Ueno has not stopped disliking Shoko. She has stopped actively attacking her. The distance between them "shrinks," as the article puts it, but does not close.

## What the Film Cut and Why

The 2016 KyoAni film adaptation directed by Naoko Yamada excises almost all of this. The film's Ueno is simpler: the antagonist whose cruelty motivates the central story and who remains peripheral afterward. Her unrequited love for Shoya, the hospital assault, the ferris wheel confrontation, and her complex epilogue are all removed. The film's Ueno functions as a plot element. The manga's Ueno is a person.

![A group shot from the A Silent Voice film — a world from which Ueno's complexity was largely removed](https://static0.gamerantimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/asilentvoice-amusementpark-groupshot.jpg)

This is not a production failure. The film is beautiful and emotionally coherent, and the trade was deliberate. Director Naoko Yamada explained her thinking in a [Sakuga Blog interview](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/05/30/koe-no-katachi-director-naoko-yamada-interview/): "the story is fundamentally about Shouya, so she had to focus on him and couldn't lose that focus as a director." She also reportedly did not read the full manga before production, wanting to approach the material fresh rather than as an adaptation — according to [FandomWire's coverage of her approach](https://fandomwire.com/i-deliberately-didnt-read-it-naoko-yamada-on-why-she-didnt-read-a-silent-voice-deliberately-before-making-the-film/), she deliberately avoided the complete source material to avoid making "a fan movie." Ueno's full arc is what you lose when the story is fundamentally about Shoya and only about Shoya. A film cannot give equal weight to a character who never fully changes without destabilizing the central emotional arc.

But what the film cuts is not incidental — and Yamada's stated framing makes the stakes clear. A film that is "fundamentally about Shouya" becomes a film whose implicit argument is that Shoya's level of guilt and transformation is what accountability looks like. The audience leaves believing that bullies who do enough damage tend to suffer, examine themselves, change. Ueno is the counter-evidence the film removes. She is what most bullies actually look like: someone who knows, at some level, that she did something wrong, who does not achieve transformation, and who continues living. By centering Shoya and only Shoya, the film unintentionally produces a flattering picture of how bullying culture works. The manga, by keeping Ueno present and unresolved, refuses that comfort.

## The Ending She Gets

At the manga's conclusion, Ueno is shown at the Coming of Age ceremony reunion, then at work in a shop. Her feelings for Shoya have calmed. The [A Silent Voice film Wikipedia article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)) and [manga Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)) both describe the manga's extended epilogue as showing characters in states of partial closure rather than complete transformation — and Ueno is the clearest example of this. The [Shuu's Wonderland analysis of the manga](https://shuubah.wordpress.com/2021/04/06/koe-no-katachi-a-silent-voice-in-the-eyes-of-a-physically-disabled-bully-victim/) notes that many readers find they cannot hate Ueno despite everything, precisely because the manga draws her with enough interiority to make her comprehensible even when she is doing something unjustifiable.

She is not punished for the hospital assault. She is not publicly confronted with what she did. She is not required to apologize to Shoko for the ferris wheel slap. She is just living her life, working her job, carrying whatever version of guilt or rationalization or selective memory allows her to do so. This is the ending [Yoshitoki Oima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoshitoki_%C5%8Cima) gave her, and it is the honest one.

Oima has stated in [creator interviews](https://kodansha.us/2016/06/07/interview-yoshitoki-oima-silent-voice/) that the manga's central theme is not bullying or deafness but communication — the difficulty of truly connecting with another person, the damage done when connection fails, the effort required to repair it. Notably, she told [FandomWire](https://fandomwire.com/a-silent-voice-creator-yoshitoki-oima-it-was-not-the-main-theme-on-her-movies-real-theme-that-was-born-out-of-regret/) that she deliberately chose not to write the story from Shoko's perspective because she "didn't think that was interesting" — she found it easier to approach from the bully's point of view, believing more readers could relate to Shoya than to Shoko. This is an extraordinary admission when placed next to Ueno: if Shoya's perspective is more accessible, and Ueno is closer to how bullies actually function, what does it mean that the more accessible character is also the exceptional one? Ueno is communication's failure mode maintained indefinitely. She cannot receive Shoko's persistent kindness as what it is, because the structure of her feelings about Shoko — the years of jealousy, the guilt she has never processed, the complexity of wanting Shoya while resenting the person who complicates that desire — prevents genuine reception.

The [Shuu's Wonderland analysis of the manga from a disabled bully victim's perspective](https://shuubah.wordpress.com/2021/04/06/koe-no-katachi-a-silent-voice-in-the-eyes-of-a-physically-disabled-bully-victim/) points out that the manga is not an accurate representation of how bullying usually resolves — Shoya's level of self-examination is exceptional. But the same analysis notes that the manga's real insight is the peer group: the way cruelty distributes through social structures rather than concentrating in a single bad actor. Ueno is not the exception in that group. She is the rule.

## What She Costs the Narrative

The critical charge against Ueno — that the manga is too forgiving of her, that she escapes consequences, that the narrative is complicit in her lack of accountability — is a real critique. The [CBR analysis of bullying in A Silent Voice](https://www.cbr.com/a-silent-voice-anime-film-bullying-impact/) acknowledges the tension: the manga is committed to realistic outcomes, and realistic outcomes include people who harm others substantially and then continue their lives without commensurate consequence.

But the alternative — a manga that punishes Ueno more dramatically, that requires her to achieve Shoya's level of redemption as a narrative obligation — would be the less honest work. People like Ueno do not become Shoya. They become people who work in shops and remember, sometimes, what they did when they were younger, and live with that in whatever way they can manage. The manga does not say this is fine. It says this is real.

The [Koe no Katachi theme analysis at Anime Kaishaku](https://animekaishaku.wordpress.com/2020/11/15/koe-no-katachi/) frames the manga's thesis as being ultimately about the possibility of change — but the operative word is *possibility*. Shoya demonstrates it. Ueno demonstrates its limits. Both are necessary for the thesis to be true rather than merely wished.

Without Ueno, *Koe no Katachi* is a story about a bully who became a good person through guilt, which is a redemption narrative and a comfortable one. With Ueno, it is a story about a peer group that participated in systematic cruelty, most of whom moved on without transformation, one of whom did not. That is the story that the film could not hold. That is the story the manga tells.
