In January 2023, [Call of the Night won the 68th Shogakukan Manga Award in the shōnen category](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-01-18/call-of-the-night-blue-orchestra-medalist-ui-kon-manga-win-68th-shogakukan-manga-awards/.193945), alongside *Ao no Orchestra*. It's published in [Weekly Shōnen Sunday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night), the same magazine that runs *Detective Conan* and *Frieren*. By every industry metric, Kotoyama's vampire manga is a shōnen property.

And yet, both seasons of the anime adaptation aired on [Noitamina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noitamina) — Fuji TV's late-night programming block, the one that launched in 2005 specifically to attract women in their twenties and thirties who had abandoned anime because anime had nothing for them. The block that built its reputation on *Honey and Clover*, *Paradise Kiss*, *Nodame Cantabile*, and *Princess Jellyfish*. The block whose founding producer, Yoko Matsuzaki, explicitly designed it to pull in viewers who wanted [storylines closer to Japanese live-action dramas](https://www.uk-anime.net/articles/noitaminA_-_Turning_animation_on_its_head.html) than to anything in a shōnen magazine.

These two facts — shōnen award winner, Noitamina slot — aren't contradictory. But sitting next to each other, they reveal something true about what *Call of the Night* actually is. The Shogakukan award tells you where it was published. Noitamina tells you what the series is made of.

## What Noitamina Is, and Why It Matters

Noitamina is "animation" spelled backwards, which was Fuji TV's way of announcing that the block intended to invert what anime meant. [The mandate was explicit from the start](https://www.cbr.com/noitamina-anime-block-history/): expand anime beyond its conventional young male audience by producing shows with the emotional architecture of adult drama. Not more violence, not more power scaling — more interiority. More ambiguity. More willingness to let a story sit still and feel something without resolving it.

The early Noitamina slate — *Honey and Clover* (2005), *Paradise Kiss* (2005), *Nodame Cantabile* (2007) — was almost exclusively josei: manga written for adult women. These series achieved [solid ratings of around 3%](https://www.uk-anime.net/articles/noitaminA_-_Turning_animation_on_its_head.html) and attracted international licensing interest at a time when late-night anime wasn't supposed to do that. The formula worked because Noitamina had correctly identified that the audience it wanted — adults who'd grown up with anime and felt the medium had left them behind — would watch something that felt like it was made for people rather than demographics.

Over time, the block expanded its definition without abandoning its identity. *AnoHana* (2011) came from the shōnen tradition but belonged on Noitamina because it was 11 episodes of grief and regret rather than battle arcs. *Psycho-Pass* (2012) was hard sci-fi, but the block's sensibility kept it focused on moral corrosion over spectacle. The block's ethos wasn't "josei adaptations only" — it was [conceptual distinctiveness, thematic depth, and a relationship with the audience that assumed they were adults](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Creator/Noitamina).

When *Call of the Night* landed in the summer 2022 slot, it was following shows like *The Promised Neverland* and *Carole & Tuesday* — not *Dragon Ball* and *My Hero Academia*. The company it keeps is telling.

## The Shōnen Label and What It Actually Measures

Calling *Call of the Night* shōnen isn't wrong. But what does that label actually tell you about the series?

[Shōnen manga is a publishing demographic, not a content category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen_manga). The label means the magazine targets adolescent boys. It doesn't mean the work will feature combat, power systems, rivalries, or any of the genre conventions that have become synonymous with "shōnen" in the discourse. Weekly Shōnen Sunday in particular skews older than its competitors — [its readership has a notably high ratio of readers over 23](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2019-04-22/shueisha-reveals-new-circulation-numbers-demographics-for-its-manga-magazines/.145991) — and has always been home to quieter, more atmospheric work than the Jump ecosystem.

*Frieren* is a Weekly Shōnen Sunday title. So is *Detective Conan*. The label is a publishing address, not a creative description.

*Call of the Night* does nothing that the shōnen label predicts. There's no rival to overcome. There's no power fantasy. Ko Yamori's goal — becoming a vampire — isn't about strength or freedom in any conventional shōnen sense; it's about finding an exit from a life that felt like wearing a costume. The story's conflict isn't action-driven; it's conditional: [transformation requires the vampire to genuinely fall in love with the human](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night/episode-13/.190400), a premise that forecloses the entire shōnen toolkit because you cannot fight your way into someone loving you.

The [Shogakukan award committee recognized its quality](https://otakuusamagazine.com/call-of-the-night-medalist-take-home-shogakukan-manga-awards/). Noitamina recognized its nature. These are different recognitions.

## What Makes Call of the Night Noitamina Material

Compare the series' actual DNA to the Noitamina checklist.

Emotional interiority over external conflict. *Call of the Night* is principally interested in what Ko and Nazuna think and feel at 2 a.m., not in what happens to them. The early chapters — which the anime reproduces with unusual fidelity — are long stretches of the two of them wandering, talking, not quite saying what they mean. This is the rhythm of *Honey and Clover*, not *My Hero Academia*.

Atmosphere as argument. The series uses the night not as backdrop but as thesis. The neon-saturated visuals, the club-influenced score by [Yoshiaki Dewa](https://doublesama.com/call-of-the-night/), the specific texture of urban Japan at 3 a.m. — these aren't decoration. They're the work's argument that this space is more alive, more honest, more real than the daytime world Ko fled. The aesthetic is doing analytical work — which is exactly what Noitamina shows do when they're operating at full capacity.

Tonal ambiguity without resolution. [Director Tomoyuki Itamura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomoyuki_Itamura), who shaped the series' visual language, came from the Monogatari franchise at Shaft — another series that uses stylization to externalize internal states rather than to signal genre. His approach to *Call of the Night* carries the same sensibility: color as psychology, composition as emotional argument. [ANN reviewers immediately compared the adaptation to Monogatari](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/manga.php?id=24350) not because they share a genre but because they share a method.

Romance built on genuine emotional complexity. The love condition — the transformation rule — functions less as a plot mechanic and more as a Noitamina-style device that keeps the story in a productive state of irresolution. *Honey and Clover* ran for years on romantic feelings that couldn't be simply resolved; *Call of the Night* engineered a structural reason why its central relationship couldn't simply arrive anywhere. This isn't shōnen's "will-they-won't-they" delayed by obstacles. It's the Noitamina model of using irresolution as a way to examine what desire actually is.

## The Audience It Actually Found

The [7th Crunchyroll Anime Awards nominations for the first season](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2023-01-19/crunchyroll-announces-nominees-for-7th-annual-anime-awards/.193969) — Best New Series, Best Romance, Best Ending Sequence — tell you something about who actually watched the show. These weren't votes from an audience following a shōnen power-fantasy through its arc. They were votes from viewers who wanted atmosphere, emotional honesty, and the sense that the people making it were more interested in what the characters were feeling than in what would happen next.

The [Star Crossed Anime review](https://starcrossedanime.com/yofukashi-no-uta-anime-review-68-100/) — even in its skepticism about the series' tonal splits — was applying standards the shōnen label doesn't require. Criticism that a shōnen series "fails to fully commit" to either romance or thriller is implicitly demanding Noitamina-level coherence. You don't apply that standard to a series unless it invited it first. *Call of the Night* invited it. That's the point.

## The Deeper Question the Placement Raises

What the *Call of the Night*-Noitamina pairing actually demonstrates is that demographic labels in anime and manga have always been publishing logistics, not creative descriptions — and that as the industry evolves, the gap between the two is widening.

Shōnen is where *Call of the Night* was published. Noitamina is what it aimed at. The fact that both can be true simultaneously says something about how the shōnen category has stretched: a label designed to describe magazines targeting boys now houses work that is, by any aesthetic measure, in conversation with adult dramatic anime. This isn't a new observation — [Shueisha's own 2019 demographic data](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2019-04-22/shueisha-reveals-new-circulation-numbers-demographics-for-its-manga-magazines/.145991) showed that readers 19 and older account for over half of Weekly Shōnen Jump's readership, with the 25+ bracket alone forming the single largest demographic group — but *Call of the Night* makes the category-reality gap unusually visible because the Noitamina placement is so specific. It's not just "adults read it too." It's "Fuji TV's programmer for adults explicitly selected it."

Noitamina's job was never to adapt josei manga. Its job was to [find stories that assumed the audience was grown up](https://www.cbr.com/best-anime-for-fans-of-noitamina-josei-dark-hidden-gems/). *AnoHana* was shōnen. *Psycho-Pass* defies easy classification. *Call of the Night* ran in Weekly Shōnen Sunday. The block's programmers looked past all of that and saw what the series was actually made of.

That instinct — identifying the work rather than the label — is what Noitamina has always done when it works best. And in *Call of the Night*, it found a series that was doing exactly what the block was designed to champion: expanding what anime is assumed to be able to do, for people who didn't expect to find themselves watching it at 2 a.m. feeling understood.

Which is, if you think about it, precisely the show's subject matter.
