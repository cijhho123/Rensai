The most common criticism of Shinji Ikari is that he is a bad protagonist — passive, self-pitying, frustrating to watch, constitutionally incapable of making a decision when one is needed. What this criticism misses is that a better version of Shinji already exists. [Yoshiyuki Sadamoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion_(manga)), who designed the characters for the original anime and then wrote and drew the manga adaptation for 18 years (1994-2013), gave us exactly the Shinji that anime critics claim they wanted: sarcastic, proactive, capable of initiative, willing to fight even when afraid. Manga Shinji rushes to Asuka's defense during the Mass Production Eva battle while anime Shinji freezes in despair. Manga Shinji snaps back when condescended to. Manga Shinji drives his own story forward.

The existence of this version is the most important piece of evidence about what [Hideaki Anno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hideaki_Anno) was doing. Anno could have written that Shinji. Sadamoto did write that Shinji, working from the same source material, the same character concepts, the same narrative framework. Anno chose not to. The anime's Shinji — the one who refuses to pilot, who sits in hallways, who cannot respond to grief with anything other than withdrawal — was not the result of poor writing or insufficient attention to conventional narrative satisfaction. He was the result of Anno wanting to say something that the more capable, more genre-compliant manga Shinji could not say.

## What the Manga Shinji Reveals

[Sadamoto himself has been candid](https://www.evamonkey.com/writings/animerica-interview-yoshiyuki-sadamoto.php) that the manga is his own interpretation rather than a faithful adaptation of the anime: he prefers "clear, straightforward stories" and took the manga in the direction of his own preferences even when that diverged significantly from the anime's approach. He acknowledged that the anime "never actually gives people an answer" — it offers a feeling and demands the reader "figure out the rest for yourself." Sadamoto wanted something more resolved.

The result is a manga that functions, as [CBR's comparative analysis notes](https://www.cbr.com/evangelion-manga-better-than-anime/), as "a traditional sci-fi epic" where earned character development leads to something resembling resolution and happiness. Shinji is "an active protagonist, taking matters into his own hands" rather than the anime's reactive, paralyzed version. The psychological collapse scenes — the extended interior monologue sequences, the therapy-session introspection — are stripped away or minimized. The manga gives Shinji [clarity of external action](https://screenrant.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-manga-explainer/) in place of the anime's excruciating interiority.

And here is what the manga proves: those interiority sequences in the anime were not filler or budget-saving measures. They were the point. The moments in the anime where Shinji sits with his cassette player and does not act, where the camera holds on his face through extended silence, where he asks himself the same unanswered questions in episode after episode — these are the episodes of the anime that critics call frustrating and that are also the episodes that most precisely capture what Anno was building. The manga removes them because Sadamoto did not want to make that piece. Anno made only that piece.

## The Critique Encoded in the Character

In the [June 1996 Newtype interview](https://gwern.net/doc/anime/eva/1996-newtype-anno-interview), Anno stated directly that from episode 16 onwards, Evangelion moved into exploring what the human mind is about — that he was writing about himself. A friend had lent him a book on psychological illness that gave him "a shock, as if he finally found what he needed to say." [Anno has been explicit in multiple sources](https://the-artifice.com/hideaki-anno-neon-genesis-evangelion/) that the parts of EVA he dislikes are the parts that reflect his own otaku self. This is a significant admission. Anno made a series that was going to be consumed primarily by the same audience Shinji represents: the withdrawn, the avoidant, the people who use fiction as a retreat from the demands of connection and growth. Hiroki Azuma's foundational [critical work on otaku culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otaku:_Japan%27s_Database_Animals) documented how 1990s otaku consumption had become structured around the extraction of narrative elements as database content rather than engagement with a work's full world — a pattern of consumption that Anno recognized in himself and in his audience. He made his protagonist a portrait of that pattern — not a flattering portrait, but an honest one.

The [hedgehog's dilemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedgehog%27s_Dilemma_(Neon_Genesis_Evangelion)) that episode four establishes is not just a description of Shinji's psychology; it is a description of the emotional posture of the specific audience Anno was addressing. The closer hedgehogs get, the more they injure each other with their spines. Shinji's refusal to close distances — with his father, with Asuka, with Rei, with Kaworu — is not a character flaw in the genre-convention sense of a protagonist who needs to learn courage. It is a psychological portrait of a person who has decided that the cost of connection exceeds its benefit, and who will build elaborate defensive structures to avoid the test of discovering whether that's true.

[Manga Shinji does not have this problem](https://www.cbr.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-differences-anime-manga/) in the same way. He is bolder, more sarcastic, more willing to engage. Which means he cannot be that specific portrait. A more capable Shinji cannot carry Anno's argument about what the audience actually was.

## The Ending as Thesis Statement

The divergence between the two versions reaches its most legible form in their endings. The anime's [episodes 25 and 26](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) abandon conventional narrative structure almost entirely, collapsing into psychological theater and abstract self-examination before resolving — controversially — in Shinji's interior acceptance rather than any external event. [The End of Evangelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion), which provides an alternate theatrical ending, is graphically disturbing and structurally ambiguous: civilization ends, Shinji chooses, the choice is not presented as either triumph or defeat. The darkness of that ending is inseparable from the argument: Anno wanted you to feel the cost of Shinji's psychology, not to receive a resolution from it.

The manga ends with Shinji proactively fighting, Asuka surviving, Rei achieving something like peace, and a future that feels possible. Sadamoto's Shinji earns his ending by doing what genre protagonists do. It is a satisfying, conventional close to a long story. It is also, compared to the anime, relatively unchallenging. It does not ask the reader to examine themselves; it asks them to feel for the characters. This is not a failure — it is a different project.

What the comparison of endings confirms is that the anime's refusal of conventional resolution was structural and intentional. Anno was not failing to provide satisfaction. He was declining to provide it because the satisfaction would have resolved what he needed to remain unresolved. [The production troubles](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/TroubledProduction/NeonGenesisEvangelion) that led to the TV ending — running out of time and budget, Anno's own psychological state deteriorating — coincided with an ending that represented the psychological endpoint Anno had been building toward. As [the Evageeks production archive of staff statements](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff) documents, supplemental materials indicate The End of Evangelion's Episode 26' showed what Anno's plans had always been moving toward — suggesting the constraints freed him to reach an endpoint more conventional production pressures might have softened.

## The Critic Who Wants Manga Shinji

There is a coherent position that the manga is better than the anime: more resolved, more character-driven in the conventional sense, more satisfying to read as a complete narrative. [That position has been argued](https://www.cbr.com/evangelion-manga-better-than-anime/) and is defensible. Sadamoto worked on it for 18 years and it is a serious piece of work.

But the position that anime Shinji is bad writing — that Anno simply failed to make a compelling protagonist and Sadamoto fixed it — is not defensible given what we know about the two works. [Anno's depression was material to the anime's construction](https://discover.hubpages.com/entertainment/Neon-Genesis-Evangelion-A-Psychological-Analysis); the inability to close distances, the failure of internal motivation, the radical interiority that produces no external movement — these were not limitations Anno was working around. They were the tools he was working with. The question "why doesn't Shinji just get in the robot" has the same answer as "why doesn't someone with clinical depression just feel better": the incapacity is not a choice within the story's logic, it is the logic's subject.

What Anno understood, and what the manga comparison makes precise, is that the mecha genre before Evangelion depended on protagonists who embodied wish-fulfillment: capable, decisive, ultimately triumphant. The genre had built its emotional machinery around the assumption that the audience wanted to identify with competence. Anno looked at his audience — the 1990s otaku using fiction as a substitute for engagement with the social world — and made a protagonist who told that audience the truth about the identification they had been making. [Shinji is what you actually are](https://searchofno9.wordpress.com/2019/10/14/we-are-all-shinji-ikari/), not what the genre said you were.

The manga gave you the version that fits the genre's promise. Anno gave you the version that did not fit it on purpose, because the fit was always a lie.

That Sadamoto could make the genre-satisfying Shinji so easily, working from the same material, is the most elegant possible demonstration of what Anno was refusing to do. The two Shinjis exist in permanent dialogue. The anime needs the manga to prove it was a choice. The manga needs the anime to prove that choices have consequences for what stories can say.
