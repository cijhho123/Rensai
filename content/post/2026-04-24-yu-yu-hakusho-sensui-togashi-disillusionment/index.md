Yoshihiro Togashi ended *Yu Yu Hakusho* because he was done with it. This is not a reading of the ending or an inference from the text — Togashi said it explicitly. His first emotion when the series concluded was ["relief and pleasure."](https://fandomwire.com/true-reason-yoshihiro-togashi-was-happy-with-yu-yu-hakushos-brutally-abrupt-ending-had-nothing-to-do-with-his-failing-health/) He had ["already exhausted everything I could do with the Yu Yu Hakusho cast,"](https://fandomwire.com/true-reason-yoshihiro-togashi-was-happy-with-yu-yu-hakushos-brutally-abrupt-ending-had-nothing-to-do-with-his-failing-health/) described the main characters as "more or less set in place — which makes them not much fun to draw." His solution, during the period before the ending was forced on him, was to ["put more heart into the bad guys."](https://fandomwire.com/true-reason-yoshihiro-togashi-was-happy-with-yu-yu-hakushos-brutally-abrupt-ending-had-nothing-to-do-with-his-failing-health/)

This is also the complete creative logic of the Chapter Black arc.

The Chapter Black Saga (episodes 67-94 of the anime) is the point where *Yu Yu Hakusho* stops being a shonen tournament series and becomes something closer to a meditation on whether the thing the hero has been protecting is worth protecting. It features Togashi's most psychologically complex antagonist: Shinobu Sensui, a former Spirit Detective — Yusuke's predecessor in the role — who watched humanity commit atrocities and concluded it was irredeemable. Sensui exists because Togashi had run out of creative investment in his heroes and redirected everything into the villain. The result is the best arc in the series and, arguably, the most psychologically sophisticated antagonist in 1990s shonen anime.

## What Togashi Had Built and What It Cost Him

*Yu Yu Hakusho* ran in *Weekly Shonen Jump* from December 1990 to July 1994. The production grind was, by Togashi's account, brutal: [half a day off per week through the Dark Tournament's run](https://www.looper.com/1084068/hunter-x-hunter-writer-yoshihiro-togashis-health-struggles-help-explain-the-mangas-long-hiatus/), chest pain, back pain severe enough that he worked lying down, a work rhythm that left him functioning but depleted. This context matters because the creative exhaustion Togashi described is not a separate fact from the series' quality — it is directly expressed in the series' structure.

The Spirit Detective and Dark Tournament arcs are excellent mainstream shonen. Yusuke Urameshi was a genuine innovation for the genre in 1990: a delinquent protagonist who was [snarky, cynical, and authentically himself rather than the optimistic Son Goku archetype](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-yusuke-great-deliquent-main-character/). The [Dark Tournament](https://gamerant.com/yu-yu-hakusho-best-tournament-arc-in-anime/) gave every significant character meaningful development through combat and raised the stakes of its format to a point that most tournament arcs still haven't matched. Studio Pierrot's production under [director Noriyuki Abe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noriyuki_Abe) was dense and detailed — [the Sakuga Blog's retrospective on the series](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/10/14/yu-yu-hakushos-exceptional-production/) notes that YYH attracted exceptional animation talent and maintained a consistency of quality across its run that Pierrot would rarely match again. The anime won the [Animage Anime Grand Prix Award in 1994](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=387).

But Togashi had said himself that by the time he was doing all of this, the main characters were "set in place." He knew who they were. He had established the chemistry of Team Urameshi — Yusuke's volatile energy, Kuwabara's earnest loyalty, Kurama's calculating elegance, Hiei's contained ferocity. This chemistry was the engine of everything through the Dark Tournament. It was also, Togashi admitted, no longer interesting to him as a creative problem.

He originally wanted to [end the series after Chapter Black](https://fandomwire.com/true-reason-yoshihiro-togashi-was-happy-with-yu-yu-hakushos-brutally-abrupt-ending-had-nothing-to-do-with-his-failing-health/) — a full year before the actual finale, which he was pressured by editors to continue. The Three Kings arc that followed, covering the demon world power struggle, is broadly considered the series' weakest section — [fan retrospectives consistently describe it as rushed and undercooked](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-dark-tournament-chapter-black-best-arc/), the product of a mangaka who had already said what he had to say and was now being obligated to continue. What that means structurally is that the Chapter Black arc represents the last point at which Togashi was genuinely invested in *Yu Yu Hakusho*. Everything he could still do with the material, he put there.

## Sensui as the Proxy

Shinobu Sensui is the character Togashi had room to design.

His premise reverses the series' foundational logic. Yusuke begins as a street tough who doesn't care about protecting anyone and gradually discovers he does. Sensui began as someone who cared deeply — a Spirit Detective with an almost puritanical hatred of demons, a belief that the division between human good and demon evil was absolute and worth enforcing — and watched that belief collapse when he witnessed the worst humans were capable of. [The "Chapter Black" tape, a supernatural recording of humanity's atrocities](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-dark-tournament-chapter-black-best-arc/), broke him: not by showing him something he couldn't handle, but by showing him that the humans he had been protecting were capable of everything he had been fighting demons for.

His response to this collapse is the psychological structure of the arc. [Sensui developed six additional personalities](https://villains.fandom.com/wiki/Shinobu_Sensui) as a way of compartmentalizing what he could no longer integrate into a single self — Minoru the orator and manipulator, Kazuya the gleeful killer, others representing fragments of the person the original Sensui could no longer be. This is not the usual shonen villain's "multiple forms" power progression. It is a portrait of psychological dissociation, of someone whose moral framework shattered so completely that the self that held it couldn't survive intact.

What makes this work is the specific nature of Sensui's question, which is also the arc's central question: not "are humans worth protecting" in the abstract, but *how do you answer that after you've seen the evidence?* The "Chapter Black" tape exists as a device to make Sensui's position not simply fanatical but rationally grounded. He saw what he saw. His conclusion — that humanity had forfeited its claim to protection — follows from that evidence. The arc does not present him as simply wrong. It gives him an argument.

## What the Heroes Cannot Answer

This is where Togashi's creative investment becomes visible in the text. When Yusuke eventually defeats Sensui, the series does not answer Sensui's question. It cannot. Yusuke's counter-argument is not philosophical — it is physical. He wins the fight. Sensui dies. But the terms of the debate Sensui established — the evidence of the tape, the reality of human cruelty, the question of whether the protection Yusuke provides is actually merited — those are not resolved. They are closed off by the plot without being answered by the narrative.

This is not a flaw. It is what the arc is: Togashi expressing, through the villain he was actually interested in writing, a question that his heroes — characters who were "set in place," who existed to fight and win — could not engage with at the level Sensui posed it. Yusuke's victory over Sensui is the resolution of the plot. It is not the resolution of the theme.

The [fan debate over Chapter Black versus Dark Tournament](https://www.cbr.com/yu-yu-hakusho-dark-tournament-chapter-black-best-arc/) as the series' best arc usually frames this as a question of taste — action-heavy character development versus philosophical darkness. But the more interesting frame is the one Togashi provided himself: the Dark Tournament is the peak of his investment in the main cast; Chapter Black is the peak of his investment in the villain he needed to keep writing. You can see both arcs clearly if you accept that they are the products of a creator at different points in his relationship to his own material.

## The Argument He Carried Forward

Togashi left *Yu Yu Hakusho* and went almost immediately to *Level E* — a 16-chapter comedy experiment about an alien prince and human bureaucracy, [described as "0% fighting and 100% humor"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Level_E) — before beginning *Hunter x Hunter* in 1998. He has described HxH as the ["perfected version" of Yu Yu Hakusho](https://screenrant.com/hunter-x-hunter-yuyu-hakusho-similarities-togashi/), which is a precise thing to say. The perfection he was aiming for is not difficult to identify in retrospect.

In HxH, Togashi's most sustained creative energy goes into his antagonists. The [Phantom Troupe](https://hunterxhunter.fandom.com/wiki/Phantom_Troupe) and Chrollo Lucilfer. The Chimera Ant King Meruem — whose arc is one of the most ambitious things in battle shonen, a genuinely humanist examination of what a being designed to consume humanity discovers when it encounters something that can't be consumed. These are Sensui's successors: characters with positions, not just power levels; characters whose questions the plot cannot dismiss by having Gon punch them hard enough.

The pattern is consistent. Togashi, freed from the constraint of a hero whose psychology was already established, builds his most interesting work in the spaces his antagonists occupy. This was already true in *Yu Yu Hakusho* — Togashi said so himself. The Chapter Black arc did not happen because he was inspired by the arc's themes in the abstract. It happened because he was done with Yusuke and still had things to say, and Sensui was the character he had room to say them through.

*Yu Yu Hakusho* earned [over 50 million manga copies sold in Japan by 2013](https://www.cbr.com/yyh-timeless-influential-anime-different-than-dbz/) and [directly inspired the mangaka of Naruto, Black Clover, and Jujutsu Kaisen](https://www.cbr.com/yyh-timeless-influential-anime-different-than-dbz/) among others. Its structural DNA — the team of four fighters with distinct specializations, the escalating tournament format, the rival-turned-ally trajectory — runs through a generation of shonen that followed. But the thing that makes it worth revisiting thirty years later is not the DNA it donated to Bleach or Naruto. It is Sensui. It is what Togashi made when he finally stopped trying to write a series he had run out of ideas for and started writing the character he actually had something left to say.

The series ended because Togashi was done. But the arc that marks the end of his creative investment in it is also the arc where he built the best thing in it. That is the particular, melancholy logic of Chapter Black.
