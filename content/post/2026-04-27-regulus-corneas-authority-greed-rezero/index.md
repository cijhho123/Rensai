The fan nickname for Regulus Corneas in Re:ZERO circles is "[the Sin Archbishop of Yapping](https://x.com/JanchoRZ/status/1863606584108089556)." It's a joke about the length of his speeches, and it lands because the speeches are genuinely, bewilderingly long — the kind of monologue where a character starts talking about their feelings and doesn't stop for three pages while everyone around them is waiting to die. But the nickname also misses what makes the speeches work as characterization, which is not their length but their specific content. Regulus doesn't yap. He testifies. Every speech is a detailed account of how he, personally, has been wronged by the present situation, how the people opposing him have failed their obligations, and how his violence against them is a reluctant, measured response to their provocation.

He frames every atrocity as self-defense. He frames it in the first person, using the word "I" more times per sentence than any other character in the series. He is not performing villainy. He genuinely believes what he's saying.

That is what makes him the most uncomfortable villain in Re:ZERO. Not his power level — which is, [per the author's own confirmation, the highest of any Archbishop](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Regulus_Corneas) — but the specific psychology the show chose to give him.

![Regulus Corneas furious at being challenged](https://static0.gamerantimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/rezero-mad-regulus-corneas.jpg)

## The Mechanics Are the Metaphor

Before getting into what Regulus says, it's worth understanding exactly how his power works, because Re:ZERO put real thought into designing the Authority of Greed to mirror its bearer's psychology.

Regulus's power has [two components called Lion's Heart and Little King](https://screenrant.com/re-zeros-witch-cult-sin-archbishops-strongest-villains-explained/). Lion's Heart lets him stop the flow of time affecting anything he considers his own — his body, his clothes, anything he touches. He becomes physically invincible; nothing can damage him because nothing can physically affect him. The weakness is that this strains his own heart, which means he can only use it for brief intervals before the cardiac stress would kill him.

Little King resolves this. By distributing his heart among the women he has married — [currently 53 wives](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Regulus_Corneas), kept in secure locations throughout Pristella — Regulus transfers the cardiac burden of his invincibility away from himself. His wives hold his heart. This lets him maintain Lion's Heart indefinitely, because the cost is being paid by people he has imprisoned.

What this means structurally: Regulus is the most powerful fighter in Re:ZERO's world, but his power is entirely contingent on maintaining control over his captives. His invincibility is not his. It is borrowed, extracted from 53 women who [hate him and want to return to their regular lives](https://gamerant.com/rezero-season-3-episode-12-review/) but cannot escape because he has killed their families, erased their worlds, and made the cost of flight higher than the cost of staying. His strength is built on their continued imprisonment, and their continued imprisonment is maintained by the threat of his strength.

This is not an allegory. It is a diagram.

## The Rhetoric of the Small King

Regulus's name comes from the star Regulus in the Leo constellation — the [brightest star in Leo, the "Little King"](https://boomzappow.com/all-the-sin-archbishops-of-the-witch-cult-in-rezero/). The "little" part is doing work. Regulus does not describe himself as powerful or dangerous. He describes himself as humble, fragile, and small. His speeches, [which have been documented and translated extensively by the light novel community](https://witchculttranslation.com/2019/03/19/arc-5-chapter-59-regulus-corneas/), follow a recognizable pattern: he identifies a minor social friction (someone did not greet him correctly, someone challenged his right to do something, someone failed to show sufficient deference) and then escalates this into a grand philosophical case for why his response — which is invariably murderous — is justified.

The "rights" framing is essential. [Regulus speaks constantly about rights](https://otakumode.com/otapedia/anime/re_zero/rezero_regulus_corneas) — not grand rights, not political rights, but the right to not be made uncomfortable, the right to not be looked down upon, the right to have his feelings acknowledged. He calls himself a "pacifist" and a "fulfilled, desireless individual." He preaches that excessive desire is ruinous — this from a man with 53 imprisoned wives. He says "I am the victim here" in variations across essentially every encounter he has. He tells people that he is exceptionally considerate and polite, then uses this self-assessment to indict them for not reciprocating in kind. He frames even the act of fighting back against him as a kind of abuse.

The speeches are so long because they have to cover the ground between "I was mildly inconvenienced" and "therefore mass murder is warranted." That's a lot of logical steps to fabricate. [As the Witch Cult Translations rendering of his Arc 5 chapter shows](https://witchculttranslation.com/2019/03/19/arc-5-chapter-59-regulus-corneas/), the text commits to this rhetoric at length — Nagatsuki is not cutting it short to spare the reader. The discomfort of reading it is the point.

This maps onto a recognizable real-world pattern. [Game Rant's analysis of Regulus](https://gamerant.com/rezero-demonstrates-the-reality-of-domestic-violence/) connects his behavior explicitly to domestic abuse — the control, the isolation, the dehumanization (he refers to his wives by number, not by name), the way his victims' will to escape is broken not through direct force but by destroying every place they had to return to. [The Otaku Mode character profile frames it as pathological narcissism combined with parasitic dependency](https://otakumode.com/otapedia/anime/re_zero/rezero_regulus_corneas) — someone who cannot tolerate vulnerability, so he converts other people into extensions of himself. These are specific features of a specific pattern, and Re:ZERO reproduces them with unusual fidelity for a fantasy story.

## Why He Is Not Petelgeuse

The comparison to Petelgeuse (Betelgeuse) Romanée-Conti is instructive. Petelgeuse is the Archbishop of Sloth, and he is the one most audiences encounter first: a cackling, self-flagellating zealot who shrieks about "diligence" while ordering atrocities, rips out his own hair, and monologues with operatic intensity. He is a theatrical villain — grotesque, unsettling, memorable — but theatrical. The awfulness is clearly performed. You can watch him and recognize him as a category: the religious fanatic, the ecstatic sadist, the villain who enjoys himself too much.

Regulus doesn't enjoy himself. [He suffers](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Regulus_Corneas). Every encounter is presented from his perspective as an ordeal he did not deserve. The entire time he is crushing Emilia's wrist or killing her loved ones in Pristella, he is simultaneously the wronged party. There is no gap between his self-image and his behavior — which is what makes him harder to process as a villain than Petelgeuse's theatrical horror. Theatrical villains are legible. Regulus is uncomfortably mundane.

This is also why [his debut episode in Season 3 holds a 9.4 rating on IMDB](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt34089046/) — the creative direction of the confrontation leans into the unsettling flatness of his self-presentation. He does not announce himself as a monster. He arrives as someone who has been inconvenienced and intends to say something about it.

## The 53 Punches

The defeat in Season 3 Episode 12 is designed as carefully as the power itself.

Emilia goes to the church where the wives are gathered and does something no one else tried: she asks them what they want. [She talks to the main wife, Sylphy, and through her to the other 52](https://gamerant.com/rezero-season-3-episode-12-review/). The wives admit that they hate Regulus and want their lives back. Emilia offers them a path: she will briefly freeze their hearts — temporarily stop them — long enough for Subaru to destroy Regulus's distributed heart while it lacks a refuge. It requires the wives to consent to something that will, for a moment, be indistinguishable from dying. They agree.

The mechanics are exact. His invincibility requires the continued living imprisonment of the people who hold his heart. The defeat requires those people to choose, freely and with full knowledge of the risk, to withdraw from that imprisonment. The system that sustained his power is dismantled by the consent of the people it was exploiting.

![Regulus brought low — the Little King without his Kingdom](https://static0.gamerantimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/rezero-weakened-regulus.jpg)

What follows is Emilia hitting him fifty-three times. Once for each wife. It is explicitly retributive, formalized, counted — [one of the more deliberate pieces of thematic staging in Season 3](https://www.cbr.com/rezero-season3-episode12-review/). The person who has spent the series being told she looks like a witch, being kept from her own memories, being defined by what she meant to others before she was allowed to be herself — that person gets to conduct the exact arithmetic of his accountability. Fifty-three specific people. Fifty-three punches.

His final speech, as he dies, continues in exactly the same register. [He asks why he is being persecuted like this](https://gamerant.com/rezero-season-3-episode-12-review/). He wonders what he did to deserve such treatment. He has lost, catastrophically, and the experience is being processed through the same lens as everything else: the world has once again failed to recognize his rights.

He drowns in a hole in the earth, still talking.

## What Re:ZERO Is Doing

Regulus is the strongest Archbishop because Re:ZERO needed the villain whose power is the most structurally dependent on other people's suffering to also be the hardest to defeat through conventional means. You can't just hit him hard enough. You have to dismantle the system that makes him invincible, and that system is maintained by the ongoing destruction of fifty-three other human beings.

The series is not subtle about the parallel. [The Game Rant piece goes so far as to apply the same lens to Subaru's behavior in earlier arcs](https://gamerant.com/rezero-demonstrates-the-reality-of-domestic-violence/) — his love-bombing of Emilia, his inability to respect her autonomy in Season 1 — suggesting that Re:ZERO is examining a spectrum, not just a monster. Regulus is the extreme of a pattern the series traces with more ambivalence elsewhere.

That reading is arguably a step too far. Subaru's Arc 4 breakdown — where he finally accepts help, stops needing to be the lone savior, and earns Beatrice's choice rather than demanding it — is precisely the arc Regulus cannot and will not take. Regulus does not grow. His self-perception is sealed. [Subaru is specifically written as someone who can be wrong about what love requires and learn](https://animetiger.com/subaru-natsuki-rezero-character-analysis/); Regulus is written as someone who has chosen to be incapable of that. The comparison identifies something real, but conflating the two requires ignoring the series' central structural argument that one of these orientations can change. But the comparison does identify something real about how the story uses its villains. The Archbishops aren't random monstrosities. They are specific failures. [Petelgeuse is manic fanaticism](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/ReZeroWitchCult). Gluttony is nihilistic consumption. [Regulus is the worst version of the entitlement that ordinary possessive love can become](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Regulus_Corneas) — the person who cannot tell the difference between needing someone and owning them, and whose inability to make that distinction has been given enough power to act without consequences.

Until the people he needed decided, together, to stop.
