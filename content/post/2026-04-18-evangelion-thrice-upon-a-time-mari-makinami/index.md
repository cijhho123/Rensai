Evangelion fandom runs on interpretation. It always has. The franchise was practically engineered for it — every character is a wound walking around in a plugsuit, every scene has a psychoanalytic subtext, every title card is a thesis statement. Fans have spent thirty years mapping Asuka's narcissistic defenses, parsing Rei's philosophical void, charting Kaworu's Christ-geometry. The characters are designed to reward obsessive reading.

Which makes Mari Makinami Illustrious such a problem.

She shows up in the Rebuild films cracking jokes, mashing buttons in her Entry Plug, wearing a wide grin that never quite resolves into an interior. She has no visible wound. No parent she's trying to escape or protect or become. She doesn't have the kind of past you can write an EvaGeeks wiki entry about. Fans have spent fifteen years trying to fix this — constructing elaborate theories about her connection to Gendo, her possible immortality, her real relationship to Yui — because the alternative, that she just is what she appears to be, doesn't compute in the grammar of Evangelion.

But that alternative is the correct one. And [*Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_3.0+1.0_Thrice_Upon_a_Time) (2021) builds its entire emotional architecture on it.

![Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time key visual](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/3e/Evangelion_3.0%2B1.0_poster.jpg)

## Anno Couldn't Write Her Either

Here is the uncomfortable production fact at the center of all of this: Anno himself couldn't figure out Mari. [According to the documented production history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mari_Illustrious_Makinami), in the very first drafts of the Rebuild films she was to do little more than appear in the pre-title opening sequence and observe the Tenth Angel's destructive power — then gone. He kept giving generic directions to his colleagues at Khara: the new character loves animals. Assistant director Kazuya Tsurumaki noted that Mari seemed to have "multiple personalities" and was a different character in each scene. Anno feared he was forcing her into the story artificially, to prove Rebuild wasn't just the TV series with a bigger budget.

What looks like a character that slipped through the cracks of the development process is actually, in retrospect, Anno working against his own habits. Every other significant Eva character comes pre-loaded with a psychological profile Anno could draw from his own depression, his own relationship with his father, his own complicated feelings about women and dependency. Mari came from somewhere else — and so she had to be built differently, from the outside in rather than the inside out.

## The Machinery of the Eva Harem

About two-thirds through Thrice Upon a Time, the film drops what amounts to a confession. It is revealed that Rei, Asuka, and Mari — all three Eva pilots who orbit Shinji — were genetically engineered and psychologically "tuned" to be compatible with him. The in-universe explanation is Nerv experimentation. The actual meaning is something else entirely.

This is Anno breaking the fourth wall without breaking it. He is not talking about the Rebuild continuity's science. He is talking about the last twenty-five years of anime romance mechanics — the manufactured compatibility, the harem that orbits a passive protagonist because the story requires it. Three women carefully constructed around one boy's damage. Every trauma arc, every tsundere mask-over-wound, every fragile girl made whole by being chosen — he built that architecture. The "tuned" revelation is his acknowledgment that he did.

And then Mari walks in — the character who was never tuned for anyone, who Anno couldn't diagram, whose backstory he couldn't access because she didn't have one waiting to be excavated.

## What Village-3 Is Actually Doing

The film's long first act in Village-3 baffles viewers expecting the kinetic pace of the Paris sequence that opens it. Shinji sits in a ruined train car, catatonic. Rei works rice paddies. Misato's crew argues logistics. The village has cats and elderly farmers and routines that have nothing to do with Human Instrumentality or Second Impact or any of the weight the franchise has been accruing since 1995.

This is not filler. This is the film teaching you how to read what comes next.

Village-3 — [modeled on the real Oyama Senmaida terraced rice fields](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Village-3) — insists on the texture of life that isn't about Shinji Ikari. The paddy workers don't care about AT Fields. The elderly woman who hands Rei a rice ball just wants to see her eat something. The world keeps going whether or not Third Impact is on the horizon. For an audience that has spent decades consuming a franchise whose entire cosmology rotates around one traumatized teenager's willingness to get in the robot, this is a genuinely radical proposition.

Shinji recovers here — slowly, resentfully, and then actually. Not because someone with the right trauma-key unlocks his wound. Because the world keeps requiring small acts of participation from him, and he keeps performing them, and eventually participation becomes a habit.

## The Character Who Won't Be Figured Out

Mari's role in this structure is precise. She is the character who cannot be decoded into loving Shinji, because she was never encoded in the first place. Asuka's attachment runs through her aggression, her abandonment complex, her desperate need to be acknowledged as exceptional — all traceable, all analyzable, all ultimately explicable as symptoms of specific wounds. Rei's devotion is inseparable from her constructed purpose. Kaworu's love is messianic by design.

Mari just... likes him. Finds him interesting. Keeps showing up. Anno gives this almost no backstory and no explanation — and after twenty-five years of a franchise that explained everything through trauma, this is the strangest possible choice he could have made.

It is also the most honest. Because the things that actually pull people forward — the relationships that break the cycle rather than perpetuating it — don't usually arrive trailing psychological documentation. They arrive sideways, from outside the established system of wounds and compensations. They're recognizable in retrospect, after the fact, not in the moment of first contact.

## Running Out of the Station

[The final scene of Thrice Upon a Time](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Ube-Shinkawa_Station) was shot at Ube-Shinkawa Station in Ube, Yamaguchi Prefecture — Hideaki Anno's actual hometown. The animators didn't invent a stylized version of a Japanese train station. They went to the real one, digitally embellished it, and placed their animated characters inside it. Adult Shinji sits with a briefcase. Kaworu and Rei and Asuka mill about on the opposite platform in ordinary clothes, released from their roles. Then Mari grabs Shinji's hand and they run.

Out of the station. Into the real world. Into footage that is, unmistakably, not animation.

The ending credit sequence plays over Utada Hikaru's "One Last Kiss," which contains the line: *"I was the king of a world built on comic books and daydreams."* The past tense is the whole argument. Anno is not condemning his audience for loving Evangelion. He spent thirty years inside it too. He is saying — with the specific ache of someone who built the cage and then had to dismantle it — that the world outside is still there, still running, still worth running toward.

When asked why he chose a train station for this final scene, [Anno said it was a "secret."](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Ube-Shinkawa_Station) He hasn't elaborated further. That refusal is characteristically precise — a director who has explained everything else in Evangelion with exhausting thoroughness, declining to explain this. What he will say is that he chose his own hometown. That he placed Shinji in Ube. That the film which closes a franchise built on interiority ends in a real place no one can fully own.

![The animated Ube-Shinkawa Station sequence from Evangelion 3.0+1.0](https://i0.wp.com/www.wayfarerdaves.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/1-210824-107-Eva-1024x436.jpg)

The film earned [¥10.28 billion at the Japanese box office](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2021/6/21/evangelion-3010-becomes-the-10th-highest-grossing-anime-film-in-japan-with-933-billion-yen-at-jp-box-office), making it the highest-grossing entry in the franchise's history. Twenty-five years after Shinji first refused to get in the robot, Japan showed up to watch him finally walk away from it — and the audience sat with that ending and felt, against all expectation, satisfied.

The question Anno leaves hanging is whether satisfaction is the right response. Thrice Upon a Time earns it, then immediately asks you to examine why you wanted it. The thing that saves Shinji is not diagnosable. The relationship that breaks the cycle cannot be charted. Mari runs with him into a real world the franchise was never able to contain, and the film ends before we can follow them. Some things, Anno insists, aren't meant to be decoded. He spent three decades teaching his audience how to read him — and then, at the end, wrote something he refuses to explain.
