There's a scene early in *The Apothecary Diaries* where Jinshi deploys his face — the slight tilt of the head, the engineered smile — and the court ladies around him stumble like they've been hit. It's played partly for comedy, partly as establishing information: this is what Jinshi does, this is what he is, this is the weapon. Then Maomao walks past, notices his expression with the clinical detachment of someone examining a botanical specimen, and keeps walking.

The scene is about a dozen things at once. But the thing it is most precisely about is this: [Jinshi](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/Jinshi) has been so completely transformed into an instrument that he doesn't know what he is when the instrument isn't working. And Maomao is the first person in his life who hasn't used him the way the instrument is designed to be used.

## The Weapon He Became

[According to the Apothecary Diaries wiki](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/Jinshi), Jinshi is described as having "the looks of a celestial maiden" — a beauty so thoroughgoing that a direct look at his face causes most people to stumble. The series deploys this early and often, not as pure wish fulfillment but as political information. Beauty in the imperial court is not neutral. It's leverage. Jinshi knows this and uses it explicitly: he manipulates information networks, manages spies, sways decisions, all while appearing to be nothing more than an exceptionally handsome eunuch who is loyal to the emperor.

He is, in other words, a person who has been turned into a tool of power so completely that he has made the tool part of his identity. The public face — angelic, composed, vaguely unthreatening — is not just performance. It is how he understands himself. [The character wiki notes](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/Jinshi) that while he appears serene and collected, he possesses "a severe lack of self-confidence," believing he pales against the Emperor's shadow. The beauty is not a gift he's comfortable with. It's a compensation. He has made a weapon out of the one thing he has that's undeniable, because he doesn't trust that anything else about him is sufficient.

This is the psychological foundation the series builds him on. And it's what makes Maomao's indifference so destabilizing — not just for the romance, but for his sense of self.

## The False Eunuch and the Real Problem

The details of [Jinshi's true identity](https://www.thegamer.com/the-apothecary-diaries-jinshis-true-identity-explained/) are worth unpacking because they're not just a plot twist. He is Ka Zuigetsu, the Emperor's son, who chose to become a eunuch rather than the Crown Prince after winning a game of Go against his father. He uses suppressants to maintain the appearance. The eunuch identity is not something imposed on him — it's something he selected, a deliberate self-diminishment.

What this means is that Jinshi has not just accepted a role he was assigned. He has *chosen* a role designed to make him less dangerous, less threatening, less legible as a person with political ambitions or a future. He has opted into a version of himself that is easier to deploy and harder to fear. The beauty remains — it cannot be suppressed — but everything else is narrowed. He is an instrument, and he chose to be one.

The tragedy the series sets up is that this choice was rational. In the imperial court, where being the heir makes you a target, being a beautiful and useful eunuch is survival. Jinshi is not passive or naive about what he has done. He knows. [His voice actor, Takeo Otsuka, has described](https://screenrant.com/the-apothecary-diaries-jinshi-secret-identity-maomao-confession/) Jinshi's hesitation to reveal his true identity to Maomao as rooted in fear — "he was afraid of ruining their current relationship." But what is "their current relationship" if not the one context in his life where his identity doesn't determine everything?

## Why Maomao Specifically

[The Apothecary Diaries](https://myanimelist.net/anime/53446/Kusuriya_no_Hitorigoto) structures its central relationship around a specific asymmetry: Jinshi is used to everyone wanting something from his face, and Maomao is constitutionally indifferent to it. She is not playing hard to get. She is genuinely uninterested in what he is performing, which means she is — accidentally, entirely without intending to — the first person who is looking for whatever is underneath the performance.

As the [Game Rant analysis of their dynamic notes](https://gamerant.com/the-apothecary-diaries-why-is-jinshi-so-fascinated-with-maomao/), Jinshi finds that "Maomao is the only woman he's ever met that doesn't just see his beauty but the man underneath." This is accurate but understates what's actually happening. It's not that she sees through the performance. It's that she's looking somewhere else entirely — and a specific, recurring pattern in the early episodes makes this concrete: Jinshi performs his smile, Maomao's eyes drop to whatever is in her hands. A medicine. A plant specimen. A symptom. He doesn't exist in her field of focus. The performance lands on empty air.

![Maomao and palace workers in a January 2025 promotional image](https://comicbook.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/01/Apothecary-Diaries-January-2025-promotional-image.jpg?w=1024)

For a person who has oriented his entire existence around controlling how he is perceived, this is novel to the point of being alarming. [CBR's analysis of the relationship](https://www.cbr.com/apothecary-diaries-maomao-jinshi-romance/) identifies the dynamic as more realistic than typical shojo couples precisely because the obstacles between them aren't misunderstandings or personality conflicts — they're structural. Maomao is aware that Jinshi has a secret identity, is aware of the political consequences of becoming personally involved with an imperial prince, and has made a rational calculation that this is a bad idea. She is not in denial. She is correctly afraid of what it would cost. [A Tumblr analysis of Maomao's relationship dynamics](https://www.tumblr.com/alexiethymia/732852925324460032/maomaos-way-of-affection) describes how her way of showing care is almost always practical rather than sentimental, which makes her hard to read as a romantic subject — but also means that when she does extend warmth, it is unambiguously genuine rather than performed.

And Jinshi cannot make her see it differently using the tool he always uses. So he has to find another way to be interesting. Which means he has to find another way to exist.

## The Dual Self and the Public/Private Split

The series is careful to give us both versions of Jinshi. [The character has "almost two selves"](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/Jinshi): the composed celestial eunuch he presents to the court, and the "immature, needy, emotional teenager with low self-esteem" that only select people see. [Maomao notices the split early](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/Jinshi/Relationships) — she observes that he has a "double-sided personality," composed in public and childish in private, registering this with the same clinical neutrality she applies to any other data point.

What's significant is that Jinshi does not hide the private self from Maomao. Around her, the performance keeps slipping. He sulks when she doesn't react the way he expects. He becomes petulant, demanding in small ways that feel almost adolescent. He pushes in ways that reveal genuine uncertainty underneath the composure. This is not strategic — he is not calculating his vulnerability as a tactic. It's that the performance requires an audience that plays its role, and Maomao refuses to play. When the script breaks down, he improvises, and the improvisation is just: *him, unmediated*. This is the only relationship in Jinshi's life where that happens. He cannot control what she sees, which means she is the only one who might be seeing something real.

[Creator Natsu Hyuuga has spoken about](https://screenrant.com/the-apothecary-diaries-anime-romance-threatened-jinshi-maomao/) the pressure she faces from fans and even family members demanding more romance between the pair. She describes feeling "almost threatened while writing" the romance elements, given that her primary interest is the mystery and court politics. This is worth taking seriously as information about what the series is actually doing. The romance between Jinshi and Maomao is not the series' subject. It is a side effect of two people who are both performing different aspects of themselves constantly, and who have found, in their strange dynamic, a context where the performance is slightly less necessary.

## What Season 2 Does with This

[The Apothecary Diaries Season 2's reception](https://gamerant.com/the-apothecary-diaries-2025-highest-rated-anime-mal/) — highest-rated TV anime of 2025 on MyAnimeList with an 8.91, compared to Season 1's already impressive 8.86 — is partly attributable to how much further it goes with both characters. Season 2 reveals Jinshi's identity more explicitly, showing him in the armor and political context that his eunuch performance was designed to obscure. It makes concrete what the first season kept abstract: the thing Jinshi is hiding is not just a secret. It's a whole political future he has been trying to escape.

[AniTrendz's Season 2 review](https://anitrendz.net/news/2025/07/13/the-apothecary-diaries-season-2-review/) credits the season's success to "consistency, character-driven storytelling, and an audience that kept coming back week after week." Reviewers specifically noted that the season "deepened its mysteries and relationships" rather than escalating toward spectacle, and that [Jinshi's traumatic backstory](https://www.deathsdoorprods.com/archives/116422) — including flashbacks to a childhood in which "everything he showed love for was taken away" — added another layer to his desperate need for a relationship he cannot lose. What this means in practice is that the series trusts the audience to care about who Jinshi and Maomao are, not just what they do. The relationship between a man who has made himself into a weapon and a woman who refuses to be a target has accumulated enough texture that Season 2 can afford to sit in the spaces between plot beats.

## The Cruelest Joke

The series' central irony is that Jinshi built the most effective kind of power — the kind that doesn't look like power — and then found himself in a situation where it is completely inert. His beauty and charm, deployed against a court of hundreds, work on everyone. Deployed against one apothecary who has decided she would rather examine the makeup poison than look at his face, they produce nothing. The weapon that has defined his identity and secured his survival is gorgeous and useless.

[Natsu Hyuuga's insistence](https://screenrant.com/the-apothecary-diaries-anime-romance-threatened-jinshi-maomao/) that the romance develops slowly, according to the characters' logic rather than audience demand, is itself a kind of argument about what the series is interested in. A Jinshi who gets what he wants quickly would not be a character study. He would be a fantasy. A Jinshi who has to figure out how to exist without the weapon — who has to discover what he is when the charm doesn't work, what he has to offer that isn't a performance — is something more unusual in manga and anime: a male lead whose arc is about learning to be a person rather than an instrument.

That arc is not finished yet. [Season 3 has been announced](https://animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-04-26/apothecary-diaries-3rd-season-tv-anime-announced-for-spring-2026-movie-for-december-2026-premiere/.224264), and an original anime film is coming in December 2026. The series has more to say about what Jinshi does when his secret is fully out — when the eunuch performance is no longer necessary and he has to construct some other way of being present.

Whatever that turns out to be will not look like the thing that worked in the throne room. It will have to be something he doesn't already know how to do.
