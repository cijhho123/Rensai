Bakemonogatari's distinctive visual grammar — the flash-cut text cards, the Dutch angles, the impossible architecture, the head tilts, the abstract color fields that replace backgrounds — is usually described as SHAFT's style. This is true but unhelpful. It tells you *who*, not *why*, and "why" turns out to be the more interesting answer.

The actual series director of Bakemonogatari was [Tatsuya Oishi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatsuya_Oishi_(director)), working under supervising director Akiyuki Shinbo. Oishi has described making the show with the stylism of Jean-Luc Godard. This is not a casual comparison. It is the explanation for everything.

## The Problem the Show Had to Solve

NisiOisin's light novels are constitutively untranslatable to standard animation. The previous analysis in this series has covered [the palindromic pen name and the literary architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nisio_Isin): kanji puns that only exist in Japanese, wordplay that requires the exact ideographic character to land, a prose style where the character *is* their mode of speech. An earlier entry established that SHAFT's visual grammar was invented to honor a text whose literary techniques cannot survive medium change.

But that analysis describes what SHAFT did. It does not describe how, or where the specific solutions came from.

The fundamental problem: NisiOisin's novels are 90% conversation. [Studio SHAFT had to animate something that is, by definition, people talking in rooms](https://www.cbr.com/light-novel-adaptations-solo-leveling-bakemonogatari/). Two people talking in a room is not cinematic raw material. It is theatre — and badly staged theatre at that, since the characters often sit still for extended passages and the camera has nothing to do. SHAFT's solution was to stop trying to make the conversations look like they were not conversations, and start treating the conversations as the visual event.

This required a fundamental reconception of what animation can do.

## What Godard Actually Did

Jean-Luc Godard's *À bout de souffle* (1960) begins in the middle of a monologue addressed directly to the camera. His jump cuts — edits that violate continuity within the same shot, preserving dialogue but discarding visual logic — were not stylistic decoration. They were a position: the film does not need to create the illusion of continuous space. Cinema can be honest about being cut-together.

[The Godardian reference is documented in the Sakuga Blog's Kizumonogatari staff interview](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2021/03/31/kizumonogatari-staff-interview-director-tatsuya-oishi/): Oishi directed Bakemonogatari with Godard's stylism and "directed it in a way that he felt was 'logical', rather than chaotic, removing things he personally didn't like or things that didn't meet his sense of beauty." This maps precisely onto what the show does. Where Godard used jump cuts to say "this is edited film, not life," Bakemonogatari uses rapid editorial cuts to say "this is constructed anime, not observation." The text cards — which appear for fractions of seconds, displaying walls of kanji that no viewer can read in real time — are not informational. They are Brechtian interrupts: devices that, like Brecht's *Verfremdungseffekt*, break the show's surface to prevent unconscious immersion. Brecht's theatrical theory insisted the audience should never forget they were watching a performance; Oishi's text cards do the same — they [deliberately disrupt immersion](https://neetknowledgebase.wordpress.com/2018/03/18/shaft-a-visual-journey/) and force the viewer into a critical rather than absorbed posture. Godard did this with intertitles. Oishi does it with flash typography.

The Dutch angle, the extreme head tilt, the characters placed in impossible or unlocatable architectural spaces — these are similarly anti-illusionist devices. A Dutch angle does not describe the physical relationship between two characters in a room. It describes a psychological one. [The "SHAFT kakudō," as the head tilt is known in Japanese](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/shaft-head-tilt) — Shinbo's signature that Oishi systematized — places a character at an angle that no person would naturally occupy, foregrounding the artificiality of the pose. It says: this character is being presented to you, not observed.

## The Budget Constraint That Became a Grammar

There is another dimension to this that the Godard comparison risks obscuring: SHAFT's techniques were also driven by production reality.

[Bakemonogatari was made on a notably lower budget than SHAFT's later productions](https://neetknowledgebase.wordpress.com/tag/bakemonogatari/). Limited budget means limited movement — character animation is expensive, and extended scenes of two people talking require either quality movement throughout or a different strategy entirely. SHAFT chose the second option. If the characters cannot move much, then motion shifts to editing: cuts become the kinetic element of the sequence. If the environment cannot be detailed and inhabited, then abstract architectural geometry takes its place — cheaper to produce, and incidentally more expressive of the characters' psychological states.

[The text cards reduced animation costs](http://the-visual-medium.blogspot.com/2012/01/shaft-style.html) while creating a visual rhythm that worked as a substitute for physical movement. [Oishi had developed this technique earlier in the SHAFT Pani Poni Dash opening sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatsuya_Oishi_(director)), and Bakemonogatari expanded it from opening-credits use into full narrative deployment. A wall of kanji on a black card for a third of a second does not convey specific information to most viewers. It creates a pulse — a visual beat that can be felt before it can be read. This is not a concession to budget. It is the discovery of a technique.

The production crunch was visible on-screen: [Episode 10 aired in an obviously incomplete state](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(anime_series)), with color cards labeled "cut due to circumstances" replacing unfinished animation sequences. Later, [Episode 12 was broadcast in a narrower CinemaScope aspect ratio to reduce animation costs](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Trivia/Bakemonogatari). The final three episodes — concluding the Tsubasa Cat arc — were released as streaming-only web episodes months after broadcast ended, not ready for the original schedule.

This is sometimes cited as evidence that SHAFT couldn't keep up. A more accurate reading: the series' visual vocabulary was so non-standard, so dependent on specific rapid-editing and graphic-design choices rather than conventional animation, that the timeline for producing it didn't fit broadcast norms. [An academic study in *Word & Image* journal examined how the show's profuse text-on-screen creates what it calls a "reflexive" quality](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/02666286.2021.1988481) — the typography functions cinematically, not linguistically. The technique that budget constraints forced them to invent was also the technique that standard TV production couldn't accommodate.

## Shinbo, Oishi, and the Collaboration

The attribution of Bakemonogatari's style to Shinbo alone misses something important. [Shinbo specifically requested Oishi as series director because he wanted someone who could "visualize words themselves"](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2021/03/31/kizumonogatari-staff-interview-director-tatsuya-oishi/). [The ANN feature on SHAFT's production approach](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2017-01-18/the-secret-of-studio-shaft/.110526) confirms that Shinbo told staff he wanted to "put literal words onscreen" to highlight how brilliantly the source material was written — his approach was to treat the text as raw visual material, "similar to editing the original novel." Oishi supplied the cinematic grammar that this ambition required: the Godard-influenced editing, the text cards as narrative device, the bold color-field composition. Shinbo contributed the head tilt — developed from [Osamu Dezaki's exaggerated compositional staging](https://gamerant.com/what-is-the-shaft-head-tilt/) — and the general aesthetic direction.

[Oishi is identified by the Sakuga Blog as the central figure of SHAFT's most experimental phase in the mid-to-late 2000s](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/09/01/the-three-monogataris/), with the techniques now associated with "SHAFT style" being described as "watered-down versions of his [original] techniques." What the Monogatari franchise later became — consistent, recognizable, commercially polished — is a domestication of something Oishi did more radically at the outset. *Nisemonogatari* and *Second Season* are identifiably SHAFT, but the controlled abstraction of Bakemonogatari — where the visual language is in tension with itself, never settling — is specific to Oishi's involvement.

[Bakemonogatari is described as having "the most consistent element in its presentation be the lack of consistency"](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Bakemonogatari_(anime_series)). This is Oishi's Godard inheritance: the refusal to stabilize a house style, the commitment to treating each visual problem as a visual problem rather than applying a template.

## What the Technique Actually Does

The standard description of SHAFT's visual language as "weird" or "surreal" gets at the surface but misses the mechanism. What the techniques do, concretely, is convert dialogue from background into event.

Bakemonogatari has no other events. The visual grammar — the cuts, the angles, the text, the impossible spaces — has to carry the full burden of cinematic interest for a series that is, structurally, an extended series of people talking. [Kotaku's Bakemonogatari analysis described it as "an amazingly well-directed anime" noting how the direction "transforms ordinary locations into unsettling spaces" through camera work that "meaningfully support[s] the storytelling."](https://kotaku.com/bakemonogatari-is-an-amazingly-well-directed-anime-513331590)

Godard solved this problem in French New Wave cinema by making the dialogue's staging a subject in itself. His characters talk against film technique the way jazz musicians play against the chord changes — the conversation is one thing, and the camera's relationship to it is another. The gap between what is being said and how it is being shot creates meaning that neither element could produce alone.

![Hitagi Senjougahara staple gun scene - Bakemonogatari's visual confrontation](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/3/78012.jpg)

This is exactly what Bakemonogatari does. Senjougahara's extended weapon deployment in the opening arc is staged as abstract geometric horror — straight lines, scissors, clinical abstraction — while the dialogue is flirtatious and wry. The visual register and the verbal register contradict each other, and the tension between them is where the character lives. Standard staging would put the camera where the action is. SHAFT puts the camera where the character's psychology is, which is not the same place.

[The show's record-breaking BD sales in 2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakemonogatari) — which became the benchmark for anime home video in Japan — are sometimes explained as the discovery of a new audience for dialogue-driven anime. What they actually prove is that a visual grammar invented to solve a production problem turned out to be more appropriate to the material than conventional animation would have been. The budget constraint became the technique; the technique became the art; and a decade of SHAFT imitators spent years trying to copy the surface of something they didn't understand was actually a specific response to a specific literary problem — one that required the Godard solution to solve.
