[*Oshi no Ko*'s manga ending](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-good-bad-controversy/) earned near-universal derision when it ran in November 2024. [Dexerto called it "terrible."](https://www.dexerto.com/anime/oshi-no-ko-manga-slammed-for-terrible-ending-2974344/) [*Screen Rant* said it "more than earns the derision."](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-good-bad-controversy/) Even defenses of the ending [tend to argue that it was appropriate rather than good](https://flawfinder.wordpress.com/2024/11/08/no-oshi-no-kos-ending-does-not-suck/). The complaints are familiar: Aqua dies without personal fulfillment; Kana never tells him how she feels; Ruby repeats Ai's pattern of lying to hide her pain; the final arc is rushed; the villain's death is anticlimactic. The series that built its reputation on unflinching industry critique ended, [as many critics noted](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-good-bad-controversy/), by seemingly endorsing the very cycle it had spent 150+ chapters criticizing.

These criticisms are largely correct. [Aka Akasaka himself confirmed the ending matched his original vision](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-author-finally-speaks-about-controversial-ending/), though that defense hasn't satisfied the readers who felt specific arcs deserved more. And yet there's one character whose arc concludes with exactly the coherence and emotional honesty the rest of the series abandoned. [Akane Kurokawa](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Akane_Kurokawa) doesn't get a happy ending. She gets something rarer: a completed one.

## What a Completed Arc Looks Like

Most discussion of the *Oshi no Ko* ending treats "better ending" as synonymous with "happier ending." [The *Game Rant* piece on characters who deserved better](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-characters-who-deserve-better-endings/) frames Akane's arc as tragic because "no amount of recognition or success could replace the love she had with Aqua." This is true. It's also not what makes an arc work or fail.

A completed arc means one thing: a character has moved through a transformation that changes who they are, and at the end of the story, they are somewhere different than where they started — with the passage between those two points doing something meaningful. Completion is not happiness. Completion is coherence.

By this measure, Akane's arc is the only one in the series that fully delivers.

She enters [*Love for Real*](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Dating_Reality_Show) as a young theater actress seeking exposure—technically skilled, emotionally reserved, not yet tested. [The cyberbullying arc](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Akane_Kurokawa/Plot)—in which a scripted moment of conflict on a reality show spirals into mass harassment after video clips are decontextualized online—breaks her, reduces her to something that needed saving, and forces her into contact with her own fragility in a way that could have ended her entirely. [She is rescued by Aqua](https://beneaththetangles.com/2023/05/29/akane-kurokawa-and-a-savior-who-sees-and-saves/), and out of that rescue, she builds something—a relationship, a method, a willingness to go into darkness without being destroyed by it.

The relationship ends. [She engineers the ending herself, with characteristic analytical precision](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Akane_Kurokawa/Relationships). She attends the funeral. She wins a national film award. She appears in the crowd at Ruby's comeback performance. [The light novel *Futari no Etude*](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-new-novel-expands-manga-finale/) shows her and Kana still bickering, still competing, still alive enough to be difficult with each other.

This is what surviving looks like. Not peace. Not happiness. The continued capacity for engagement.

## Comparing the Other Arcs

The complaints about the ending cluster around the other three central characters, and they're illuminating:

**Aqua**: [He achieves his revenge and dies](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-explained/). His arc is complete in the narrow sense that the project he organized his life around concludes. But the series spent considerable time establishing that Aqua is more than his revenge—that there's a version of him that could have lived a different life, attended medical school, loved someone. None of that potential is resolved. His death is not tragic in the classical sense (tragedy requires a fall from a height the character achieved); it's more accurately described as arrested. He ran out of road before he ran out of character.

**Ruby**: [She performs at Tokyo Dome and hides her pain, exactly as Ai did](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-explained/). Critics rightly note that this appears to validate the cycle the series spent years critiquing. Whether it's intentional irony or an authorial failure depends on how generous you're feeling. Either way, her arc doesn't close—it's presented as a continuation, a baton passed forward in a relay that hasn't finished.

**Kana**: [She screams her love for Aqua at his funeral](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Kana_Arima), alone, having never told him. The series positions this as devastating, which it is. The *Futari no Etude* novel exists specifically because [the manga's treatment of Kana's arc needed expanding](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/oshi-no-ko-novel-kana-akane-endings/)—the ending left her in mid-grief, with nowhere specific to go. She's the character whose arc is most visibly incomplete at the manga's conclusion.

Against these, Akane's ending stands differently. She doesn't get what she wanted. But she knows what happened, understands why, and is still functioning. In a series defined by characters who break or get broken, Akane demonstrates what it looks like to bend and stay standing.

## Why Fan Reception Gets This Wrong

[The *Game Rant* article arguing Akane deserved better](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-characters-who-deserve-better-endings/) frames her arc through the lens of romantic fulfillment: the problem is that she lost Aqua, and winning an acting award doesn't compensate for that. This reading treats the acting career as a consolation prize and the relationship as the real prize.

This gets the series' own priorities backwards. *Oshi no Ko* is not a romance—it's an industry critique with romance as one of its instruments. [The series is explicit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oshi_no_Ko) that relationships in the entertainment industry are shaped and often distorted by professional contexts, power dynamics, and image management. Aqua and Akane's relationship began on a reality dating show and was immediately complicated by his revenge plot. To say Akane's arc fails because she didn't end up with Aqua is to apply the logic of a romance series to a story that has always been about something else.

What Akane's arc actually shows—and what the critics of her ending miss—is what it looks like to have a self that persists through damage. She was damaged before Aqua, and she was damaged by losing him, and she emerged from both with her capabilities intact. [In a series where most characters are defined by their wounds](https://anihk.com/en-us/blogs/characters/oshi-no-ko-akane-kurokawa-ending-explained), Akane is defined by what she built from them.

## The One Thing the Ending Understood

[Aka Akasaka, when asked about the controversial ending, said he put his "heart and soul" into it and that it ended as he intended](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-controversy-manga-artist/). Whether or not you believe the rest of the finale earned that defense, there's evidence that Akasaka understood what Akane's arc was.

The [*Futari no Etude* novel](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-new-novel-expands-manga-finale/)—the post-manga epilogue he supervised, [released December 18, 2024](https://anitrendz.net/news/2024/11/08/oshi-no-ko-light-novel-about-kana-and-akane-to-release-in-december/)—focuses not on Aqua's legacy or Ruby's idol career but on Kana and Akane starring together in an improvisational theatre production. They practice scenes, bicker, and recall a childhood audition they both attended. [According to the novel's plot summary](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/oshi-no-ko-novel-kana-akane-endings/), they still don't get along—but they respect each other, and they practice. This is what continuity looks like for these characters: still difficult, still in process, still showing up. The implication is that *Oshi no Ko*'s real ongoing story belongs to them, and that story is about work, not grief.

Akane's arc works because it never pretended the ending was arrival. She didn't arrive anywhere. She is still in motion, still carrying what she built and what she lost, still showing up to the next thing. That's not a consolation prize.

![Oshi no Ko Season 2 key visual](https://i0.wp.com/anitrendz.net/news/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/oshinokoseason2_mainvisual.jpg?resize=696%2C984&ssl=1)

The critics of the *Oshi no Ko* ending are mostly right. The finale is rushed. The villain's death is anticlimactic. Ruby's pattern repetition creates a thematic problem the series doesn't resolve. Aqua died without becoming who he might have been.

But one character in that finale received exactly the ending her arc required: not resolution, not happiness, not romantic fulfillment, but continuity. The ability to keep going in a series where going is what matters.

Akane Kurokawa survived *Oshi no Ko*. That's the ending that actually works.
