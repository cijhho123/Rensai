There is a complaint you hear about [*Call of the Night*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) somewhere in the middle of its run. The early volumes — Ko wandering empty streets, Nazuna's nightly feedings rendered in electric blue and violet, the whole thing operating like a piece of city-pop playing from a phone in a dark room — have an atmosphere that feels irreplaceable. Then the private detective shows up. Then other vampires arrive. Then there are fight scenes, faction politics, a vampire council. The complaint says Kotoyama lost the thread, that he traded the show's soul for plot machinery.

The complaint is wrong. Not because the genre shifts are seamless — they are not — but because they were structurally necessary in a way that reveals something important about what *Call of the Night* was actually doing the whole time.

## The Condition That Froze Everything

To understand why the story had to expand outward, you have to understand what the two vampire conditions actually do to the romance at a structural level. Kotoyama established early that Ko transforming into a vampire requires that Ko genuinely fall in love with Nazuna — and separately, that a vampire's bite becomes lethal once the vampire genuinely loves the human. These two conditions don't just create romantic tension. [They create a locked door.](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Manga/CallOfTheNight)

The moment Ko starts falling for Nazuna in earnest, the transformation goal becomes paradoxical. The closer he gets to loving her, the closer she gets to being able to kill him by accident. And if she loves him back, her bite becomes lethal — so she can never safely complete the transformation even if he wants it. The love between them, as it grows, systematically destroys the mechanism through which it was supposed to be expressed. This is not a plot complication. It is a structural trap that Kotoyama built into the premise, and it means that the romantic throughline — Ko becoming a vampire, Ko and Nazuna reaching some kind of resolution — cannot advance through the usual manga romance escalation. There is no "next step."

So what do you do when your central plot cannot move forward?

You grow the world around it.

## Kyoko's One-Woman War

The arrival of [Kyoko Mejiro (working as Anko Uguisu)](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kyouko_Mejiro), the vampire hunter, marks the moment *Call of the Night* converts from a moody two-person romance into a thriller. Her backstory is the series' darkest: a vampire transformed her father, who then murdered her mother before Kyoko killed him in self-defense, leaving her consumed by a decades-long vendetta against all vampires. On its surface this is classic genre machinery — give the protagonists an antagonist, raise stakes, add urgency.

But Kyoko is doing something more specific than that. She is asking Ko the question the love conditions have made it impossible for the romance itself to ask. Her challenge forces Ko to examine whether vampirism is genuinely what he wants or whether he has been telling himself a comfortable story about wanting transformation while actually being attached to his human life — a debate that [readers on the AniList forums were still relitigating](https://anilist.co/forum/thread/71224) long after the manga ended. Anko's pursuit of Ko isn't primarily a physical threat — it is a confrontation with his self-deception. The thriller framework gives Kotoyama a mechanism to apply pressure to Ko's interiority that the romance cannot apply, because the romance is the thing Ko is rationalizing.

[The Outerhaven's review](https://www.theouterhaven.net/2022/09/anime-review-yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night/) of the first anime season noted that the Anko arc shifts the series from "slice-of-night-life" into something more tense without quite becoming a different show. That's the balance Kotoyama was threading: using genre to create pressure while keeping the emotional center recognizable.

## The Action Arcs and the Taxonomy of Impossible Love

When the vampire council and the independent vampires enter the picture — when the series starts generating actual fight sequences, supernatural powers, faction intrigue — the complaint about genre drift becomes loudest. This is where many readers felt Kotoyama had capitulated to shonen convention.

What these arcs actually contain is a roster of secondary vampires who are each defined by their relationship to an impossible desire. [Kiku Hoshimi](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Vampires), the sociopathic vampire who has been running experiments on humans for decades, is obsessed with a theory that a vampire can become human by drinking the blood of someone who loves them. This is precisely the inverse of the transformation conditions Kotoyama established — a vampire trying to reverse her condition through love, just as Ko is trying to acquire vampirism through love. The symmetry is not accidental. Kiku's arc is the transformation condition examined from the other direction, by a character who has had centuries to calcify her response to an impossible goal into something brutal and methodical.

The other vampires fill out this taxonomy with specificity. Seri Kikyou, the pragmatic fixer who keeps the peace between vampires and humans, has made her peace with impossibility through bureaucratic distance — desire is a problem to be managed, not felt. Kabura Midori, the ancient who has watched centuries of humans come and go, has arrived at resigned acceptance: if you love something that dies, outlive it, then find another. The council vampires as a class represent predatory control — channeling desire into hierarchy rather than relationship. [Each of these figures](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/CallOfTheNight) embodies a distinct long-term adaptation to the same condition that Ko and Nazuna are only beginning to face. Kotoyama is mapping the entire space of responses to "what do you do when what you want can't be had?"

Ko and Nazuna are one data point in that map. The genre expansion is how Kotoyama filled in the rest.

## What the Genre Drift Is Actually About

[CBR's analysis of how the series subverts the school setting](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-subverts-anime-school-setting/) makes the point that Ko's rejection of school represents a rejection of the conformist escalation path of Japanese adolescence — study, enter, advance, repeat. The night is where that path doesn't exist. But there is a corresponding argument about genre: *Call of the Night* keeps shifting genre because genre, like the school system, is a set of expectations about escalation. Romance manga escalates toward confession and union. Thriller escalates toward resolution of threat. Action escalates toward decisive victory. Kotoyama repeatedly invokes these escalation grammars and then refuses to let them resolve cleanly — because the love conditions have made clean resolution structurally impossible.

The thriller arc with Anko doesn't end with Ko defeating the vampire hunter. It ends with Kyoko's own painful reckoning with what her vendetta cost her. The action arcs don't end with a power fantasy triumph. They end with the vampire council existing, with Nazuna's precarious position among her kind not really resolved, with the fundamental situation unchanged. Each genre cycle ends back where it started: Ko and Nazuna, attached to each other, unable to complete what they started, still out in the night.

[Anime Herald noted](https://www.animeherald.com/2025/09/20/how-call-of-the-night-uses-its-vampires-to-build-its-structure/) that Kotoyama uses the day-night cycle specifically as a structural reset — nights end and the cast must absorb what happened before the next night opens. This is the same principle operating at the macro level. Each genre cycle resets to the locked door and asks: given everything you now know about the world around this impossible love, are you still going to keep trying?

## The Ending Is the Answer

Chapter 200's final beat — [Nazuna and Ko reuniting after three years of separation](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained), Nazuna having drifted to a small village near the Arctic and built a quiet life with the people there, Ko having spent the years training to be strong enough to survive what Nazuna's love might accidentally do to him — is the genre drift made literal. The series tried romance, then thriller, then action, and in each mode it arrived at the same conclusion: the thing these two people have cannot be completed, cannot be made safe, cannot be given a clean ending. So they choose each other anyway.

"Play tag with me until death do us part" is not a romantic declaration in the traditional sense. It is an acceptance of the game — the permanent chase, the always-almost-catching — as the thing itself rather than a means to an endpoint. Fan reactions split hard: [forum threads on GameFAQs](https://gamefaqs.gamespot.com/boards/2000121-anime-and-manga-other-titles/80677604) show readers who felt cheated by an ending that declined to resolve anything, while others found it the only honest conclusion the conditions allowed. Both responses make sense. A definitive resolution would have required either the love conditions to be satisfied (which would change the characters beyond recognition) or one of them to give up (which would betray everything the series built). The openness of the ending is not a failure to commit — it is the thesis delivered at the emotional register instead of the analytical one.

[Kotoyama won the 68th Shogakukan Manga Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) for *Call of the Night* in 2023, which is one of manga's most prestigious recognitions. You can read that award as validation of the series' visual style or its romantic premise. But I think it's recognizing something structural: a series that understood what kind of story it was telling well enough to let every genre it entered prove the same point, until there was nothing left to prove except the commitment itself.

## Why the Complaint Gets It Backwards

[Fans who came to *Call of the Night* for the atmosphere of the early volumes](https://countzeroor.com/2022/10/15/call-of-the-night-anime-review/) — the city-pop melancholy, the intimate scale, the sensation of being the only two people awake in a sleeping city — are not wrong to love what those volumes did. The first few volumes of this manga are close to perfect as a specific aesthetic experience. Creepy Nuts' ["Daten" (堕天)](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2022/8/4/creepy-nuts-releases-call-of-the-night-opening-theme-mv-filled-with-collage-pop-art), the anime's first opening, captures exactly that feeling: hip-hop built around the specific texture of bottomless urban night, the floating sensation of being unmoored from schedule and obligation. Director Tomoyuki Itamura [described wanting the song to convey "the feeling of floating"](https://thenaturalaristocrat.com/2024/09/01/creepy-nuts-video-interview-call-of-the-night-theme-songs/) as Ko and Nazuna fly through the city sky.

But the complaint assumes that atmosphere should have remained the series' mode indefinitely — and that would require the love conditions to never develop, Ko to never grow, the story to remain suspended in its own vibes. Which is, in fact, exactly what Ko thought he wanted: a permanent exit from time, from progression, from the obligation to become someone. The genre expansion is the series refusing to give him that. The thriller and action arcs are the night refusing to stay still.

This refusal is embedded in Kotoyama's visual grammar as well. [The early volumes](https://animeuknews.net/2021/04/call-of-the-night-volume-1-review/) use negative space generously — empty panels of city streets, long gutters between beats, Kotoyama's linework at its most minimal so the darkness can breathe. The action arcs are denser: panels multiply, bodies fill the space, speed lines interrupt the dark. The visual register changes because it has to. Emptiness was the condition of the early books' intimacy. That intimacy required Ko and Nazuna to be alone in the night, which required the world to be small. Once Kotoyama made the world larger, the darkness had to share space with everything it now contained.

What Kotoyama understood, and what the complaint misses, is that the intimacy of those early volumes was only possible because the world was small and the characters were frozen. The world had to grow because the characters had to. The genre had to shift because the romance couldn't. Everything that got added — the vampire council, the hunters, the secondary cast with their own impossible desires — was grown around the locked door of the central love story. Not to distract from it. To illuminate what it costs to keep standing in front of it.

The locked door never opened. That's the point. [Ko worked alongside Anko](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kyouko_Mejiro) as her detective assistant after everything, still attending high school, building a human life he had once wanted to abandon. Nazuna learned what it was to be loved, and then left to protect him from it, and then came back because the separation didn't fix anything. The final image of them in the snow, agreeing to chase each other indefinitely, is neither a happy ending nor a tragic one. It is the only ending the conditions allowed, embraced as if it were a choice.

Which, in the end, it is.
