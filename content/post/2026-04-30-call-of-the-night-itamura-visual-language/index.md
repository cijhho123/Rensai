Watch the first five minutes of any Monogatari episode Tomoyuki Itamura directed — white space, geometric abstraction, text flashing across the frame in crisp blocks, the visual language announcing itself as literary animation before a single line of dialogue lands. Then watch the first five minutes of the Call of the Night anime. A boy stepping off a balcony into the Tokyo dark, the sky a deep bruised purple, amber streetlights haloed by humidity, the whole city breathing warmth and invitation.

Same director. Completely different argument.

That gap — between the cold, cerebral SHAFT aesthetic Itamura spent a decade working inside and the vivid, atmospheric world he built at Liden Films — is one of the more interesting stories in recent anime production. It tells you something specific about what SHAFT's house style was actually doing to the work it produced, about what kind of director Itamura actually is, and about why the Call of the Night anime looks the way it does — which is to say, unlike almost anything else.

## The Reactive Director

When Itamura took over the Monogatari franchise from Tatsuya Oishi with [Nisemonogatari in 2012](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/people.php?id=79397), he inherited a visual system at a crossroads. Oishi's Bakemonogatari had been built around what [Sakuga Blog describes](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/tag/tatsuya-oishi/) as a "metaphorical" directorial philosophy: the visuals operated as independent commentary on NisiOisiN's prose, creating surreal tableaux that existed in dialogue with the text rather than subordinate to it. Oishi described his approach as stylistically influenced by Jean-Luc Godard — surrealism layered onto minimalist flat shapes and gradients, [environments as psychological states](https://ponzianialexvic107.wordpress.com/2018/10/30/bakemonogatari-when-art-and-surreal-design-meet-story-telling-and-animation/), color fields that ignored literal space to establish emotional space. The studio and its director treated the source material as raw material for their own visual statement.

Itamura did something different. As [Sakuga Blog's analysis of the Monogatari franchise](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/09/01/the-three-monogataris/) summarizes, his approach was "reactive" rather than metaphorical: under his tenure, "the adaptation aspired to preserve NisioisiN's actual writing in a much more explicit form, conceiving the visuals as a way to accompany the text rather than as an embodiment of it." Where Oishi's visuals might contradict or reframe the narration, Itamura's were designed to serve it — to find the visual register that made NisiOisiN's prose land harder, rather than to impose a separate layer of meaning.

This wasn't timidity. Itamura brought real craft to the work. He developed an increasingly sophisticated use of on-screen text — paper collage-style transitions, highlighted phrases that pulled the unreliable narration into visible form. His color contrasts within SHAFT's established aesthetic were sharper. But the underlying philosophy was service to the source: visuals that made NisiOisiN's text better, not visuals that competed with it.

That's what "reactive" means here. Not passive — precise. Itamura read his source material and built accordingly.

The problem — if you want to call it that — was that SHAFT's house style has its own gravity. The studio's aesthetic, developed under chief director Akiyuki Shinbo, tends toward [minimalist theater](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2017-01-18/the-secret-of-studio-shaft/.110526): characters posed like actors against stripped-down backgrounds, color used as flat contrast rather than atmosphere, real-world textures and photographs woven into animation to create deliberate dissonance. It's a style built for psychological unease, for intellectual provocation, for the specific texture of NisiOisiN's writing. When Itamura left SHAFT after Owarimonogatari Part II, the question was what he'd do without that gravitational field.

## The Case Study of Vanitas as a Rehearsal

His first post-SHAFT series directorial job was [The Case Study of Vanitas](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/convention/2021/anime-expo-lite-and-aniplex-online-fest/aniplex-online-fest-making-of-the-case-study-of-vanitas/.174540) at Bones in 2021 — another vampire story, not by coincidence. When asked about his approach to adapting Jun Mochizuki's steampunk vampire manga at the Aniplex Online Fest, Itamura's answer was telling: "Aesthetic may have been the first word to come to mind."

Not character. Not narrative. Aesthetic.

At BONES with Vanitas, Itamura demonstrated that his reactive instinct — reading the source and building accordingly — wasn't SHAFT-specific. The Vanitas anime translated Mochizuki's Victorian Paris into something genuinely ornate: Baroque and Rococo influences on the interior design, kinetic action sequences that honored the manga's staging, a color palette that went heavy on golds and deep blues to serve the period Gothic atmosphere. He also began bringing his own collaborators: key staff who had worked on Monogatari under him followed him to this production, beginning to establish what would become a recognizable Itamura ensemble.

Vanitas is not a great anime — its pacing is uneven and the gothic romance sometimes tips into camp — and it is worth asking whether the reactive philosophy partly explains why. Mochizuki's manga has pacing problems too, and a director who is committed to fidelity over intervention will amplify those problems rather than correct them. The ornate visual system Itamura built for Vanitas is genuinely beautiful; the show around it sometimes does not deserve it. But it established the thing that mattered: that Itamura's reactive philosophy could survive contact with a radically different visual source than NisiOisiN's prose. He could read a fundamentally visual manga and build an animated equivalent. That discipline, if not the lesson about when to override the source, would matter when Call of the Night came around.

## Building the Night at Liden Films

When Itamura joined Liden Films to direct [Call of the Night](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/01/20/call-of-the-night-episode-1/), he brought his Monogatari-era collaborator Tetsuya Miyanishi as chief director — a deliberate continuity with his SHAFT years. For Season 2, the ensemble deepened: Nao Miyoshi (who had assisted on Vanitas) joined as co-assistant director alongside Izumi Takizawa, described in production materials as a "longtime collaborator and fellow ex-SHAFT member." Itamura was not just changing studios; he was building a portable production unit capable of working anywhere.

What that unit built for Call of the Night is, by consensus, [one of the finest-looking anime of the 2020s](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night/episodes-1-3/.187922). Critics at ANN noted that the series "breathes bright post-Impressionist color into its nightscapes, where most series turn night scenes into a muted muddy mess." The WrongEveryTime review of the first episode described the color design in technical terms: day scenes "entirely saturated by light, lacking color altogether," while night scenes rendered in "deep purples and occasional intersection's orange glow, with light implying intention — making it easier to see the city as a living organism through the glow of apartment lights."

What's striking about this is how precisely it inverts the SHAFT aesthetic. Where SHAFT's visual language operates through abstraction and minimalism — geometry over atmosphere, negative space over environmental richness — the Call of the Night anime is almost aggressive in its atmospheric density. As [Geek to Geek Media's analysis](https://geektogeekmedia.com/geekery/keywords/call-of-the-night/) notes, rather than the uniform dark blue-greens that characterize most night scenes in animation, the production uses hazier greens and yellows, moody reds, energetic purples that actively shift within individual shots, creating a sense of the night as a living environment rather than a theatrical backdrop. The lighting precision is notable: where most animation simply darkens a scene to indicate night, this production ensures every light source has a distinct color temperature and that sources interact with each other, making the city feel inhabited.

![Yofukashi no Uta Season 2 key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1509/148453l.jpg)

The reason this works — and the reason it looks nothing like Monogatari — is that Itamura read Kotoyama's manga and understood that the neon palette isn't decorative. It's the argument. The manga uses vibrant gradients and nocturnal color to make a philosophical claim: the night is better. More honest, more alive, more genuinely human than the bleached daylight world Ko is fleeing. The art style makes this claim before any character articulates it. Itamura's job wasn't to impose a visual system on top of that — it was to amplify it, to make the anime do with motion and color timing what the manga does with static gradients.

The result is that the theatrical character direction Itamura carried from SHAFT gets deployed very differently here. In Monogatari, theatrical acting was the constant mode — every scene processed through that lens. [As WrongEveryTime observes](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/01/20/call-of-the-night-episode-1/), in Call of the Night it "is used as an accent to a world between 10PM and 6AM where everything moves with a lethargic gait." The drama happens against a backdrop of atmospheric immersion rather than minimalist abstraction. When Itamura breaks out the theatrical toolkit, it hits harder precisely because it's not the default register. Nazuna's first appearance in Episode 1 is built around a [fisheye perspective](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/01/20/call-of-the-night-episode-1/) that distorts the frame to make her feel genuinely alien — but it works because the surrounding scenes have been deliberately unhurried. By [Episode 11](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night/episode-11/.189817), Itamura pulls the same trick in reverse: Anko the vampire hunter is introduced in sepia, the neon palette entirely stripped away, her silhouette buried under an overpass like a figure from a different, colder genre. The show's warmth becomes legible as warmth specifically because he withholds it.

## What the Constants Reveal

Three vampire-adjacent projects. Three radically different visual systems. But some things don't change.

Across Monogatari, Vanitas, and Call of the Night, Itamura deploys theatrical character acting, sharp color contrast as structural device, and strategic visual interruptions — moments where the style shifts registers to punctuate an emotional beat. These are his actual fingerprints. Everything else — warm versus cold, atmospheric versus minimalist, text-driven versus color-driven — adapts to serve whoever wrote the source material.

This makes Itamura an unusual figure in anime direction. He is not an auteur in the Shinbo mold, stamping a recognizable style across everything he touches. He is something closer to a precision translator — a director who reads a source deeply enough to build the visual system that specific source needs, and who has built a toolkit large enough to build very different systems without contradiction.

The [Season 2 premiere](https://animecorner.me/call-of-the-night-season-2-premiere-brings-back-the-vibes-weve-missed/) bears this out: critics praised it as "one of the most visually impressive anime of Summer 2025," not because it repeated Season 1's choices mechanically but because the production team had internalized the visual logic well enough to develop it. [ANN's review of Episode 6](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night-season-2/episode-6/.227530) is particularly illuminating: the episode's storyboards, handled by Nao Miyoshi in his first solo storyboarding credit, carry unmistakable "Sayonara, Zetsubou-Sensei and Monogatari vibes in many of the cuts and edits" — cartoony close-ups, a brief GameBoy mode sequence, a character holding a Senjougahara pose in front of a spiral staircase. The SHAFT vocabulary surfaces in the scene that earned it. This isn't regression; it's someone from the same school of visual thinking as Itamura applying those tools with precision, having learned from the director himself when and why they work.

## The Argument the Color Makes

The most important thing about the Call of the Night anime's visual language is that it's not decorative. When critics describe the neon palette as "beautiful" or "stunning" — and they do, consistently — they're responding to something real, but they're not quite naming it.

What the color does is argue. Every saturated purple sky, every amber streetlight haloed against darkness, every moment where the city resolves from mud to luminescence as Ko crosses from his daytime life into the night — this is the series making the same case Kotoyama makes in the manga, making it in the medium of color and atmosphere before a single line of dialogue. The [WrongEveryTime analysis](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/01/20/call-of-the-night-episode-1/) names the opening sequence as "a battle between teal and pink hues — a visual articulation of the show's emphasis on the two worlds of day and night." That's not a description of aesthetics. That's a description of thesis.

[Season 2's review at Lost in Anime](https://lostinanime.com/2025/09/yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night-season-2-12-end-and-series-review/) called the completed series "one of the most emotional and enjoyable viewing experiences in the medium," but the emotion is inseparable from the visuals — the [ButWhyTho review](https://butwhytho.net/2025/09/call-of-the-night-season-2-review/) noted that Liden Films "brings their A-game," and that the "animation does a lot of heavy lifting when it comes to the mood." That heavy lifting isn't accidental. Itamura built a system where the visuals carry argumentative weight, where you understand what the show believes before you could articulate it.

That's what a decade of making visuals serve text teaches you, if you learn the right lesson: not that text is primary and images secondary, but that the relationship between the two is where all the interesting work happens. SHAFT taught Itamura to read a source and respond to it with precision. What Liden Films gave him was a source that didn't need text to carry the argument — a source where the image was already the thesis.

He showed up and made it move.
