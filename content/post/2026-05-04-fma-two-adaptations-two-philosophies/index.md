The thing about Fullmetal Alchemist is that equivalent exchange either works or it doesn't. That single question — is the world fundamentally fair, do effort and sacrifice produce results proportional to their cost — is what the entire franchise is built on, and the two anime adaptations give you opposite answers with equal conviction.

[*Fullmetal Alchemist* (2003)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(TV_series)), directed by Seiji Mizushima at Bones, says no. In the 2003 version, antagonist Dante has a monologue that functions as the show's philosophical thesis: effort isn't always rewarded with equivalent gain. The world is not balanced. The brothers are punished not for insufficient payment but because the universe does not keep ledgers. Edward ends up in a parallel world — [our world, shortly before World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(TV_series)) — separated from Alphonse, and the story concludes without restoration, without fairness, without the clean reciprocity the alchemy system promised.

[*Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood* (2009)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist:_Brotherhood), directed by Yasuhiro Irie, says yes. In Brotherhood, Edward sacrifices his ability to perform alchemy — the most important thing in his life — and Alphonse is restored. Equivalent exchange holds. The cost is real but the equation balances. The show ends with the Elrics alive, whole, and moving forward. The universe is, after sufficient sacrifice, navigable.

The debate about which version is better has been running for fifteen years and will never resolve because it is not actually about quality. It is about which answer you find more honest.

## What Arakawa Actually Did

The 2003 adaptation's divergence from the manga was not a failure or a rescue attempt. Hiromu Arakawa was present in development meetings and [requested that the Bones team create a different ending](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(TV_series)) from the one she was writing for the manga. She did not want to repeat the same resolution across both media. According to accounts of those meetings, when she saw the finished 2003 anime — with its dramatically different Homunculi origins, its alternate-world ending, its fundamentally darker philosophy — she was [reportedly surprised and pleased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(TV_series)) at how far the staff's own interpretation had diverged.

This is an unusually honest creative arrangement. Most mangaka who see their work adapted try to influence the result toward their own vision. Arakawa did the opposite: she gave director Mizushima explicit permission to have a different vision. The 2003 anime's pessimism was not an accident or a compromise. It was an authorized alternate reading of the same source material.

When Brotherhood was produced five years later, after the manga had concluded, it could finally be what it was always advertised as: a faithful adaptation. Director Yasuhiro Irie followed the manga closely, [completing the full story in 64 episodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist:_Brotherhood) to the 2003 series' 51, allowing the full cast of international characters — Ling Yao, Mei Chang, General Armstrong — to exist, and restoring the Homunculi to their roles as Father's creations representing the seven deadly sins rather than the 2003 version's more personal origins.

Brotherhood's reputation is essentially unimpeachable on craft grounds. It held [MAL's top-ranked anime position](https://www.cbr.com/fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood-top-anime-title-mal-lose/) for years and sits at [9.1 on IMDB](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1355642/), which makes it one of the highest-rated television series in any medium on the platform. As shonen action, it is close to a ceiling.

## The Homunculi Are Where the Philosophy Lives

The clearest illustration of the two shows' different worldviews is not the endings but the Homunculi — specifically what they are and where they come from.

In [Brotherhood](https://fma.fandom.com/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist:_Brotherhood), the Homunculi are Father's weapons: manufactured representations of the seven deadly sins, created to be disposable vessels and enforcers. Pride. Lust. Greed. Envy. Sloth. Gluttony. Wrath. They are external — they exist to show what humanity must overcome. Father is a being who extracted all human weakness from himself and crystallized it into these creatures. The Homunculi as a group are the sins of one being, spread into seven bodies. Defeating them is defeating the distilled failure modes of consciousness. It is a heroic, categorically-mapped conflict.

In [2003](https://fma.fandom.com/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(2003_Anime)), the Homunculi originate from failed human transmutations. Sloth is what remains of Trisha Elric — the brothers' mother, the very person they tried to bring back. Wrath is what remains of Izumi Curtis's lost child. The villains are not external abstractions of sin. They are the literal consequences of the protagonists' actions and the actions of people they love. The brothers created Sloth. They must eventually destroy her. The horror is inescapable and deeply personal.

This distinction is not a minor plot difference. It is the 2003 adaptation saying: the worst things in this story come from the good people's mistakes, not from an external evil. There is no Father to ultimately defeat. There is only the web of consequences spreading outward from people trying to do right and failing.

The [fan discourse on this question](https://www.resetera.com/threads/what-aspects-were-done-better-in-fullmetal-alchemist-2003-than-fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood-and-vice-versa.474873/) has settled, approximately, on Brotherhood as the better-crafted work and 2003 as the more emotionally affecting one. This is a real distinction. Brotherhood has more fluid animation, a larger and more varied cast, and a plot that actually resolves its own setup. [The 2003 anime rushes Hughes's death](https://medium.com/anitay-official/the-fullmetal-alchemist-2003-anime-is-a-masterpiece-of-adaptation-7f017377b7b) relative to Brotherhood — Hughes appears less frequently and his loss, while still effective, hits with less weight. Brotherhood, which spent more time establishing him, makes his death one of the most devastating moments in the entire medium.

But 2003 does something Brotherhood cannot do: it keeps the stakes personal throughout. It never gets to the scale of Brotherhood's final arc, where armies mobilize and alchemy reshapes the earth. And not getting there is the point.

## What "Better" Actually Means Here

The conventional framing is that Brotherhood "fixed" or "completed" FMA as a story. This is partially true and partially a misread of what 2003 was doing.

The [2003 anime is not a failed adaptation](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2019/01/18/neither-are-perfect-fma-2003-vs-fma-brotherhood/) that ran out of source material and improvised. It is an adaptation that, with Arakawa's blessing, asked a different question than the manga. The manga asked: what if equivalent exchange, pursued honestly and at real cost, can actually work? The 2003 anime asked: what if it can't?

The hardest evidence for the 2003 interpretation is Nina Tucker — a character who appears briefly in Brotherhood and at greater length in 2003. Nina's transformation into a chimera and subsequent death is the purest case in either show of someone suffering without deserving to, without any alchemy producing or receiving equivalent value. The 2003 anime lingers there. It gives Tucker's family time that Brotherhood does not, precisely because Brotherhood has a full manga to adapt and cannot slow down. The cost of Brotherhood's superior pacing is that some of the show's darkest moments feel slightly transactional — terrible things happen to move the plot rather than to sit with what they mean.

This is not a knock on Brotherhood. It is an argument about what the two shows are doing. Brotherhood is making a case for the structure of the world. The 2003 anime is making a case that the structure is not what we think it is — and that case requires time to establish how badly equivalence can fail.

Arakawa, in choosing an optimistic ending for the manga, made a decision about which reading she found more true. But the fact that she actively encouraged Mizushima's darker reading — that she reportedly [received the 2003 ending with surprise and pleasure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist_(TV_series)) at how far it diverged — suggests the pessimistic answer was always latent in the material, waiting for someone willing to pull it out.

## Why the Debate Will Never End

The FMA 2003 vs Brotherhood debate has outlasted dozens of other adaptation controversies because it is not a question of fidelity or craft. It is a question about how you understand the world.

If you believe — after accounting for all the randomness and cruelty and unearned suffering you can observe — that serious, costly commitment to a purpose can still produce proportional results, you will find Brotherhood's ending earned and right. The brothers worked hard, paid enormous prices, and ultimately got what they were seeking. The universe responded to their effort. That is a coherent account of what dedication can mean.

If you believe that serious, costly commitment does not guarantee proportional results — that the universe does not keep ledgers, that good people suffer consequences they did not earn, that effort is necessary but not sufficient — you will find the 2003 ending more honest, even if less satisfying. Edward's exile is not punishment for failure. It is the universe operating without reference to whether his sacrifice was adequate. That is a different, darker account of the same human experience.

Arakawa, writing the manga, chose the former. Director Mizushima, given freedom to choose, chose the latter. Brotherhood codified the former as the definitive version. And the question of which is more truthful remains open — because it is not a question about anime. It is a question about whether the world has the structure of a story that rewards equivalent exchange.

What Fullmetal Alchemist understood, and what neither adaptation alone captures entirely, is that serious people can disagree about this. Both shows were right about that.
