Before *Koe no Katachi* could be serialized in Weekly Shōnen Magazine, Kodansha's legal department conducted an extensive consultation with the [Japanese Federation of the Deaf](https://www.jfd.or.jp/en/). The one-shot version had already won the 80th Weekly Shōnen Magazine Newcomer Award in 2008, but the editors held it for three years — not because of quality concerns, but because [the severity of its content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)), specifically the bullying of a deaf child, made them uncertain whether publication was appropriate for a shōnen magazine's readership. The JFD reviewed the work and gave their endorsement. Serialization began in August 2013.

The consultation was thorough. It ensured that the Japanese Sign Language depicted in the manga was accurate, and the JFD's [involvement was credited in the production](https://iopn.library.illinois.edu/journals/jams/article/download/1128/979/4726). This is the kind of editorial due diligence that rarely happens. *Koe no Katachi* was, by any institutional measure, vetted.

And yet, a decade later, the work is [regularly cited](https://louis.uah.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1450&context=uah-theses) by disability scholars and Deaf critics as an example of the exact representational failure it was supposed to avoid: a deaf character whose deafness is her entire personality, who exists to be suffering and forgiven, who cannot access the same interiority and complexity afforded to the hearing characters around her.

How do you consult the Japanese Federation of the Deaf and still produce Shoko Nishimiya?

The answer is in something Yoshitoki Oima said in a [long interview with the Koe no Katachi wiki](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Long_Interview_with_Yoshitoki_Oima): "From my perspective, Shoko's hearing impairment was one of the motifs used to make readers aware of the theme of the work, but was not the main theme I felt compelled to portray." The theme Oima wanted to portray — the reason she wrote the manga — was the difficulty of communicating emotions between people. Shoko's deafness was the device through which that difficulty became visible.

That sentence explains everything. And it changes nothing.

## What a Motif Does to a Person

When a character's defining condition is a "motif" in the author's design, the condition is doing service work for the narrative rather than defining a person. The motif illuminates something else — in this case, communicative difficulty among human beings. The person carrying the motif is, in that design logic, a vehicle. She exists to make an abstract theme concrete.

This is not unusual in fiction. Characters carry metaphorical weight all the time. What makes it a problem in *Koe no Katachi* is specific: Shoko's deafness is not an aspect of her personhood that happens to resonate thematically. It IS her personhood, in the way the manga represents her. We learn almost nothing about Shoko's inner life beyond the experiences her deafness mediates. She has no hobby the manga explores in depth, no intellectual interest we see developed, no anger that persists, no desires the narrative grants space to. What we have is a character whose suffering — the suffering produced by her deafness in a hostile school environment — generates the events that prompt a hearing boy to change.

[Academic analysis from the University of Alabama](https://louis.uah.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1450&context=uah-theses) identifies this as the manga's audist structure. [Audism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audism) — a term coined by Tom Humphries in 1975 and theorized by Harlan Lane in *The Mask of Benevolence* (1992) — describes the system by which hearing norms and hearing perspectives are treated as the default human position. In *Koe no Katachi*, the narrative privileges hearing experience and perspective, marginalizes Deaf experience and perspective, and presents Deaf identity through the lens of the hearing majority's discomfort and guilt. Shoko is never the subject of the narrative. She is its occasion.

What makes this particularly legible is the contrast with how the manga treats Shoya's interior life. We spend enormous page-time inside Shoya's guilt, his self-hatred, his recalibration of his relationship to the world. His face is the manga's recurring image of emotional life — the [X's over other people's faces](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dko_Nishimiya), the withdrawal, the reconstruction. Shoko's interior is mediated almost entirely through what Shoya perceives of it. We do not know what Shoko thinks about Shoya until the narrative requires her to express it. When she does, she is forgiving.

## The Saint Who Should Have Been Angry

This is the disability studies critique in its most specific form, and it is worth taking seriously on the manga's own terms rather than applying it from outside: Shoko almost never gets angry at her abusers. She smiles at them. She apologizes to them. Her suicide attempt, when it comes, is driven by self-loathing — by the belief that she is a burden to others — not by righteous despair at the treatment she received.

[Critics of the work's representation](https://videogamesmakemehappy.wordpress.com/2021/12/04/a-silent-voice-koe-no-katachi-is-a-terrible-representation-of-the-deaf-community-lets-talk-about-why/) note that Shoko's [internalized ableism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)) — her belief that she is inherently burdensome, her willingness to absorb blame for her own victimization — is presented in the narrative as a personal character trait rather than a product of systemic deafening culture. The manga does not interrogate why she has been taught to feel this way. It uses her self-effacement as emotional texture: she is generous, patient, suffering, forgiving. She is, in the specific tradition that disability scholars have named, a "[supercrip](https://dsq-sds.org/article/id/1493/)" — a disabled character who demonstrates moral virtue precisely through her relationship to her disability, and whose extraordinary capacity for forgiveness and patience exists to inspire non-disabled characters rather than to define her as a person.

This framing serves the narrative's actual interest. A Shoko who was angry, resentful, and difficult would be a Shoko who cannot be saved. She would demand something from the story rather than receiving it. The manga's emotional logic requires Shoko to be available for Shoya's redemption, and the way she is available is by having already done the hardest emotional work herself — the forgiveness that Shoya hasn't yet earned.

Oima's design intent explains this. If deafness is a "motif" for communicative difficulty, then Shoko's passivity and forgiveness are the motif's payload: the story needs a character who has been failed by human communication and who nevertheless reaches toward it. Shoko's inability to communicate easily, and her persistence in trying despite repeated failures, is the image of the theme. Her anger would obscure the image. So the anger is gone.

## What the Consultation Could Not Fix

It is worth returning to the JFD consultation to understand its limits. An institutional endorsement of this kind can ensure accuracy: that the JSL depicted is correct, that the practical experience of deafness in Japanese school systems is rendered faithfully, that nothing in the work repeats false claims about Deaf people's capabilities or limitations. [The Arcadia University analysis of deaf representation](https://scholarworks.arcadia.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1039&context=senior_theses) notes that the anime film's sign language is among the most accurately rendered in any mainstream Japanese production — a genuine achievement.

What institutional consultation cannot fix is the question of whose story this is and why. The JFD could confirm that the JSL in the panels was accurate and that the educational environment depicted was recognizable. They could not, and presumably were not asked to, address whether the authorial design decision — deafness as motif, not subject — produced a character who reproduced the very Othering that the manga was meant to critique.

This is not a cynical observation about the JFD. It is an observation about what institutional processes do and don't do. Consultation produces accuracy. It does not produce authenticity. Authenticity — in this context, a Deaf character with full interiority, contradictions, anger, and desires that exist independently of her relationship to a hearing protagonist's growth — requires a different kind of authorial decision at the design stage. One that Oima, by her own description, did not make.

## What the Work Is Doing Instead

The most charitable reading of *Koe no Katachi* — and I think it is the correct one — is that it is a manga about the specific shape of guilt in someone who has caused harm and knows it. Oima wanted to explore what it costs to watch, to actually see the person you have hurt, and to keep watching without flinching. Shoya's [X's over other people's faces](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Koe_no_Katachi_(Manga)) are the manga's best image: the way people protect themselves from the cost of genuine attention by refusing to look directly at each other.

This is a real and interesting theme. The manga executes it with considerable skill. The problem is that executing this theme required the use of Shoko Nishimiya in a way that made her a demonstration rather than a character. Every scene in which Shoya learns to look at people is a scene in which Shoko is being looked at. She is the object of the sustained attention the manga teaches. She rarely returns the gaze on her own terms.

[The manga's most common defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)) — that it was well-researched, that it raised awareness of bullying of disabled children, that it treats its subject with unusual seriousness for its genre — is accurate. It is also beside the point. The failure is not one of research or sincerity. It is a design failure that follows directly from the authorial intent Oima described: using a deaf person's experience as "one of the motifs" to illuminate something the author found more interesting than that person's experience.

## The Question It Leaves

Oima's next major work was [*To Your Eternity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Your_Eternity), a manga about an immortal being that takes different physical forms — including human ones — and observes humanity from outside. Where *Koe no Katachi* placed a hearing boy at the center and used a Deaf girl's experience to illuminate his emotional life, *To Your Eternity* makes the observer's distance the subject itself — the Beholder learns what it means to feel human loss by experiencing the deaths of the humans it has temporarily inhabited. Oima is interested in the same question across both works: what does it cost to actually encounter another person's pain? The later manga's innovation is to locate this question in a perspective that is explicitly non-human, which removes the need for a human character to be flattened into a motif. The problem in *Koe no Katachi* was not that Oima lacked the capacity for this kind of thinking. It was that she hadn't yet found the structure that could hold it without requiring Shoko to carry it alone.

This suggests that the problem was not indifference to representation but a specific limit in how Oima understood the story she was telling. She was writing about communicative failure from the inside — from Shoya's position as a person whose shame had closed him off from other people. The story needed a person on the other side of that closure. Shoko filled the role.

The gap between what Shoko needed to be for Shoya's story and what Shoko might have been if she were the story's subject is *Koe no Katachi*'s real silence. It is not the silence of deafness. It is the silence of a person who was written to be useful rather than whole.
