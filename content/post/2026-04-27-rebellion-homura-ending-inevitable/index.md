Gen Urobuchi's original ending for *Rebellion* was simple and clean: Ultimate Madoka, having sensed that Homura had been trapped inside a labyrinth of her own unconscious making, would appear at the film's close and take Homura into the Law of Cycles. Homura's obsession would end in the way it was always supposed to — not with Homura winning, but with Homura being freed. She would finally get to be with Madoka. The story would close.

[Akiyuki Shinbo refused this.](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Gen_Urobuchi) Producer Iwakami refused it too. The franchise needed to continue. Urobuchi was struggling with what story remained, and Shinbo's suggestion — "just make Madoka and Homura into enemies" — became the breakthrough that produced the film's actual ending: Homura absorbing the grief of every magical girl in existence, seizing Madoka's divine power, tearing the universe apart, and rebuilding it according to her own design. Devil Homura. A world where Madoka is human, present, and reachable, but no longer God.

The [fan community has never settled on an answer](https://www.resetera.com/threads/do-you-think-homura-akemi-was-right-to-do-what-she-did-at-the-end-of-madoka-magica-rebellion-major-spoilers-for-madoka-magica.624895/) — the debate still runs a decade later. The debate about whether Homura's act was love or violation has been running since 2013 and shows no signs of resolution. Tumblr meta, [wiki speculation pages](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Community:Rebellion), and dedicated "Homura Did Nothing Wrong" fandoms all orbit the same unresolvable question. Urobuchi himself has confirmed that the morality was left deliberately ambiguous.

What almost no one says is this: the ending that came from commercial necessity is more honest to who Homura is than the ending her author intended. The mercy she was offered is the mercy she could never have accepted.

---

## What Thirty Loops Taught Us

To understand why Urobuchi's original ending was wrong for the story he'd already told, you have to go back to the TV series and think about what Homura has been doing for thirty-plus timeline iterations.

Homura's wish — to go back and protect Madoka, to re-encounter her as a guardian instead of a burden — has a surface purpose (protect Madoka) and a deeper one that the series spends twelve episodes revealing. She says she wants Madoka to survive. What she actually cannot tolerate is a world in which Madoka is absent. Every loop she runs, Madoka dies or contracts or is destroyed, and Homura runs the loop again — not because she thinks she can succeed, but because the alternative to running the loop is accepting a reality without Madoka in it. [Urobuchi confirmed at a Nitro+ panel that Homura went through "approaching 100" timelines](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Homura_Akemi), each roughly a month long — meaning she has relived the same events across approximately eight years of subjective time. She has been doing this instead of letting go.

The Law of Cycles is the universe's merciful offer to end this loop. Madoka has transcended into a divine presence that erases the suffering of magical girls at the moment of their deaths. This means Homura, when she eventually dies, will be taken by Madoka. This is the happy ending from a certain angle: Homura's suffering ends, she joins Madoka, the obsession terminates in fulfillment. Urobuchi wanted Rebellion to deliver this ending. Madoka would appear, see what Homura's grief had created, and take her home.

The problem is that this ending requires Homura to accept it. And Homura doesn't accept things. She has never accepted any version of "Madoka is safe now, she just isn't here." The Law of Cycles would give Homura what she says she wants. It would not give her what she actually wants. What she actually wants is Madoka alive and present and capable of being talked to and held and mundanely annoyed. Death and ascension don't satisfy this. Transcendence doesn't satisfy this. She wants Madoka as a person, not as a mercy-delivering cosmic principle.

This is the psychological truth that Urobuchi's planned ending would have smoothed over. He would have let the obsession resolve gracefully. The character he'd actually written would not have allowed that.

## The Ending Shinbo Made

When Shinbo suggested making Madoka and Homura into enemies, what he intuited — perhaps without being able to articulate it this way — was that Homura's love for Madoka is not compatible with Madoka's happiness as a transcendent being. The two things are in direct conflict. Madoka's sacrifice was the correct ethical action and resulted in the elimination of suffering for magical girls across all timelines. Homura thinks it was wrong. Not strategically wrong or practically wrong, but cosmologically wrong — the universe should not work this way, Madoka should not have had to become this, the correct response to Madoka's sacrifice is not gratitude but rage.

[Devil Homura's act](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Devil_Homura) is not a corruption of her love. It is the logical endpoint of what that love has always been: an insistence that Madoka as a present, touchable, mortal person is worth more than any abstract good Madoka-as-God might accomplish. She literally tears a god in half to get the human back. This is precisely what Homura has been trying to do across every timeline — not save the world, not optimize outcomes, but preserve a specific person's specific presence.

[The pamphlet interview material from Urobuchi](https://feral-phoenix.livejournal.com/685568.html) reveals that he ultimately accepted the ending as a completion of Homura's arc. His statement — "I feel like I've written all there is to Madoka in the TV series and now I've written all there is to Homura in this movie; I feel like I've had both of them graduate" — is important. He did not say he'd been forced to write an ending that felt false. He said the ending completed the character. He found the truth inside the commercial compromise.

What Urobuchi recognized, retroactively, is that Devil Homura is not a new Homura. She is the same Homura who has been running [those nearly hundred loops](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Madoka_Magica_Timeline), finally given access to the power they were building toward. The ascension ending would have had Homura receive mercy from outside. The actual ending has Homura refuse mercy from outside, as she has always done, and create her own solution, as she has always tried to do. The tragedy is not that Homura "fell." The tragedy is that she was always going to do this, and the universe offering her a graceful exit didn't change that.

## The Villain The Show Earned

The controversy about whether Homura is a "villain" in Rebellion misunderstands the film's structure. [Urobuchi has confirmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homura_Akemi) that he intended the morality to remain ambiguous — he "hates black and white morality and prefers to leave things up for fan interpretations." But this ambiguity is not simply neutral. The film has a position. It just makes you work to find it.

Devil Homura's new world is not straightforwardly worse. Mami is alive. Sayaka is alive. Nagisa is alive. Madoka doesn't know what she gave up, but she's happy. The people Homura loves are present and unharmed. If the goal was "Homura creates a world with as little suffering as possible," she succeeded. The question the film refuses to answer is whether the cost — Madoka's erasure of her own divine will and purpose — is justified by the happiness of the world Homura built on top of it.

What the film does instead of answering this is show you Homura's face. In the final moments of the film, after the Devil persona is fully established and the new world is stable, [Homura begins to cry](https://madoka.fandom.com/wiki/Devil_Homura). She knows exactly what she has done. She is not pleased with herself. She is not triumphant. She has achieved everything she wanted and she recognizes that achieving it required her to become its opposite. Her love for Madoka was the reason she couldn't let Madoka go. And her love for Madoka is the reason she understands, immediately, that what she's done to Madoka is not love.

This is the ending the TV series never gave us — not because the TV series was incomplete, but because the TV series was about Madoka, and this film is about Homura. [The TV series ended with a sacrifice.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puella_Magi_Madoka_Magica) The film ends with a different kind of loss: a character finally getting everything she ever wanted, and in the moment of getting it, feeling what it cost. That is a harder emotional space than sacrifice, and it required a "betrayal ending" to reach it.

## What Urobuchi's Original Ending Would Have Cost

If Urobuchi had gotten his way — Madoka appearing, Homura being gently taken into the Law of Cycles — the film would have been graceful and emotionally satisfying and much less interesting.

The graceful ending would have confirmed the TV series' premise: sacrifice is the right response to a broken universe, and the people who accept that gracefully are the ones the universe rewards. Homura would have ended up rewarded, absorbed into Madoka's presence, her obsession resolved by external mercy rather than internal action. The film would have said: the loops were worth it; she gets to rest now.

What the actual ending says is harder and more truthful: the loops were not worth it, not because they failed but because they produced a version of Homura that cannot accept the graceful exit even when it's offered. The universe, in the form of Madoka-as-God, was offering Homura everything. Homura took more than she was offered, and in doing so, revealed that "everything" was never the right category. She wanted something specific and irreplaceable, and she was willing to become whatever was necessary to have it.

[CBR's analysis](https://www.cbr.com/madoka-magica-rebellion-betrayed-iconic-magical-girl-anime/) frames this as Rebellion "betraying the spirit" of the original series. I think this is backwards. Rebellion betrays the comfort of the original series' ending, which is different. The original series' ending is emotionally satisfying and philosophically seductive — sacrifice is meaningful, the universe recognizes it, things are better now. Rebellion looks at the person who generated that sacrifice and asks: but what about her? What did the universe's better state cost her specifically, in her specific psychology, and what happens when someone with that psychology finally has enough power to push back?

![Madoka and Homura in the final confrontation of Rebellion](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1/86337.jpg)

The answer is Devil Homura. And the answer is correct.

## The Remaining Question

Urobuchi said he felt both characters had "graduated" after their respective stories. The graduation was not final. [*Walpurgisnacht: Rising*](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Puella_Magi_Madoka_Magica_the_Movie:_%E2%80%94Walpurgisnacht:_Rising%E2%80%94) — the [long-delayed sequel film](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-08-16/madoka-magica-walpurgisnacht-rising-anime-film-delayed-to-winter-2025/.214368) co-directed by Shinbo, with Urobuchi returning as scriptwriter — is scheduled for release in August 2026. Devil Homura presumably continues with it.

What the sequel inherits is the unresolved question *Rebellion* was too honest to answer: the new world is stable, but Madoka does not remember choosing it, and Homura does not forget what she did to get it. What [the Anime Feminist analysis correctly identifies](https://www.animefeminist.com/against-the-world-madoka-rebellion-saviorism-and-abolitionist-schooling/) as the film's central problem — that Homura's love is a form of saviorism, a conviction that she knows better than Madoka what Madoka needs — will need to be addressed, vindicated, or dismantled.

*Rebellion* was made because Shinbo suggested an ending more honest than the author's, and it turned out better than it had any right to. Whether the sequel can do the same thing twice is the question the franchise is now staked on.
