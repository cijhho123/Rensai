The most revealing thing Hideaki Anno said about the Rebuild of Evangelion didn't come from a director's statement or a production diary. It came from rewatching his own show. According to Khara producer Toshimichi Otsuki, writing in [Newtype USA's December 2006 issue](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/2053/Newtype-USA-December-2006-Eva-transcript/), Anno watched the entire original *Neon Genesis Evangelion* back to back in preparation for the remake — and came out the other side saying: *"This show really is interesting, isn't it? I never realized how interesting it was."*

After a decade spent reexamining and re-explaining and publicly breaking down over the franchise, Anno was getting to [remake it the way he always wanted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebuild_of_Evangelion). [Studio Khara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khara_(studio)), which he founded in 2006 specifically to make these films, would give him the resources and control he never had at Gainax — no budget collapsing mid-run, no scripts rewritten week-to-week, no episodes animated by borrowed staff on borrowed time.

A year later, *Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone* arrived in Japanese theaters. And Anno's dream version of the story he first told in 1995 looks almost exactly like the original.

That's not a criticism. It's the most interesting thing about the film.

## The Freedom to Repeat Yourself

The production history of the original NGE has become its own mythology: the [budget running out by episode 13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion), [scripts rewritten week-to-week as Gainax teetered](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/TroubledProduction/NeonGenesisEvangelion), the number of Angels dropped from 28 to 17, the final two episodes retreating into still frames and voice recordings. The story that surrounds the show says: Anno made something extraordinary *despite* his circumstances.

What 1.0 suggests is more complicated. It says: the circumstances broke things Anno could fix — the animation quality, some of the pacing, the visual execution of specific sequences — but the story itself was never what was broken.

Consider what actually changes in 1.0 versus the first six episodes of NGE. [According to the EvaWiki differences page](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Differences_between_Evangelion_1.0_and_Neon_Genesis_Evangelion), the alterations are largely cosmetic and emotional: Sachiel's blood is now red instead of blue. Shamshel is more serpentine. Shinji's motivation for piloting is shifted — he gets into Unit-01 because Rei is injured and helpless in front of him, not because the show's staging maneuvers him there through falling ceiling lights. Misato knows more, explains more, earlier. The script is cleaned up. The production values are dramatically higher.

The skeleton is identical. The arc — boy summoned to giant robot, boy refuses, boy sees wounded girl, boy caves, boy fights three Angels, boy saves wounded girl, boy learns nothing comforting about any of it — proceeds beat for beat as it did in 1995.

## What Ramiel Actually Proves

The film's centerpiece is Operation Yashima, the battle against the Fifth Angel. In the original series, Ramiel is [a floating blue octahedron](https://wiki.evageeks.org/ramiel) that fires a particle beam. Haunting and strange, but static. In 1.0, it becomes something genuinely alien: a shapeshifting crystalline entity that reconfigures itself geometrically, screams, [vocalizes in whale-like tones when wounded](https://www.cbr.com/evangelion-original-rebuild-differences/), and reduces Tokyo-3's entire conventional military to scrap before Shinji's first shot is even lined up.

The scene became the benchmark argument for why the Rebuild exists. [Fans who considered themselves lukewarm on 1.0 as a whole would still defend the Yashima sequence](https://sakurasunrise.blog/2019/08/27/what-do-the-evangelion-rebuilds-do-better/). It's the single clearest demonstration of what Anno meant by "no longer constrained by technological limitations" — a sequence that works emotionally in both versions but is only fully *realized* in the second one.

But the same logic applies in reverse. The reason Ramiel is worth upgrading is because the *context* around it — Shinji's reluctance, Rei's sacrifice of her body, the stakes of failure — was already structurally sound. Anno didn't redesign the scene's emotional architecture. He redesigned the stage. The scaffold was always right.

## The Trojan Horse at the End of the Film

What makes 1.0 more than a high-budget restoration project are the moments where it quietly stops being one.

The film opens on a blood-red ocean. [This is the first signal](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=9600): in the original series, Second Impact left the seas red, but that visual read differently in 1995 than it does in 2007, after *End of Evangelion* showed audiences a world dissolved into LCL on a crimson shore. The opening frame of 1.0 carries a memory that the original didn't have. Anno is not just rebuilding — he is referencing.

Then, in the final sequence, Kaworu Nagisa rises from a coffin on the lunar surface. He speaks to SEELE monoliths in a scene [the EvaWiki describes as "entirely new" and "unlike anything in the original series"](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Evangelion_1.0:_You_Are_(Not)_Alone). Behind him: a restrained giant that resembles Lilith. He says he cannot wait to meet Shinji. His tone is not anticipation. It's something closer to obligation.

This scene ignited [the "sequel theory" that would follow the Rebuild films for years](https://www.neogaf.com/threads/that-theory-surrounding-the-evangelion-rebuild-series-spoilers.1186274/): the idea, later essentially confirmed in 3.0+1.0, that Kaworu carries memories of previous iterations, that this is not a remake at all but a continuation disguised as one. [EvaGeeks forum discussions from 2007 onward](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=20961) document how the community pieced this together almost immediately, even before 2.0 changed everything. Fascinatingly, producer Otsuki had told Newtype USA that ["the plot is completely different"](https://www.evamonkey.com/transcript-of-newtype-usa-article/) from the original — a statement that, in hindsight, was truer than the audience understood when 1.0 looked like a faithful remake. He just wasn't describing 1.0.

He was telling you that he was lying. Not to deceive you — to set up the only version of this story he thought could actually end.

## What It Reveals About NGE

Here's what 1.0 actually argues, in the language of every choice Anno made: the story he told in 1995, the structure he built under circumstances that were collapsing around him, was right. Given full creative control, unlimited budget, a purpose-built studio, and a decade of perspective, Hideaki Anno produced an opening act that is — structurally, emotionally, thematically — almost identical to the one he produced in crisis.

That matters more than it might seem. The dominant critical narrative around NGE positions it as a work that succeeded *in spite of* its production collapse, as something accidentally transcendent. 1.0 complicates that reading. It suggests Anno was doing what he intended all along, at least through Act One. The budget chaos broke his ending, not his beginning. When he finally got to fix it, he fixed the execution and left the blueprints intact.

[Letterboxd reviews of 1.0](https://letterboxd.com/film/evangelion-10-you-are-not-alone/) are instructive in their split: new viewers often found it thin (98 minutes of compressed exposition can't build the depth 13 episodes achieves), while existing fans sometimes found it redundant — [one critic calling it a "worse-off 'better animated' version" without meaningful additions](https://cinematicdoctrine.com/2024/09/21/evangelion-1-11-you-are-not-alone-a-needless-revamp/). Both responses are fair. Both are reading for the wrong thing.

1.0 is not primarily a story. It's a document. It's Anno, in possession of everything he lacked in 1995, affirming that the foundation he laid then was load-bearing. The changes he made are a form of annotation — marking what he wanted to clarify, upgrade, or quietly undermine. What he left alone is the real statement.

And then, in its final thirty seconds, the film reveals that it was never quite a remake. It was always the beginning of a goodbye.

*Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone* [grossed approximately ¥2 billion in Japanese theaters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_1.0_You_Are_(Not)_Alone) and generated significant home video revenue — the commercial bedrock that allowed Anno to fund three more films. Otsuki had [stated the goal was to close the door on the post-Eva era for good](https://www.evamonkey.com/transcript-of-newtype-usa-article/). Anno's method for doing that was first to prove that what he'd built was sound — and only then to tear it apart.

The experiment worked. Anno got to see how his story played when built right, and then spent three more films burning it down on purpose.
