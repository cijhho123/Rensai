At a specific moment in [*Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_2.0_You_Can_(Not)_Advance), the Shiro Sagisu orchestral score drops away and a woman's voice begins to sing. The song is "[Tsubasa wo Kudasai](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Tsubasa_wo_Kudasai)" — "Please Give Me Wings." Unit-01 is charging toward the Angel that has absorbed Rei's soul. Shinji is screaming her name. The sky turns blood red. The Eva's eyes ignite. And over all of it, this gentle, familiar melody.

For viewers outside Japan, the scene reads as strange — an effective piece of soundtrack dissonance, the kind of jarring musical choice Hideaki Anno has always favored. For Japanese viewers, the effect is different and more specific. They've sung this song. [In choir textbooks, at graduation ceremonies, in elementary and junior high schools throughout Japan since the mid-1970s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsubasa_o_Kudasai) — "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is one of the most deeply embedded collective musical memories in modern Japanese culture. Anno didn't just choose a gentle song to contrast with apocalypse. He chose *the* song that a generation of Japanese viewers sung together at the moments when childhood was ending.

The choice is the most precise formal decision in the film — the moment where 2.0's manipulation of its audience is most complete, and most honest about what it's doing.

## The Song and What It Carries

["Tsubasa wo Kudasai" was composed in 1970 by Kunihiko Murai](https://skdesu.com/en/you-know-music-tsubasa-wo-kudasai), debuted at the Nemu Popular Festival in Shima, Mie Prefecture, and became a folk-pop song that would spend the next fifty years in Japanese schools. The publisher Kyouiku Geijutsu-sha included it in music textbooks, first for high school students in 1976, then junior high schools in 1978, and eventually elementary school materials in 1996. By the time Evangelion 2.0 premiered in 2009, multiple generations of Japanese students had sung this song together in class, at school events, and at graduation ceremonies.

The lyrics are about wanting freedom from sorrow. The narrator asks for wings to fly above the earth, to reach a world without sadness, to escape the weight of ordinary life. It's a song about the desire for transcendence — not violent or dramatic transcendence, but the specific bittersweet transcendence of childhood ending, of standing at the threshold of something new. In Japan's school calendar, it carries the emotional register of final moments: the last gathering before everyone scatters, the ritual acknowledgment that something is ending.

This is the song Anno chose to play while a fourteen-year-old boy inadvertently begins the apocalypse by trying to save someone he loves.

## What the Scene Is Doing

The [Near Third Impact sequence](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Near_Third_Impact) is structurally designed to feel earned. The whole film has been engineering this moment: the dinner party (Rei learning to want things), Asuka's defeat, the Dummy System's brutality, Shinji's paralysis replaced by action. When Shinji finally charges toward the Angel protecting Rei, the audience has been positioned — through forty minutes of crowd-pleasing action cinema — to want this. The film [has spent its second act becoming the more accessible, more heroic, more satisfying version of Evangelion](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Evangelion_2.0:_You_Can_(Not)_Advance) that audiences always claimed to want.

Then "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" begins, and something shifts. Unit-01 goes to Beast Mode: the restraints fall away, the eyes change, the machine becomes something primal and terrifying. Then it grows wings — [literal wings of light, identical to those seen during Second Impact](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Wings_of_Light) — and rises toward the Angel. Misato, watching, recognizes them. The lyrics say *give me wings*. The Eva grows wings. The song's desire has been literally fulfilled, and the fulfillment is triggering the end of the world.

![Unit-01 in near-transcendent state during the Near Third Impact — the visual language of the scene is ascension, not catastrophe](https://wondersinthedark.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/01/engelion2_3.jpg)

The song's graduation register does something specific to the emotional experience of this scene: it reframes what's happening as a transition rather than a disaster. Graduation songs frame endings as bittersweet but necessary — the loss of the familiar is acknowledged, but as a condition of growth, not as pure destruction. When Anno scores Near Third Impact with a song the audience has sung at such moments, he primes them to receive the apocalypse through that filter. It's not catastrophe. It's a graduation. One phase ending, another beginning. The world Shinji has known is ending because he chose — genuinely, sincerely, not out of paralysis but out of love — to reach for something.

[The EvaGeeks community noted this directly](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=11424&start=20): the juxtaposition between the gentle song and the apocalyptic imagery would be "totally lost to viewers outside Japan if they did not know the background." Anno knew who he was talking to. The Rebuild project was always in dialogue with its Japanese fanbase first — with the generation that had grown up with NGE, that had spent fifteen years asking for a more hopeful ending. "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" addresses that audience specifically, in a language only they share.

## The Specificity of Folk Memory

What makes this choice different from ordinary soundtrack dissonance is that folk memory operates below deliberate thought. When a Japanese viewer hears "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" in the context of a school ceremony — which most have, repeatedly — they don't process it as a cultural artifact. They feel it as a bodily memory: the specific emotion that belongs to endings, to thresholds, to the specific bittersweet quality of standing in a gym in March knowing the year is over. That response doesn't disappear when the song appears in a film. It accompanies the scene without being invited.

Anno is not simply choosing a gentle song to contrast with violence. He's triggering that associative response in his audience and redirecting it toward the scene's content. The audience doesn't choose to feel the Near Third Impact as graduation-like; they feel it that way because the song makes them feel it that way, before they can think about whether that framing is accurate. [The Rebuild project's stated theme of healing and growing up](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2021-08-15/growing-up-with-evangelion/.176258) — announced explicitly in Anno's post-3.0+1.0 statements — is embedded in this scene at the level of musical experience: this is what growing up sounds like to an entire generation of Japanese viewers.

This is the manipulation's depth. [The Rebuild films have multiple layers of audience engineering](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:Third_Impact_(NTE)): the action-cinema pleasures of 2.0's middle section, the deliberate satisfaction of watching "active Shinji" do what NGE-Shinji wouldn't, the emotional architecture of the dinner party. All of these engineer the audience's complicity in the Near Third Impact. "Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is the final layer — the one that works not through narrative or genre but through the body's stored emotional responses to collective song.

![The film's visual scale during the climactic sequence — apocalypse presented with the emotional register of bittersweet departure](https://wondersinthedark.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/01/engelion2_1.jpg)

## When the Song Stops

"Tsubasa wo Kudasai" is still playing when [Kaworu descends with the Lance of Cassius](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Kaworu_Nagisa), halts the process, and declares — in the film's final ninety seconds — that he will bring happiness "this time." The Near Third Impact ends. The song ends. The world is still there.

The graduation that was beginning doesn't happen. But the audience was brought all the way to the threshold of it, feeling what it was going to feel like — bittersweet, earned, the right kind of ending for a long childhood — before the film pulls them back and says: not yet. Not this way.

Anno's relationship to his source material in the Rebuild project is the relationship of someone who understands exactly how his audience is constructed. He helped build that audience; he knows what they've been asking for; he knows the emotional logic they're operating under. [The choice of "Tsubasa wo Kudasai"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsubasa_o_Kudasai) is the most precise expression of that knowledge: a decision that only works if the filmmaker and the audience share the same cultural touchstone, the same folk memory, the same song.

The wings the song asks for are the same wings the Eva grows. The world of sadness the song wants to escape is the same world Shinji has been living in since episode one. The graduation the song implies is the one the Rebuild films are working toward. Anno knows this. He performed it back at you.
