In *End of Evangelion*, Ritsuko Akagi reprograms the Magi supercomputer for self-destruction. The three biocomputers — Melchior, Balthasar, Casper — are her mother Naoko's mind, partitioned and encoded into silicon when Naoko completed them sixteen years earlier. Each one is a different face of the same woman: Naoko as scientist (Melchior), Naoko as mother (Balthasar), Naoko as woman (Casper).

Two of them obey Ritsuko. Casper does not.

[Casper is the part of Naoko that loved Gendo Ikari](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Ritsuko_Akagi). And Naoko-as-woman, encoded in glass and circuitry, chose Gendo over her daughter even in death. What Ritsuko sees in that moment — her mother's ghost, through the machine her mother built, refusing to let her escape — is the precise form her tragedy takes. She was not simply betrayed by a man. She was betrayed by the part of her mother she spent her whole life trying to excise from herself.

This is why Ritsuko Akagi is Neon Genesis Evangelion's most complete portrait of inherited psychological destruction. The analysis that follows focuses on the original TV series (1995–96) and End of Evangelion (1997); Rebuild handles her differently and that's a separate conversation.

## The Woman Who Built Herself Into a Machine

Naoko Akagi was [NERV's founding chief scientist](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Naoko_Akagi), the creator of the Magi biocomputer system and the architect of the Evangelion project before her death. The Magi are her most enduring work: three supercomputers that don't run standard code but contain a digitized transcription of Naoko's personality, partitioned into its three primary aspects. She described them as her three faces in constant negotiation — the scientist seeking truth, the mother seeking to nurture, the woman seeking love. The struggle between them is rendered architectural: the three units sometimes disagree, which is the point. Naoko was trying to make something that thought the way she did.

Then Naoko strangled the first Rei Ayanami.

[The child had told her](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Naoko_Akagi) — almost certainly on Gendo's instigation — that Gendo called Naoko an "old hag," no longer useful. Naoko killed the child in a moment of rage, then threw herself from the Magi. She had known Gendo was using her. She continued anyway. When Gendo's cruelty was finally made undeniable, she killed not him but the thing he loved more than her — and then herself.

The Magi are what remain. They are not a monument to Naoko's scientific achievement. They are the architecture of a mind that destroyed itself, preserved in functioning hardware.

## The Daughter Who Dyed Her Hair

Ritsuko [dyed her naturally dark hair blonde](https://cbr.com/neon-genesis-evangelion-trivia-facts-ritsuko-akagi/) as a deliberate act of differentiation from her mother. She would not be Naoko. She would not be the woman who gave everything to a project and a man who would discard her.

She became Naoko.

[The relationship to Gendo](https://ophanimkei.com/media/ritsuko) began before NERV — a schoolgirl crush that matured into a professional partnership, then an affair, then something Ritsuko herself described, in one of the series' most unsparing lines, as leaving her no longer happy "even when you make love to me." She knew precisely what she was doing. [Fan analysis of her character](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=16139) consistently notes this: her tragedy is not ignorance but awareness. She knew Gendo had used her mother. She knew he was using her as a substitute for her mother — and for Yui. She continued.

The scientific-minded Ritsuko hated the "woman" part of her mother's mind most specifically. She described Casper with contempt: the irrational component, the one governed by feeling rather than data. And yet Casper is the part of Naoko that prevented Ritsuko from dying on her own terms in End of Evangelion. The woman who despised her mother's feminine weakness could not outrun it because it was coded into the very infrastructure of her professional existence.

What distinguishes Ritsuko from NGE's other psychologically damaged characters is this visibility. [Hideaki Anno's series](https://opus.ing/posts/how-hideaki-anno-depression-influenced-evangelion) builds its entire cast from people who cannot confront themselves — Shinji's passivity is an inability to know what he wants; Asuka's arrogance is terror of being seen; Gendo converts his grief into institutional power so successfully he almost passes as purposeful. Ritsuko mostly keeps working. She shows up. She does the science. She continues the relationship she knows is destroying her. The difference is that she sees herself clearly while doing it, and that clear sight changes nothing.

## The Rei Repetition

Ritsuko's relationship to Rei Ayanami is the sharpest mirror of her mother's. [She destroys the Dummy Plug plant](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Episode:23) — the tank of Rei clones — in Episode 23, in the presence of Shinji and Misato, announcing "I've killed Rei." The act is driven by jealousy of the thing Gendo prefers to her: not Naoko's jealousy of Rei-as-the-first-child but Ritsuko's jealousy of Rei-as-Yui's-vessel, the incarnation of the woman whose memory Gendo actually serves.

The structural parallel to Naoko is exact. Naoko killed Rei because Rei told her Gendo no longer needed her. Ritsuko kills the Rei clones because she can't destroy the thing Gendo actually loves (Yui, and the project that makes Yui real) and destroying the clones is the nearest available substitute. Both Akagi women, at their moment of rage, turn it not toward Gendo but toward Rei. Neither can bring themselves to destroy the object of Gendo's actual devotion. Both choose the easier target.

[This repetition is not accidental](https://adamworu.tumblr.com/post/112958136055/theres-the-three-faces-of-naoko-that-created-the). Anno structured the Akagi women to illustrate how damage replicates across generations without requiring any visible instruction. Naoko didn't teach Ritsuko to love Gendo. Naoko barely parented Ritsuko at all — [the EvaWiki notes](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Ritsuko_Akagi) that Naoko provided "insufficient supervision" during Ritsuko's childhood. The inheritance was structural, not pedagogical. Ritsuko absorbed her mother's psychological architecture by growing up in its shadow and then occupying her mother's professional position, surrounded by everything Naoko built.

## The End and What Casper Means

When Ritsuko attempts to destroy the Magi in End of Evangelion, she is not making a political statement about NERV's ethics. She is trying to take Gendo with her. The same structure as Naoko: she cannot kill herself without also trying to destroy the man who used her, and she cannot destroy him directly, so she targets the systems around him.

Two-thirds of her mother's mind agrees to self-destruct. Balthasar (the mother) agrees. Melchior (the scientist) agrees. But Casper, the part of Naoko that loved Gendo, refuses. [The shocked Ritsuko reads this as her mother choosing Gendo over her daughter one final time](https://adamworu.tumblr.com/post/120035576130/evangelion-ritsuko-is-tragic). The machine her mother built — in which the "mother" component votes for the daughter's escape and the "woman" component votes against it — gives Ritsuko the definitive answer to the question she'd been asking her entire life about which one her mother valued more.

The answer is Gendo. Even in death. Even encoded in hardware. Even at the moment Ritsuko is trying to die.

What makes this scene crushing is its precision. It doesn't just say Naoko was a bad mother. It says Naoko's love for Gendo was so foundational to her psychological architecture that it survived her death, was encoded without caveat into the system she built, and operates exactly as it always did — choosing Gendo over her own child, even when that child needs to be chosen more than she has ever needed anything.

## The Final Scene

Gendo's last words to Ritsuko before he shoots her in the chest are one of End of Evangelion's deliberate mysteries. In the film, his voice becomes inaudible mid-sentence — the line is muted. Anno, however, [privately shared a hint with voice actress Yuriko Yamaguchi](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=270) during recording, and the content of that hint remains secret. Yamaguchi noted that with that single hint, "she and Ritsuko Akagi were utterly defeated." Whatever he said was real enough to devastate the actress delivering it.

The [EvaGeeks forum thread on the subject](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Theory_and_Analysis:Gendo%27s_Silent_Words) offers several theories: "I truly needed you," an apology, a variant of "I love you." All of them land the same way. Ritsuko's response — "Liar" — doesn't depend on which specific words Gendo used. It depends on her complete understanding of the man saying them. She replies to the intent before she can hear the words.

She knew from very early in their relationship that whatever he offered was manufactured for utility, that she was a replacement for her mother who was a replacement for Yui. She says "Liar" not in shock but in confirmation — a final verification of everything she already knew. The scene is not a revelation for Ritsuko. It is a receipt.

Her tragedy is not blindness. It is the specific horror of clear-eyed self-destruction — the knowledge that you are repeating your mother's mistake without being able to stop. She dies having confirmed what she already knew. The "Liar" is not for Gendo's benefit. It's the last line of a private accounting she's been running for years.

## What the Magi Leave Behind

[The EvaGeeks community](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=16139) debates whether Ritsuko is a hero, a villain, or something else. The correct answer is that she is neither — she is the most naturalistic portrait of intergenerational psychological damage in a series that, as [Anno's own account of its production makes clear](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-interview-mental-health/), was built from his own psychology of self-destruction. The characters' wounds are not imagined; they are patterned from the inside. Ritsuko's specific construction — the clear-eyed woman who acts against her own knowledge — is perhaps the most precise of all of them, because she has no convenient excuse of not knowing.

What she cannot do is escape the machine her mother built. Not the Magi specifically, but the whole inheritance: the professional position, the relationship to Gendo, the three-part split between the woman who loves and the woman who knows better. Naoko encoded that split in silicon because it was the truth of who she was. Ritsuko inherited it because she grew up inside it.

The self-destruction command Ritsuko inputs into the Magi is the only time in the series she tries to break the loop. Casper refuses.

The loop continues until Gendo shoots her.
