The most honest thing Sunrider: Liberation Day ever did was add an expansion that lets you travel back in time and stop its own story from happening.

[Re]turn — the third major free update to Liberation Day, released as "Captain's Edition" after the original 2016 launch [burned down in fan backlash](http://evnchronicles.blogspot.com/2018/07/sunrider-liberation-day-review-vn.html) — is labeled explicitly as non-canon. It's not a retcon. It's not a revision. It's a parallel track: the developers, Love in Space, wrote a time-travel story specifically so players who hated what happened in the main game could experience a version where protagonist Kayto Shields prevents those events entirely. The main canon remains intact. The non-canon [Re]turn remains available. Both exist simultaneously.

That's a strange creative position to be in, and it says something real about what went wrong with Liberation Day — and, more broadly, what can go wrong when an OELVN developer tries to graduate from indie strategy hybrid to full cinematic visual novel.

---

## The Franchise That Shouldn't Have Worked

[Sunrider: Mask of Arcadius](https://store.steampowered.com/app/313730/Sunrider_Mask_of_Arcadius/) — the 2014 free-to-play precursor — is an unlikely success story. Love in Space released it at no cost on Steam, and it was [downloaded by around 600,000 players](http://evnchronicles.blogspot.com/2018/07/sunrider-liberation-day-review-vn.html), generating enough goodwill to make a commercial sequel viable. It pulled off a formula that most studios struggle with: meaningful strategy gameplay wrapped in genuine VN storytelling, with enough player choice to make decisions feel consequential. [PC Gamer covered the franchise](https://www.pcgamer.com/gog-adds-a-hot-selection-of-visual-novels-sunrider-mask-of-arcadius-is-free/) as proof that the visual novel format could work in English-language markets. Its [Metacritic reception](https://www.metacritic.com/game/sunrider-mask-of-arcadius/) reflected a community pleasantly surprised by how well the pieces fit together.

What Mask of Arcadius had, implicitly, was a tone that matched its ambitions. It was an English developer doing what English developers do well: iterating on a genre template, keeping the pacing tight, avoiding the pitfalls of over-stylization. The space opera elements were played with genuine enthusiasm rather than reverence.

Then Love in Space got commercial money and a Sekai Project publishing deal, and tried to become something else.

---

## The "Japanization" Decision

EVN Chronicles, reviewing Liberation Day in 2018, used a phrase that stuck: the game was "a quintessence of a 'weeb game' trying to be more Japanese than actual Japanese VNs themselves and ending up laughable in its obvious over-stylisation." [That's a harsh read](http://evnchronicles.blogspot.com/2018/07/sunrider-liberation-day-review-vn.html), but it's pointed at something real. Liberation Day launched with Japanese voice acting, a bombastic Japanese-style theme song, animated cutscenes, and production values clearly aimed at signaling *this is a serious commercial visual novel now*. The strategy gameplay remained largely intact. The VN layer was overhauled to feel more like what Japanese players would recognize from the genre's domestic tradition.

The problem: the tradition it was imitating wasn't built on player agency. High-production Japanese visual novels — your Fate/stay nights, your Clanads — typically exercise strong authorial control. The player's "choices" are often route selectors or approval trackers, not genuine forks in the narrative logic. That works because those games are written with that model in mind from the start. The protagonist's actions carry the story; the reader experiences them.

Mask of Arcadius had trained its audience to expect something different. Choices in that game felt like they had weight — not always mechanically, but in tone. The sequel's job was to honor that expectation while escalating the stakes.

Instead, Liberation Day shipped with a story that was, as players and critics immediately recognized, "[more linear than its predecessor, with no control over character actions](https://sunrider.fandom.com/wiki/Sunrider:_Liberation_Day)." The protagonist's romance was predetermined. The narrative beats were fixed. And the ending — a cliffhanger that [nullified much of what players had worked toward](https://steamcommunity.com/app/358750/discussions/0/405693392918389582/) — landed like a slap.

## The Chigara Problem

The specific crystallization of Liberation Day's agency failure was the forced romance with Chigara Lynn Ashada. [From the Steam discussions](https://steamcommunity.com/app/358750/discussions/0/405693392927039565/) to the Love in Space forums, the same complaint surfaced repeatedly: most of the anger at Liberation Day came down to the lack of choice in the romance options, with the Chigara route imposed on the player regardless of what they'd done in the previous game.

What makes this thornier than standard "VN railroading" is that it was structurally intentional. Love in Space had [apparently planned to have each game give intimacy scenes with a different crew member](https://sunrider.fandom.com/wiki/Sunrider:_Liberation_Day) — Ava for Mask of Arcadius, Chigara for Liberation Day, Sola hinted for the next entry — meaning the forced Chigara arc wasn't a writing mistake but a deliberate design choice to keep each game focused on one character's arc. The problem is that this model treats the relationship system as episodic content rather than as a meaningful expression of who the player's Kayto actually is.

When players arrived in Liberation Day having cultivated relationships with other crew members in the first game, being locked into a predetermined romance felt like the developers weren't interested in who *they* thought Kayto was. They were experiencing someone else's idea of the protagonist. And the betrayal built into that story — [Chigara is revealed to be an unwitting Prototype agent](https://sunrider.fandom.com/wiki/Chigara_Lynn_Ashada), manipulated from birth — deepened the sense of helplessness. The player couldn't choose who Kayto loved, and then watched that love used as the mechanism of his greatest defeat.

The [TV Tropes analysis page](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Analysis/Sunrider) for the game notes that Liberation Day deconstructs Kayto's heroism specifically — that his need to be the decisive captain is systematically undermined. Whether intentional or not, this thematic element bled into the player experience in ways the developers hadn't accounted for.

## Two Patches and a Time Machine

Love in Space's response to the backlash was rapid. [Version 2.0](https://sunrider.fandom.com/wiki/Sunrider:_Liberation_Day) added roughly 20-30 minutes of post-ending content and several new choices, addressing the most complained-about gaps. The game was rebranded to "Captain's Edition." Version 3.0 added [Re]turn — the non-canon time-travel expansion that lets Kayto prevent the main story's events.

[Re]turn is well-reviewed. [The EVN Chronicles review](http://evnchronicles.blogspot.com/2018/07/sunrider-liberation-day-review-vn.html) describes it as providing the satisfying VN closure the main campaign lacked. It has multiple endings. It has genuine choices. It has the feel of a game where the developer understood what the audience came for. The irony is that [Re]turn is a better visual novel than Liberation Day, created specifically because Liberation Day wasn't the visual novel it needed to be — and the developer was aware enough to know that, and talented enough to make the correction, but unwilling to actually apply that correction to the canonical text.

What they could not do — or chose not to do — was revise the main story. The events of Liberation Day remain Liberation Day. What happened happened. [Re]turn exists as a pressure valve: players who couldn't accept the original ending get an exit. Players who want the "real" story can pretend [Re]turn is it, while the officially recognized canon sits untouched.

## What This Tells You About OELVNs

Liberation Day's specific failure mode is worth naming because it's not unique to Love in Space. OELVN developers occupy an awkward position: they're working in a Japanese-coded medium, often trying to match Japanese production values, but their audience frequently has different expectations about player agency that come from Western game design sensibilities. The Japanese VN tradition is fine with railroading because it grew up in a different context — one where the experience is primarily literary, where the author's vision is the thing being communicated.

Western players, even those who genuinely love the VN format, often want at minimum the *feeling* that their choices matter. [As Den of Geek noted in their survey of the OELVN rise](https://www.denofgeek.com/games/the-rise-of-the-western-visual-novel/), Western developers tend to foreground interactivity in a way their Japanese counterparts don't — and this creates an expectation gap when a Western studio imports the Japanese linear-narrative model without importing the cultural scaffolding that makes readers comfortable with it. The backlash that hit Liberation Day mirrors this: player investment was built on an implied promise of consequence that the writing then broke.

The problem isn't that Love in Space made a railroad visual novel. It's that they made one while keeping the scaffolding of a choices-matter system and while trading on the goodwill of an audience primed for player agency by the first game. The mismatch between form and function was the crack in the foundation.

[Sunrider 4: The Captain's Return](https://store.steampowered.com/app/2251620/Sunrider_4_The_Captains_Return/), the 2022 sequel, reportedly learned from this — building on Liberation Day's setup while expanding the choice and consequence systems. The [Fuwanovel review](https://forums.fuwanovel.moe/blogs/entry/3856-sunrider-4-the-captain%E2%80%99s-return-review-%E2%80%93-a-glorious-return/) notes that Love in Space wasn't entirely happy with the rushed additions they'd made to Liberation Day and trimmed back elements that didn't fit their long-term vision. So the developers were, ultimately, aware of the failure and tried to correct it.

But [Re]turn is still there, labeled non-canon, a time-travel story that exists specifically so you can undo what the main game did. That's not a patch. That's not a fix. It's a monument to the problem — an artifact of the moment Love in Space acknowledged, in game-length form, that the story they told wasn't the one their audience needed.
