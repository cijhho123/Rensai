Here is what [*Attack on Titan*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/16498/Shingeki_no_Kyojin) appears to be about: Eren Yaeger, a boy who watched his mother eaten alive, grows up to pursue absolute freedom through increasingly monstrous means, and ultimately commits a genocide that kills eighty percent of humanity. The series is framed as his story from the first chapter to the last. He narrates the opening. His back is on the cover. The final arc puts his interiority at the center — his time-looped memories, his half-justified rationale, his tears.

And yet [Hajime Isayama created Mikasa Ackermann first](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-creator-revealed-first-character-created-series-not-eren/). Not Eren. Not Armin. Mikasa — the silent, devastating woman who spends 139 chapters protecting someone she loves — was the conceptual origin of the entire series. That fact is not incidental. In the end, *Attack on Titan* isn't Eren's story. It is the story of two women separated by two thousand years, both enslaved by love, and what it looks like when one of them finally manages to choose differently.

![Attack on Titan — Mikasa and the series' central question of freedom and servitude](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/1948/120625.jpg)

## The Actual Question

For a series obsessed with freedom — "I want to see what's beyond those walls," the central line of Season 1 — *Attack on Titan* spends surprisingly little time asking what freedom actually is. The answer Eren eventually gives is: freedom is removing every constraint on your people, even if that means ending everyone else's freedom permanently. This answer produces the Rumbling. It kills eighty percent of the world. It also fails on its own terms, since Paradis Island is eventually destroyed anyway in the epilogue — the cycle of war resumes after a few generations, untouched by Eren's sacrifice.

The reason Eren's answer fails is not that his plan was poorly executed. It's that his answer was to a different question than the one the series had been asking. The real question is not "how do you break the walls that contain your people?" It is: "why do people choose their chains?"

[Ymir Fritz chose hers for two thousand years](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Ymir_Fritz). As a child in Marley, she was enslaved by the tribe that would become the Eldian Empire. When she accidentally discovered the source of all living matter, King Fritz made her his concubine — a gift of attention to a woman who had never received any — and she interpreted exploitation as love. She died protecting him from an assassination attempt, which is how she describes her own death: as a gift freely given. And then, because the Titans require a human host or they dissolve, her daughters were forced to eat her and carry her power forward. For two millennia, in the Paths dimension where the Founding Titan operates, she continued to build and maintain the Titans' bodies, enslaved to the will of whoever wielded the Founding Titan's power. She had more power than any living being. [She used it exclusively to serve](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-ymir-fritz-love-freedom-goals-eren/).

This is the problem the series has been building toward since before the Survey Corps, before the walls, before Eren's mother was eaten alive. It is not a military or political problem. It is a psychological one: the capacity to remain enslaved to a person when all physical chains have been removed, because the chains were never physical to begin with.

## The Ackermann Question

Eren understands something about Mikasa before the audience does. In [one of the series' crueler scenes](https://screenrant.com/attack-titan-eren-mikasa-hate-reason/) — the café confrontation in Chapter 112, which breaks something irretrievable between them — he tells her that her feelings for him are not freely chosen. The Ackermann clan, he explains, were genetically engineered to protect a host. When she "awakened" her power in response to Eren's call to fight back, she didn't choose to protect him; her biology selected him as her host. Everything she has felt since is an artifact of engineering.

It is a devastating claim. It is also, crucially, [a lie](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-episode-87-spoilers-eren-hate-mikasa-lie/). Armin tells her so in Chapter 139 after Eren is dead. The Ackermann awakening requires genuine emotional activation — and Eren gave Mikasa his scarf *before* she awakened, meaning he initiated the bond through an act of care, not the other way around. No other Ackermann exhibits the same devotion pattern; Levi is loyal to humanity's survival, not to any individual. The awakening was real, but what it awakened was already there.

Why does Eren lie? In his final conversation with Armin in [Chapter 139](https://www.epicdope.com/attack-on-titan-chapter-139-explained-eren-and-mikasas-fate-revealed-all-questions-clarified/), the manga confirms he was in love with Mikasa and desperately did not want her to mourn him. He pushed her away so the mourning would be less. This backfired in the most terrible way possible, because the story required her to mourn him in the most literal sense — by killing him — and to do it while still knowing, beneath all his cruelty, that he loved her.

What the Ackermann lie does narratively is force the reader to interrogate the same question Ymir Fritz's story raises: can love be freely chosen, or is it always a form of servitude? The series answers this question through Mikasa. Yes. Love can be chosen. But choosing it means you have to be able to let it go — even by ending it.

The Ackermann clan are [the only Eldians immune to the Founding Titan's memory manipulation](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Ackerman_clan). Every other person under the walls could have their memories rewritten, their sense of history erased, their understanding of freedom shaped by whoever held the Founding Titan. The Ackermanns could not be touched. They were, structurally, the only free people in Paradis. This is not a narrative accident — it is a design choice that carries enormous weight. If the series' central question is "why do people choose their chains?", then the character who answers that question had to be someone whose chains were never metaphysical. Mikasa's love for Eren could not be engineered by the Founding Titan. It could not be rewritten or erased. It was entirely, irreducibly her own — which is precisely what makes her sacrifice meaningful in a way Eren's cannot be. He was shaped by powers beyond himself. She acted in spite of them.

## What Ymir Was Waiting For

[Chapter 122, "From You, 2000 Years Ago,"](https://thereviewmonster.blog/2019/10/05/attack-on-titan-chapter-122-from-you-2000-years-ago-review-the-tragedy-of-ymir-fritz/) is one of the most quietly devastating chapters in the series. We finally see Ymir's origin: the child running from hunters, her feet cut off when she stumbled, her only reprieve a moment of attention from a man who used her as a weapon and called it love. Two thousand years of building the Titans that murdered countless thousands. Two thousand years of waiting, in the Paths dimension, for something the manga doesn't name until much later.

What was she waiting for? The [CBR analysis of Ymir's goals](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-ymir-fritz-love-freedom-goals-eren/) frames it as follows: she was waiting for someone to demonstrate that the cycle could end — that a person could love completely and still make a choice that cost them that love. Not the choice of indifference. Not the choice of someone who stopped caring. The hardest possible version: the choice of someone who loved with everything they had and chose the world over the person they loved anyway.

Eren tries to give her this, in his own way. He shows her his memories, his vision of a world where Titans no longer exist, his willingness to be killed for Eldian freedom. But Eren's sacrifice is contaminated — he commits genocide, meaning what he's sacrificing is not himself but everyone else. He also, in his final conversation with Armin, reveals he genuinely did not want to die and spent the last few months hoping someone would find another way. What Eren gives Ymir is not a demonstration of freedom. It is a demonstration of someone doing what they could not avoid doing and trying to make it mean something.

[What Mikasa gives her is different](https://www.cbr.com/aot-mikasa-freed-ymir-how/). Mikasa has a choice. She is not the one wearing the Attack Titan, not the one caught in the memory loop, not the one whose past self made decisions that locked his future into place. She chooses, with full knowledge of what she's doing and full knowledge of what it will cost her, to kill the person she has loved since she was nine years old. She decapitates him. She holds his head. She kisses him. And in that moment, according to the finale's logic, [Ymir finally sees what she had been waiting for](https://fictionhorizon.com/attack-on-titan-heres-how-mikasa-freed-ymir/) — that love does not have to mean servitude, that it is possible to love completely and act against your love anyway, because some things matter more.

The curse of the Titans ends not because Eren killed eighty percent of the world. It ends because of what Mikasa did after.

## The False Framing

The series' framing of Eren as protagonist is deliberate but not innocent. Isayama spent 139 chapters building the case that this was Eren's story — his trauma, his conviction, his sacrifice. He gave Eren access to the reader's interiority in ways Mikasa almost never received. We know what Eren thinks. We rarely know what Mikasa thinks, except in moments the story chooses to reveal.

This is partly what makes the ending work and partly why it frustrated so many readers. After a decade of following Eren's consciousness, the story concludes by telling us his consciousness was never the point. [Isayama has noted in interviews](https://fandomwire.com/attack-on-titan-hajime-isayama-always-intended-to-make-mikasa-the-main-character/) that Mikasa was always central to his vision of the series — the first character he designed, the one whose emotional journey closes the narrative. The final chapter is named "Toward the Tree on That Hill," which refers to the tree in the field where Mikasa eventually buries Eren's head, in the country she travels to after the war. The series ends not at Paradis Island, not with the political aftermath of the Rumbling, but with Mikasa sitting by a tree where she has buried the person she killed.

If that's not a protagonist's ending, it's a very strange choice of framing.

The gap in reader perception — the widespread surprise at the ending, the petitions for a "Snyder Cut" of the anime, the chapter 139 controversy — is, I think, partly explained by this. Readers followed Eren's consciousness. They invested in his interiority, his goal, his eventual choice. When the series pulled back from that consciousness in the final chapters to show us the Mikasa/Ymir resolution, many readers experienced it as the story abandoning its protagonist. What the series was actually doing was revealing which character had always been the structural center — the one whose choice made the mythology resolve, the one who was never going to be controlled by the Founding Titan's power, the one Isayama thought of first.

## Why Eren's Answer Fails — And What the Epilogue Is Actually Saying

There is a persistent reading of the finale in which Eren is the tragic hero: a man who gave up his own soul so his friends could be the ones to stop him and emerge as heroes, with the political capital to build peace. Eren articulates something like this in Chapter 139 — he wanted his friends to be seen as the heroes who stopped the Rumbling, so Eldia could survive in the aftermath. The reading has textual support. But it requires you to also accept the epilogue, in which Paradis Island is eventually attacked and destroyed by a coalition of nations regardless of the Alliance's heroism. The cycle of hatred resumes. Eren's plan accomplished nothing lasting.

[The Paradis epilogue is often treated as a flaw in the ending](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-manga-ending-controversy/). I think it is a thesis statement about what Eren's answer was actually worth. What it says is: strategic genocide, total war, sacrificing your humanity for your people's freedom — none of this breaks the cycle. It delays it, possibly by a few generations. The thing that breaks the 2000-year cycle is not Eren. It is Ymir's liberation, which comes through Mikasa, and which ends the power of the Titans entirely. After that, the war that follows is just a war — terrible, but human in scale, without supernatural entities eating people alive. The cycle of hatred persists. The curse of Ymir does not.

This distinction is everything. Isayama's series is not an argument that humanity's political conflicts can be resolved by the right person making the right sacrifice. It is an argument that a specific 2000-year-old supernatural system of oppression — one built on a woman's inability to choose her own love — could only end when someone finally demonstrated genuine free will under conditions of total loss.

[Isayama has stated publicly](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/attack-on-titan-ending-reaction/) that he stands by his ending. The frustration many readers feel is real, but I think it is directed at the wrong target. The ending doesn't fail Eren. It reveals that Eren was never equipped to answer the question the series had been asking — because Eren, for all his talk of freedom, was as determined by his circumstances as anyone. He saw his own future memories and followed them. He was, in the deepest sense, not free at all.

Mikasa was.
