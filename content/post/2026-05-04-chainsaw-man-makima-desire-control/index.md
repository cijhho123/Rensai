In Chapter 39 of Chainsaw Man, Makima and Denji spend an all-night movie marathon at the cinema. She sits through film after film without visible response. Then the Soviet war drama ["Ballad of a Soldier"](https://fictionhorizon.com/why-do-makima-and-denji-cry-while-watching-the-movie-in-chainsaw-man/) plays, and during the scene where the soldier finally reunites with his mother, Makima cries.

It is the only time she does.

She has watched people in pain, people in love, people in crisis, and nothing reached through the composed surface. A mother holding her son — and the Control Devil, the being born from humanity's fear of domination, the character designed to manage and direct everything and everyone in her field — cries.

[Tatsuki Fujimoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatsuki_Fujimoto) built this moment to mean something specific. Makima is not crying because she is moved by love in the abstract. She is crying because she wants what she cannot have, and cannot give: to be held by someone who simply accepts her. She is crying because she is the Control Devil, which means she experiences every relationship as hierarchy, which means she is constitutionally incapable of receiving the exact thing the movie is showing her.

And so is Denji.

## The Mama in the Name

Fujimoto revealed in a Shueisha special interview that [Makima's name contains "mama"](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/chainsaw-man-creator-makima-name-explained-disturbing-fact/). Remove the "ki" — the same cut a chainsaw makes — and you have Mama. His conclusion was precise: "Denji has always been pursuing maternal things."

This is not a metaphor. Denji's mother died young. He grew up in debt-driven servitude, working as a Devil Hunter for the Yakuza, eating scraps and sleeping on the floor. Nobody held him. Nobody acknowledged him as a specific person rather than a useful object. When Makima extends a hand and says he can live with her and follow her rules and she will take care of him, Denji hears an offer of conditional parental recognition and accepts it without hesitation. [He doesn't understand the difference between romantic desire and the longing to be seen by someone with the power to define your worth](https://www.dualshockers.com/chainsaw-man-denji-makima-simp/), because he has never had a version of either.

The series presents this confusion transparently. Denji thinks he wants Makima. What he wants is a mother. The desire is the same desire: to matter to someone, to have his existence acknowledged, to be held and not just used. Makima's name contains the structure of his longing hidden inside it, and he is the last person who could read it.

## What the Control Power Actually Is

The Control Devil's ability has a specific activation condition: [she can only control beings she "subjectively considers inferior"](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Makima). This is not domination in the theatrical sense — not a force that imposes itself on the unwilling. It requires that she feel superior to the target.

This is the series' most economical piece of design. The power is the literalization of contempt as a prerequisite for connection. Makima cannot be in a relationship with anyone as an equal. The moment she could genuinely see someone on her level, she would lose her ability to control them. Her relational mode is structurally hierarchical: she can approach, she can manage, she can shape outcomes, but she cannot meet. The capacity for genuine encounter is the one thing the Control Devil cannot have.

So she studies. [She never actually smoked](https://epicstream.com/article/things-that-inspired-chainsaw-man-creator-tatsuki-fujimoto) — she imitated the gesture because she had no lived experience, only second-hand observation through movies and proximity to humans. Everything she performs as "personality" is assembled from external sources, because the Control Devil has no interior life that was built through experience. She is hierarchy wearing a face.

And [she never memorized Denji's scent](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-makima-most-evil-act-against-denji-psychological-warfare/). She identifies people she finds interesting or useful by smell. She spent months managing Denji, shaping his desires, dangling scraps of hope and approval in front of him — and the scent she associated with him was not his own. It was Pochita's. She never saw Denji the person. She saw the Chainsaw Devil wearing a Denji suit.

## The Archetype She Is Built From

Fujimoto designed Makima from two specific influences: [Benten from The Eccentric Family and Haruko Haruhara from FLCL](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Makima). Both are "unfathomable beings higher than humans with their own sadness that the lowly cannot comprehend." His attraction to both characters was specifically about this structure: someone impossibly elevated who has inner suffering, but whose suffering is inaccessible to the people below them.

This is the exact structure Fujimoto named as his direct influence — the Benten type, the Haruko type — and it maps onto a broader pattern in the shonen manga he grew up reading. The powerful older woman: the mentor, the authority figure, occasionally kind, frequently cool, always slightly out of reach. Who seems to have something going on inside her that you can't quite see. Who, if you could only perform well enough, might turn and acknowledge you.

[Makima fulfills every dimension of this fantasy](https://www.thenerdsjunction.com/post/control-devil-makima-in-chainsaw-man): composed, powerful, occasionally approving, always managing the distance between herself and Denji with precision. She gives him just enough — food, housing, a hand to hold once — that his desire has something to attach to. She is designed to be desirable, and she is the Control Devil, which means the desirability is the control mechanism.

The revelation the series builds to is not that Makima is evil. It is that the fantasy itself is the mechanism of control. Not Makima specifically — the entire structure of wanting to be seen and approved by someone with power over you. Denji's longing is legitimate; the series is not mocking him for it. But his longing gets weaponized by someone who understood what it was better than he did, and who positioned herself as its object.

## Both Crying in the Dark

The Ballad of a Soldier scene is the series' most honest moment because it reveals the symmetry underneath the apparent asymmetry.

[Makima cries at a mother-son reunion](https://epicstream.com/article/chainsaw-man-why-did-makima-cry-in-the-cinema). Denji, reading her reaction, cries too — not because the film moved him, but because seeing Makima respond like a person makes him love her more. He is moved by her emotion, not the movie's. He is moved by the proof that there is something inside her that can be reached.

There isn't, or not in the way he thinks. What Makima is experiencing in that moment is the recognition of something she cannot possess: unconditional acceptance, the thing the soldier's mother offers with no calculus of worth attached. The one relationship the Control Devil cannot have is the one that requires both parties to abandon the question of who is superior. A mother holding her child is not a hierarchy. It is just presence.

She wants that. She cannot have it because having it would require her to stop being what she is. And Denji wants the same thing from her, for the same reason, and for the same reason cannot receive it.

[Fujimoto described the Benten parallel](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interview/2021-04-21/chainsaw-man-creator-tatsuki-fujimoto/.171315) as an "unfathomable being who is higher than humans, but has their own sadness, and the lowly ones cannot be there for that sadness." This is the precise formulation of why Makima is tragic rather than simply villainous. She has something she cannot express and nobody beneath her can reach. The cinema moment is the one place the structure slips — one scene from a 1959 Soviet film about a boy who gets to go home and hug his mother, and the Control Devil shows what she wanted all along.

## What the Fantasy Actually Is

The Makima dynamic is Chainsaw Man's argument about a specific kind of desire that runs through shonen manga without usually being named: the desire to be seen by the right person.

Not loved. Not understood. Seen. Acknowledged as a specific individual whose existence registers as meaningful to someone with the power to define worth. [Denji's entire arc in Part 1](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Denji) is [the accumulation and loss of small dignities](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-does-denji-want-a-normal-life/): a real bed, food without strings, someone to sleep next to who chose to be there. The series keeps giving him scraps of what he's looking for and then taking them away or revealing that the terms were never what he thought. Makima is the apex of this pattern — the character who most completely seemed to offer recognition and most completely withheld the actual thing.

The reveal that she never memorized his face does not make her into a simple villain. It makes her into an argument. The fantasy of the ideal woman who will someday turn and see you specifically — who will single you out from everyone else and confirm that yes, you in particular, are worth something — is a fantasy about recognition, not romance. And recognition is the one thing the Control Devil cannot grant, because granting it would mean releasing control.

![Denji](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/3/492407.jpg)

Nayuta, who [carries Makima's memories and devil nature into Part 2](https://www.cbr.com/chainsaw-man-what-happened-to-makima/), [is given to Denji to raise](https://chainsaw-man.fandom.com/wiki/Control_Devil). The series offers a revision: not the inaccessible authority who manages you, but a child who needs you to be the one with power. The same desire — to matter to someone — gets a structure where it is not automatically weaponized against him. He is the one with something to give.

It is not a happy ending. It is an honest one. The thing Denji wanted from Makima, she showed him she could not provide in the one moment she let him see through. A mother and her son hugging in a Soviet film. The only scene that reached her. The one shape of love that required nothing from the person receiving it except to be there.

She cried because she recognized it. She could not recognize Denji's face.
