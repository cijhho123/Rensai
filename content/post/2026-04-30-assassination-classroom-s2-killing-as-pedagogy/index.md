The punchline of *Assassination Classroom* is that nobody can kill the teacher. For forty-odd episodes across two seasons, Class 3-E tries everything — poison, guns, traps, alliances, elaborate schemes engineered with the precision of people who have learned, from Koro-sensei himself, how to think. He evades every attempt, grades the assassination forms with a red pen, and docks points for poor technique. The show runs on this loop: children try to kill their teacher, fail, and learn something in the process. It is structurally a gag.

Then Season 2 ends, and the gag is over. The students kill him. They actually do it, and it destroys them, and it was always going to happen, and the whole point is that by the time it does, that killing is the most loving thing they could possibly do for him.

This is the argument *Assassination Classroom* has been making from the beginning, but it takes the full run of Season 2 — the backstory revelations, the tonal shift, the agonizing final arc — to make it land. The assassination was never a dark joke or a structural conceit. It was the graduation ceremony. It just took fifty episodes to understand what kind of school this was.

## The System That Made Class 3-E

To understand what Koro-sensei is doing, you have to understand what he's undoing.

[Kunugigaoka Junior High's Class 3-E](https://ansatsukyoshitsu.fandom.com/wiki/Class_3-E) is not just a struggling class. It is a designed punishment — a deliberate segregation of the bottom five percent of students whose visible failure is meant to terrify the other ninety-five into performance. Chairman Gakuho Asano built the system on a variant of the 20:20:60 principle: twenty percent exceptional, twenty percent worthless, sixty percent ordinary. Class E exists to populate the "worthless" slot visibly enough that the sixty percent will claw upward rather than slide down into it. The students in E are not being educated. They are being used.

[This is a direct satire of Japan's actual education structure](https://www.cbr.com/assassination-classroom-japan-educational-system-bullying-suicide/), where exam result rankings are published openly and the pressure to perform compounds from middle school onward. Japan's [juken culture](https://www.tokyoweekender.com/japan-life/juken-culture-japan-exam-pressure/) — the high-stakes entrance examination system — runs entrance exams for junior high schools, high schools, and universities, with families enrolling children in cram schools (*juku*) sometimes as early as age three. The stress the system produces is real and documented: students have experienced nervous breakdowns requiring hospitalization before they turn thirteen. The mechanism described in the series — test scores as pure hierarchy, with low achievers treated as cautionary examples rather than students in need of support — is [not Matsui's invention](https://ansatsu-database.tumblr.com/post/117414628805/the-class-e-system-a-satire-of-real-life). It's an exaggeration of something real enough that Japanese readers would recognize it immediately.

What Koro-sensei does by taking Class 3-E's assignment is refuse the premise. His entire pedagogy is built on the assumption [that every student has something specific and excellent in them](https://mageinabarrel.com/2015/06/20/assassination-classrooms-eternal-schoolhouse/) — not the same thing, not measured the same way, but real and worth developing. He tailors lessons individually, notices what each student is struggling with, and [uses their failures as teaching material rather than evidence of their inadequacy](https://www.inverse.com/article/17949-assassination-classroom-manga-2012-series). This is the fantasy at the center of the show: a teacher who can give each student everything they need, simultaneously, at Mach 20.

It's explicitly a fantasy. One of the things Season 2 is willing to acknowledge — that Season 1 largely sidesteps — is that Koro-sensei's pedagogical ideal is [structurally impossible](https://myshortbread.wordpress.com/2015/04/20/assassination-classroom-exam-culture-and-pedagogy/). No human teacher could replicate what he does. He works at superhuman speed, has perfect memory, and can clone himself across forty tasks at once. What the series is describing is not a teaching method but a teaching aspiration — the belief in students so total and unconditional that it reshapes who they are. The octopus form is the point: this is something no human teacher can be, which is why we're mourning it before it even dies.

## What Season 2 Needs to Do

Season 1 of *Assassination Classroom* works primarily as an action-comedy. The assassination attempts are funny, the class dynamics are warm, Koro-sensei's emoji face cycling through emotions is a gifted visual gag. The show earns affection for its characters across twenty-two episodes of relatively light stakes. Some threads run deeper — Nagisa's gender and identity, the class's growing competence — but the tonal register stays bright.

[Season 2 takes that goodwill as operating capital and spends every last coin of it.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_Classroom_season_2) The second season has a structural task Season 1 didn't: it needs to make Koro-sensei's death feel necessary. Not just sad — necessary. If he dies because the government forces it, or because an outside threat eliminates him, the pedagogy is just cut short. It becomes a tragedy about potential denied. For the assassination to be the *culmination* of what he taught, the show has to make two things true simultaneously: the students must genuinely not want to kill him, and they must genuinely choose to do it anyway, for him.

The backstory arcs are how Season 2 earns that. [The Reaper's origin story](https://ansatsukyoshitsu.fandom.com/wiki/Korosensei) — Koro-sensei as a nameless assassin sold into violence as a child, who became the world's most feared killer before Yanagisawa's experiments transformed him into something inhuman — reframes the entire show. The octopus is not a monster that learned to teach. He is a person who chose to use monstrous power to become, deliberately, weak enough to love. The tentacles, when implanted, ask what the subject wishes to become. Koro-sensei requested weakness. He asked to be vulnerable, limited, killable. This is the most important detail in the series.

The relationship with [Aguri Yukimura](https://ansatsukyoshitsu.fandom.com/wiki/Aguri_Yukimura) — the teacher who first showed him what teaching could be, and who died asking him to take care of her students — is the emotional center that the finale needs. Season 2's "Past Time" episodes reveal the full weight of their relationship: Aguri was the researcher's assistant who talked to the imprisoned experiment subject every night for a year, gradually converting someone who had never known anything but death into someone who understood care. She gave him the concept of a birthday — naming a day for him because he had never had one. When she died shielding him from a tentacle explosion, her last request was that he go teach her students. The tragedy of Aguri is not just that she died. It's that she spent her short life performing, under terrible constraints, exactly the kind of teaching the series argues for: patient, specific, unconditional. She watched *him* into being.

Season 2 retroactively reframes everything in Season 1 as the continuation of Aguri's classroom. Every student Koro-sensei refuses to give up on is a student she never got to teach.

[Kayano's arc in Season 2](https://thechuunicorner.com/2016/12/18/reviewdiscussion-about-assassination-classroom-second-season/), the reveal that she is Aguri's sister and has been running her own long-game assassination plot, works because it doubles the tragedy: both Koro-sensei and Kayano are people made by loss, trying to honor someone who died. When Koro-sensei chooses to open up and let Kayano's poison tentacles kill him if it comes to that — rather than fight her — the scene lands because it shows exactly what kind of person he has become. He will die for his students. He has always been willing to die for his students.

## Why They Have to Do It Themselves

Here is the choice that defines the final arc, and it is more precise than the show sometimes gets credit for: Koro-sensei does not have to die by his students' hands. He is going to be destroyed by the government regardless. He could be killed by soldiers, by Yanagisawa's weapons, by the military laser the world has been preparing. His death is not the students' responsibility.

But he gives them the choice. [He asks them to be the ones to do it.](https://hamliet.home.blog/2018/10/07/could-you-explain-the-ending-of-assasination/)

This is the pedagogy completing itself. The whole year has been about restoring agency to a group of students who had it systematically stripped away — who were told they were failures, sorted into a disposable class, and treated as objects in another person's lesson plan. Koro-sensei's teaching has been, at every point, about giving them back the ability to make their own choices, to trust their own assessments, to act on their own judgment. The assassination mission, which started as a government operation using children as disposable tools, has been quietly transformed into something else: a year-long project in self-determination.

So when the end comes, he does not let the government take him. He makes it their decision. He gives them back, one last time, the agency that education is supposed to develop and that Kunugigaoka's system was designed to destroy. [The final roll call](https://ansatsukyoshitsu.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_46) — where Koro-sensei takes attendance one last time and every student of Class 3-E answers — is heartbreaking because it's also deeply correct. He is calling their names. He wants them to know he sees them. That he always saw them.

[Yusei Matsui said in interviews that his central theme was simple: teachers watch their students.](https://screenanarchy.com/2016/10/interview-assassination-classroom-creator-matsui-yusei-on-movie-adaptations-and-saying-goodb.html) Not grade them. Not evaluate them. Watch them — in the sense of witnessing who they are and who they are becoming. The roll call is the most complete expression of this. He is watching them one last time, and by asking them to kill him, he is trusting what he saw.

Nagisa delivers the blow. This is correct. Nagisa is the character who has most internalized the contradiction at the heart of the series — the killing skill and the caring impulse running in the same person — and his arc has always been about finding out which one defines him. [By Season 2's end, he has found his answer.](https://anibproductions.wordpress.com/2018/05/31/whats-in-a-character-nagisa-shiota/) He is going to be a teacher. The assassination ability and the teaching vocation are not opposites; they share the same skill set. Perfect reading of others. Attention to weakness and strength. The ability to get under someone's skin. He could use these to kill. He chooses to use them to teach. Koro-sensei didn't teach him how to kill. He taught him how to choose.

## The Impossible Ideal and Its Necessary Ending

The show has a critic in [one review noting that the ending is emotionally "dragged out"](https://thechuunicorner.com/2016/12/18/reviewdiscussion-about-assassination-classroom-second-season/) and structurally inelegant, and this is not wrong. The final arc does run long; the Yanagisawa battle introduces a power-level escalation that feels borrowed from a different kind of shonen — mutant tentacle super-forms, an antagonist powered by pure hate — that sits uneasily next to a show that works best as intimate classroom drama. It almost derails the ending: the Yanagisawa conflict is resolved through physical combat rather than the accumulated pedagogical logic the show has spent two seasons building, and it lands like a gear change into a different series. The epilogue then covers years of aftermath at breakneck speed after two episodes of agonized death scene. [Another critic argues](https://criticoffilm.wordpress.com/2017/01/29/assassination-classroom-the-series-that-shouldve-failed/) the series should have collapsed under the weight of its contradictions — a comedy that becomes grief, a death-wish premise that inverts into a love story — and that it survives primarily through charm and the fundamental watchability of Koro-sensei's character.

Both critiques are fair and both miss the same thing. The emotional excess of the finale is not a bug. A show about students who learned to feel things from a teacher who chose to be vulnerable is going to be maximally emotional when that teacher dies. If the final arc were restrained and elegant, it would be a different show — and it would be lying. [Matsui himself was in tears drawing the final chapters](https://fandomwire.com/for-six-months-i-could-not-take-a-day-off-assassination-classroom-mangaka-was-put-through-hell-for-the-mangas-ending/), working through an exhausting six-month period coordinating the manga's ending with the anime and live-action adaptations simultaneously. The excess is authentic. It is a story about people who loved something impossible, and the impossible thing is gone, and they are destroyed by it and built by it at the same time.

This is also why the pedagogy is described as a fantasy and presented as worth mourning. Real teachers cannot do what Koro-sensei does. [Real Japanese classrooms look more like Kunugigaoka than Class 3-E.](https://anime-ashoka.medium.com/class-3-e-the-assassination-classroom-33b9175bdce6) The exam culture is real; the tiering is real; the disposal of students the system has written off is real. What *Assassination Classroom* argues is not that education can be fixed by being nicer. It argues that the thing education should be — the watching, the witnessing, the trust that something specific and excellent exists in each student — is worth imagining even in its impossible form, because the imagining tells us what the real thing is failing to do.

The ending doesn't pretend Koro-sensei's model can continue. It can't. The world cannot contain a Mach-20 octopus who grades papers with a red pen and cries at graduation. The model ends. What persists is the students it made: Nagisa in front of a classroom of delinquents, using everything he was taught, choosing the teacher path because it's the only future that honors what he was given. [Seven years later](https://ansatsukyoshitsu.fandom.com/wiki/Nagisa_Shiota), he is smaller than his students, often underestimated, and entirely capable of making them take him seriously — using the same skills, the same attention, the same absolute belief in every person in the room.

One of the best analytical readings of the show [argues that its central principle is](https://mageinabarrel.com/2015/06/20/assassination-classrooms-eternal-schoolhouse/): to no longer learn or grow is to stagnate — a lesson that applies not just to the students, but to Koro-sensei himself, who spends the year being changed by the class he is changing. His teaching is mutual. He learns from them; they learn from him; the classroom is the site where both become more fully themselves. It applies equally to the dead-end ideal of education-as-rank-sorting, which the series argues is stagnant at its core — a system that confuses the measurement of humans with the development of them.

The assassination was always going to happen. Season 2 makes it matter by insisting that it was always going to be the students who did it, with love, with grief, on their own terms. That is not a tragedy. That is what it looks like when a teacher's work is finished.
