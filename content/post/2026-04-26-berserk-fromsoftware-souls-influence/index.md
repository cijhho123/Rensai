Every Berserk anime has failed. Not in the way that mediocre adaptations fail — with dull execution or a miscast — but at the level of concept. The [1997 TV series](https://myanimelist.net/anime/33/Berserk) worked within its cuts but couldn't survive them long-term. The [2016-17 trilogy](https://myanimelist.net/anime/32379/Berserk_2016) produced CGI so lifeless it became a punchline. The three-film compilation starting in [2012](https://myanimelist.net/anime/10533/Berserk_Ougon_Jidai-hen_I__Haou_no_Tamago) was the closest anyone got, and still the Eclipse felt muted, the weight of Guts' Dragon Slayer unconvincing, the intricacy of Miura's ink-work simply absent.

Meanwhile, for the past fifteen years, Hidetaka Miyazaki has been making Berserk adaptations that nobody called adaptations. The [Dark Souls series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_Souls), [Bloodborne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloodborne), and [Elden Ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elden_Ring) are saturated with Miura's DNA — in design, in theme, in the precise way both make their protagonists feel cosmically outmatched and refuse to explain why they're still standing. They didn't adapt Berserk. They did something more lasting: they understood it.

![Guts, the Black Swordsman, wielding the Dragon Slayer — the weapon that defines Berserk's visual identity](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/manga/1/157897.jpg)

## What Miyazaki Kept on His Shelf

Miyazaki has never been coy about this. In a widely-cited [Rolling Stone / Glixel interview](https://www.neogaf.com/threads/hidetaka-miyazaki-rolling-stone-glixel-interview.1289549/), asked what books he keeps on his shelf, he named Berserk alongside Go Nagai's Devilman — two works defined by their refusal to offer the protagonist anything resembling grace. Both portray worlds in which cosmic machinery grinds down human beings, and the only meaningful question is what a person does with that.

The specific moment where this influence became visible on-screen came with the [Catarina armor in Dark Souls](https://fandomwire.com/i-liked-it-immediately-hidetaka-miyazaki-instantly-loved-the-biggest-berserk-easter-egg-in-dark-souls/). Miyazaki requested a fantasy armor from designer Mr. Waragai, received sketches, and immediately singled out one design: the absurdly round, spherical, onion-shaped set he later identified as being based on Bazuso, the giant mercenary Guts cuts down in the manga's early chapters. "It was truly distinctive," Miyazaki said. "I liked it immediately." The Catarina set became one of the most beloved pieces of equipment in the series — beloved partly *because* it looks preposterous, which is very much the point of Bazuso as a character.

This is not trivia. It tells you how Miyazaki's relationship to Berserk works: not as homage-building or Easter egg scattering, but as aesthetic instinct. He recognized Miura's sensibility and reached for it directly.

## The Design Translation

The most immediate layer of influence runs through weapons and armor. The [Greatsword in Dark Souls](https://gamerant.com/dark-souls-berserk-references/) is, visually and functionally, Guts' Dragon Slayer — an absurdly large blade that no normal human should be able to lift, whose item descriptions acknowledge the fact. The conceit of Berserk is that Guts does lift it anyway, and Dark Souls makes that the mechanical premise of an entire weapon class. Players who build around Strength weapons are, on some level, playing as Guts.

Elden Ring deepens this across every area of the game. The [Greatsword of Solitude](https://www.thegamer.com/elden-ring-shadow-of-the-erdtree-all-best-berserk-references/) from the Shadow of the Erdtree DLC is, according to the gaming press, "From's most Guts-like guy in a long list of Guts-like guys" — dropped by the Blackgaol Knight in an armor set combining spiked pauldrons and flowing black cape in direct Berserker Armor aesthetic. The [Blaidd character](https://gamerant.com/elden-ring-all-berserk-references/) — introduced in promotional material before the game launched, in a pose that mirrors Guts' iconic two-handed grip stance — was [identified as a Guts reference](https://www.vintageisthenewold.com/faq/is-blaidd-modeled-after-guts) by Berserk fans before they had played a single minute of the game. But the structural parallel runs deeper than the silhouette. Blaidd is Ranni's devoted half-wolf bodyguard, the point man who executes her will and whose entire arc is defined by an impossible loyalty that ultimately destroys him. That is the exact narrative architecture of Guts' relationship to Griffith during the Band of the Hawk years: a protector whose devotion is total and whose tragedy is that the person he protects requires, at some level, his sacrifice. Dark Souls itself established this pattern earlier — [Artorias of the Abyss](https://www.cbr.com/easy-to-miss-berserk-references-fromsoftware-games/) in the original game is a previous iteration of the same Guts archetype — but Blaidd makes it explicit.

The environmental translation is equally deliberate. Berserk's [Hill of Swords](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-references-fromsoftware-games/) — the snow-covered hill where blades are stabbed into the earth as tributes to the fallen Band of the Falcon — finds its direct counterpart in Elden Ring's Sword Monuments: fourteen scattered structures dedicated to fallen warriors, same silhouette, same function, same grief. The Erdtree's looming presence over the Lands Between echoes the [World Spiral Tree](https://www.knowll.com/e/131/complete-list-of-berserk-influences-on-elden-ring) Miura introduced in the Fantasia arc — a vast supernatural structure whose existence signals that the real world has been irrevocably altered by something the protagonists cannot fully see.

[Ranni the Witch](https://gettotext.com/how-did-the-berserk-manga-influence-the-world-of-elden-ring/), one of Elden Ring's most compelling characters, is built from Schierke: isolated from the world, trained by a legendary older witch who lived in a woodland tower, wearing an absurdly large hat, offering the player-character a path outside the ordained ending. Schierke is the character who, in Berserk, allows Guts to resist the Berserker Armor's pull toward self-annihilation through sheer emotional presence. Ranni is the character who offers a way out of the Great Will's ordained cycle. The structural parallel is not coincidental.

## The Thematic Translation

Design homage is one thing. What makes the Soulsborne games genuine successors to Berserk's vision is that Miyazaki translated the philosophical architecture, not just the visual surface.

Berserk's central mechanism is the [Brand of Sacrifice](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-rules-of-causality-explained/) — a mark burned onto Guts' and Casca's bodies during the Eclipse, designating them as targets for demons and damning them to a life of unceasing assault. It is a mark that says: you have been selected for destruction by something larger than you, and nothing you do will change that designation. The [Darksign in Dark Souls](https://screenrant.com/dark-souls-fromsoftware-berserk-manga-inspired-how/) functions identically — an immutable fiery mark on the bearer's body, condemning them to cycles of death and revival, separating them from ordinary humanity. Both marks operate as metaphors for a specific psychological state: the experience of living in a body that has been claimed by something traumatic, of carrying a wound the world can sense.

The critical difference between how Berserk and Dark Souls handle this is instructive — and worth sitting with rather than moving past quickly. Guts is not the Chosen One. He has no destiny, no prophecy surrounding him, no cosmic purpose. He fights because he refuses not to, and because he has people he will not let the world take from him. Miura makes this radical: in a world structured entirely by causality and the God Hand's manipulation, the character at the center of the story is the one who was not selected. His survival is an insult to the architecture of fate.

[Dark Souls](https://www.fandom.com/articles/dark-souls-sekiro-berserk) softens this: the Chosen Undead is, structurally, chosen — marked by a prophecy, set on a path with a cosmically ordained endpoint. The cursed-body imagery survives intact, the grinding world survives intact, but the player-character has been given a purpose that Miura explicitly denies Guts. This is not a failure of understanding — it is a recognition that games require agency architecture that manga does not. A player needs to feel that their decisions matter, which means the world needs to acknowledge their existence in a way Berserk's world never acknowledges Guts'. What Miyazaki preserved was the emotional texture of fighting something designed to destroy you. What he adjusted was the framework that makes that fight meaningful to a player rather than just to a reader. That distinction is the clearest example of how influence works when it is actually absorbed rather than copied.

What the games preserved without softening is the atmosphere of [a world that punishes decency](https://techraptor.net/gaming/features/souls-series-love-of-berserk). In both Berserk and Dark Souls, the institutions of civilization — religion, aristocracy, military — exist primarily to grind ordinary people down. Berserk's Holy Iron Chain Knights and their dogmatic inquisition find a direct parallel in Anor Londo's architecture of theocratic grandeur concealing rot. The God Hand's manipulation of human fate maps onto the way the Age of Fire's "divine" order turns out to have been a desperate maintenance job on a collapsing lie. Both works reserve their moral clarity not for the powerful, but for the stubborn and the small — people who show compassion when the world has given them every reason not to.

## Why the Medium Works

Here is what every Berserk anime has struggled to do: make you feel the weight of the Dragon Slayer.

In the manga, Miura communicates it through the labor of the drawing — the crosshatching, the impossible density of detail in the blade's pitted surface, the way Guts' body deforms under the effort of a swing. It takes time to read a Miura panel the way he intended. The visual weight is inseparable from the reader's time.

Animation does not have this. It has to *simulate* weight through impact frames, sound design, and camera movement, and Berserk's anime adaptations have consistently failed at this simulation. The [2016 series](https://screenrant.com/berserk-best-manga-adaptations-bad-theory/) became notorious for its weightless CGI swings; the problem was not budget or technology but fundamental category error. Berserk's violence is not kinetic — it is geological. It is about mass, consequence, and the accumulation of damage over time.

Video games, uniquely, can restore this. When you swing the Greatsword in Dark Souls, there is a commitment cost — the animation is long, you are vulnerable during it, and hitting or missing matters. When you finally stagger a boss, it is because you managed something the world told you was impossible, in exactly the way Guts manages things the world tells him are impossible. The [soulsborne formula](https://gamerant.com/berserk-dark-souls-bloodborne-sekiro-inspiration-influence/) is not just aesthetically similar to Berserk — it *enacts* the same psychology. You persist against accumulated punishment until the thing that was crushing you falls. That is not an adaptation of Berserk. That is what Berserk is.

## Elden Ring as Mourning

Elden Ring released on February 25, 2022. Kentaro Miura died on May 6, 2021, from acute aortic dissection at age 54, with [Berserk unfinished at chapter 364](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2021-05-20/creators-offer-condolences-for-berserk-creator-kentarou-miura-passing/.172994).

The game was in development when Miura died. Whether specific elements were added as tribute or were always planned is not public record, but the density of Berserk references in Elden Ring — and particularly in the [Shadow of the Erdtree DLC](https://www.thegamer.com/elden-ring-berserk-kentaro-miura/), released after years of additional development — carries the texture of acknowledgment. The Sword Monuments. The Blackgaol Knight's Greatsword of Solitude, placed in the DLC's opening area.

And Messmer the Impaler, the DLC's central villain, who is [arguably the most considered Berserk reference in the entire series](https://gamerant.com/elden-ring-dlc-trailer-berserk-manga-references-explained/). Messmer wears a dragon-winged helmet with a serpent coiled at the crest, has bright red hair, and serves as an elite enforcer of a higher order whose tragic backstory complicates his role as antagonist. This maps precisely onto Grunbeld — the God Hand's apostle-general who nearly kills Guts in the Millennium Falcon arc, who wears a dragon-crested helm and whose transformation into the [Great Flame Dragon apostle form](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Grunbeld) midway through the battle mirrors Messmer's own serpentine second phase. Both encounters follow the same structure: a formidably armored warrior reveals a monstrous transformation just when the fight seemed winnable. The design debt is precise enough that it functions less as homage than as a direct citation.

FromSoftware does not speak openly about tributes. But [creative communities have their own language](https://www.cbr.com/elden-ring-berserk-tribute-kentaro-miura/), and Elden Ring is fluent in Berserk's in a way that, by this point, exceeds any plausible interpretation of coincidence. Miyazaki was paying a debt, in the only currency that survives a person — the continued life of their ideas in work that comes after them.

## What This Actually Means

The debate about whether Berserk deserves a faithful anime adaptation will continue as long as the manga exists. It does deserve one. The [existing attempts](https://gamerant.com/will-berserk-ever-get-the-anime-adaptation-it-deserves/) have not come close to earning the source material.

But the Soulsborne games have already demonstrated something more interesting than faithful adaptation: that Berserk's deepest contribution to culture was not the plot, the characters, or even the visual style — it was a philosophy of making. A conviction that darkness earned honestly, violence that carries actual weight, and a protagonist who fights not because destiny selected him but because he cannot stop, produces something audiences will return to for decades.

Miyazaki understood this before most critics did. He built it into every game he made. Berserk's greatest legacy is not an unfinished manga or an anime that keeps failing to happen. It is the design language of an entire game genre, carrying Miura's sensibility forward into a medium he never worked in — and, if the Sword Monuments in the Lands Between mean anything, still carrying it now.
