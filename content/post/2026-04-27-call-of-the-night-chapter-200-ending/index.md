Vampire romance as a genre has a small set of acceptable endpoints. The human becomes a vampire, or the vampire becomes human, or one of them dies, or both. The structure of the genre demands resolution of its central asymmetry — the mortal and the immortal cannot just continue indefinitely, so something has to give.

[Call of the Night spent four years finding a fourth option.](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-11-15/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-200th-chapter/.204474)

Chapter 200 — the final chapter, titled "Final Night: And Then" — does not resolve the series' central problem. Kou Yamori is still a half-vampire who never completed the transformation. Nazuna Nanakusa still loves him, which means her bite is still lethal to him. The two conditions the series built its entire architecture around — a human's love enables vampirism, a vampire's love makes the bite fatal — are still in place. Nothing has been solved. The ending just formalizes all of this and names it a commitment.

"Play tag with me till death do us part."

That line is what Kotoyama chose to close the series. Not a kiss, not a bite, not a transformation. A game. And understanding why that is exactly right requires understanding what the series was always actually about.

## The Conditions Were Never Going to Resolve

The two conditions create a logical trap that becomes clearer as the series progresses. [For Ko to become a vampire, he needs to be bitten by a vampire who loves him.](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori) For Nazuna to bite Ko without killing him, she cannot love him. The moment she genuinely loves him, the bite becomes lethal. The series positions the love required for transformation as the same thing that makes transformation impossible.

Readers waiting for a clever mechanism to resolve this were waiting for something the series was never going to provide. [Kotoyama described the series as "a rebellious rom-com"](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) — deliberately subverting the conventions of the form. The conventional rom-com, and by extension the conventional vampire romance, provides resolution. The conditions exist so they cannot be conventionally resolved. That is the rebellious part.

The three years between Chapter 199 and Chapter 200 happen because Nazuna has enough love for Ko to know she cannot be near him without risking killing him. [She separates from him specifically because she loves him](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa), then spends those years in the Arctic among a small community of humans and vampires, taking care of an elderly woman, playing with children, living a life that has Ko-shaped absence in it. This is not an ending point. It is the condition of their relationship made spatial: she loves him at a remove because proximity is dangerous.

When Ko comes to find her anyway, what gets resolved is not the conditions. What gets resolved is whether both of them are willing to accept the conditions as the terms of their life together.

## What "Play Tag" Actually Means

The proposal is a reference to the series' internal logic, not just a whimsical metaphor. Ko developed a [half-vampire state across the later arcs](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori) — not full transformation, but [a condition triggered by emotional or physical pressure](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Vampires) that gives him access to vampire-level capabilities. This half-state is the reason the game works: if Nazuna loses control and tries to bite him, Ko is strong enough to fend her off. The "tag" is literal. He has to be fast enough to escape her when she can't stop herself.

"Till death do us part" acknowledges the actual stakes. If she catches him — if he is ever unable to evade her when she cannot control her impulse — he dies. This is not a safety that gets removed from the series. [It is written into the ending as a permanent condition](https://animehunch.com/kotoyamas-call-of-the-night-manga-to-end-with-chapter-200/). They are choosing a relationship with mortality built into its structure, specifically because the love that would cause that death is what makes the relationship real.

There is something almost exact about this as a resolution. The series started with a boy who wanted to escape his life — who wanted to become a vampire not for immortality but for permission to stop performing the daylight world's script. The game they are now playing forever is itself a kind of escape from resolution. Not from life, but from the demand that their relationship conform to a recognizable shape.

![Nazuna Nanakusa — the vampire who needed to learn how to be loved](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/CallOfTheNight1-e1617818816737.jpg)

## What Nazuna Saying "The Man I Love" Does

The other moment in Chapter 200 that matters: [Nazuna introduces Ko to the children of her Arctic community as "the man she loves."](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained)

The significance of this for Nazuna specifically is enormous. Her entire arc — the one the previous chapters spent establishing — is that she engineered distance as a form of self-protection. Professional cuddling services. Controlled bite transactions. The two-condition rule she announced to Ko in the first chapter was also her own trap: she could only transform someone who loved her, which meant she had to let herself be loved, which she had spent her entire vampire life avoiding.

Nazuna saying "this is the man I love" out loud, to people who are her community, is the series' completion of her arc. She is not hiding the love anymore. She is not using the distance as protection. She is naming it publicly and inviting Ko into the life she built during their three years apart.

This is the transformation the series was actually tracking. Not Ko becoming a vampire. Nazuna allowing herself to be known.

## Why the Fandom Was Split

The fandom split on this ending, and the split is instructive. [The GameFAQs thread](https://gamefaqs.gamespot.com/boards/2000121-anime-and-manga-other-titles/80677604?page=1) runs two pages of readers arguing about whether "play tag" means they're together or not, what the children mean, whether three years apart was earned, whether Ko's half-vampire state is a satisfying endpoint. [A YouTube video titled "Call of the Night Ending Sucked" has tens of thousands of views](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OEsygFfZvis). The disappointed contingent wanted completion: Ko becomes a vampire, the conditions resolve, the relationship achieves some stable form the series agrees to show you.

That is a reasonable expectation to bring to a romance. It is not the expectation this series built for. [Kotoyama described the series from the start as a "rebellious rom-com"](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html), built against the form's conventions — and [the first volume review at CBR](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-vol-1-review/) correctly noted that it inverts its genre at every turn. The ending's incompleteness is not a fumble. It is the position the series was always defending: that the in-between is not a failure state, that wanting things to keep going is not a problem to be solved.

But what would "more" look like here? The conditions resolve: Ko dies or Nazuna stops loving him. The love continues under its existing constraints: they live the way they do in Chapter 200. The ending Kotoyama chose is the one that keeps both characters and both arcs intact. Ko does not die and does not complete transformation. Nazuna does not stop loving him and does not lose the bite risk. They are both exactly who they became during the series. The ending puts them together within those conditions, names those conditions as the game, and stops there.

## The Genre It Built Against

[The vampire romance as a genre has specific shapes](https://www.animefeminist.com/call-of-the-night-episode-1/), and one of the things Call of the Night has always been doing is using those shapes to tell a different story. The seductive vampire who falls for their human prey. The transformation as union. The immortality as escape from loss. Call of the Night takes each of these and bends them: Nazuna's bite is a transaction before it becomes a feeling. The transformation requires human love rather than vampire seduction. The immortality is never available to Ko because the completion of his transformation is permanently blocked by the same love that would make it meaningful.

The ending is a refusal of the genre's promise. It doesn't deliver the union the romance structure implies. It delivers something more honest: two people who found each other and agreed to play a game whose stakes are real, in a place where the night lasts long enough to give them time.

The story is complete. What they chose to do with it — refuse genre resolution and call that commitment — is exactly what the first chapter promised.
