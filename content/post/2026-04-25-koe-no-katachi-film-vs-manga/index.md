There is a scene in Naoko Yamada's 2016 film where Kensuke Ushio's score becomes muffled, processed, as if heard through water or a wall — [a microphone placed inside a piano to capture the mechanics of sound rather than its clarity](https://carnivorouslreviews.wordpress.com/2018/07/15/the-warmth-of-voice-a-short-analysis-of-koe-no-katachis-beautiful-sound-design-and-shot-composition/). It's a technical choice that does something no panel in Yoshitoki Oima's manga can: it puts you inside Shoko's experience. Not watching someone who can't hear. Actually not quite hearing.

That moment is the film's argument. And it's a genuine one. Yamada's adaptation of [*Koe no Katachi*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)) is one of the more precise pieces of cinema about what communication feels like when it fails — and it achieves that through sound design, shot composition, and sign language animation that [required Kyoto Animation to consult multiple organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)), including the Japanese Federation of the Deaf. The film is a masterpiece in a specific register.

But it got there through a series of editorial decisions that made Shoya Ishida more sympathetic, more immediately readable, more cinematically legible — and in doing so, filed down the manga's rougher, more honest edges.

## What the Film Actually Does

Yamada described [her approach to the adaptation](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2017/05/30/koe-no-katachi-director-naoko-yamada-interview/) as deconstructing the manga rather than translating it — she wanted to capture its "soul" while converting it to audiovisual media. [She noted that animation allowed her to express the absence of sound through color and vibration in ways prose cannot](https://www.heyuguys.com/naoko-yamada-a-silent-voice-interview/) — treating silence not as a gap but as a substance with its own visual texture. What that meant in practice was building a film around the experiential gap between Shoya and Shoko, using every tool available to cinema.

The result is remarkable. When the ambient sound drops out and the score turns hollow, you feel the isolation the manga can only describe. When [POV shots blur the faces of everyone Shoya can't connect with](https://movies.mxdwn.com/feature/the-clever-use-of-communication-in-a-silent-voice-the-movie/), you understand his social withdrawal as a perceptual experience, not just a plot fact. The film also makes [Shoko's perception of vibration physically present](https://carnivorouslreviews.wordpress.com/2018/07/15/the-warmth-of-voice-a-short-analysis-of-koe-no-katachis-beautiful-sound-design-and-shot-composition/) — sounds she can't hear rendered as visible tremors, the tactile experience of a world that communicates through channels she can partially access. The film's sign language sequences were animated with frame-by-frame accuracy — [over two hours of carefully referenced sign language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)) throughout the runtime — making Shoko's primary mode of communication visually present in a way prose can only approximate.

This is what adaptation can do when it's working: find the things its medium does uniquely well and use them to argue the source material's thesis more directly than the source could. The film's sound design is its version of Oima's pages. It's doing the same work in a different language.

## What the Film Simplified

The manga's Shoya is considerably less sympathetic. [He judges people internally — constantly](https://gamerant.com/a-silent-voice-the-anime-manga-differences/). He snaps. His inner voice is often unkind, toward others and himself, in ways the film excises to keep him readable as a protagonist audiences can follow for two hours. This is not a minor adjustment. Shoya's internal ugliness in the manga is part of the argument: he's not just someone who did something terrible once and is now suffering for it, he's someone whose way of relating to the world is still damaged, still prone to judgment, still working against him even when he's trying to do better. The manga earns his eventual change more slowly and with more friction.

The film's Shoya arrives already softened — the internal monologue is gone, replaced by visual externalizations of his state that are moving but less ambiguous. He's sad. He's trying. He deserves to heal. These things are all true, but they're all that's left. The manga also contained a whole axis of moral difficulty that [the film largely cut](https://screenrant.com/silent-voice-film-manga-differences-better/): the collaborative film project initiated by Shoya's friend Tomohiro Nagatsuka, which eventually pulls the entire main cast together to make a movie. The film they produce [is a failure by conventional standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)) — it doesn't win the competition, it doesn't resolve anyone's emotional crises neatly — but the act of making it heals something. The group comes apart and reassembles around the project. Characters [learn to inhabit the same space](https://www.cbr.com/a-silent-voice-anime-manga-comparisons/) without needing to confess or forgive first. That's healing through creation rather than through emotional confrontation, and it's far more true to how people actually process things.

## Ueno and the Unresolved

The character who makes this clearest is Naoka Ueno. In both versions, she's the one who never quite arrives at accountability — she bullied Shoko alongside Shoya, harbors resentment rather than remorse, and ends the story without a clean redemption arc. The manga [spends considerable time with her internal conflict](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Naoka_Ueno), making her cruelty legible as something other than villainy — she's possessive, jealous, and limited, with genuine feelings that she channels destructively — but it doesn't resolve her. By the manga's end she is [working at a clothing store, somewhat softened but essentially unchanged](https://www.liveanime.org/post/silent-voice-manga-characters), and the characters have had to decide whether to continue existing near someone like that. The film compresses her to functional antagonism and then moves past her.

This matters because Ueno is the test of what the series is actually arguing. If the story believes reconciliation and growth are universally possible — the redemption arc thesis — then Ueno is a problem. She doesn't resolve. She doesn't arrive at understanding. She's left as she is, which [fan discourse has never stopped debating](https://www.novelupdatesforum.com/blogs/i-just-cant-hate-ueno-in-koe-no-katachi.979/) precisely because it's the most honest thing in the series. The film sidesteps her to preserve narrative momentum. The manga sits with her discomfort longer.

## Two Different Arguments About the Same Subject

This is the real point of the film/manga divide: they're not telling the same story in different formats. They're making related but distinct arguments.

Yamada's film is a film about what empathy feels like. The sound design, the sign language, the muffled piano — all of it is saying: here is what it is like to be in a world that wasn't built for how you communicate. If you feel that, you understand Shoko. The filmmaking is the thesis. What gets cut is anything that would interrupt that experiential project.

[Oima's manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)) is a story about why empathy is hard. The messy internal Shoya, the unresolved Ueno, the collaborative film that heals through making rather than talking — these are all saying: people who have hurt each other don't just get better because they want to. The friction is the point.

Both are true. The film's emotional accessibility is a genuine achievement — the [critical consensus and awards recognition](https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/a_silent_voice) reflect something real about what Yamada created. But the reason the manga endures alongside it, rather than being superseded by it, is that [the manga does something the film can't](https://blog.alltheanime.com/a-silent-voice-anime-vs-manga/): it maintains the difficulty. It insists that the characters not be easier to love than they actually are.

The film made Shoya more likable. The manga never did. That's a loss and a choice.

There's a broader question in that gap: what do we want adaptation to do for us? Yamada, [in her own words](https://www.newsweek.com/silent-voice-director-anime-movie-interview-701147), described her goal as making audiences "feel they're watching something happening now, to people around them" — prioritizing presence and immediacy over narrative density. That's a valid artistic aim. It produced a film that millions of people have found devastating and true. But it's a film that works by making its characters easier to inhabit, and the manga works by making its characters harder to dismiss.

Feeling the thing and understanding it are not the same act. The film is very good at the first. The manga, at its best, insists on the second — and trusts you to stay with it even when the people on the page aren't making that easy.

Whether that trade is worth making depends on what you came for. But it's worth knowing that a trade was made.
