Hajime Isayama gave an interview in late 2023 that should permanently change how people argue about Attack on Titan's ending. Speaking about his creative process, he drew an explicit parallel: "I was tied down to what I had originally envisioned when I was young. And so, manga became a very restrictive art form for me, similar to how the massive powers that Eren acquired ended up restricting him."

Read that again. The author of Attack on Titan is telling you that he experienced the same philosophical trap he built for his protagonist. The manga's central thesis — that the pursuit of absolute freedom eventually becomes its own cage — is not just something Isayama wrote about. It is something he lived through the act of writing the story itself.

This is the key the ending discourse has been missing for three years.

![Attack on Titan manga cover art featuring Eren Yeager](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Shingeki_no_kyojin_manga_vol_1.jpg/220px-Shingeki_no_kyojin_manga_vol_1.jpg)

## What Freedom Actually Means in This Story

Attack on Titan announces its theme in the opening lines of [chapter one](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_on_Titan_(Manga)): a young Eren Yeager weeps, dreaming of a world beyond the walls, insisting "I want to see and understand the world outside." Freedom — from the walls, from fear, from the predetermined fate of being devoured — is the series' animating desire.

By the final arc, Eren has acquired something like total power. He controls the Founding Titan. He has seen the future through the Attack Titan's memory-sending ability. He has literally walked backward and forward through time. And he uses this omnipotence to murder [eighty percent of humanity outside Paradis](https://www.cbr.com/why-attack-titan-changed-studio-final-season/), the Rumbling flattening cities and populations that never directly wronged the island's inhabitants.

The argument from [multiple analysts and philosophers who've written about the series](https://calxylian.com/attack-on-titan-paradox-of-fate-and-freedom/) is that this is the point: the more power Eren accumulates, the more constrained he becomes by the logic of what he's already seen himself do. The mechanism matters here: the [Attack Titan can send memories forward and backward through time](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Titan), which means that Eren's future self was already shaping his past self's choices before he understood it — a closed causal loop where the end determines the beginning and neither can be changed. He cannot deviate from the path because his future self already took it, and his future self was watching his past self do it. The Attack Titan's [memory loop is a closed causal system](https://www.academia.edu/143081083/To_The_Boy_Who_Sought_Freedom_The_Philosophy_of_Freedom_and_Social_Contract_Theory_in_Attack_on_Titan) — the most radical freedom becomes the most total determinism, because the man who can see the future can only ever confirm it.

Scholars applying compatibilism to the text have noted that [Eren and free will can be simultaneously true and false](https://www.tumblr.com/cosmicjoke/748971130535149568/thats-a-great-comparison-actually-and-yeah) — his choices feel chosen and yet were predetermined by the structure he inhabits. The series refuses to resolve this. It presents the paradox and lets it stand.

Isayama was, in his own telling, living this same paradox.

## The Author's Causal Loop

He decided the ending as a young man. He committed to it. And for years — while readers watched Eren transform from an angry teenager into something monstrous and then into something tragic, while the fandom grew to millions and the anime won global audiences — [the ending Isayama had fixed in his youth remained fixed](https://winteriscoming.net/2023/11/07/attack-on-titan-creator-hajime-isayama-shares-why-he-couldnt-change-the-ending/). "That is something I had in mind right from the get-go," he said. "The ending of the story didn't change much."

This should not be read as a defense of the ending's quality. Isayama himself, [reported by Automaton Media](https://automaton-media.com/en/news/20231120-23148/), "regretted the original story's ending for a long time" — felt such distress that he couldn't get food down his throat. He told the New York Times that [if he had been completely free, he should have been able to change it](https://fandomwire.com/if-i-was-completely-free-i-should-have-been-able-to-change-the-ending-hajime-isayama-on-being-too-tied-down-to-change-attack-on-titan/).

He wasn't free. He had already seen the end.

This is not incidental biographical trivia. It is the structure of the story mirrored in its production. Eren's tragedy is that his power to see the future removes his ability to be anything other than what the future requires of him. Isayama's creative tragedy is that his commitment to a vision from youth removed his ability to be anything other than what that vision required of him. The [anime's finale, which reworked the Armin-Eren confrontation](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-finale-fixes-manga-problem-eren-armin/) to remove Armin's troubling apparent gratitude for the Rumbling, was the version Isayama said finally brought him peace. It was, in effect, the freedom he couldn't give himself — someone else adjusted the ending he was locked into.

Watching an adaptation correct your regret because you couldn't correct it yourself: that is what it looks like to be Eren Yeager, except the author's walls were made of creative commitment rather than stone.

## What the Fandom Proved

The irony that the ending discourse generated is almost too perfect.

Attack on Titan was designed to be morally ambiguous. [Isayama, when asked how readers should interpret his work](https://newrepublic.com/article/160193/attack-titan-alt-rights-favorite-manga), said: "Being a writer, I believe it is impolite to instruct your readers the way of how to read your story." This is principled and probably honest. It is also a choice that had consequences.

The subreddit r/yeagerbomb — and its successor r/jeagerbomb, after Reddit banned the first — became a documented center of genuine radicalization. [CBR's reporting on the community](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-eren-yeager-praise-reddit-controversy/) described it as "a thriving haven of incel subculture, from its fixation on hypermasculinity to its denigration of women." Members described Eren not as a tragic figure who committed atrocity but as a divine judge. They compared the Rumbling favorably to the Holocaust. They harassed Mikasa Ackerman's voice actress. Both subreddits were eventually banned.

The [New Republic piece from 2021](https://newrepublic.com/article/160193/attack-titan-alt-rights-favorite-manga) documented the parallel phenomenon on 4chan's /pol/ board, where users had been reading the Eldians as a stand-in for white people besieged by subhuman outsiders, and Marley as a Jewish-coded oppressor convincing Eldians to hate themselves. On /pol/ since 2016, [the board archived 57 distinct threads with Attack on Titan in the subject](https://newrepublic.com/article/160193/attack-titan-alt-rights-favorite-manga). In 2014, a user with a Nazi flag called it "one of the most redpilled shows I have seen in a long time."

This is the darkest joke in Attack on Titan's reception history: the series stages, in explicit narrative terms, the exact process by which ordinary people normalize mass violence. It shows, with uncomfortable detail, how a young man convinced himself that genocide was necessary and correct. And a subset of the fandom — confronting a story that was supposed to make them examine their own capacity for that reasoning — instead finished the sentence. They agreed with Eren. They found him inspiring.

The text predicted them. It walked them through the cognitive architecture of radicalization and they took it as a roadmap.

## The Politics the Story Couldn't Settle

The series has been read as [a critique of Japanese imperialism](https://newlinesmag.com/review/a-show-that-pits-japan-against-the-world-brings-a-dark-past-to-life/), as an unconscious defense of it, as [a commentary on the cycle of colonial violence](https://spectrejournal.com/in-the-belly-of-the-beast/), and as an alt-right manifesto. These contradictory readings are not all equally valid, but they are all possible — because the text genuinely sustains each of them. [Isayama researched German cultures and Roman-Germanic conflicts](https://www.slashfilm.com/1170975/why-attack-on-titan-drew-inspiration-from-real-history/) when building the world; the German names, the aesthetics, the structure of Eldian "ethnic guilt" — these choices create a specific resonance that the narrative both uses and complicates, without fully resolving.

This is, again, the trap. When [Collider defended the series against charges of fascism](https://collider.com/attack-on-titan-fascist/) and [Anime Feminist catalogued its problems](https://www.animefeminist.com/my-fave-is-problematic-attack-on-titan/), both were correct about what they saw. The series permits contradictory readings because it was built from contradictory materials: a genuine attempt to critique cycles of violence wrapped in aesthetics that romanticize militarism, a story about the horror of ethnic persecution that required a persecuted ethnic group to be made legible through European-fascist imagery. A young Isayama reached for the most legible visual grammar he knew — militarism, walled civilization, ethnic difference as cosmic curse — and locked it into the series' foundation before he was old enough to fully understand what he was building. By the time the Marley arc made those politics explicit and genuinely complex, the foundation was already poured. He could complicate it; he couldn't change it. That is the trap again, applied to worldbuilding rather than plot.

## Why the Controversy Is the Correct Response

Here is what I think the ending discourse gets wrong: it treats the controversy as a sign that Isayama failed, when the controversy is in many ways proof that he succeeded.

A story that makes you argue this hard about whether genocide can be justified is a story that has genuinely confronted you with the question. A protagonist who generates this much ideological projection — who becomes a vessel for both anti-fascist critique and genuine fascist enthusiasm — is a protagonist drawn with enough ambiguity to function as a genuine moral stress test.

[Screenrant's retrospective, one year after the anime finale](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-anime-ending-good-bad-review/), argued that the ending holds up precisely because it refuses comfort. Eren chose mass murder, died for it, and the people who loved him had to go on living in a world still organized around the violence his death failed to resolve. There is no catharsis. There is only consequence.

That is an honest ending. It is also an ending that Isayama — by his own admission — partially failed to land in the manga, in ways the anime corrected. The [Armin scene revision](https://www.cbr.com/aot-finale-manga-vs-anime/), in particular, is significant: the manga's Armin seemed to offer something like absolution to Eren, which muddied the moral accounting. The anime's Armin doesn't. The anime's Armin calls Eren out. This small change is load-bearing — it's what makes the narrative's moral position legible.

Isayama, trapped in a cage of his own construction, needed the anime staff to do what he couldn't: be free from the original vision long enough to fix it.

## The Ending Was Always Going to Be This

If you accept the autobiographical parallel — if you understand that Isayama's experience of creative constraint was structurally identical to Eren's experience of temporal constraint — then the ending stops being a mystery. It was never going to be happy. Not because [Isayama thought a happy ending was "hokey"](https://fandomwire.com/its-not-plausible-in-the-world-were-living-in-the-upsetting-reason-hajime-isayama-gave-up-on-his-dreams-of-a-happy-ending-for-attack-on-titan-that-divided-the-fandom-forever/) or implausible, though he said both things. It was never going to be happy because the story's internal logic — the causal loop, the Attack Titan's tragic gift of foresight — demanded an ending where the dreamer's dream consumed him.

[Isayama locked both of them in](https://slashfilm.com/1439560/attack-on-titan-creator-planned-ending-start/): the boy who wanted to see the world beyond the walls, and the mangaka who wanted to tell his story. Both got exactly as far as the vision they committed to would allow.

The freedom to change it came too late. For Eren, that's the tragedy. For Isayama, it's the irony. For the reader willing to sit with the discomfort, it's what makes Attack on Titan worth taking seriously long after the final page.
