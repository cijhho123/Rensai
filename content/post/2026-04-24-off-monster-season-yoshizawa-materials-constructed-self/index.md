In Japanese folklore, shikigami are spirit-servants conjured by onmyōji and bound into physical form — [most often paper dolls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shikigami), small cut manikins that the summoner marks with intention and sends out into the world to act in their stead. They look like what they're supposed to look like. They do what they're told. But they are not what they appear to be.

Yotsugi Ononoki is a shikigami. She is also, technically, a corpse — a [human who died and was resurrected as a doll-like tsukumogami](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Yotsugi_Ononoki), a magical tool in the shape of a girl, deadpan-expressioned and straw-hat-wearing, assigned to surveil Tsukihi Araragi without revealing herself. To maintain her humanoid form, she must eat and sleep like a person. She must perform the routines of human biology or she will drift back toward the construct she is. She is, quite literally, paper learning to be flesh.

And Midori Yoshizawa — the [first female director in the Monogatari franchise's fifteen-year history](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-03-24/monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-anime-teaser-confirms-staff-2024-launch-with-/.209164), taking the reins of *Off & Monster Season* after [Akiyuki Shinbo stepped back to chief director](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midori_Yoshizawa) — repeatedly reaches for paper.

![Monogatari Series: Off & Monster Season key visual](https://monogatari-usa.com/oms/assets/img/kv.jpg)

This is not a coincidence worth noting and moving on from. It is the argument.

## What Yoshizawa Replaced and What She Kept

The received understanding of Shaft house style in the Monogatari franchise centers on Shinbo: the [Shaft head tilt](https://gamerant.com/what-is-the-shaft-head-tilt/), the geometric backgrounds, the information-dense word cards flashing too fast to read on first pass, the abstracted spaces that refuse to be rooms and insist on being psychological states. Shinbo built a formal language for a franchise about people who process reality as text — who talk their way toward and away from truth, who encounter monsters that are projections of psychological need. The [original Bakemonogatari](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2024/summer/monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season/.212230) runs on intellect and language and the feeling that everything meaningful has already been said; the visual style serves that perfectly.

Yoshizawa's sensibility is different in one specific way that matters: she is, as [Sakuga Blog's Kevin Cirugeda described her](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/08/23/monogataris-past-present-and-future-midori-yoshizawa-oms/), "a more emotionally expressive storyteller" than her predecessors at Shaft. Not illogical, not formless — but less committed to abstraction as a resting state. And the formal signature she brings to *Off & Monster Season* is physical material: paper cutouts, hand-made puppets, analog drawings, and [live-action footage integrated into animated sequences](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/12/anime-analysis-monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-2024-by-midori-yoshizawa/) without apologizing for existing there.

The question is what these choices do. The answer is that they do what the narrative of *Off & Monster Season* cannot quite do in words: they show, in the literal substance of the frame, the boundary between what is made and what is alive.

## Shikigami and Paper

You cannot fully appreciate what Yoshizawa does with Yotsugi without knowing what a shikigami is. [In the onmyōdō tradition](https://thejapanbox.com/blogs/japanese-mythology/shikigami), shikigami are summoned servants that take their visible form from paper — folded, cut, marked. They can look like birds, children, people. They can be made to look human. The point is that they are the summoner's will compressed into a physical form that convinces.

Yotsugi's specific nature sits exactly at this junction. She was [resurrected by members of an Occult Research Club](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Yotsugi_Ononoki) from a neglected corpse, bound as a shikigami, and assigned to watch over Tsukihi. Her most distinctive trait — the expressionlessness, the flat affect, the "I say this without any particular feeling" — is a consequence of her constructed nature. She has feelings. She cannot express them. The exterior does not match the interior. The paper does not fully become flesh.

When Yoshizawa reaches for paper cutouts to animate sequences involving Yotsugi, or uses puppet-like articulation rather than fluid human motion, she is not decorating. She is showing you exactly what Yotsugi is. The [Wikipedia description of Yoshizawa's style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midori_Yoshizawa) notes she "has prominently made use of unconventional materials not often used in televised anime such as paper cutouts, analog drawings, and hand-made puppets." The "unconventional" framing misses the point. These materials are conventional for what they're depicting — conventional to the folklore that created shikigami, which were always paper things pretending toward personhood.

One early *Off & Monster Season* scene puts this directly: a sequence involving multiple Tsukihis suspended [like figures in a crib mobile](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Off_%26_Monster_Season_Episode_01:_Tsukihi_Undo), light casting shadows through them, the shadows more present than the figures. Tsukihi is a phoenix — an immortal apparition wearing a human face, the wrong thing living in the right body. The shadow puppet aesthetic captures this more efficiently than any amount of dialogue: she casts more than she is, she cannot be held, the shadows she throws are not her fault.

## The Food Is Real, and That Is the Argument

The live-action footage in *Off & Monster Season* is concentrated around food.

Yoshizawa has said this is [a deliberate choice distinct from the franchise's previous live-action insertions](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/08/23/monogataris-past-present-and-future-midori-yoshizawa-oms/), creating "a new impression" — and the reason becomes clear when you know who is eating. Yotsugi Ononoki must eat and sleep to maintain her humanoid form. Food is not pleasure for her; it is maintenance of the threshold between creature and person. When the camera cuts to photorealistic food — real plates, real textures, real light — it is marking the organic world as the destination these constructed characters strain toward. The food is genuinely real on screen in a way that Yotsugi, technically, is not.

This payoff extends into the Wazamonogatari arc and its central sub-story, [*Acerola Bon Appétit*](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Wazamonogatari), which directly concerns Shinobu Oshino's pre-history — the 600 years before she met Araragi, before she was capable of forming any bond at all. The title is not subtle about what it's examining. Shinobu is a vampire; her existence is defined by consumption. What she eats is what she becomes, what she loses, what she grieves. The [Asian Movie Pulse review](https://asianmoviepulse.com/2024/12/anime-analysis-monogatari-series-off-and-monster-season-2024-by-midori-yoshizawa/) notes that the themes of "eating and being eaten" run through *Off & Monster Season* as the franchise's primary metaphor for how beings become themselves: through intake, through what they choose to absorb. The [*Doublesama* episode reviews](https://doublesama.com/monogatari-series-off-monster-season/) of the Wazamonogatari arc specifically trace how this theme accumulates across Shinobu's centuries of isolation — she is most "monstrous" not when she feeds but when she stops, when she ceases wanting anything at all.

When Yoshizawa renders the food in live-action — pulls it out of the animated frame and gives it photographic reality — she is making that thesis visible. The organic world doesn't just exist differently from these characters. It is rendered differently. The gap in medium is the gap in ontology.

## Nadeko's Windows

The [*Wrongeverytime* analysis of episode one](https://wrongeverytime.com/2024/11/25/monogatari-off-monster-season-episode-1/) points out something specific about Yoshizawa's design of Nadeko's living space: the room is laid out so that its windows naturally echo manga panel compositions. What makes this more than a visual flourish is who Nadeko is. She spent the first several arcs of the franchise being consumed — by Koyomi, by the snake goddess she became, by the gap between who she was and what was wanted from her. She was a character who existed entirely in response to external demand. Her project in *Off & Monster Season* is to escape this: to become a manga artist, to be a person who makes rather than only receives.

The window-as-panel design is Yoshizawa's answer to a directorial question that must have come early in prep: how do you show that Nadeko is becoming an artist before she is one? The answer is in the architecture. The panels are already there, waiting. Every time she looks outside, she is already inside the medium she is learning to work in. When she [confronts five past versions of herself](https://doublesama.com/monogatari-series-off-monster-season-episode-6/) across the Nadeko Draw arc, each version is framed and cropped as if within a panel. She does not enter a story; her story has been staged around her. Yoshizawa is telling you what Nadeko is before Nadeko has become it yet — not through exposition, but through the spatial grammar of the room itself.

The [*GameRant* preview](https://gamerant.com/summer-2024-preview-monogatari-series-off-monster-season/) described the new season as one that "focuses on the aftermath of Araragi's journey" for the women he encountered. Yoshizawa makes that aftermath visible by building the future into the present mise en scène: the room already knows what Nadeko will be, even when Nadeko doesn't.

## What Abstraction Cannot Do Here

Shinbo's abstraction works for Araragi because Araragi processes the world as a text problem. His encounters with oddities are linguistic negotiations; the geometric backgrounds that refuse to be real are appropriate because Araragi's engagement with the world is always mediated through language and analysis. His world feels abstract because his experience of it is.

*Off & Monster Season* centers on characters whose problem is not mediation but access — they want to feel what humans feel, eat what humans eat, make what humans make. Abstraction would be the wrong register. The paper, the puppets, the live-action food, the manga-panel windows are all strategies toward the concrete, toward what the characters themselves are reaching for but haven't arrived at. Yoshizawa doesn't switch to abstraction in these moments; she stays in the physical and asks the physical to carry the weight.

The [Beneath the Tangles first impression](https://beneaththetangles.com/2024/07/06/first-impression-monogatari-series-off-monster-season/) notes that the animation in the first episode feels "more active than the static, framed look that defined so much of Monogatari for so long" — the camera swinging in closer, the angles more human-scaled. This reading is correct but incomplete. It's not just more active. It's more present. It's in the room with the characters rather than above them, watching the architecture fold around them. This is a director choosing to be inside the question rather than framing it from outside.

## The Form Makes the Argument

There is a version of *Off & Monster Season* that simply tells the stories of Yotsugi, Nadeko, and Shinobu in standard Shaft style — witty word cards, Shaft tilts, abstract backgrounds — and reaches the same thematic conclusions through dialogue and plotting. [The AniList entry scores the show at 8.2](https://anilist.co/anime/173533/Monogatari-Series-Off--Monster-Season); the narratives work regardless of the specific formal choices.

But Yoshizawa's approach is doing something more. When the medium she uses to depict a paper-doll creature is actually paper — when she uses shadow puppetry to show the character who is not what she appears to be, when she cuts from animated fantasy to live-action food to mark the moment a constructed being tries to consume its way toward humanity — she is making an argument that exists entirely at the level of form. The script can say "Yotsugi wonders whether she is really alive." The visual language can say it by depicting her in a medium that raises the same question about its own objects.

![Shinobu Oshino character visual](https://monogatari-usa.com/oms/assets/img/c_s_shinobu.png)

The [*Sony Music Cocotame* interview series on the production handover](https://cocotame.jp/en/series/101833/) frames the new team as one that understood what it was inheriting — not just a house style but a mandate for the franchise to grow past what it had established. What Yoshizawa seems to have understood specifically is that the stories the new season needed to tell could not be told in the old visual register. Araragi's franchise was about language as evasion and revelation; these characters' stories are about bodies, about eating and making and being seen. The formal language had to shift to match.

This is why Yoshizawa is the right director for this material in a way that transcends technical continuity. It is not that she absorbed the franchise's DNA and evolved it; it is that the specific question *Off & Monster Season* is asking — what does it mean to be constructed, and when does the construction become real? — is a question her material instincts have always been trying to answer.

The [Shaft head tilt](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/shaft-head-tilt) is famous because it makes the impossible look elegant. What Yoshizawa builds is something less obvious and more exact: a visual vocabulary that makes the impossible look like exactly what it is, while you watch it try to become something else.
