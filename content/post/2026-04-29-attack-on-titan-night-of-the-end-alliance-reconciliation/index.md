Reiner Braun quotes Marco Bott to the man whose best friend Reiner helped kill, using the dead boy's own words about dialogue and reconciliation to argue for an alliance with his murderer. This is what "Night of the End" does. It is one of the most morally precise scenes in Attack on Titan, and it is almost unbearable to watch.

![Attack on Titan: Final Season Part 2 key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1948/120625l.jpg)

The [twenty-fifth episode of the Final Season](https://www.denofgeek.com/tv/attack-on-titan-season-4-episode-25-review-night-of-the-end/) gathers the remnant Scouts and the Marleyan Warriors around a campfire somewhere in the Hizuru region. What follows is a confessional session that the show has been building toward since Season 1. Annie reveals she stripped Marco of his omni-directional gear on Reiner's orders. Reiner explains that he then killed the Titan that was eating Marco — a mercy that arrived roughly three years too late. Jean, who has carried Marco's death across the entire series as his defining wound, processes this information by beating Reiner bloody on the ground. He kicks him twice. Gabi steps between them.

Then Jean agrees to the alliance.

He never forgives Reiner. He says as much, explicitly. He will always be a Scout. He will fight alongside the people responsible for his best friend's murder not because he has moved past what happened, but because he has concluded that stopping the Rumbling matters more than the accounting he is owed.

This is the show's most honest scene. It is also, for many viewers, its most troubling.

## What the Scene Costs to Stage

The confrontation at the campfire is often discussed as [the emotional payoff](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/attack-on-titan-season-4-reveals-truth-marco-death/) of a five-season mystery — who killed Marco, and how. What it actually is goes further than payoff. It is a formal statement about what the coalition at the center of Final Season Part 2 actually is and isn't.

It isn't reconciliation. Reconciliation requires forgiveness, and [Jean makes clear he offers none](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-jean-revenge-marco-death/). He punishes Reiner physically because there is no legal framework for what Reiner did, and because hitting someone is the only language available to him that communicates the size of what he lost. Then he stops — not because the punishment is proportionate, because it isn't and couldn't be, but because Gabi shields Reiner with her body and something in Jean recognizes that he is now inflicting on Gabi the same violence he is raging against.

What the alliance IS: a coalition of shared desperation, entered into by people who have done unforgivable things to each other and who need each other to survive a worse disaster. [The Rumbling is not a metaphor at this point in the narrative](https://aniviewer.com/the-rumblings-true-cost-aot-genocide-ethics/) — it is a death toll calculated in billions, an extinction event moving at infantry pace across every continent. Against that scale, Jean's grief for Marco is real and legitimate and insufficient to act on.

Isayama has, through the logic of the plot, made sure there is no version of "Jean gets to mourn properly and still make it to Fort Salta in time." The accounting has been deferred by catastrophe. The campfire scene is the show acknowledging that deferral honestly rather than pretending it isn't happening.

## The Unbearable Detail

Reiner quotes Marco to Jean. During the confession, as he tries to explain why the alliance is possible despite everything that has happened, Reiner invokes the words Marco said in his final moments: *"We haven't even tried to talk this out yet."* The irony is explicit — Marco said these words right before Reiner let him die, and Reiner has the audacity to repeat them as an argument for communication and coalition.

The scene lets this sit without comment. It does not frame Reiner's invocation as hypocrisy or as wisdom. It is both, simultaneously. [Marco's words were right when he said them](https://www.denofgeek.com/tv/attack-on-titan-season-4-episode-25-review-night-of-the-end/) — the tragedy of his death was that it prevented exactly the kind of dialogue he was requesting. And Reiner deploying them now is grotesque and also the only possible move. He has nothing else to offer. He cannot give Marco back. He can offer Marco's argument about the alternative to violence and hope that it means something coming from the mouth of the man who ignored it.

Jean does not tell him to shut up. He listens. Then he agrees.

This is what distinguishes "Night of the End" from every other alliance formation in shonen anime. The genre convention is that enemies become allies through a moment of shared vulnerability — a battle where someone saves someone's life, a revelation that humanizes the opponent, a speech about a common dream. [The usual shonen toolkit](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Analysis/AttackOnTitan) works by erasing or dissolving the grievance. Jean's alliance with Reiner explicitly preserves the grievance. He will never forgive Reiner. He is choosing to act anyway. These are not contradictions — they are the specific shape of how adults navigate moral catastrophe.

## The Debt the Scene Can't Address

"Night of the End" does what it can. It confronts individual crimes: Marco, specifically. What it cannot address — and the show, to its credit, does not try to force it to address — is the systemic account.

Reiner and Bertholdt brought down Wall Maria. Bertholdt is already dead by the time this alliance forms — he cannot be held accountable at all. The fall of Wall Maria did not kill a single person the way Marco was killed. It killed hundreds of thousands through starvation and Titan attack and [the military's subsequent sacrifice of civilians to retake the inner walls](https://appuals.com/aot-s4-part-2-recap/). This is not a personal crime of the kind you can process around a campfire. It is a crime of a different order — structural, political, military — and there is no version of the confessional scene that can accommodate it. The math doesn't work. Jean beating Reiner bloody over Marco is emotionally honest. Jean beating Reiner for Wall Maria would be absurd — you cannot punch someone for a war crime of that magnitude and then shake hands.

The show does not try. [Levi Ackermann, who had every reason to kill Annie Leonhart the moment they were on the same boat](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-alliance-methods-backlash-explained/), spends much of the alliance arc physically incapacitated — not because the show forgot about their history, but because there is nothing useful Levi confronting Annie can produce inside a tactical emergency. The confrontation between Levi and Annie would take longer than they have. It would require a kind of processing that the Rumbling does not permit. The show stages the avoidance honestly: Levi is injured, asleep, functionally absent from the scenes Annie occupies. The confrontation hasn't happened. The narrative's honesty is in admitting it hasn't, not in manufacturing a version of it that would feel false.

This is the most criticized structural choice in Part 2, and it is also the most defensible. [Fans who wanted Annie to face accountability](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-alliance-methods-backlash-explained/) are not wrong that her arc lacks it. The show is not pretending she has been held accountable. It is saying that accountability, in a crisis of this scale, has been displaced by necessity. That displacement is not endorsement. It is the tragedy of the situation.

## What the Alliance Is Actually Arguing

The coalition at the heart of Final Season Part 2 is not morally coherent in the way that shonen alliances usually are. It is not built on shared values, or on the resolution of past enmity, or on the kind of [mutual recognition that turns enemies into nakama](https://thegeekiary.com/attack-on-titan-the-final-chapters-review-part-2/121530). It is built on shared catastrophe and the specific calculation that the alternative is worse.

This is not a compromise of the series' moral sophistication — it is an extension of it. Attack on Titan has spent four seasons establishing that the categories of "hero" and "villain" are manufactured by perspective and circumstance, that the warriors who destroyed Paradis had been told they were saving the world, that the Scouts who fight with honor are fighting inside a military-political system that routinely sacrifices civilians. Notably, the Warriors DO achieve reconciliation among themselves — [Reiner and Annie reach an accord before the final battle](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/attack-on-titan-manga-chapter-132-annie-reiner-reconciliation-titans/), two people who share the same guilt forgiving each other because they can. The Scout-Warrior reconciliation is structurally different: it cannot happen because the sides' crimes are not symmetric. The alliance is not the resolution of that asymmetry. It is what that asymmetry looks like when everyone who embodies it is still alive and needs to make a tactical decision.

Jean will never forgive Reiner. He has said so. He fights at his side anyway. The show does not frame this as Jean finding inner peace, or as the power of friendship, or as growth. It frames it as a man choosing to act on the only value he has left that hasn't been compromised: the desire to stop something worse.

[Director Yuichiro Hayashi, reflecting on his growth through the production](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-aot-final-season-part-2-interview/), noted that by Part 2 he had become "much more familiar with Attack on Titan" — comfortable enough to trust the material to sustain its own weight without underlining. The campfire scene is the evidence. It does not tell you how to feel about Jean's decision. It gives you the decision and leaves you with the discomfort of understanding it.

## The Review That Called It the Weakest Adaptation

One episode review [described "Night of the End" as "the weakest adaptation"](https://thereviewmonster.blog/2022/03/15/attack-on-titan-the-final-season-episode-25-night-of-the-end-review-the-weakest-adaptation/) of its manga chapter — arguing that MAPPA's version lost the nuance and visual expressiveness that Isayama's original panels conveyed in the characters' body language and facial expressions around the fire. The criticism has specific merit: Jean's emotional oscillation in the manga, moving between rage and exhaustion and reluctant clarity within a few pages, reportedly lands with more textural weight than the anime equivalent. The adaptation flattens some of what makes the scene cruel and specific.

But this is a separate claim from whether the scene does its job. Even at reduced expressiveness, "Night of the End" makes the argument it needs to make. [MAPPA's growth between Part 1 and Part 2](https://www.cbr.com/attack-on-titan-aot-final-season-part-2-interview/) is visible in the production's willingness to let difficult silence sit — to not push the score too hard during the campfire exchange, to let Jean's decision register in a static close-up rather than a swelling theme. The adaptation may not be the fullest version of the scene. It is still the version that Part 2 cannot function without.

Without "Night of the End," the alliance is inexplicable. With it, the alliance is morally coherent in a way that doesn't require resolution, only honesty. Jean knows who Reiner is. Jean has not forgiven him. Jean is fighting alongside him. Three true sentences. No contradiction between them.

That's what Part 2 is actually about, underneath the imagery of the Rumbling and the scale of the casualties and the tactical question of whether stopping Eren is even possible. It is about what a person can do after unforgivable things have happened, when the accounting is legitimate but the catastrophe is larger. It is about the specific kind of coalition that forms not from shared values but from shared stakes — people who cannot repair what has happened between them, choosing to act on what is still left to protect.

Marco's words, in Reiner's mouth, producing a result Marco never got to see. That is the show's cruelest and most precise image for what the alliance costs, and why it happens anyway.
