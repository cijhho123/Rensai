In 2019, the mangaka Kotoyama heard a hip-hop track by a duo called Creepy Nuts. The song was called "Yofukashi no Uta" — roughly, "Song of the Night Walkers." He recalled the reaction immediately: ["I can't name the series anything else."](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) (That's from a 2020 Weekly Shonen Sunday promotional interview, since translated by fans — his words, in context.)

He reached out, got permission, and the manga was named after the song before he drew a single panel of it.

Most coverage of Call of the Night treats this as a fun origin story — a coincidental alignment between a track and a manga's aesthetic. That reading undersells what happened. The song didn't just inspire the name. It was the key that told Kotoyama what kind of work he was making, and everything that followed — the specific sensibility, the emotional register, the nighttime urban world that feels simultaneously mundane and alive — flows from that discovery. Call of the Night is a hip-hop work wearing vampire manga clothes.

## Who Creepy Nuts Are

Understanding why this matters requires understanding who Creepy Nuts are and what they represent in Japanese music.

[The duo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creepy_Nuts) consists of R-Shitei, a battle rapper who won Japan's Ultimate MC Battle three consecutive years in a row, and DJ Matsunaga, a turntablist who in 2019 won the [DMC World DJ Championship](https://www.billboard.com/music/rb-hip-hop/creepy-nuts-japan-cover-1235856697/) — not Japan's championship, the world championship. By any technical measure, they are among the most skilled practitioners in their respective crafts. They were not mainstream when Kotoyama heard them. They were underground credibility, outsider excellence, craft-above-all.

That's the register. Hip-hop — especially battle rap and turntablism — is a world of people who are very good at something that the mainstream didn't initially want. It celebrates the kid who practiced in his room until the technique was undeniable. It privileges authenticity over polish, substance over presentation, real over performed. When R-Shitei describes [their artistic approach](https://www.billboard.com/music/rb-hip-hop/creepy-nuts-japan-cover-1235856697/) as "expressing what we want to get out and releasing the pent-up feelings" rather than chasing rankings, he's describing a philosophy that every battle rapper learns in their first open mic.

Kotoyama has been [hooked on hip-hop since middle school](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) — Rip Slyme, Kick the Can Crew, the golden era of Japanese hip-hop that built a street aesthetic and youth audience in the early 2000s. This wasn't background taste. It shaped how he thinks about what it means for something to be genuinely cool rather than just aesthetically pleasing.

## The Sonic Architecture of the Anime

When the anime was greenlit in 2022, Creepy Nuts were commissioned to write the [opening theme](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Daten). The song they produced was "Daten" — 堕天, which translates to "Fallen Angel" or "Fallen Heavens." And the ending theme? The original [2019 "Yofukashi no Uta"](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Yofukashi_no_Uta) that had inspired the manga's name in the first place.

Consider what this creates structurally. Each episode of Call of the Night begins with "Daten" — the sensation of falling, the exhilaration of dropping into the bottomless night. It ends with "Yofukashi no Uta" — the title track, the night-walkers' anthem, the place you arrive when you stop trying to return to morning. [Director Tomoyuki Itamura described](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Daten) wanting the opening to convey "the feeling of floating" — the mix of uncertainty and exhilaration in the "bottomlessness of the night" and the "endless expanse of the city."

The show opens with falling. It closes with arrival. The journey between them is the episode. That's not incidental — that's a structured emotional argument delivered entirely through music.

The duo became so embedded in the series that they [made cameo appearances in Episode 6](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2022-08-10/hip-hop-duo-creepy-nuts-guest-star-in-call-of-the-night-episode-6/.188561) — voicing two men who attempt to pick up Nazuna at a night pool. They appeared as animated versions of themselves, inserted into the world they helped create. For Season 2, [Creepy Nuts returned](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-06-05/creepy-nuts-return-for-call-of-the-night-season-2-anime-opening-theme-song-in-new-trailer/.225228) with new tracks that track the series' thematic evolution. This isn't a music licensing arrangement. This is a creative partnership where the musicians are as load-bearing as the animation studio.

## Hip-Hop Aesthetics vs. Gothic Aesthetics

Most vampire fiction deploys Gothic aesthetics — the aristocratic, the supernatural, the architecturally grandiose. Even contemporary vampire manga that modernizes the mythology usually retains some trace of the Gothic register: the ancient bloodline, the castle, the hierarchy, the horror.

Call of the Night does almost none of this. Nazuna's "coffin" is a futon in a cluttered apartment. Her clients come to her through what functions as a massage booking arrangement. The nighttime Ko and Nazuna inhabit is populated by [convenience stores](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2022/8/10/call-of-the-night-theme-song-artist-creepy-nuts-get-guest-voice-spot-in-latest-episode), vending machines, empty overhead walkways, 24-hour family restaurants, and the specific bleached-out glow of commercial signage on empty streets. This is not a nightmare. This is a Tuesday at 2 AM in any medium-density Japanese city.

Hip-hop knows this space. Hip-hop is the art form that found beauty and meaning in the underpass, the parking lot, the corner store. It's the tradition that built entire aesthetic worlds from the ordinary and the overlooked — not by romanticizing poverty or struggle, but by insisting that these are real spaces inhabited by real people who have real things to say. The [translated lyrics of "Yofukashi no Uta"](https://lyricstranslate.com/en/yofukashi-no-uta-hymn-nightwalkers.html) open with exactly this logic: "Here's a hymn for nightwalkers / Let the moonlight shine on us / Let the blue light daze our eyes / Two sides neon signs have revealed." That's not Gothic atmosphere. That's a convenience store at 1 AM described as sacred. Kotoyama's nighttime is hip-hop's spatial logic applied to manga. The night isn't magical because it's supernatural. It's magical because it's the only time the world empties out enough to feel like yours.

## What This Explains That Vampire Analysis Alone Doesn't

A lot of writing about Call of the Night focuses on the vampire mythology — the transformation conditions, the emotional economy of biting, what it means for Ko to want to become a vampire. That analysis is valid. But it doesn't fully explain the series' specific emotional texture: why it feels cool without trying to feel cool, why the romance never tips into either wish-fulfillment or tragic inevitability, why the secondary vampire characters all have a specific quality of being real people who happen to be ancient.

Hip-hop explains it. [R-Shitei has described](https://www.billboard.com/music/rb-hip-hop/creepy-nuts-japan-cover-1235856697/) his creative approach as "expressing what we want to get out and releasing the pent-up feelings" rather than chasing rankings or playing to expectations — and what he's naming is hip-hop's foundational value system: realness over performance. Something is either real or it isn't, and an audience trained in hip-hop can tell immediately. Kotoyama's manga operates on this frequency. Ko's desire for vampirism is treated as genuine but also examined — the series is comfortable saying "you feel this way, but is this what you actually want?" That's not a Gothic question. Gothic is about fate and desire. This is a question about authenticity, and it's a hip-hop question.

Nazuna's refusal to be either a predator or a romantic ideal — her insistence on keeping things transactional until she can't anymore — is the behavior of someone who learned the hard way that performed emotion is worthless. [Kotoyama has said](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) he wanted to "insert the extraordinary into the ordinary" using the vampire as the fantasy element within everyday settings. What he's describing is hip-hop's fundamental operation: take the real, and show what's already extraordinary about it. Even the [CBR retrospective on Kotoyama's career](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-creator-dagashi-kashi-cooking-romance-anime/) notes the tonal shift between Dagashi Kashi and Call of the Night, from light edutainment to atmospheric melancholy — and that shift maps directly to moving from a craft-forward series to an authenticity-forward one.

## The Song Knew What the Manga Would Be

The 2019 Creepy Nuts track has its own emotional logic independent of the manga. It's about people who stay up when the world goes to sleep, who find their life begins when normal life ends. There's no ideology in it, no manifesto — just a recognition that some people's real hours are other people's empty hours, and that there's a community in that overlap.

Kotoyama heard that and recognized his manga. Not a manga he had already planned — a manga he now knew how to make. The song was the thesis statement, and he spent the next six years writing the argument.

The fact that Creepy Nuts then performed both bookends of the anime adaptation — opening and closing, falling and arriving — completes a kind of loop that most anime never achieve with their music. [The ANN review of Episode 6](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/call-of-the-night/episode-6/.188618) noted the degree to which music was inseparable from the series' atmosphere, not background but structural. The music is structural because it was structural before the manga existed. Everything else was built toward the song.

The series carries that origin in every panel. You can hear it in the empty streets. The song described a hymn for nightwalkers — [and every episode ends with it](https://animecorner.me/call-of-the-night-op%EF%BC%86ed-singers-creepy-nuts-to-make-cameo-appearance-in-episode-6/), as if to confirm that Ko is still awake, still out there, still becoming something real.
