Ko Yamori walks out of his apartment at 1 AM in the first chapter and meets a vampire. By chapter two, he has a goal: become one too. The conditions are straightforward. Nazuna bites him. He loves her when she does. He transforms. This is presented not as romance but almost as transaction — a project with legible terms, appealing precisely to a fourteen-year-old who has stopped participating in every other project society has offered him.

Ko doesn't become a vampire. [Kotoyama's manga ran for exactly 200 chapters](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-11-15/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-200th-chapter/.204474) in [Weekly Shōnen Sunday from August 2019 to January 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night), [won the 68th Shogakukan Manga Award in the shōnen category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) — an award not typically given to manga where the emotional climax is two people lying in the snow, indefinitely — and the transformation Ko announced in volume one never arrives. What does arrive is harder to summarize and considerably more interesting.

## The Transaction Logic

The [transformation condition](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Vampires) is this: a vampire bites a human, and if that human genuinely loves the vampire biting them, the human transforms. No magic words. No blood moon. Just the presence of real feeling completing what the bite alone can't. Ko understands this as a romantic project with a deliverable: make Nazuna want to bite him, become someone capable of loving her, collect the result.

What makes this framing useful to Ko is that it has rules. He has spent his middle school years opting out of every expectation placed on him — school performance, social participation, the relentless machinery of model-student life — and here, finally, is a requirement he genuinely wants to meet. The night world has its own logic, and Ko can follow it on his own terms. [As the series establishes Ko's initial psychology](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori), he's not acting out or rebelling; he's retreating into the only space that doesn't require him to perform.

[Nazuna, for her part, is structurally built to fail the condition](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa). Centuries old in lived time, trapped in an eighteen-year-old's body, she is deeply unfamiliar with genuine attachment. Her relationship economy is parasocial by design: warmth you can rent, intimacy that stops just short of real. [Her bite-for-cuddle arrangement with Ko is professional from her perspective](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa) — she engineered the transformation condition partly because she believed she'd never be able to meet it. The one thing she needs to give him is the one thing she has never given anyone.

And then, eventually, she does.

## The Second Condition

[Three years before the final chapter](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Chapter_200), Ko and Nazuna separate. Not because the love failed. Because it succeeded, and the series revealed that success carries a cost nobody mentioned in the setup.

The second rule [exists in the mythology of the vampires' world](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Vampires): ancient, unconfirmed, believed rather than proven. If a vampire loves the human whose blood they drink, the bite is lethal. Not transforming. Lethal.

Hold the two conditions side by side:

- **Transformation condition:** human loves vampire + vampire bites = transformation
- **Lethal condition:** vampire loves human + vampire bites = death

The first requires Ko's love. The second is triggered by Nazuna's. Once genuine mutual feeling exists — the emotional destination the entire 200-chapter arc was building toward — these conditions converge into an impossibility. [Nazuna cannot safely bite Ko anymore](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained). The transformation goal has not failed; it has been made structurally unavailable by the exact success of the relationship it required.

The structure of the series depends on this tension existing from the first chapter. The lethal condition isn't a late twist; it's the shadow that the transformation condition casts once the relationship succeeds. Every chapter in which Ko tries to make Nazuna love him is also, retrospectively, a chapter in which he was building toward the exact state that would make his original goal unavailable.

## What Actually Changed Ko

Here is what the narrative doesn't dramatize as a climax but treats as inevitable consequence: [Ko is not fully human by the ending](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori).

He is a half-vampire — not through the transformation condition, which was never triggered, but through sustained, full engagement with the night world over years. Nazuna's bites accumulated. Crises forced him past human limits. [A specific incident surfaced an unprecedented ability: hemokinesis, the capacity to control his own blood, something regular vampires don't possess](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori). His half-vampire state can be triggered deliberately through physical pain; [he uses an ear-piercing kit to control the transitions](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori). Even the vampires in the series treat him as anomalous — something the mythology didn't account for.

The series is saying something quiet and precise: you don't become what you want to be by completing a transaction. You become it by living. Ko didn't transform because he loved Nazuna enough to trigger a rule. He changed because he chose the night world fully and the night world changed him in return. The transaction logic he entered with — love her, get bitten, become a vampire — was always the wrong frame. Transformation happened anyway. Differently. On terms nobody negotiated.

[Early critical reception of the series](https://starcrossedanime.com/yofukashi-no-uta-anime-review-68-100/) often noted that it felt like a vampire romance that wasn't particularly interested in being a vampire romance. That reading was right for the wrong reason: it's not disinterested in vampires; it's interested in what vampires reveal about the humans drawn to them. Ko's desire to become one was never about power or immortality. It was about the permission structure — proof that there was a version of existence available to him that the school system hadn't already designated.

## The Ending, Exactly

[Chapter 200](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Chapter_200), titled "Final Night: And Then," opens three years after the separation. Nazuna has [traveled the world meeting vampires and humans](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained), eventually [settling in a village near the Arctic](https://siliconera.com/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-chapter-200/). Ko finds her. He broke their agreement — they were supposed to stay apart until Nazuna's feelings faded. [She's furious that he came](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained).

And then she tries to bite him.

And he stops her.

This is what Ko's half-vampire training was for. Nazuna's love for Ko expresses itself as a compulsion toward his blood — the same compulsion that would kill him if acted on fully. [Ko's developed abilities let him physically prevent her when the impulse surges](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori). The relationship's form is now literal: she is drawn toward him with a desire that could destroy him; he has built the capacity to hold that at arm's length. Not forever. Long enough.

[She asks him to play tag with her until the day they die](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained). Then introduces him to the local children as "the man I love."

Critical and fan reception to the ending split on exactly this point. [A YouTube video titled "Call of the Night Ending Sucked" landed shortly after chapter 200 dropped](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OEsygFfZvis) and framed the open-endedness as a failure of nerve — the series refused to pay off its own premise. The [AniList discussion thread](https://anilist.co/forum/thread/71224) surfaced the same complaint: too suspended, too deliberately unresolved. But the criticism assumes the premise was "two people in love get together" — which was Ko's stated goal, not the series' actual question. [The Lost in Anime series review](https://lostinanime.com/2025/09/yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night-season-2-12-end-and-series-review/) took the more useful position: the ending is the series finally being honest about what their relationship's form has to be. The series earned this openness. The "tag" framing isn't a deflection; it's the series being finally and completely literal about what their relationship is. Chase and distance and the ongoing choice to keep playing.

This is not a romance that withholds the happy ending. It's a romance that redefines what happy looks like when two people genuinely cannot close the distance fully without risk. Their togetherness is sustainable but precarious, which is — if you have ever been in any relationship worth having — not actually so different from the rest of us.

## The Form the Story Chose

[The series was named after a Creepy Nuts song called "Yofukashi no Uta" — a track about insomnia, restlessness, and the particular quality of being awake when everyone else has gone quiet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night). Kotoyama confirmed the naming was intentional, and [Creepy Nuts returned for Season 2's theme songs as well](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-06-05/creepy-nuts-return-for-call-of-the-night-season-2-anime-opening-theme-song-in-new-trailer/.225178/). The connection isn't decorative: hip-hop builds language out of the experience of not fitting into the available categories, and Ko's entire arc is organized around the same problem — finding a vocabulary for a kind of existence that the daylight world has no slot for.

[The LIDENFILMS anime adaptation](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-season-2-need-to-know/) translated this into atmosphere — neon, night-city glow, the specific weight of empty streets — and the [anime's late-night Noitamina broadcast slot on Fuji TV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) made the form match the content. But the manga was always the more precise vehicle for the series' argument, because the manga can do what Ko is doing when he walks out of his apartment: give you access to the interior, the exact quality of the feeling, the thinking that happens in the absence of an audience.

![Call of the Night manga cover volume 1](https://s4.anilist.co/file/anilistcdn/media/manga/cover/large/bx111233-bQQV6KWMBXz4.jpg)

The transformation Ko wanted was always the wrong unit of measurement. The question was never whether he'd become a vampire. It was whether he'd become someone who could live with wanting things — someone who could choose a life rather than drift in the absence of one. [By the time Kotoyama's 200-chapter run concluded](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-11-15/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-200th-chapter/.204474), Ko had that. The goal he articulated in chapter two dissolved into something he couldn't have named: a half-state, an open future, a woman in the Arctic snow asking him to keep running from her.

The night was never a destination. It was the space where Ko discovered he was already somewhere.
