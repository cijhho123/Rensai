There's a moment in episode seven of *Nisemonogatari* when everything the show has been doing crystallizes into a single image: Deishuu Kaiki, standing across from Senjougahara Hitagi, calmly explaining that the curse he sold Karen Araragi was a fake. There was no flame-wreathe bee. There was no supernatural affliction. Karen got sick because he told her she would, and she believed him completely. The girl who styled herself a "hero of justice," who went looking for a monster to fight — she was felled by three days of psychosomatic fever because her imagination was that susceptible to suggestion.

Kaiki laughs about it. Not cruelly. Just factually. He's a con man who sold a fake curse to a fake hero, and the fake curse worked exactly as well as a real one would have.

![Nisemonogatari key visual](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/5/73199.jpg)

This is where *Nisemonogatari* — often dismissed as the weakest entry in the [Monogatari franchise](https://anilist.co/anime/11597/Nisemonogatari) for its self-indulgent pacing and escalated fanservice — actually earns its title. *Nisemonogatari* means "fake story," and the fakeness it's most interested in isn't the supernatural kind. It's the performed kind. The kind where you wear a costume long enough that you forget you're wearing it.

## A Con Man Who Tells You He's a Con Man

Kaiki Deishuu is introduced in the Karen Bee arc as a small-time fraud selling counterfeit charms to middle schoolers. [According to the Bakemonogatari wiki](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Deishuu_Kaiki), he's a specialist in "imposter oddities" — fake supernatural phenomena designed to exploit the credulous. He doesn't believe in the paranormal. He's never encountered a genuine apparition. Everything he sells is manufactured, and he knows it.

This makes him the villain. Or it should. The problem is that Kaiki immediately undercuts the villain role by being the only character in the show who says exactly what he is. He tells Araragi he's a con man. He tells Senjougahara he's motivated by money. He doesn't pretend to have principles he doesn't have. When [a comprehensive character analysis from EAT Monogatari](https://eatmonogatari.wordpress.com/2018/07/21/a-comprehensive-analysis-of-kaiki-deishuu/) describes Kaiki's most defining trait as "conviction — a strict personal honor that contradicts his villainous persona," it's identifying something that the show uses structurally: the character who admits to being a fraud is the one who never frauds himself.

Compare that to Karen Araragi, the ostensible protagonist of the Karen Bee arc. Karen is a self-proclaimed hero of justice who patterns herself after tokusatsu shows, pursues vigilante justice in her neighborhood, and describes her mission in the grandest possible terms. [As the Bakemonogatari wiki notes](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Karen_Araragi), she "loves to do what's right and just" with a fervor that her brother Koyomi explicitly characterizes as a kind of game — a performance of heroism that Karen has confused with the real thing.

Araragi's verdict on his sisters' vigilantism is blunt: they're fakes. Not because their intentions are bad, but because they've adopted an aesthetic of heroism rather than arrived at a genuine moral code through their own experience. They play hero. [One fan analysis](http://takingabitofacloserlook.blogspot.com/2015/02/monogatari-series-nisemonogatari-and.html) puts it precisely: Karen "operates from idealistic certainty without understanding consequences," unlike Koyomi who learned his limitations through painful supernatural encounters.

The flame-wreathe bee is the show's proof. Kaiki targets Karen specifically because she's the kind of person who would believe a fake curse — her heroic imagination is the vulnerability he exploits. As [one episode analysis notes](https://umaiyomu.wordpress.com/2020/07/27/nisemonogatari-episode-7-karen-bee-part-seven-pt-3/), Kaiki explicitly states he targeted Karen because she had "an active imagination" — someone "susceptible to suggestion." He tells her he inflicted the bee on her; she fills in the rest herself. A genuine hero, one with actual self-knowledge, would be harder to con. Karen's certainty about her own righteousness is the exact flaw that makes her helpless.

## The Villain More Honest Than the Heroes

*Nisemonogatari*'s most structurally interesting move is setting Kaiki against Kagenui Yozoru, the arc's other significant antagonist, and letting the comparison destroy the idea that there's a coherent moral boundary between them.

Kagenui is a supernatural contractor who hunts immortal apparitions — beings she views as violations of the natural order. She operates from an absolute ethical framework: the supernatural shouldn't exist where it shouldn't exist, wrong should be righted, rules should be enforced. She treats her own sense of rightness as if it were objective law. [The Terminal Anime analysis of Nisemonogatari](https://terminalanime.wordpress.com/2012/03/11/nisemonogataris-true-protagonist-and-his-thematic-purpose/) frames this correctly: Kagenui holds "a firm, black and white view where good is good, evil is evil, and wrong must be righted and brought to justice." That certainty is the same flaw Karen has, just operating with actual supernatural power behind it.

Kaiki doesn't believe in objective law. He understands moral systems as arbitrary arrangements that serve whoever constructed them. He values money — his own stated philosophy — because money is "valuable yet replaceable," unlike relationships, which he avoids because they're "valuable and irreplaceable." This sounds like the philosophy of a nihilist, but it's actually the philosophy of someone who has thought hard about what he actually cares about. [As CBR notes](https://www.cbr.com/monogatari-deishuu-kaiki-most-perplexing-characters-not-araragi/), Kaiki "never breaks" his promises — his word, once given, holds absolutely. The man who admits he'll lie to you will not lie to you about the things that matter.

Kagenui would annihilate Tsukihi Araragi — an immortal phoenix who has assumed the identity of a human girl — because Tsukihi's existence violates the categories Kagenui has decided are correct. [Tsukihi, as the wiki explains](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihi_Araragi), is literally a supernatural entity that has replaced a human child, holding borrowed memories of a family that isn't biologically hers. By any strict reading, Kagenui is right: Tsukihi is a fake. But Kagenui's rightness is worse than useless because it's completely indifferent to the actual human consequences of acting on it. Her justice is perfect and therefore monstrous.

Kaiki understands this. He understands it because he's spent his career studying how people's beliefs make them vulnerable. He knows that the categories we use to sort "real" from "fake," "authentic" from "counterfeit," are constructions — useful sometimes, catastrophically rigid when applied without regard for what they destroy.

## What the Fake Bee Actually Proves

Here's the detail that keeps nagging: the fake bee worked.

Kaiki inflicted Karen with a psychosomatic illness through hypnosis and suggestion. [The episode analysis on the Bakemonogatari wiki](https://bakemonogatari.fandom.com/wiki/Nisemonogatari_Episode_07:_Karen_Bee,_Part_7) notes the show's deliberate ambiguity on this point — Kaiki later laughs at the idea that Koyomi could take half of Karen's sickness upon himself, because "it was a delusion to begin with." And yet somehow, Koyomi does take half. The fever distributes itself between them. The fake curse behaves like a real one once it's been believed into existence.

[The MDZ Anime blog's episode breakdown](https://mdzanime.wordpress.com/2012/03/17/on-going-thoughts-nisemonogatari-1111/) captures the series' conclusion on this: "Kaiki, the fake villain, is actually the greatest educator" in the series. What he teaches, through the bee, is the thing the franchise will keep returning to: belief is its own kind of causality. Karen's fever was real. The sickness was real. The mechanism was fake. Does the mechanism matter?

This is the question that makes *Nisemonogatari*'s title a thesis rather than just a descriptor. The show isn't interested in cataloguing what's fake; it's interested in what happens when fakes become load-bearing. Karen's heroism is fake but it produces real heroism — she fights back against Koyomi despite being sick, refuses to capitulate, demonstrates actual courage. Tsukihi's humanity is fake but she is genuinely loved and genuinely loves. Kaiki's curse was fake but it genuinely afflicted someone.

[The review at "Everything is bad for you"](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2020/09/12/a-review-of-nisemonogatari/) phrases the series' resolution clearly: "authenticity of intention matters more than categorical truth." That's Kaiki's thesis, stated by the person who argues against him. The con man was right about what matters.

## The Seed

Whatever one makes of *Nisemonogatari*'s [initially divisive reception](https://gamerant.com/nisemonogatari-divisive-criticism/) — the pacing is deliberately slack, the fanservice maximalist in ways that occasionally crowd out the thematic work — the franchise would later redeem this investment completely. In *Koimonogatari*, Kaiki becomes the protagonist and narrator — and immediately announces that [nothing he's about to say should be believed](https://medium.com/@filmvore/a-reliably-unrealiable-narrator-takes-over-in-koimonogatari-94931ee9726e). He warns you he's going to lie to you, then proceeds to save the people he cares about through a series of sacrifices that contradict everything he claims to value. He ends his arc by telling you he was murdered — which you know is a lie because he appears in later arcs — and in doing so demonstrates that the most truthful thing he can do is lie transparently. By the time *Koimonogatari* ends, [Kaiki has become arguably the most beloved character in the entire series](https://gamerant.com/monogatari-series-best-arc/), the one fans who entered through *Bakemonogatari* hadn't expected to feel anything about.

All of that transformation is planted in *Nisemonogatari*. It's planted in the moment when a middle-aged con man in a funeral suit stands across from a terrifying supernatural contractor and is clearly, self-evidently the more ethical person in the room. It's planted when his fake curse turns out to be real enough to matter. It's planted when he explains, without embarrassment, that he sold fake charms to middle schoolers for money, that he doesn't believe in the supernatural, that you shouldn't trust him — and then proves himself exactly as trustworthy as he said he'd be, within the limits he specified.

[The Villains Wiki entry on Kaiki](https://villains.fandom.com/wiki/Deishuu_Kaiki) categorizes him as a villain who "struggles to know where his lies end and his actual self starts." That framing is close but inverted. Kaiki knows exactly where his lies are. What he doesn't know — what the show uses him to demonstrate — is that the distinction between the lie and the self is less stable than the heroes around him would like to believe.

Karen performs heroism until she accidentally becomes heroic. Tsukihi performs being human until she actually is one. Kaiki performs being a con man so thoroughly that he accidentally becomes the person most immune to being conned.

The fake story's most honest character is the liar. That's not a paradox. That's the point.
