In 2017, two separate manga adaptations of the same light novel began serializing simultaneously in competing magazines. Neither publisher had secured exclusivity. The original author, Natsu Hyuga, [posted on social media](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/convention/2025/apothecaries-and-assassins-q-a-with-minoji-kurata/.227624) that she didn't know why there were two, expressing confusion about the situation she found herself in. What followed was a seven-year natural experiment: two skilled artists working from identical source material, making entirely different editorial choices about what mattered most.

The result tells you more about [*The Apothecary Diaries*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Apothecary_Diaries) than any single review could.

## The Accident That Became a Comparative Study

Nekokurage's version ran in [Square Enix's Monthly Big Gangan](https://squareenixmangaandbooks.square-enix-games.com/en-us/series/the-apothecary-diaries), targeting a shonen-adjacent readership with polished, expressive character art and tight pacing that leaned into the Maomao-Jinshi relationship. Minoji Kurata's version ran in [Shogakukan's Monthly Sunday Gene-X](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/The_Apothecary_Diaries_(Manga)), taking a more mature visual register and spending more pages on the medical cases, political intrigue, and court machinations that frame the mysteries. Both adaptations began in 2017. Neither knew about the other's ongoing work in any meaningful editorial sense.

By the time Western audiences encountered the property through the 2023 anime adaptation — [which used Nekokurage's manga as its visual template](https://gamerant.com/apothecary-diaries-two-manga-versions-differences/) — the choice had been made for them. Most people who know *The Apothecary Diaries* know it primarily as a romance with mystery elements, not the other way around. That's a consequence of a specific adaptation pipeline, not a neutral description of what the source material actually is.

## What Each Version Chose to Emphasize

The differences break cleanly along two axes. Nekokurage's manga is the more romance-forward of the two: [Jinshi's charisma and his dynamic with Maomao](https://epicstream.com/article/apothecary-diaries-two-manga-versions) receive extended scenes, his beauty is often underscored through the art's expressive linework, and the comedy of Maomao's irritated-yet-flustered reactions to him is a consistent source of warmth. The result reads easily as a historical romantic comedy that also happens to involve poisoning cases and court politics.

Kurata's version, [photographing palaces and rural villages in China for visual reference](https://gamerant.com/apothecary-diaries-two-manga-versions-differences/), built toward something heavier. The mysteries run longer. The medical cases receive more space for Maomao to work through the evidence. Kurata herself [noted in an interview](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/convention/2025/apothecaries-and-assassins-q-a-with-minoji-kurata/.227624) that her favorite dynamic to illustrate was Jinshi and Maomao "not as two people in a love relationship, but partnering up to solve mysteries" — which is a pointed distinction. Where Nekokurage draws them circling each other romantically, Kurata draws them as professional collaborators who happen to carry emotional weight for each other.

Neither version is wrong. Both are [drawing from the same light novel](https://litretreat.com/the-apothecary-diaries-light-novel-vs-manga-which-should-you-read/). The source text genuinely contains both things — it is a mystery novel and a romance, not one decorated with elements of the other. What the dual adaptation exposed is that the source material holds these two modes in an intentional, unresolved tension. When you have to choose one lens, you discover that the choice wasn't neutral.

## The Anime's Decision and Its Consequences

The [anime production by TOHO Animation and OLM](https://www.awn.com/animationworld/hit-anime-difference-apothecary-diaries) used Nekokurage's pacing as its template. This wasn't an arbitrary call — Nekokurage's visual approach mapped more cleanly onto anime's typical production pipeline and audience expectations, and the result was a [critical and commercial success](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt26743760/) that found an unusually broad audience, including viewers who rarely watch anime. The series was praised specifically for Maomao as an atypical female lead: [intelligent, driven by curiosity rather than romance](https://screenrant.com/anime-romance-the-apothecary-diaries-unconvential-shojo-op-ed/), and not defined by her relationship with Jinshi. That reading is accurate. But it's worth noting it's the reading produced by the adaptation that *did* emphasize romance — the one that put Jinshi's beauty front and center, that framed his dynamic with Maomao as the series' emotional core.

What Kurata's version offers is a Maomao who feels even more insistently herself because the romance is de-emphasized. When [her intelligence and pharmacological obsession](https://thegameofnerds.com/2025/01/28/apothecary-diaries-medicine-mysteries-and-maomao/) are the story's organizing principle rather than her reluctant attraction to Jinshi, the character's distinctiveness becomes even clearer. Maomao is [one of the more unusual female protagonists in contemporary manga](https://fandomwire.com/no-one-talks-enough-about-the-apothecary-diaries-as-a-historical-anime/) — not despite her romance subplot but because, in Kurata's rendering, that subplot doesn't compete with her professional identity for the frame.

This created an odd situation: [fans who sought out both versions](https://forums.j-novel.club/topic/2447/which-one-of-the-two-manga-adaptions-of-kusuriya-no-hitorigoto-do-you-like-more) often described them as complementary rather than redundant. Nekokurage for the warmth and visual expressiveness. Kurata for the substance and historical texture. You read one to feel something; you read the other to understand what happened. This is not the usual experience of competing adaptations, where one tends to supersede the other. Here the gap between them maps perfectly onto the gap in the source material itself.

## What the Gap Reveals

The dual adaptation situation — bizarre, accidental, unexplained — functions as an inadvertent X-ray of what [Natsu Hyuga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hy%C5%ABganatsu_(writer)) actually built. She wrote a light novel that originated as a [web novel on Shōsetsuka ni Narō starting in 2011](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Apothecary_Diaries), a platform more commonly associated with isekai power fantasy. *The Apothecary Diaries* is an outlier in that ecosystem — a procedural mystery built around historically grounded medicine, set in a fictionalized Tang-dynasty court, written with [a feminist sensibility](https://fandomwire.com/how-the-apothecary-diaries-is-an-open-challenge-to-gender-roles-in-classic-romance-anime/) that doesn't announce itself as one. Its romance doesn't follow wish-fulfillment conventions: Jinshi is [the objectified party](https://fandomwire.com/a-simple-role-reversal-in-the-apothecary-diaries-directly-challenges-the-biggest-issue-of-the-anime-industry/), not Maomao; the attraction is treated as inconvenient rather than destined; and the series is [deliberately unhurried about resolution](https://www.anilist.co/forum/thread/51632).

Two mangaka, working independently, looked at this material and each found half of it. The mystery half. The romance half. They were both right. And the [anime, by choosing one half as its primary register](https://kusuriya.fandom.com/wiki/The_Apothecary_Diaries_(Anime)), made a decision that the source text never quite makes — because the light novel lives in the space between them, holding the tension rather than resolving it.

![The Apothecary Diaries anime cover featuring Maomao at work](https://s4.anilist.co/file/anilistcdn/media/anime/cover/large/bx161645-QLbzHXiYRgV2.jpg)

Most adaptations reduce their source material. This one, accidentally, revealed it. The two manga versions together are closer to what the light novel actually is than either one is alone — which may be the most interesting thing you can say about an adaptation: that you need both to see the original clearly.
