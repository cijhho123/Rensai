The bar is called Shanty, and it is not quite a hostess bar in the conventional Japanese sense. The "Girls" who work there are males presenting as women — some have had surgeries, some use hormones, some wear women's clothes without medical intervention of any kind, and the customers who come in for conversation and company are fully aware of all this. It occupies a social niche that mid-2000s Japan had no official vocabulary for, serviced by people that mainstream Japanese society had no official place for, and it is staffed by a 24-year-old office boy with [myotonic dystrophy](https://www.muscular-dystrophy.org/information-and-support/conditions/myotonic-dystrophy/) who lost his white-collar job because his employer would not accommodate his illness.

This is where *Tsuki no Terasu* — released in 2007 by the doujin circle [Sakura Mint](https://archive.org/details/tsugi-terasu), translated into English as *Moonshine* by AstCd2 for [Insani's al\|together 2008 festival](http://altogether.insani.org/2008/4.html) — begins. It begins there because that is where it lives.

## The Employment Ice Age and Its Casualties

*Moonshine* is not, at its core, a story about gender identity. It is a story about two people that Japan's postwar social compact expelled, and what they find in each other before that finding becomes its own kind of trap.

The unnamed protagonist is a casualty of what economists [call the Employment Ice Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Employment_Ice_Age) — the period stretching roughly from the mid-1990s through the mid-2000s, during which Japan's bubble economy had definitively collapsed and the rigid corporate hiring system left hundreds of thousands of young workers stranded without the stable first job that Japanese social climbing required. As [the Lost Decade bled into a second lost decade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lost_Decades), the salaryman path — the organizing fiction of postwar Japanese masculinity — quietly stopped being available to large segments of the population. The protagonist had managed to get into the system, until his body made that untenable. His employer's refusal to accommodate his condition is not portrayed as exceptional cruelty; it is systemic.

Mai, the hostess he befriends at Shanty, is a casualty of a different order. She is 20, lives and works as a woman, and has not sought surgery or hormones. Her famous line arrives quietly mid-game: *"You know, I can never be a girl, and yet, I'm not a boy, either."* In 2007 Japan, that in-between position had even fewer institutional supports than it does now — [Japan would not legally recognize gender identity disorder](https://wecapable.com/disability-in-japan-laws-rights-discrimination-and-stigma/) as grounds for official transition until 2004, and social acceptance lagged far behind legal recognition. Mai does not live in the official spaces. She lives in Shanty.

What Sakura Mint understood — and what makes *Moonshine* feel like a genuine document of its moment rather than a curiosity — is that the protagonist's disability and Mai's gender non-conformity are not equivalent situations requiring the same kind of sympathy. They are different ways of arriving at the same position: outside the script. The physical architecture of postwar Japanese life (stable employment, male breadwinner, conventional family formation, retirement at 60) had room for one kind of person. These two characters are not that person. The bar exists for them, made by people like them, and the story happens there not as setting but as structural argument.

## What the Game Does with Identity

What makes *Moonshine* unusual for a 2007 doujin visual novel is not that it includes a trans character but that it refuses to let Mai's gender identity function as dramatic machinery. In the [Insani translation materials](http://www.insani.org/), the translators note that handling gender identity seriously was exceptional for doujin VNs of the period — the norm was either to avoid it entirely or to deploy it as shock value. Sakura Mint does neither.

Gender identity comes up explicitly only twice in the game. The rest of the time, we see the novel through the protagonist's perspective, and he simply sees a woman. As [the most detailed fan analysis of Moonshine notes](http://magedark.blogspot.com/2009/06/moonshine-story-of-all-those-living-in.html), he does not delve deeply into the specifics of Mai's identity because they are not, for him, the point. He is not performing enlightenment. He is doing something simpler and harder: treating her as the person he can actually see.

This narrative strategy is unusual. Most media that centers a trans or gender-nonconforming character treats that identity as the story's primary tension — the thing to be resolved, exposed, accepted, or grieved. *Moonshine* makes the social conditions the tension. The question is not "who is Mai really?" but "where does someone like Mai actually live, and is that place safe enough to stay?"

The answer, ultimately, is no. Not because the bar is hostile — it is the closest thing to safety either character has — but because safety and shelter have a cost. As [the Magedark analysis frames it](http://magedark.blogspot.com/2009/06/moonshine-story-of-all-those-living-in.html), *Moonshine* is about how "being comfortable with living fogs the image of one's true dream." Mai wants to be a jeweler. The bar, and the relationship she builds there, is holding her in place. When she leaves, it is not a rejection but an act of self-preservation of a different kind.

## A Doujin Work About Doujin Conditions

There is something worth noting about the production context. *Moonshine* was made outside the commercial visual novel industry entirely — freeware, distributed through Sakura Mint's own site, requiring no purchase, no publisher approval, no market positioning. It exists in the same underground ecosystem that produced *Narcissu* and dozens of other al\|together translations, works that [the New Leaf Journal has documented](https://thenewleafjournal.com/collection/altogether/) as a coherent and significant body of literature largely unknown outside enthusiast circles.

The al\|together festival ran three times: [2005, 2006, and 2008](http://altogether.insani.org/2005/about.html). The 2008 edition was the last. Insani, the organizing circle, has since gone offline. The games live on in [Internet Archive mirrors](https://archive.org/details/moonshine-psp.-7z) and on the hard drives of people who downloaded them a decade ago. *Moonshine*'s sequel, *Tsugi no Terasu* (2010), was never translated — a casualty of the same ecosystem collapse.

The music compounds this. The game's theme and Mai's [voice theme](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=guL4T_jSRXA) were composed by Sentive and performed by a singer then operating under the name Gazelle on Nico Nico Douga: [Yanagi Nagi](https://www.generasia.com/wiki/yanaginagi), who in 2007 was a 20-year-old releasing doujin albums under the name CorLeonis and [uploading cover songs to NicoNico](https://supercell.fandom.com/wiki/Nagi_Yanagi). She was found by Supercell's composer Ryo, became the voice of their breakout single "Kimi no Shiranai Monogatari," and eventually signed with NBCUniversal Entertainment Japan — one of the most successful trajectories that doujin underground has produced. But in 2007, she was as marginal as anyone in this story: operating in the same freeware, fan-circulated ecosystem, invisible to the industry that would later claim her. The artist who gave Moonshine its emotional core was herself a refugee from the mainstream. The piece about people outside the system was made by people who hadn't entered the system yet, and it sounds like it.

## What Gets Left Behind

The critical reception that exists — [an 8/10 from Sakura Index](https://www.sakuraindex.jp/reviews/moonshine-review/), a perfect score from [at least one early blogger](http://magedark.blogspot.com/2009/06/moonshine-story-of-all-those-living-in.html), [15th place of 31 total entries in the New Leaf Journal's comprehensive al\|together ranking](https://thenewleafjournal.com/altogether-visual-novel-ranking-20-11/) — consistently identifies the same problem: the game's first half is deliberate and emotionally precise, and the second half rushes. The [Sakura Index review](https://www.sakuraindex.jp/reviews/moonshine-review/) notes the story "feels truncated," with the emotional climax arriving early and the pacing "greatly accelerated" in the second half — not artistically incoherent, but compressed in ways that suggest either scope constraints or Sakura Mint deciding the machinery of plot mattered less than the final emotional note.

That note is Mai's departure. She leaves the bar, the protagonist, the shelter they had accidentally built, and goes to become who she was before comfort became a substitute for living. The story earns this ending because it has been honest from the beginning about what Shanty is: a refuge, not a destination. The protagonist cannot fix his illness. Mai cannot live her life inside the bar. The real world they were both expelled from has not changed. What changes is their willingness to face it.

There is a [YouTube video from 2025](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4Xi9EuwnMOg) titled *"Why a Forgotten Visual Novel is the Best Trans Story I've Read"* — testifying that seventeen years after the al\|together 2008 festival, someone found *Moonshine* and understood what it was doing. The community that produced this game no longer exists as such. The channels through which it circulated are mostly gone. The sequel, *Tsugi no Terasu*, [sits untranslated on an Internet Archive upload](https://archive.org/details/tsugi-terasu) — not blocked by any legal obstacle, simply never taken up, because the translator ecosystem that might have done the work had already dispersed. And yet the game keeps finding readers who recognize it.

That, too, is a kind of argument. *Moonshine* was made for people who already knew they were looking in the wrong places. It was the right thing in the wrong distribution system, which is more or less the condition it describes.
