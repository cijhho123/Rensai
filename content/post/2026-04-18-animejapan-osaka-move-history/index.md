On March 29, 2026, organizers announced that [AnimeJapan 2026](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2026-03-30/animejapan-reports-record-attendance-in-2026/.235945) had just shattered its own attendance record: 156,000 visitors across two public days, surpassing the previous high of 152,400 set just twelve months earlier. Then, almost in the same breath, they announced that AnimeJapan 2027 and 2028 would not be held in Tokyo at all.

For an event that has occupied Tokyo Big Sight every single year since its 2014 debut, this is not a minor logistical update. It is, quietly, the second time in its existence that AnimeJapan has staged a break from something Tokyo represented — and understanding why requires going back to a political fight that fractured Japan's anime event landscape more than a decade ago.

## The First Break: How AnimeJapan Was Born

Before AnimeJapan existed, Japan's flagship anime industry gathering was the Tokyo International Anime Fair (TAF), which had run at Tokyo Big Sight since 2002. It was a joint venture between the anime industry and the Tokyo Metropolitan Government — and Tokyo Governor Shintaro Ishihara served as the event's chairperson.

In 2010, Ishihara's government pushed Bill 156, a revision to the Tokyo ordinance on youth development that would restrict the sale of manga and anime depicting "unjustifiably glorified" sexual acts involving characters who appear underage. The publishing industry, which had already been at war with Ishihara's cultural politics for years, saw it as an existential attack on their business. [Kadokawa, Kodansha, Shueisha, Shogakukan, and six other major publishers announced a boycott of TAF](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2010-12-10/10-manga-publishers-to-boycott-tokyo-anime-fair). Tite Kubo — then at the peak of *Bleach*'s popularity — publicly backed the decision. The industry founded a rival event, the [Anime Contents Expo (ACE)](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2010-12-28/anime/manga-firms-plan-anime-contents-expo-in-march), to be held the following week at Makuhari Messe.

Bill 156 passed anyway, in December 2010. But the publishers had made their point: the anime industry would not sit quietly inside an event run by the man trying to regulate it out of existence.

TAF and ACE ran as competing events through 2013, neither quite achieving the critical mass that a unified event could. In October 2013, [the two merged to form AnimeJapan](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2013-10-09/tokyo-intl-anime-fair-anime-contents-expo-unite-to-form-animejapan), which debuted in March 2014. The new organizational committee pointedly excluded both the Tokyo Metropolitan Government and the Governor's Office — a rebuke baked into the event's founding DNA. AnimeJapan was the industry's event, by the industry, for the industry and its fans.

It has been held at Tokyo Big Sight every year since. Until now.

## Thirteen Years at One Address

What AnimeJapan became over those thirteen years is genuinely remarkable. From its first edition, the event positioned itself not as a trade show with fan days bolted on, but as the central nervous system of Japanese anime culture — the place where the industry comes to make announcements, meet press, show its hand for the coming year, and take stock of what the global audience is actually responding to.

The 2026 edition captured the full scale of what that has become: [130+ exhibitor booths, 50 stage panels across four main stages](https://finalweapon.net/2026/03/30/animejapan-2026-reaches-new-attendance-record-of-156000-attendees/), attendees from approximately 100 countries. Kyoto Animation used AnimeJapan 2026 to reveal the main trailer for *Sparks of Tomorrow*, its first wholly original TV series in years. One Piece showed the first look at the Elbaph arc. The event had grown large enough that Netflix, Crunchyroll, and broadcast networks all maintain serious presences — because if you have something major to say about anime in spring, AnimeJapan is where you say it.

The record 156,000 visitors represent more than a number. They represent the ceiling pressure that has been building on the event for years: Tokyo Big Sight, Japan's largest exhibition center, is simply running out of room. The 2026 edition had to expand into the South Exhibition Halls *and* the Rooftop Exhibition Area to accommodate the footprint. The event has outgrown its home.

## The Second Break: Osaka and What It Means

[AnimeJapan 2027 will be held at Intex Osaka on March 27–28](https://otakukart.com/animejapan-2027-and-2028-to-be-held-in-osaka-marking-first-move-from-tokyo/), with business days on March 29–30. The Osaka Convention & Tourism Bureau is supporting the transition. Major companies on the executive committee have already confirmed participation.

The stated rationale is strategic: Osaka's international profile surged after hosting the Osaka-Kansai Expo 2025, and moving west expands the event's reach into a part of Japan that has long felt like an afterthought to Tokyo's anime-industry dominance. Most of Japan's major studios — Toei, Sunrise, Trigger, MAPPA, ufotable — are Tokyo-based or Tokyo-adjacent. The industry's administrative and creative infrastructure points east. Sending AnimeJapan to Osaka is, among other things, an acknowledgment that the audience the event wants to reach is no longer defined by proximity to that infrastructure.

There is something philosophically consistent about this. AnimeJapan was born from the conviction that the anime industry did not owe Tokyo's government its loyalty. Thirteen years later, it is demonstrating that it does not owe Tokyo itself exclusive claim either. The event's identity was always about the industry and its global audience — not about Tokyo Big Sight specifically. Moving to Osaka for 2027 and 2028 makes that explicit.

## The TGS Parallel

It is worth holding this next to what is happening at Tokyo Game Show. TGS has occupied Makuhari Messe in Chiba since 1996, [drawing 263,101 visitors in 2025 from 47 countries and 1,136 exhibiting companies](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/rs-gaming-lists/tokyo-game-show-2025-recap-1235439688/), and [expanding to five public days in 2026](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-09-28/tokyo-game-show-expands-to-5-days-in-2026/.229404). Where AnimeJapan is moving, TGS is enlarging — and increasingly the two events are circling the same cultural territory. At TGS 2025, the most significant announcements included *The Rising of the Shield Hero* Season 5 and a new *Girls Band Cry* film alongside game titles. The media mix has blurred the distinction between "gaming event" and "anime event" to the point where both serve the same sprawling franchise ecosystem.

What neither event has solved is the geographic concentration problem at scale. As anime's global reach continues to expand — and as the record figures at both events demonstrate that expansion is accelerating — the assumption that fans will come to Tokyo for the industry's biggest moments is increasingly a structural bet, not a natural given.

AnimeJapan's Osaka gambit may be the industry's first serious acknowledgment that the bet needs to change. Whether it returns to Tokyo in 2029, expands to a rotating-city format, or settles permanently into Osaka's infrastructure is the open question. But the fact that the question is being asked at all — at a moment of record attendance, not crisis — says something important about how the industry sees its own future.

It was born defying Tokyo's government. Now it is quietly outgrowing Tokyo itself.
