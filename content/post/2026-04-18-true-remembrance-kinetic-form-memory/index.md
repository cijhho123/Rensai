In a medium whose defining feature is interactivity — branching paths, multiple routes, decisions that cascade across hours of story — Satomi Shiba's 2003 freeware doujin work *True Remembrance* does something that reads, at first, like a limitation: it offers you nothing to choose. [Not a single decision point](https://vndb.org/v103). No route splits. One ending. You read, and the story happens to you.

This isn't a technical constraint of an early doujin tool. It's the argument.

![True Remembrance cover art](https://t.vndb.org/cv/82/43082.jpg)

The game places you in an unnamed City that exists at the edge of normal time — set, as the opening puts it, [in the present, "20 minutes into the future"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_Remembrance). The Dolor has spread across the world: a pandemic of severe depression that, left untreated, causes Psyche Corrosion — the progressive dissolution of a person's will to survive. The only known treatment is memory erasure. Mnemonicides, specially trained individuals with inborn telepathic capability, are assigned Guests — patients — and systematically remove the memories that triggered the Dolor. The patient departs the City lighter, functional, cured.

The logic is clean on paper. You suffer because of what you remember. Remove the memory, remove the suffering. What the game spends its five-hour runtime examining is the seam in that logic: what gets removed isn't only pain. It's the specific arrangement of experience that makes a person who they are.

---

## The Forgetter Who Forgot

Blackiris is introduced as a Class Alpha Mnemonicide — [the best at what he does, a "Mnemonicide par excellence"](http://trueremembrance.insani.org/). His excellence turns out to be inseparable from a detail revealed gradually: he himself has undergone memory erasure. He is not just a physician. He is also a processed patient. The most skilled practitioner of forgetting has been professionally emptied.

The game doesn't play this for irony. It's the central structural image. Blackiris moves through the story with a vague, hovering quality to his narration — [the New Leaf Journal describes it as having "a poetic air" that avoids purple prose](https://thenewleafjournal.com/true-remembrance-insani-visual-novel-review/) — because his interiority has been revised to serve as a clinical instrument. He cures others of their memories having already had his own made irrelevant. He is what a successful cure looks like from the outside: functional, skilled, and fundamentally unmoored.

La, his new Guest, arrives carrying something he no longer has. Her speech is truncated, laconic by design rather than coldness — [multiple reviewers note how distinctive this makes her in a medium that usually differentiates characters through verbal tics rather than verbal restraint](https://ricedigital.co.uk/visual-novel-spotlight-true-remembrance/). She withholds things. She has memories she does not want erased, which puts her immediately at odds with the City's entire purpose. She is, in the game's terms, a patient who doesn't want to be cured.

---

## Two Perspectives, One Withheld Truth

*True Remembrance* structures its narration by alternating between Blackiris and La, cycling perspectives across its chapters. The structural move is not just stylistic: in La's chapters, she is doing to the reader exactly what the Mnemonicides do to their patients — she curates what we know. [She leaves things out, withholds details, edits her own telling](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/VisualNovel/TrueRemembrance). The reader's understanding of the story is being selectively processed by its own narration, without the reader's knowledge or consent. [Fan discussions of the game](https://www.myabandonware.com/game/true-remembrance-wr5) often describe the sensation of arriving at the ending and realizing how carefully information had been withheld — not to deceive, but to prepare.

This is how the game's central revelation lands. Blackiris returns throughout the story to a recurring image: a "kitten" he once cared for and lost, a small detail he touches like a bruise. When the resolution arrives, it's that La is that kitten — a figure from the period of his life that was erased, whose presence his processed memory could not fully delete, only dislocate. She didn't randomly end up assigned to him. [The City is not the neutral therapeutic space it presents itself as](https://doujin.neoseeker.com/wiki/True_Remembrance).

![True Remembrance gameplay screenshot](https://www.myabandonware.com/media/screenshots/t/true-remembrance-1dk2/true-remembrance_3.jpg)

[The twist works](https://solidbatman1.wordpress.com/2013/11/22/true-remembrance-review/) because both characters have been defined by gaps throughout — things erased, things withheld, things half-remembered. When the gaps align, the effect isn't a thriller's shock. It's something closer to grief: the recognition of what you almost lost without knowing you'd had it. And crucially, the reader has been in the same position as Blackiris: receiving a story shaped by someone else's editing, moving toward a truth that was always there.

## Why There Are No Choices

Here is where the kinetic form stops being a format and becomes a thesis.

Visual novels that offer branching paths give you agency over the story's direction. You steer the protagonist. You choose who he protects, what he prioritizes, whether the ending is earned or bitter. This interactivity is the genre's core appeal — the sense that you are not merely watching but participating in the construction of meaning.

*True Remembrance* removes this entirely. [You read; you cannot change anything](https://vndb.org/v103). The story happens to you the way memory happens to you — not chosen, not negotiated, not forking into alternative versions of itself. There is one path because that is the nature of what has already occurred.

This is the formal argument about memory that the game's premise has been building toward. The Dolor patients arrive in the City hoping to choose what they remember — to revise the terms of their own past. The Mnemonicides offer the surgery to do this. And the game, from the outside, watching you read without choices, says: you cannot choose your story in this way. It has already happened. It already made you.

[Insani's official description](http://trueremembrance.insani.org/) frames the game as raising "uncomfortable questions that come to mind when one thinks about erasing memories from someone's heart." What the kinetic format adds is that the discomfort is structural, not just thematic. You are reading through a story you cannot revise, about characters who have revised themselves, and the question is whether either of you comes out of it whole.

The [film strip text box](https://solidbatman1.wordpress.com/2013/11/22/true-remembrance-review/) framing Blackiris's narration — brown, semi-transparent, designed to look like processed film — is not incidental. Film is how you preserve the past in a fixed state. It doesn't branch. It doesn't offer alternatives. It shows you what was captured, and that's all.

## What the Title Actually Claims

"True remembrance" doesn't land as a revelation. It's the quiet thesis the whole story has been circling. Shiba Satomi is not making a simple argument against the treatment of suffering — this isn't a dismissal of depression or a claim that grief should go unaddressed. The Dolor is real. The people in the City are genuinely broken. The treatment, for many of them, functionally works.

The argument is more precise: that the path toward healing which involves wholesale erasure of the self's history is a cure that costs you the patient. What remains afterward is operational but hollowed. Blackiris is the proof of concept — skilled, attentive, and fundamentally without continuity of personhood. [He is "a Mnemonicide par excellence"](http://trueremembrance.insani.org/) because he has already paid the price the method demands.

The longevity of *True Remembrance* is its own argument. [Originally released as freeware in 2003](https://vndb.org/v103), it [received a commercial 3DS release in Japan in 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_Remembrance), nearly a decade after its debut — a form of institutional recognition that doesn't happen to games that were merely competent. The [Insani English translation](http://trueremembrance.insani.org/), released February 14, 2008 — Valentine's Day, a choice that either says something deliberate or is one of the more oblique jokes in VN localization history — was produced with [Satomi Shiba's direct creative input throughout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_Remembrance), which is unusual even by the standards of creator-sanctioned fan work. [Reviewers consistently cited the translation's prose quality](https://kipchu.wordpress.com/2012/09/22/true-remembrance-visual-novel-review/) as something that actually reads like literary English, and the [Rice Digital spotlight](https://ricedigital.co.uk/visual-novel-spotlight-true-remembrance/) describes it as "comfortably human" — a phrase that captures the game's peculiar register, which is neither cold dystopia nor warm romance but something more like the tone of a memory you can't quite date.

The [watercolor-like character sprites](http://sunaganspaas.blogspot.com/2010/05/vn-review-true-remembrance.html) — washed-out, cool, winter-pale — and the [modified photograph backgrounds common to doujin VNs of the era](https://thenewleafjournal.com/true-remembrance-insani-visual-novel-review/) point in the same direction as every other formal choice in the game: this is a work about things preserved imperfectly, things lost incompletely, and the impossibility of fully distinguishing between the two.

You, reading it, are La. You received a shaped narrative, moving toward a truth being withheld until the story decided it was time. The Mnemonicides process their patients. La processed her chapters. Shiba Satomi processed the reader. The only difference is that what the game withholds, it eventually returns — which is the argument against the Dolor's cure, and the meaning of the title. True remembrance isn't the retention of everything. It's the recognition of what was always there, waiting for you to arrive at it.
