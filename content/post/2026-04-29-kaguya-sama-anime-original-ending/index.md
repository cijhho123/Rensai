The New Year's Eve special *Kaguya-sama: Love is War – The Stairway to Adulthood* aired on December 31, 2025, covering roughly eight chapters of manga out of the approximately 130 that had gone unadapted since the first-kiss movie. By the time those two episodes ended and the announcement for an anime-original finale film dropped alongside them, the message was unmistakable: the anime version of Kaguya-sama is not going to finish the manga. It is going to finish itself.

That 130-chapter gap is not a production shortcut. It is a creative position. And after sitting with it, I think the anime team is right — though the cost of being right here is real and worth naming.

![Kaguya Shinomiya, Miyuki Shirogane, and the full student council from Kaguya-sama: Love Is War](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-anime-series-main-characters.jpg)

## The Anime Was Already Its Own Thing

Kaguya-sama's adaptation, directed by [Shinichi Omata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinichi_Omata) under the pseudonym Mamoru Hatakeyama and produced by [A-1 Pictures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A-1_Pictures), was never a transparent transfer from page to screen. It was a distinct work that used animation to do things the manga structurally cannot. The Omata-era episodes play with aspect ratios mid-scene, shift art styles entirely for a single gag — the shojo manga aesthetic, the fighting-game HUD overlay — and turn the narrator, who in the manga functions as dry commentary, into something approaching a character in his own right: a man who genuinely believes love is a military campaign and files his dispatches with unironic urgency.

That directorial identity meant the anime was doing something the manga could not simply replicate from very early on. Comedy that scans as quick on the page hits differently with Kei Haneoka's score timed to the visual flourishes. [Most people who've watched both](https://www.cbr.com/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-anime-vs-manga/) will tell you that the anime's comedic timing is superior in the early arcs, even where the manga has material the anime didn't touch. That is not a trivial observation. It means the adaptation had developed enough of its own identity by Season 2 that it was no longer simply carrying the manga's signal — it was translating it into its own frequency.

When Omata returned for the 2022 film [*The First Kiss That Never Ends*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaguya-sama:_Love_Is_War_%E2%80%93_The_First_Kiss_That_Never_Ends), which covered the Ultra Romantic arc (chapters 138–151), the result was [nominated for Best Film at the 8th Crunchyroll Anime Awards](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2024-01-17/crunchyroll-anime-awards-reveals-2024-nominees/.206574) and widely considered one of the better anime theatrical releases of that year. The film worked not because it reproduced the manga's art but because the production team understood how to externalize internal emotional state through animation in ways the static page cannot — the way a single held frame can carry what a chapter's worth of internal monologue was reaching for. [Critics noted](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/kaguya-sama/love-is-war-the-first-kiss-that-never-ends/.193211) that the production bumped up its quality across the board, with clean animation and a precision in visual storytelling that the TV series could not always sustain.

By that point, the anime was not adapting Kaguya-sama. It was performing it.

## What the Anime Left Behind

The 130 chapters that Stairway to Adulthood skipped are not nothing. That gap contains [eight fully unadapted arcs](https://vocesabianime.com/en/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-arcs-not-adapted-anime/): the New Game Arc (chapters 152–162), the School Trip Arc (179–187), the End of Secrets Arc (188–206), the Home Visit Arc (207–209), the Buddha's Stone Bowl Arc (226–234), the long Kaguya-sama Wants to Be Confessed to arc (240–261), Shirogane's Departure (262–267), and the Farewell Arc (272–281). Two more arcs received partial treatment in the special, with [significant scenes omitted](https://www.cbr.com/new-kaguya-sama-love-is-war-movie-anime-original-ending/).

Inside those chapters: Hayasaka Ai's full independence arc, in which she cuts her hair, ends her service to the Shinomiya house, and begins figuring out who she is when she is not performing a role for someone else. Ishigami and Iino's slow-burn romance — built through dozens of short chapters, the Christmas party [where Iino breaks her arm saving Ishigami from a staircase fall](https://www.cbr.com/new-kaguya-sama-love-is-war-movie-anime-original-ending/) and becomes his reluctant dependent during recovery, and the gradual collapse of their mutual antagonism into something more honest. Kaguya's choice to pursue photography rather than the Shinomiya family's planned succession path. The full scatter of the student council as the graduating class breaks apart.

Manga readers who came into Stairway to Adulthood having loved these chapters have been [vocally and specifically disappointed](https://www.comicbook.com/anime/news/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-sparks-controversy-after-highly-anticipated-trailer/). The [Anime-Planet forum thread on the special](https://www.anime-planet.com/forum/threads/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-the-stairway-to-adulthood.359164/) is dense with people who watched with full manga context and found the skips painful — not just the Ishigami/Iino romance, but the texture of the final year of high school that the anime never got to capture. One commenter put it plainly: "they skipped the best arcs."

That needs to be said before explaining why the decision is still defensible.

## Why the Manga's Final Stretch Struggled

The Shinomiya Family War — the centerpiece of the unadapted final stretch — is the arc where the manga's internal friction became most visible. After hundreds of chapters of hyperactive romantic comedy in which the series deliberately kept emotional stakes low while mining the gap between what the characters felt and what they'd admit, the manga introduced Kaguya's brother as a genuine political antagonist and the family conflict became a succession war.

The art stayed good. The writing remained technically competent. But the *tone* was simply not the tone that had built the series' audience over six years. The complaint from dedicated readers was never that this arc was bad exactly — it was that it [read like a different series wearing Kaguya-sama's clothes](https://www.cbr.com/kaguya-sama-has-delivered-both-laughs-and-drama-but-could-it-outstay-its-welcome/). The antagonists felt thin. The pacing diffused from the tight short-chapter format that made the comedy pop. And Aka Akasaka, whose comedic instincts are genuinely exceptional, showed the limits of his dramatic ones in the same space. The [ANN forum discussion](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/bbs/phpBB2/viewtopic.php?t=3180453) at the manga's conclusion reflects this split: appreciation for how Kaguya and Shirogane finally resolved alongside pointed criticism of how the family arc did.

Kaguya-sama is not an isolated case for Akasaka. Oshi no Ko, his other major work, [concluded to comparable fan controversy](https://screenrant.com/oshi-no-ko-ending-good-bad-controversy/) — divisive enough that discussions immediately arose about [whether its anime should take the same path Kaguya-sama's eventually did](https://www.soapcentral.com/anime/should-oshi-ko-anime-get-kaguya-sama-treatment-the-merit-anime-original-ending-controversial-series-explored). The pattern is consistent enough to name directly: Akasaka is a brilliant setup artist and a limited closer. His endings tend to introduce new complications in a register different from the work that preceded them, and neither series entirely recovered its footing before the final chapter. What is telling is that the Kaguya-sama anime team recognized this — and had a plan — before the manga even finished.

## The Decision

According to [Anime News Network's coverage of the December 31 announcement](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2025-12-31/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-anime-gets-new-finale-film/.232675), Akasaka and the anime team made the decision to produce an original conclusion approximately four years ago — around the time the manga was ending its run. This was not a reactive decision driven by fan backlash. It was planned in parallel with the manga's own conclusion. Akasaka wrote the original story concept for the Final Chapter film himself, describing it as something "new, but nostalgic" and confirming this will "likely be the very last entry in the series."

His statement: "I shared a wish with the anime staff: not simply to make another adaptation, but to create something entirely new, a complete original work that no one had ever seen before, made together by all of us."

That framing matters. This is not a studio deciding it knows the story better than the mangaka. It is the mangaka deciding he wants to tell the anime version of the ending, not the manga version — a second chance at closing his own series, with the medium that handled its emotional peaks more cleanly.

![First look at Kaguya-sama: Love Is War — The Final Chapter anime movie](https://comicbook.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/12/Kaguya-sama-Love-is-War-Final-Chapter-Anime-Movie.jpg?w=1024)

The [early description of the film](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/kaguya-sama-love-is-war-anime-to-end-with-final-chapter-movie-first-look-revealed/) opens with Kaguya alone, reviewing a photo album, as memories of Shuchiin Academy flood back. That retrospective structure suggests the film will at least gesture toward the full arc of relationships the TV series built — even the ones it couldn't animate directly. Whether that gesture is adequate is the real question the film will have to answer.

## What's Actually at Stake

The problem is the collateral damage. Hayasaka's arc in the manga is genuinely moving — she becomes someone whose life belongs to herself for the first time, shedding the performance that defined her entire Shinomiya-employed existence. Ishigami and Iino's romance is, in its way, a second thesis statement for what the series is about: two people learning that the armor they built for themselves doesn't protect them from the thing they actually want. Their path runs parallel to Kaguya and Shirogane's but takes a more honest route to the same destination — less game-playing, more blunt acknowledgment of vulnerability.

Miko Iino as a character is not done justice by the anime if her romantic arc disappears. The series introduced her in Season 2 precisely because it wanted a character whose rigidity and hunger for approval could mirror Kaguya's in a minor key — someone for whom the performance (in her case, strict moral discipline) was equally defensive and equally exhausting. [Her arc in the manga](https://kaguyasama-wa-kokurasetai.fandom.com/wiki/Miko_Iino) resolves that: she learns to relax, she gets the Ishigami confession, and the armor comes down. Without it, she remains permanently frozen in her Season 3 state, which was always the middle of a sentence.

These aren't filler. They are the series paying off years of investment in its supporting cast, and the anime accumulated that investment across three seasons and a film. Anime-only viewers who loved Ishigami's character work in Season 3 are going to reach the Final Chapter film without any of the emotional foundation the manga spent eighty chapters building for him.

The [Soapcentral piece on the skipped arcs](https://www.soapcentral.com/anime/kaguya-sama-the-stairway-adulthood-reportedly-skips-major-manga-arcs-how-affect-plot) gets at this directly: not every arc the anime cut was bad, and the compression argument — that condensing the material would have been better than skipping it — has real merit. The distinction between bad arcs that deserved to be cut and good arcs that got cut along with them matters.

## The Honest Trade

What the anime is doing is making the right structural choice and accepting a genuine cost to do it. It is choosing which parts of Kaguya-sama it can render with full confidence, and being willing to set down the parts it cannot. For anime-only viewers, this means the story they watched will be complete, shaped, and properly closed. For manga readers, it means their full version of events will exist only in print — and will likely never exist in any other form.

Whether that trade is worth it depends on which version of the series you came to love first and what you think adaptation is actually for. If adaptation is fidelity — the obligation to transmit the source in full — then the Kaguya-sama anime is failing its brief by a third. If adaptation is interpretation — using a different medium to find the best version of a story that medium can tell — then what the team has done over four seasons, one movie, a TV special, and now a Final Chapter film is coherent from beginning to end.

The anime was its own work all along. Akasaka and Omata are simply being honest about that now.

The Final Chapter film will tell us whether they had the judgment to back that claim up with something worth the loss.
