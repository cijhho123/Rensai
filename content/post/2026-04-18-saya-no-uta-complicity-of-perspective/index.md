In Saya no Uta, the world looks like this: walls of wet flesh, strangers who are mobile tangles of organs and exposed teeth, the smell of rotting meat from every direction, the sound of human speech rendered as grunting and screeching. This is Fuminori Sakisaka's world after an experimental brain surgery saves his life at the cost of his perception. He is not going mad. He is fully aware that something is wrong with him. He plans to kill himself.

Then he meets Saya — a young girl who appears to him as she does to you: human, luminous, dressed in white. She is the only thing in his world that looks right. He falls in love with her. So, as Gen Urobuchi constructs things, does the reader.

The horror of [Saya no Uta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saya_no_Uta:_The_Song_of_Saya) — [developed by Nitroplus and released in 2003](https://www.grimoireofhorror.com/the-yurei/game-insight-saya-no-uta/) — is not what Saya is. It's that by the time you find out what Saya is, you don't want to stop.

## The Trap the Medium Sets

The visual novel format's most powerful formal property is its demand for readerly identification. A film can cut away from its protagonist; the camera goes where the director points it. A novel in third person can maintain ironic distance. A VN in first person — the perspective Urobuchi uses throughout most of Saya no Uta — does neither. The reader inhabits the narrator. When Fuminori looks at the world and sees gore, the game shows you gore. When he looks at Saya and sees beauty, the game shows you beauty. You don't have a vantage point outside his perception. You are inside it.

[This dual perspective is crucial](https://animeanemoscope.wordpress.com/2013/06/09/saya-no-uta-review-distorted-morality-and-a-horrifying-reality/): the game periodically breaks to the perspectives of other characters — Fuminori's friends, investigators, people who stumble into the house — and from those viewpoints, you see the world as it actually is. You see Saya as she actually is. And you see Fuminori from outside, as a pale, strange man who avoids everyone and snaps when approached. The juxtaposition is devastating. Each time you return to Fuminori's perspective, you feel what he feels — relief, the warmth of Saya, the ugly menace of the rest of the world — while carrying the knowledge that everything you are seeing is wrong.

This is not just a horror trick. It is a formal argument about how perspective shapes love. Fuminori's love for Saya is genuine by any measure that matters — it is everything he cares about, everything that keeps him functional, the one true thing in a world that has become uninhabitable. The love is not less real because his perception is damaged. The love is what it is. Urobuchi does not let you dismiss it.

## Urobuchi's Lovecraft Problem

[Gen Urobuchi wrote Saya no Uta during a period he described as lonely](https://www.thegamer.com/gen-urobuchi-interview-rusty-rabbit-madoka-magica-saya-no-uta/), drawing directly from H.P. Lovecraft's cosmic horror after deep engagement with those texts. He also called the work a product of his rebellious youth — something made before he fully understood where his own limits were. This is visible in the work. Saya no Uta goes places that feel like a writer daring himself.

The Lovecraftian influence is real and sophisticated — [critics have consistently recognized it as "truly Lovecraftian on both an aesthetic and thematic level"](https://beneaththetangles.com/2013/07/31/saya-no-uta-a-reminder-of-sinful-nature/) — but Urobuchi inverts the traditional cosmic horror structure. [Lovecraft's horror works through revelation](https://www.grimoireofhorror.com/the-yurei/game-insight-saya-no-uta/): the protagonist begins in normalcy, encounters something anomalous, and is destroyed by learning what the universe actually contains. The horror requires that the protagonist and reader share the same starting assumption — that the world is basically legible, that reality is reliable. Saya no Uta starts *after* that structure collapses. Fuminori already knows his world is wrong before page one. He has no illusion of normalcy to lose.

The reversal is: in traditional cosmic horror, the character's perception is accurate and the world is wrong. Here, the world is accurate and the character's perception is wrong. But Urobuchi places the reader inside the wrong perception. You begin the game having never experienced a functioning version of the story's reality. You only know what Fuminori knows. In this sense, the game replicates the experience of congenital cognitive difference — the condition is not the intrusion, it is the ground condition. Saya is not a corruption of Fuminori's normal life. She is the shape his life took after catastrophe.

## What the Endings Understand

The game offers three endings, none of which qualify as good. Understanding what each one does reveals the full architecture of the argument.

In the first ending, [Fuminori recovers — the agnosia is cured](https://saya-no-uta.fandom.com/wiki/Fuminori_Sakisaka) — and wakes to discover he has been living as a cannibal, that his home was a charnel house, that Saya was something incomprehensible. He turns himself in. He is institutionalized. This is the ending that looks like justice. But there is something deeply disquieting about it: Fuminori's recovery does not undo his love for Saya. It only removes him from her world and places him in a cleaner cage.

[In the second ending](https://saya-no-uta.fandom.com/wiki/Saya), Saya is destroyed — frozen by the characters who have been hunting her — and Fuminori dies. The horror here is what Yoh, the character who loves Fuminori, is willing to accept as collateral damage. The violence is not on the side of the monsters.

The third ending is the one people remember. Saya releases spores that begin converting all life on Earth into the same being she is. The world remade in her image. [The game presents this ending from Fuminori's perspective as joy](https://henrique-lage.medium.com/the-song-of-saya-happy-endings-9ce000310b79): everyone will now see as he sees. Everyone will know a world that looks like home. The horror is that you have been inside his perspective long enough to feel, for a moment, exactly why this would be a relief.

This is Urobuchi's most precise move. He has spent the entire game building the emotional logic for a conclusion that is, from any external vantage, an apocalypse — and then he trusts the reader to experience both reactions at once. The love is real. The apocalypse is real. The VN format does not let you separate them.

## Why This Couldn't Be a Film

[PC Gamer's review of Saya no Uta](https://www.pcgamer.com/i-love-saya-no-uta-even-though-its-incredibly-messed-up/) calls it "one of the most terrifying narratives ever conceived in a visual novel," which is true but misses something. The terrifying part is not just the narrative — it is how the medium delivers the narrative. A film version of Saya no Uta would require the camera to take a position. The moment a director frames Saya for an outside audience, she either looks human (in which case the love story plays conventionally, and the cosmic horror reveal lands as a twist) or she looks monstrous (in which case the audience never invests in the relationship the way Fuminori does). Neither option replicates what the game does: keeping you in lockstep with a perception you know is broken, for long enough that you love what it loves.

The VN's first-person present-tense has no edit cuts. When you read Fuminori seeing Saya, you see Saya. The gap between his broken perception and the reader's is collapsed precisely because the medium provides no vantage point for the gap. This is why the academic literature around the work — including [a Project MUSE analysis examining Saya no Uta and queer antisociality in VNs](https://muse.jhu.edu/article/772393/summary) — tends to focus on what the form is doing rather than just what the content contains. The form is the argument.

## Urobuchi's Career in Miniature

Looking back, Saya no Uta reads as the compressed version of every thematic obsession Urobuchi developed across his subsequent career. The use of isolation to make someone comprehensible — to make their extreme choices feel earned rather than simply wrong — is what he later did with [Homura Akemi in Puella Magi Madoka Magica](https://wiki.puella-magi.net/Homura_Akemi), whose love for Madoka escalates across a dozen timelines into an act that remakes the world. The structure of love-that-destroys is Saya no Uta's structure, scaled up. [The VN's Steam release description](https://store.steampowered.com/app/702050/The_Song_of_Saya/) itself emphasizes how the work "makes the player sympathize with the villains" — which is an unusual marketing choice, and a revealing one. The question of whether an inhuman entity can constitute a genuine relationship is the question Fate/Zero asks about Caster and Ryuunosuke.

But Saya no Uta does it rawer, with less structure, because it came first. [Urobuchi later said](https://www.thegamer.com/gen-urobuchi-interview-rusty-rabbit-madoka-magica-saya-no-uta/) that the player responses encouraged him — that people's willingness to follow Fuminori through what he does "truly encouraged" him. The willingness of the audience to feel what a broken mind feels, to love what a broken mind loves, and to grieve when that love ends — that was the proof of concept.

[Richard Eisenbeis of Kotaku wrote](https://kotaku.com/saya-no-uta-the-song-of-saya-the-kotaku-review-509012142) that Saya no Uta "manages to make the abominable, beautiful" — and that observation captures exactly what the VN format made possible. Get close enough to anything — damaged, monstrous, irrevocably wrong — and the love it feels becomes legible.

![The game's visual contrast between Fuminori's perception (flesh and horror) and Saya (beauty) is the core formal mechanic of the narrative](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/manga/1/107702l.jpg)

Every horror narrative relies on the reader's separation from the monster. The camera pulls back. The third person reasserts distance. What Saya no Uta understood — and what the VN format alone made possible — is what happens when you take that distance away entirely: not sympathy for the monster, but something more unsettling, which is that there was no monster to begin with. There was only a perspective, and you shared it.
