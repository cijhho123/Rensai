There is a moment early in *Another* that is more disturbing than anything the show will do with a broken umbrella or a falling elevator. A transfer student named Kouichi Sakakibara arrives at [Yomiyama North Middle School](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Yomiyama_North_Middle_School) and notices a girl standing alone in the elevator to the basement morgue. She tells him her name is Mei Misaki. He talks to her. He finds her again the next day, at her desk in class 3-3.

Nobody else reacts. Nobody else speaks to her. When Kouichi asks his classmates about her, they look away. When he asks a teacher, the teacher goes rigid. Mei Misaki, as far as class 3-3 is concerned, [does not exist](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki). She sits in the room. She breathes. She answers when Kouichi speaks to her. But the social contract of the classroom has decided that she is not there, and everyone is enforcing that contract with a unanimity that is genuinely unnerving.

That is *Another*'s real horror. And the anime is so busy getting to its Final Destination deaths that it never quite reckons with what it has set up.

![Mei Misaki standing alone, eyepatch, doll in hand](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2c/Another_coverart.png)

## The Ritual and What It Actually Is

The curse afflicting [class 3-3](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Class_3-3) at Yomiyama North began in 1972, when a popular student named Misaki died mid-year and his classmates, unable to process the loss, simply refused to acknowledge it. They left his desk as it was. They talked about him in present tense. When the class photo was developed at the end of the year, Misaki appeared in it — and he had been dead for months. Something came back with him, or perhaps never quite left. Since then, every year that class 3-3 is active, one extra student is present: a dead person occupying a desk, indistinguishable from the living, who doesn't know what they are. And because death cannot account for the extra body, it takes [other people in compensating waves](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/The_Calamity) — classmates, family members, teachers — until the school year ends.

The solution the school discovered: if the class can maintain a fiction that one student does not exist — treating them as socially invisible, refusing to speak to them or acknowledge them in any way — then the accounting balances. The "non-existent" student effectively occupies the slot the dead person would have, and the deaths stop.

Read that again. The prescribed response to a supernatural curse that kills people is to take a living classmate and, through collective social agreement, [erase them from the fabric of daily life](https://www.scifidimensions.com/what-does-misaki-meis-eye-patch-and-the-curse-represent-in-another/). Not harm them physically. Not exclude them from the building. Just refuse, unanimously and completely, to let them exist in social space. No eye contact. No spoken words. No acknowledgment if they trip and fall in the hallway.

This is not a horror metaphor. This is a direct description of *ijime* — the Japanese term for bullying, which in its most severe forms takes precisely this shape: organized social ostracism, where an entire classroom apparatus turns against a single student and treats them as though they are not present. Academic research on ijime [documents exactly this group dynamic](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222844949_Nature_and_Correlates_of_Ijime-Bullying_in_Japanese_Middle_School): a pattern beginning with two or three students and spreading until the entire class is participating, with classmates who speak to the targeted person becoming targets themselves. The [*shikato* form of ijime](https://espace.library.uq.edu.au/view/UQ:7721/tm_rhiz.pdf) — complete exclusion and silent treatment — is documented as particularly damaging precisely because Japanese social structure makes group membership essential to functioning life. The ritual in *Another* has logic, history, and teeth. It is recognizable because it is real.

## What Mei Misaki's Design Understands

The character of Mei Misaki is constructed around this concept with unusual care. Her left eye is covered by a medical eyepatch; beneath it is a prosthetic glass eye, a doll's eye, given to her by her mother. The eye, as Mei describes it, can perceive things ordinary eyes cannot — including [the color of death](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki) that clings to people who are about to die or who are already dead. She wears the patch to avoid seeing what she does not want to know.

The doll's eye is a beautiful piece of character design because it's doing two things simultaneously. On the plot level, it's the tool that will eventually identify the dead student in class 3-3 and resolve the mystery. On the thematic level, it's a literalization of Mei's condition: she is a girl who sees too much, and the response of everyone around her is to pretend she sees nothing, because pretending she doesn't exist is easier than dealing with what she perceives.

Her isolation is not passive. It has been assigned to her. And she has internalized it so completely that she participates in her own non-existence with a kind of exhausted dignity. [When Kouichi speaks to her](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_1_-_Rough_Sketch), she is visibly surprised that someone is treating her as real. She has stopped expecting it.

This setup is genuinely unsettling in a way that has nothing to do with jump scares or gore. The horror is social, institutional, and recognizable. It uses the school — a space that Japanese horror has always understood as a site of supernatural unease, from [Hanako-san in the bathroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanako-san) to the *gakkou no kaidan* tradition of school ghost stories — as a container for anxieties about belonging and erasure that Japanese adolescent culture produces in abundance.

## Yukito Ayatsuji's Hybrid

Understanding what *Another* is trying to do requires understanding where it came from. Yukito Ayatsuji is primarily a [honkaku mystery writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukito_Ayatsuji) — a tradition that prizes logical puzzle construction, the kind of fair-play detective fiction descended from Agatha Christie and Ellery Queen. His Mansion series established him as one of the leading figures in the neo-honkaku revival of the 1980s and '90s. He has spoken about discovering Ellery Queen at eleven and being captured by the [logical rigor of the impossible crime](https://jiescribano.wordpress.com/2025/02/02/ayatsuji-yukito/).

*Another*, published in 2009, was his attempt to fuse that puzzle-mystery structure with supernatural horror. The core conceits are pure honkaku: a closed environment (one classroom, one school year), a defined set of suspects (everyone in class 3-3 could be the dead student), a hidden identity that the reader/viewer and protagonist are racing to identify. The supernatural elements are real — the curse exists, the deaths are real — but the solution is a logical deduction from available evidence. The [novel was nominated for the Honkaku Mystery Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukito_Ayatsuji) in 2010 and sparked debate among judges precisely because it used supernatural elements within a framework that honkaku purists reserve for naturalistic explanation.

The anime, directed by [Tsutomu Mizushima](https://anidb.net/anime/8556) and produced by P.A. Works, captures this hybrid structure. Its first half is almost entirely atmospheric mystery: the strange silence around Mei, the institutional memory of past calamities, Kouichi's gradual discovery of what class 3-3 has been doing and why. The visual direction during these episodes is extraordinary. The [Ani-Gamers review](https://anigamers.com/reviews/another/) accurately identifies how the animation team built a world of decay — "water-stained walls, paint-stripped wood with scrapes and obvious wear, and rusted metal surfaces" — that makes the school feel like a building that has been absorbing death for thirty years. Characters are composed to look small against their environments, isolated in frame, overwhelmed by space. The oppressive atmosphere is earned through visual grammar, not through soundtrack stings.

## Where It Goes Wrong — and Why

The problem is the second half. As the mystery resolves and the curse activates fully, *Another* shifts registers. The atmospheric ghost story becomes a [Final Destination film](https://fandomwire.com/this-horror-anime-is-the-final-destination-alternative-you-didnt-know-you-needed/): a sequence of elaborately staged, mechanically absurd deaths that pile up in rapid succession. An umbrella through a throat. A staircase collapse. Students killing each other in a burning building. The deaths are graphic, inventive, and increasingly difficult to take seriously — which is [a common observation in critical discussion](https://imdb.com/title/tt2176165/reviews/) of the finale. What had been genuinely unsettling becomes, in places, inadvertently comic.

This shift is structurally legible: it's Ayatsuji's honkaku skeleton showing. The novel was always building toward a solution, and the horror deaths are the mechanism by which the puzzle generates urgency. But the anime's visual investment in atmospheric dread — the production design, the slow pacing, the camera work — makes the pivot feel like a betrayal. The tool that P.A. Works was best at using (rendering emotional states through visual space) becomes irrelevant once the show switches to kill sequences.

More importantly, the shift abandons the thematic material the show had been quietly building. The "non-existence" ritual — the institutionalized social death — recedes from view once the actual deaths begin. The show never asks what it means that the designated solution to the curse is to perform a sustained act of communal cruelty on a classmate. [Nobody in class 3-3 is shown experiencing guilt about this](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/The_Calamity). The teachers who organize and enforce the ritual are not framed as perpetrators of anything. The harm of treating a living person as though they do not exist is noted by Kouichi — he breaks the ritual by talking to Mei — but the show treats his rebellion as a plot mechanism rather than a moral position.

There is something almost eerie about how *Another* sets up a horror concept rooted in the real psychology of adolescent social violence and then declines to follow it anywhere meaningful. The curse is eventually resolved by identifying and killing the dead student. The "non-existence" ritual is vindicated as the correct approach in years when it works. The show ends without having examined what it costs to perform that ritual on someone, or what kind of classroom culture enables it.

## What the Atmosphere Achieved

This is not an argument that *Another* is a failure. It [topped Ranker's list of the best horror anime of all time](https://www.ranker.com/list/top-10-horror-anime/ranker-anime) for years, and that reputation is earned in its first half. Its opening seven episodes are an object lesson in constructing dread through visual design rather than shock. P.A. Works — better known for slice-of-life productions like *Angel Beats!* and *True Tears* — demonstrated in *Another* that they could sustain oppressive atmosphere over a half-cours without rupturing it. The score by [Kow Otani](https://anidb.net/anime/8556) and Ali Project's [opening theme "Kyōmu Densen"](https://anidb.net/anime/8556) compose a horror register the visuals could match.

Mei Misaki is one of the stronger horror protagonists of her generation: a character whose design, backstory, and thematic function are internally coherent in a way that the narrative of her own show doesn't always match. The glass eye, the dolls her mother makes, the quality of her solitude — these cohere into a figure who embodies the show's real argument even when the writing forgets to make it. Her design is doing the thematic work the script eventually abandons.

The [light novel in English translation](https://www.amazon.com/Another-light-novel-Yukito-Ayatsuji/dp/0316339105) (published by Yen Press, translated by Karen McGillicuddy) is worth reading alongside the anime: Ayatsuji's prose earns the mystery architecture through an accumulation of dread that the anime only has time to gesture toward. The [manga adaptation by Hiro Kiyohara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)), serialized 2010–2012, also preserves more of the early-chapters atmosphere — it's a useful comparison point for seeing how much the anime's second-half pivot was a production choice rather than an adaptation inevitability.

## The Ghost It Actually Was

The tragedy of *Another*, as a piece of horror, is that it was haunted by a concept it never fully inhabited. A story about a community that manages supernatural death by performing social death on one of its own — and in which the community is never asked to examine what that says about it — could have been something genuinely disturbing in the way that great horror is disturbing: not because people get hurt in creative ways, but because the world it describes is recognizable.

The Japanese school system's relationship with organized bullying — [documented across decades of Ministry of Education reports](https://japandaily.jp/ijime-the-silent-crisis-shattering-japans-youth/) — the institutional silences that surround it, the way authority structures often enforce rather than interrupt patterns of erasure: this is not obscure subject matter. *Another* touched it and then pulled back into genre mechanics. The umbrella through the throat is memorable. But the image of an entire classroom maintaining, by collective agreement, that a student in their midst does not exist — that one has stayed with me longer.

The horror *Another* was capable of telling was the one about what ordinary people do when they decide the rules permit it.
