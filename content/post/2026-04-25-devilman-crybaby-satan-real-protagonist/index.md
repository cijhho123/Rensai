The show opens with boys racing down a mountain road, and you are supposed to think you're watching Akira Fudo's story. Everything about the Devilman mythology confirms this: sensitive crybaby merges with ancient demon, inherits its power, fights to protect the humans he loves. The arc is heroic. The framing is classical. Then the world ends, and you understand you were watching the wrong character the entire time.

*Devilman Crybaby*, Masaaki Yuasa's 2018 Netflix adaptation of Go Nagai's 1972 manga, is not primarily about becoming a monster while remaining human. It's about Satan failing to understand love until the exact moment he makes love impossible. Every act of violence, every mob, every brutal execution the show stages across its ten episodes is evidence in a case building toward a single image: Satan sitting in the ruins of everything, crying over the body of the person he destroyed the world to protect.

That image is the thesis. Everything else is the setup.

## What Ryo Asuka Actually Wants

Ryo Asuka introduces himself as someone who has figured it out. [He has a theory about love](https://medium.com/@Krellitlikeitis/sympathy-for-the-devil-a-lesson-in-love-from-devilman-crybaby-a6c5947a787f): it doesn't exist. What people call love is proximity and self-interest wearing a disguise. Attachment is weakness. The only honest thing is power, and the only honest relationship is between equals who acknowledge this. He recruits Akira into the demon-fighting arrangement not out of affection but calculation — Akira is useful, Akira is strong, Akira will survive what's coming.

Except Ryo has already told us this is a lie, in the first episode, when he risks his life to save Akira from the Sabbath without any tactical reason to do so. The show is one long argument between what Ryo believes and what Ryo does, and the gap between those two things is where the tragedy lives.

[As analyzed by Anime Feminist](https://www.animefeminist.com/feature-devilman-crybaby-legacies-queerness-and-diversifying-remakes/), Ryo's queerness in Crybaby is coded as monstrous not because of his sexuality but because of his absence of compassion — the show takes pains to distinguish between the two. Satan's love for Akira is the one place where he cannot fully be the cold calculating fallen angel, and the series spends ten episodes asking whether that crack in the armor is enough to change anything. The answer, when it arrives, is devastating: it is not enough, but it is real, and realizing it is real is what destroys him.

[Women Write About Comics observed](https://womenwriteaboutcomics.com/2017/12/not-a-hero-vs-normative-storytelling-in-devilman/) that the entire series hinges on a single moment — Ryo crying over Akira's corpse as the world resets — and that this moment was always the destination. Which means every scene before it is a step in Satan's education. Not Akira's.

## The Violence Is the Argument

This reading reframes the show's most controversial element. *Devilman Crybaby* was immediately labeled gratuitous by critics who found its graphic violence and explicit sexuality excessive. [Common Sense Media documented](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/tv-reviews/devilman-crybaby) beheadings, eviscerations, group sex, and demonic gore in clinical detail, and even sympathetic critics qualified their praise with Adults Only warnings. [ANN's 2018 preview](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/preview-guide/2018/winter/devilman-crybaby/.126094) called the Sabbath club scene a "drug-fueled orgy of lights, color, and brazen sexuality" — which is accurate, and also misses the point entirely.

If Akira is the protagonist, the violence is excessive. A hero's journey doesn't require this much carnage. But if Satan is the protagonist, the carnage is doing something specific: it is building the record of what Satan cannot feel.

Every human death in this show is evidence. The lynch mob tearing apart the demon girl's family. The social media doxing that gets Miki's family killed. The mass panic that turns neighbors into executioners. Satan watches all of this happen and does not intervene — not because he is evil in the conventional sense, but because his theory holds. Humans are exactly what he always said they were. Monstrous. Unworthy of preservation. The violence confirms his worldview, episode after episode, until the logic snaps against Akira's death.

[Game Rant's thematic breakdown](https://gamerant.com/devilman-crybaby-shocking-themes/) notes that Crybaby consistently frames the human violence as equivalent to — or worse than — the demonic. The bigotry metaphor is not subtle: queer couples dancing in a club are followed by scenes of their doxing and public persecution. Demons show up at the margins of human violence and are mostly beside the point. The argument is that Satan's contempt for humanity is empirically justified. The show agrees with him on the evidence. It just thinks he's wrong about the conclusion.

[Kotaku's writers noted](https://kotaku.com/what-we-loved-about-devilman-crybaby-1821909262) that the show's most effective moments are the quiet ones — Akira sitting at the edge of a pool, Miki's father reading the news, a street rapper moving a skeptic to tears. Those moments matter because they show what Akira can feel that Ryo cannot. The violence demonstrates Satan's thesis. The quiet moments are Akira's rebuttal. And Ryo, who arranged everything, watches his counterpart demonstrate the very thing he insists doesn't exist.

## Yuasa's Bodies

Masaaki Yuasa's visual approach amplifies this reading in ways specific to animation. The bodies in *Crybaby* don't behave like bodies. They stretch and compress and merge and tear in ways that foreground sensation over anatomy. [The BFI's profile of Yuasa](https://www.bfi.org.uk/features/where-begin-with-masaaki-yuasa) notes that he "conveys the world as it feels to the characters inhabiting it" rather than as it objectively appears — his distortions are emotional truths rendered as physical fact.

The lynch mob scenes present shadows against firelight rather than individualized people. [Honey's Anime analyzed](https://honeysanime.com/masaaki-yuasa-and-the-animation-of-devilman-crybaby/) how this abstraction creates emotional resonance deeper than realism would allow — the mob becomes pure mob-ness, panic and violence without faces that might create false sympathy. Satan looks at this and sees proof of his theory. But Yuasa's visual grammar also applies to Akira's transformation sequences, where the physical merging with Amon is rendered as total sensory overwhelm — ecstatic, terrifying, and irrevocable. Akira absorbs a demon and keeps his tears. Satan watches and cannot understand how.

[An academic paper on ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/390488762_Neither_Human_Nor_Demon_A_Comparative_Analysis_of_the_Devilman_Body_in_Go_Nagai's_Devilman_and_Masaaki_Yuasa's_Devilman_Crybaby) applies Julia Kristeva's theory of abjection to Akira's hybrid body — the horror of something that is neither fully human nor fully other, existing in the liminal space between categories. But Satan's body is also abject in this reading: a fallen angel who cannot understand his own emotional state until it kills him. Akira knows what he is, even as a hybrid. Ryo spends ten episodes not knowing.

## What Netflix Made Possible — and Required

It's worth accounting for the production context, because it directly shaped what kind of story could be told. [Yuasa explained to Sakuga Blog](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2018/01/08/devilman-crybaby-interview-with-director-masaaki-yuasa/) that he approached the adaptation with a specific mindset: "If Nagai-sensei could have done as he pleased, he'd have gone this far." This is an unusual creative philosophy — not adapting the actual text, but channeling the suppressed intent of the original author. Magazine publication in 1972 imposed constraints that a 2018 Netflix commission does not.

[Go Nagai confirmed and embraced this approach](https://fandomwire.com/i-felt-it-was-scary-devilman-crybaby-creator-on-the-original-devilman-manga-thats-still-too-disturbing-to-recreate-today/), giving Yuasa complete freedom: "Do whatever you want. If there's something you want to change, you can change it, and no matter how it changes, I'll be happy." In a [joint interview with GigaZine](https://gigazine.net/gsc_news/en/20180116-devilman-crybaby-go-nagai-masaaki-yuasa-interview/), Yuasa also noted that the show's themes feel more urgent now because "people are a lot more connected, for good and bad" — the social media mob violence of the final episodes is a modernization of Nagai's anti-war metaphor into something specifically twenty-first century.

The global simultaneous drop — all ten episodes on January 5, 2018 — created an unmediated discourse moment that [ANN described](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/this-week-in-anime/2018-01-09/.126203) as one of the most divisive anime responses of that year. No waiting, no week-to-week pacing, no slow build of audience opinion. Everyone hit the finale at the same time and the reaction was immediate and loud.

What Netflix specifically enabled was the apocalyptic ending. [As a survey of Devilman adaptations on Medium makes clear](https://medium.com/@ahmed.alsheikh/the-best-devilman-adaptations-d6055b844c99), every previous animated version — the 1970s TV series, the OVAs — either restructured the finale or stopped before Nagai's original conclusion. [Devilman Crybaby is the only animated adaptation](https://www.looper.com/406546/the-ending-of-devilman-crybaby-explained/) to commit to the full ending: Ryo revealed as Satan, Akira killed, the world reset. Crybaby required it, because Satan's education requires the full weight of what he did. You cannot learn this lesson partially. You do not get to keep the world.

[Screen Rant has argued](https://screenrant.com/netflix-best-original-anime-devilman-crybaby-forgotten-op-ed/) that *Crybaby* made Netflix a serious contender in anime and has since been quietly abandoned by the platform — the show [won the Crunchyroll Anime Award for Anime of the Year in 2018](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2018-02-24/crunchyroll-anime-awards-2018-winners/.128993) and has effectively been treated as a legacy acquisition since. That's a legitimate grievance about platform behavior. But its consequence is worth naming: the show that was supposed to launch Netflix's anime ambitions is now a cult object, discovered late and word-of-mouth, which might actually suit it. [ComicBook.com's follow-up with Yuasa](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/devilman-crybaby-director-netflix-future-anime/) captured his measured optimism about what Netflix could make possible. Whether that optimism was warranted is a different conversation.

## The Ending as Proof

The world ends because Satan loves Akira and doesn't know what to do with that. This is not subtext. The final episode states it directly: Satan cradles what remains of Akira as the divine reset arrives. He started a war he couldn't win to protect someone he killed. His theory about love was wrong. He knows this now.

Satan's flaw is not his queerness, not his coldness, not his ambition. His flaw is that he understands power but not compassion. He protected Akira from demons without ever asking what Akira wanted. He destroyed humanity to save one person without understanding that you cannot save a person by destroying everything they love. He was trying to express love using only the tools he had, which were force and sacrifice, and both of those tools failed him completely at the finish line.

The crybaby of the title is Akira — the boy who cried at everything, who felt everything too much, who kept crying even after he became a monster. But the cry that closes the show belongs to Satan, who never could before. The show gives him one moment of genuine human response and times it to arrive when there is nothing left to save.

That timing is the argument. Not about shock value, not about graphic content, not about whether queer representation in dark narratives owes its characters a better ending — though all of those conversations are worth having, and [Anime Feminist](https://www.animefeminist.com/feature-devilman-crybaby-legacies-queerness-and-diversifying-remakes/) has made a compelling case that the show's treatment of Satan's love offers something the "Bury Your Gays" critique doesn't fully account for. The argument Crybaby is making is structural: Yuasa organized ten episodes of escalating catastrophe as a delivery mechanism for one being learning one thing.

It was always Ryo's story. Akira just had to be there so the ending would mean something.
