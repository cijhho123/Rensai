Every villain in [Attack on Titan](https://myanimelist.net/anime/16498/Shingeki_no_Kyojin) eventually becomes comprehensible. That is one of the series' strongest qualities: it traces the logic from early wound to final act, and the logic holds. Reiner became a monster because he was made into one. The Marleyans discriminate because they were taught to fear. Grisha radicalized because a system destroyed the people he loved. The causes are structural, the suffering is generational, the people inside the machine are trying to survive it.

Zeke Yeager is different. His logic is not just comprehensible — it is [internally airtight](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Zeke_Yeager). He has thought through the problem of Eldian existence more carefully than anyone else in the series, arrived at a conclusion that most people find horrifying, and committed to it with a clarity that the narrative cannot simply dismiss. You can disagree with Zeke. What you cannot do is accuse him of not having done the work.

His plan: use the Founding Titan's power to sterilize all Eldians, ending the bloodline within a generation. No more Subjects of Ymir. No more Titans. No more inherited war. No more children born into a world where their existence is used as a weapon by whoever currently holds power over them. The suffering that has run on a loop for over two thousand years, stopped.

He calls this salvation.

## Where the Plan Comes From

Zeke was raised as a weapon. His parents — [Grisha Yeager and Dina Fritz](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Grisha_Yeager) — were [Restorationists](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Eldian_Restorationists), members of a movement dedicated to reclaiming Eldian power through revolution. They enrolled their child in the Warrior program not as a choice but as a cover, using Zeke to protect themselves from Marleyan surveillance. Zeke spent his childhood absorbing his father's ideology while simultaneously being used by it, and eventually turned his parents in to Marleyan authorities to protect himself — and possibly, in his own accounting, because he already understood what he understood: that the cycle his parents were fighting to extend was the problem, not its current masters.

The other foundational wound is [Tom Ksaver](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Tom_Ksaver), the Beast Titan before Zeke, who inherited his power and effectively became his surrogate father. Ksaver had lost his family — his Eldian wife killed herself after learning she was pregnant, unable to bear bringing another Eldian into the world's hatred. Ksaver's own conclusion was the same one Zeke would later formalize: "I wish we had never been born." Not as despair. As philosophy. As the only humane answer to a world that finds your existence intolerable.

This is [Schopenhauerian pessimism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/schopenhauer/#Ethi) in its purest anime form: the belief that life contains more suffering than it is worth, and that non-existence is preferable to existence under conditions of guaranteed torment. It is the ethics of compassion turned inside out. If you genuinely love people, you do not want them to suffer. If you cannot prevent the suffering, you prevent the people.

Zeke does not hate Eldians. That is the point, and it is the thing that makes him more disturbing than any character who simply wants the other side dead. He loves Eldians — or something he conceptualizes as love — which is why he wants to spare future generations from a world that will always find a reason to destroy them.

## The Paths Confrontation

The scene that forces the philosophical issue into full light is in the Paths, the space inside the Coordinate where Zeke traps Eren after triggering the Rumbling. [In the Paths](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-zekes-master-plan-explained/), Zeke's plan was to access Eren's memories of their father, confirm Grisha's abuse of Eren, and use that as evidence to convince Eren that he too was a casualty of the ideology — a child weaponized, as Zeke was.

What he finds instead is not what he expected. He watches Grisha's memories — including the scenes Isayama has been withholding for arcs. He watches Grisha change. The abuse Zeke has constructed his entire worldview around did not happen the way he remembered it. Grisha eventually understood what he had done to Zeke. He wept for it. He told Eren he was sorry. He became, in whatever limited way a person who made catastrophic choices can become, something other than a monster.

Zeke's response to this is the most psychologically acute moment in his arc: he does not abandon his philosophy. He cannot. The philosophy is not built on Grisha; it is built on Ksaver, on his dead stepfamily, on a world that has never found a reason to stop hating Eldians. The abuse was corroborating evidence, not the cause. Taking it away leaves the structure intact.

But Eren's response is the confrontation the series was building toward. "I am not fighting because someone made me," [Eren says](https://screenrant.com/how-eren-transformed-from-hero-to-villain-attack-on-titan/). "I was born into this world, and that is enough." The stakes of his argument are opposite to Zeke's: where Zeke says existence produces suffering and non-existence is the mercy, Eren says existence is the precondition of everything worth having, including freedom, and no suffering negates that. You cannot give someone the gift of never having to suffer by ensuring they never exist. You can only take from them the thing that makes suffering matter: the life they would have had.

The two positions are genuinely irreconcilable. That is unusual in [shonen anime](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=9929), which tends to resolve ideological debates by having the protagonist shout the right thing with sufficient conviction. Here, neither Zeke nor Eren is shown to be clearly wrong by the narrative. They are shown to be absolutely committed to opposite positions, with legitimate reasons rooted in genuine experience and coherent philosophy, and the story is honest about the fact that their argument cannot be solved through talking.

## Why Zeke Functions Differently Than Other AoT Villains

The Marleyans function as villains through institutional racism — [they teach hatred because it serves the state](https://www.thedailybeast.com/obsessed/how-attack-on-titan-became-one-of-the-most-impactful-polarizing-anime-ever/), and individual Marleyans are either complicit or oblivious. The Reiss family functions as a villain through cowardice — they have the power to change things and refuse. Eren in the final arc functions as a villain through certainty — he has decided the Rumbling is the only answer and will not hear otherwise.

Zeke functions as a villain through a premise: that suffering has a value threshold, and when the expected suffering of a life exceeds its expected good, bringing that life into existence is a harm. This is a position held by [real philosophers](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/life-meaning/), most famously David Benatar, whose [antinatalist work](https://www.oxfordscholarship.com/view/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199296422.001.0001/acprof-9780199296422) argues precisely this. It is a minority position but not a fringe one. The difference between Benatar and Zeke is that Benatar believes this should change individual decisions about reproduction; Zeke has the Founding Titan and intends to solve the problem industrially.

What makes this dramatically interesting is that the series cannot simply demonstrate Zeke is wrong by showing us the consequences of his plan, because the plan never gets enacted. The consequences of the alternative — Eren's Rumbling — are shown in full, and they are atrocity. [Zeke's euthanasia](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Euthanasia_plan) is at least conceptually limited: it ends one lineage over one generation. Eren's "freedom" kills [roughly eighty percent of humanity outside Paradis](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Rumbling). The utilitarian calculus, if you run it, does not automatically favor the protagonist's approach.

Attack on Titan does not point to Zeke and say: see, this is evil. It points to Zeke and says: see, this is what happens when someone who has genuinely experienced the worst of what the system produces decides to do something about it. The horror is that the logic works.

## The Philosophy That Outlasted the Evidence

The Paths sequence makes clear how much of Zeke's plan is rooted in Grisha — not the actual Grisha, but the version Zeke spent his life constructing. Grisha enrolled his son in a program that destroyed children. He got a new family and poured his revolutionary passion into a new cause while Zeke was being weaponized. When Zeke turned him in, it was not just self-preservation; it was a verdict on the ideology Grisha embodied.

[The Paths memory sequence](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Zeke_Yeager) then shows Zeke something he did not expect: Grisha had changed. He recognized what he had done to his son. He wept for it. In Isayama's [telling of this arc](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Grisha_Yeager), Grisha becomes something other than the ideologue Zeke condemned — a human who caused enormous harm, came to understand it, and could not undo it.

The psychological observation the scene forces is one of the sharpest in the entire series: philosophies do not dissolve when the evidence that generated them changes. People find experiences, construct frameworks around them, and then the frameworks acquire their own momentum. Zeke had already committed more fully to the conclusion than to its original cause. Taking away Grisha's abuse as justification does not remove the euthanasia plan; it just reveals that the plan was never really about Grisha in the first place. It was about Ksaver. It was about the wife who killed herself rather than bring another Eldian child into a world that hated them. It was about a conclusion that felt like compassion before it felt like anything else.

## What the Series Actually Says

Attack on Titan does not answer the Zeke/Eren debate by having one side win an argument. It answers it by showing consequences. Zeke's plan is preempted by Eren, who turns the Founding Titan's power toward the Rumbling instead of the euthanasia. The euthanasia never happens. What happens instead is the genocide of the world outside Paradis, carried out in the name of Eldian freedom.

If the question is "which ideology causes less harm," the series gives you an uncomfortable answer: it is unclear. Zeke's plan ends one bloodline. Eren's plan ends most of humanity. That these two things are even comparable — that the narrative forces you to compare them — is an indication of how seriously the series takes Zeke's position. He is not the alternative to the real monster. He is one of two monsters, and the real question is which one you find more livable.

The implicit argument the series makes through [Mikasa's resolution](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Mikasa_Ackermann) — killing Eren, freeing Ymir Fritz from her two-thousand-year enslavement by demonstrating that love can exist without submission — is adjacent to the Zeke/Eren debate but does not resolve it. What Mikasa proves is that someone shaped entirely by love for another person can still act against that person when acting against them is the only free act left. Ymir Fritz spent millennia bound because she loved the king who enslaved her; Mikasa demonstrates that love which claims everything, including the right to end a life, is not actually love anymore. This is relevant to Zeke insofar as his "love" for Eldians requires ending them — but it is not a philosophical refutation of antinatalism. It is a clarification of what the word means.

Zeke dies convinced he was right. He dies after helping Eren access the Founding Titan's power, in a plan that backfires catastrophically — not because Zeke was wrong about the problem, but because Eren had a different solution and used Zeke to enable it. In a series full of people who are betrayed by the systems they serve, [Zeke is the one who is betrayed by the person](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-would-zekes-euthanasia-plan-have-worked/) he trusted most, in service of an outcome [he found worse than doing nothing](https://www.thedailybeast.com/obsessed/how-attack-on-titan-became-one-of-the-most-impactful-polarizing-anime-ever/).

He is the series' most complete tragedy. Not because his plan was good. Because it was coherent, and coherence was not enough.

## The Question It Leaves

The reason Zeke endures as a character, long past the final arc, is that his philosophical position is genuinely unresolved by the narrative. You can watch the series conclude and feel satisfied that Eren was stopped, that Ymir was freed, that something like peace became possible. You cannot feel satisfied that Zeke was answered. The logic still sits there: generations of Eldians suffered because they were born Eldian, and the series does not successfully refute the claim that they would have been spared that suffering by not being born.

What it does instead is something harder and more honest: it shows you what it costs to act on that logic, and asks whether you want to live in the world that follows from it. Zeke's answer was yes. The series' answer, through its characters and its consequences, is that nobody who loves the people actually in front of them can arrive at yes — that the abstract Eldian future Zeke wants to protect is always purchased at the cost of the concrete Eldians who actually exist.

That is not a philosophical refutation. It is a moral one. The series is not arguing Zeke is wrong about suffering. It is arguing that responding to the existence of suffering by eliminating the sufferers is a choice that reveals something about what you think people are worth.

The kindest monster is still a monster. But he is a monster made by the same world that made everyone else, and Attack on Titan insists you understand how.
