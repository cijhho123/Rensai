The visual grammar of fiction has been consistent for most of human storytelling: darkness is dangerous. The night is where predators move, where the unconscious goes to work, where danger comes from. Light means safety. Morning means you survived. The protagonist who gets lost in the dark is in trouble; the protagonist who stands in full sunlight has won.

*Call of the Night* (Yofukashi no Uta) decides to do something simple and quietly radical. It inverts this. The night is rendered in vibrant neon — electric blues, purples, gold, green, every color the city offers when it thinks no one is watching. The morning is grey and washed-out, bright but bleached of color. Saturated with light and emptied of everything else.

This isn't an aesthetic quirk. It's an argument.

## What the Color Palette Is Actually Saying

[Kotoyama serialized *Yofukashi no Uta* in Weekly Shōnen Sunday from August 2019 to January 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night), twenty volumes over four years. The anime adaptation, [produced by Liden Films and directed by Tomoyuki Itamura](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=24624), arrived in Summer 2022 and confronted its production designers with a very specific problem: how do you make night feel like freedom rather than threat?

The solution they arrived at is among the more interesting visual choices in recent anime. Scenes taking place at night use deep, saturated colors — ambient neon, reflected streetlight, the specific gold of late-night convenience stores, the blue-purple of a city sky that never goes fully dark. [As reviewers noted, the series uses smatterings of gold, green, blue, and purple against a nighttime backdrop, creating an atmosphere that is simultaneously lush and urban](https://animehajime.com/2022/10/22/call-of-the-night/). The night is a place where color *lives*.

The morning, by contrast, is rendered in dull, desaturated grey-white. [The scenes in daylight look harsher and bleaker despite the presence of direct sunlight](https://www.theouterhaven.net/2022/09/anime-review-yofukashi-no-uta-call-of-the-night/). Sun isn't warmth in *Call of the Night*. It's absence of shade. It's the flattening of everything the night made interesting.

This visual choice is doing philosophical work. Fiction has trained us to read bright, lit environments as good and dark environments as threatening. *Call of the Night* is systematically retrained that reflex across thirteen episodes of anime (and twenty manga volumes). By the time you're comfortable in the show's aesthetic, you have learned, visually, that the night is the real world and the day is the performance. You haven't been told this. You've been conditioned to feel it. That's the difference between a theme stated and a theme demonstrated.

## Ko's Insomnia Is Not a Disorder

[Ko Yamori is a fourteen-year-old honor-roll student who stops going to school](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori). He can't sleep during normal hours. He starts wandering the city at night. He meets Nazuna, a vampire.

The show's premise, framed conventionally, is a teen with insomnia having a supernatural adventure. The show's actual premise, once you look at how it's constructed, is: a teen whose body is correctly registering that the world it's being asked to operate in during daylight hours is not the world it wants to be in.

Ko isn't broken. [His insomnia is a rational response to the grinding inauthenticity of his daily life](https://artificialnightsky.wordpress.com/2022/07/30/yofukashi-no-utas-insomnia-analysis/): the performance of being a good student, the social dynamics of school that he can't make himself care about, the specific exhaustion of being someone who has met all the external metrics of success and found it empty. He doesn't have a sleep disorder. He has a society disorder. [The series positions this not as individual dysfunction but as a systemic failure — Ko's burnout is a form of correct reading of an alienating environment](https://ogiuemaniax.com/2025/10/19/going-unsteady-call-of-the-night-full-review/).

The night accepts him as he is. [The night is punk — it's a rejection of conformity and embodies anti-establishment behaviors](https://artificialnightsky.wordpress.com/2022/07/30/yofukashi-no-utas-insomnia-analysis/). Nobody out at 2 AM is there because the schedule demands it. The late-night city is populated by people who couldn't sleep, wouldn't sleep, or chose something else over sleep — all of them exempt from the daytime performance for a few hours. Ko, who has always been out of sync with the schedule, finds he fits perfectly into this world that exists between other people's sleeping and waking.

This is the political argument *Call of the Night* is quietly making: the schedule itself is the problem. The institutions and rhythms of the day — school, family expectations, social hierarchies — are the antagonist. Not in the dramatic sense of a villain, but in the structural sense of a system that doesn't accommodate everyone and doesn't admit it. Ko's insomnia is his immune response to that system.

## The Vampire Rule Changes Everything

[Traditional vampire mythology is about predation and infection](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-anime-humanizes-deconstructs-vampires/). The vampire bites; the victim transforms, cursed or blessed depending on the story. The human's consent is largely beside the point.

*Call of the Night* introduces a single change to this mythology that rewrites its entire moral structure: [in this world, a human can only be transformed into a vampire if they are bitten by a vampire they genuinely love](https://screenrant.com/call-night-manga-shonen-jump-vampires-blood-transform/). If you don't love them, the bite does nothing. The vampire feeds; you remain human.

This makes vampirism, in this story, into a coming-of-age metaphor about emotional authenticity. Ko can't just *choose* to become a vampire the way a character in most stories chooses transformation. He has to develop a feeling that is real — not performed, not rationalized, not manufactured to meet the condition. He has to love Nazuna, or he can't change. The threshold for transformation is genuine emotional access.

For a fourteen-year-old honor-roll student who left school because he couldn't make himself feel anything about the life he was living, this is an interesting problem. [The series frames Ko's search for vampirism not as a death wish or a power fantasy but as a genuine question about whether he can access something real](https://aftermath.site/coming-of-age-anime-call-of-the-night-vampires/). Can he feel something that isn't performed? Can he love someone who isn't part of the system he's retreating from?

This also reframes Nazuna's side of the relationship. A vampire who falls in love with a human becomes dangerous to them — her bite, under those conditions, becomes lethal rather than transformative. [The series eventually reaches a point where Nazuna's genuine love for Ko creates a paradox: she can't bite him without endangering him, and he can't transform without her bite](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained). The fantasy of the night becoming permanent, of Ko joining Nazuna in her world, runs into the same problem that every genuine relationship eventually runs into: love changes the terms.

## Kotoyama's Philosophy

[Kotoyama, in interviews about the series, described his intent as drawing "a rebellious rom-com"](https://ogiuemaniax.com/2025/10/19/going-unsteady-call-of-the-night-full-review/) — specifically using a frame of rebellion rather than escape or fantasy. The distinction matters. Escape implies you're leaving something behind and forgetting it. Rebellion implies you're leaving something behind and declaring that it was wrong.

His earlier series [*Dagashi Kashi* (2014-2018)](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/people.php?id=142614) was built around a similar philosophy — drawing "with a preparedness to show what I like without hiding." That series was an extended love letter to Japanese convenience-store candy, treating the subject with the depth and sincerity usually reserved for more "serious" topics. *Call of the Night* does the same thing with insomnia and the late-night city. The willingness to treat apparently frivolous subjects as worthy of genuine examination is the through-line between the two works, and it's what separates both from more generic shonen offerings.

[The title itself came from a Creepy Nuts song — "Yofukashi no Uta" — which Kotoyama heard and immediately decided was the only possible name for the series](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html). The song celebrates insomnia and nocturnal life not as a malady but as a preference: staying awake because the night has things the day doesn't, because sleep would mean missing something, because the darkness is company. The manga extends this sensibility across twenty volumes of a specific and sincere argument that Ko's impulse isn't pathological. It's just inconvenient for the people who need him to function on schedule.

## The Controversy and the Series' Answer

[The series generates controversy primarily around the age gap between Ko (14) and Nazuna (a centuries-old vampire who presents as a young woman)](https://www.anime-planet.com/forum/threads/call-of-the-night.339899/). This is a legitimate concern and the show doesn't entirely resolve it. The power dynamic between a centuries-old immortal and a fourteen-year-old is structurally asymmetric regardless of how emotionally even-handed the writing tries to be.

But the controversy also tends to miss what the series is actually doing with the relationship. [The series consistently foregrounds the impossibility and difficulty of the connection rather than romanticizing it](https://theshyotaku.com/call-of-the-night-vampires-and-romance-done-right/). Ko and Nazuna cannot simply be together; the rules of their world prevent it. Their relationship is most interesting not when it's delivering wish fulfillment but when it's forcing them to confront what they actually feel versus what they want to feel — the same problem Ko has with school, with sleeping, with everything the day demands of him.

This is exactly where the day/night framework is most useful as a lens. Daylight logic demands that feelings be legible, schedulable, and categorically appropriate — this is romantic, this is family, this is friendship, all clearly labeled and manageable. The night says: feel what you actually feel, even if it's confusing, even if it doesn't resolve into a recognizable category. Ko's feelings for Nazuna don't resolve into clean love-story territory. They complicate. [The series treats this complication as honesty rather than as a failure of the romance](https://ogiuemaniax.com/2025/10/19/going-unsteady-call-of-the-night-full-review/), which is exactly how it treats Ko's insomnia.

## What Gets Made from the Inversion

The decision to make the morning grey and the night vivid is the animating logic of the entire series. Everything else follows from it: the insomnia as correct assessment, the vampirism as authentic transformation, the rebellion as genuine rather than performed.

Most fiction that features alienated teenagers eventually offers the day as the resolution. The teen finds their place, returns to the world, is reintegrated into the schedule. *Call of the Night* doesn't do this. Ko doesn't learn to sleep at normal hours. [He learns that some people are genuinely wired differently from the schedule, and that "fixing" this would mean extinguishing something real about himself](https://ogiuemaniax.com/2025/10/19/going-unsteady-call-of-the-night-full-review/).

The night, in this series, is not a temporary refuge from the real world. It is the real world, for people like Ko. The day is the temporary performance.

The colors told you this from episode one. The morning was always dull.
