Midway through [*Another*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/11111/Another), Mei Misaki gives the series' most important line from inside a doll shop surrounded by glass eyes. "Dolls are hollow, you see?" she says. "Completely hollow in body and soul. That void connects them with death. But hollow things seek to fill their emptiness."

This is not character texture. It is a description of the curse.

The "Another" phenomenon in *Another* — the supernatural engine that has been killing Class 3-3 students and their relatives every year since 1972 — works precisely the way Mei describes a doll. Something that should be dead takes on the shape and weight and memory of a living person. It occupies a space in the class. It attends school. Its parents believe in it. Its classmates interact with it. And none of them can see the void where a soul should be, because the curse suppresses that knowledge. The "Another" is the most elaborately realized doll in the series — and Mei Misaki is the only character who can see what hollow things actually are.

![Another — Mei Misaki and the doll shop where the series' thesis lives](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/11111/78428.jpg)

## What a Doll Does

A doll is not a representation of a person. It is a *substitute* for a person — an object created to fill a space where a human being is missing, shaped to satisfy the grief of those who need something to hold. The distinction matters. Representation says "this stands for that." Substitution says "this *is* that now."

The 1972 incident that started the curse was an act of pure substitution. A popular student in Class 3-3 died mid-year. His classmates — and then, astonishingly, his teachers — could not accept the loss. They began treating him as if he were still alive. They set a place for him at graduation. The pretense was so total that reality itself eventually bent to accommodate it: he appeared in the graduation photo. And from that first act of grief-as-denial, the curse began. Every year, one dead student joins the living class and is believed to be alive by everyone, including themselves, until the deaths start accumulating and someone figures out who the hollow thing is.

[The mechanism is a literalization of what Mei says about dolls](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Countermeasures). Hollow things seek to fill their emptiness. The class produced a void through grief and refusal, and something filled it. That something looks human, behaves human, and has human memories. What it does not have — what Mei's glass eye can see — is the vital sign of a living soul. It is a body performing life from the inside of death.

This is why the doll shop is not coincidence. [Mei works there precisely because dolls are what she understands](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki), and what she understands is the difference between appearance and presence — between a thing that looks like it contains a soul and a thing that actually does. The shop is her training ground. She was raised to see voids.

## The Genealogy of Substitution

The doll that most illuminates Mei's position is the one on the shelf resembling her. [According to both the anime and the light novel by Yukito Ayatsuji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)) — the novel expands this backstory more fully than the adaptation — the doll was created to represent one of the twins Yukiyo lost during pregnancy. Mei had a twin sister, Misaki Fujioka, who died young from a heart condition. Misaki Fujioka was adopted separately, raised in different circumstances by a different branch of the family, and died before she and Mei could properly know each other. The doll on the shelf is a ghost of the sister — a hollow substitute for a loss that Mei's grandmother tried to make tangible through craft.

Mei, then, is not simply a person with an unusual glass eye and a doll shop connection. She is a person born into a family that deals in substitution. Her grandmother makes substitutes for the dead. Her own existence was shadowed from birth by a twin who filled a different space — raised by a different mother, never quite present, never quite absent. Mei has been surrounded by hollow things seeking to fill emptiness since before she could name what she was seeing. The curse didn't make her the class's designated non-existent person by accident. It selected someone who had already been practicing living beside the hollow.

The glass eye itself is part of this genealogy. [Given to her by her grandmother](https://www.scifidimensions.com/what-does-misaki-meis-eye-patch-and-the-curse-represent-in-another/), not grown in her skull — artificial, a doll's eye in a living person's face. Mei covers it with a patch not out of vanity but because the eye sees death: specifically, it perceives the colored aura that distinguishes the living from the dead. She keeps one eye closed to function in ordinary space, and opens it only when she needs to see clearly. This is a precise metaphor for how the curse works on everyone else: they keep the seeing eye closed by design, the curse suppressing the vision that would reveal the hollow thing in their midst.

## The Aesthetics of the Uncanny

![Mei Misaki — the character who sees the void where the curse hides its hollow thing](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/14/267565.jpg)

[P.A. Works is known for its background art](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/features/2016/8/27/feature-why-are-the-backgrounds-of-pa-works-so-beautiful-an-interview-with-anime-art-director-kazuki-higashiji) — the almost architectural precision of its painted environments, the light through windows, the way rooms feel inhabited. In *Another*, art director Kazuki Higashiji used this skill strategically against its own usual effect. The Yomiyama setting is beautiful and also suffocating. Gorgeous backgrounds make the town feel more real, not less — and a more real town with a more real curse is more disturbing than any stylized horror environment would be.

The character animation goes further. [Critics have noted that the "slightly jilted nature of the animation" gives the characters "the appearance of life-like dolls at times"](https://www.cbr.com/another-stylistic-elements-make-best-horror-anime/), creating a dissociative eeriness where the humans look slightly less human than the detailed backgrounds they move through. This is almost certainly intentional. In a show about a hollow thing that looks human, making all the humans look slightly hollow is a precise visual strategy. You cannot trust that the person on screen is fully alive, because the animation style has stripped some vitality from everyone. The "Another" does not stand out from the living class not just because the curse suppresses recognition, but because nobody in Yomiyama looks entirely present.

The red color choices also work against the palette. [*Another* deliberately uses muted tones throughout the series](https://anime-ashoka.medium.com/another-the-anime-thats-redefining-the-horror-genre-4dc624d862da), reserving red for violent moments so it hits with maximum impact against the grey-brown town. But this also means the moments of death are the most visually vivid moments in the series — the most alive-feeling, aesthetically, are the moments when someone is being killed. Life is muted. Death is saturated. The show's color system inverts the expected relationship between living and dying, which is exactly what the curse does thematically.

## What the Hollow Thing Wants

Mei's formulation — "hollow things seek to fill their emptiness" — raises a question the series doesn't fully answer but gestures toward: does the "Another" *want* anything? Is the curse passive (a structural phenomenon that fills a void automatically) or is there something in the hollow thing that reaches toward fullness?

[The mechanics, per the anime's logic](https://canime.wordpress.com/2013/09/15/another-anime-review/), suggest the phenomenon is purely structural: a dead person enters Class 3-3 and everyone's memories reorganize around their presence, including the dead person's own memories. They don't know they're dead. They are not a ghost experiencing their haunting. They are a genuine subjective person with no access to the fact of their own death, and around them reality has restructured itself to make that impossible to notice.

This is the cruelest part of the concept, and the part the series most consistently underplays in favor of the Final Destination death sequences. The "Another" is not a monster. It is a person who is dead but does not know it, surrounded by people who have been made to accept them, living a life that is procedurally identical to a real life in every respect except the most fundamental one. The hollow thing is not seeking — it is simply present, filling the space it was pushed into, with no capacity to know what it lacks. The suffering is not the "Another"'s. The suffering belongs entirely to the class trying to figure out which of their classmates is dead, and to whoever the dead person turns out to be when the revelation comes.

What the series does not pursue, and probably should have, is the implication of the "Another"'s experience at the moment of death. When the class identifies and kills the "Another" to stop the curse, they are killing a person who genuinely does not know they are dead. That person has memories, attachments, and a complete subjective life that has been organized by the phenomenon around an impossibility: their own continued existence. The ethics of this situation are genuinely disturbing — [the series tends to frame the killing as necessary tragedy](https://canime.wordpress.com/2013/09/15/another-anime-review/), which sidesteps the question of what it means to kill someone who experiences themselves as innocent.

This is also, at its root, a horror story about grief. The first "Another" was created because a class could not let their dead friend go. Every subsequent one is the curse reproducing that original act of denial at mechanical scale. The hollow thing at the center of each year's Class 3-3 is the class's own refusal to accept that people die — literalized into a presence that must be identified and killed to stop the dying. The doll metaphor completes this: you cannot grieve what you have replaced with a substitute. The town of Yomiyama has been building a new doll every year for five decades. The only way to stop the mechanism is to accept the void it was designed to hide.

## The Shop at the Edge of the Story

The doll shop reappears at critical moments in *Another* — [Mei's grandmother's establishment](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Mei_Misaki) is where Koichi finds her at the beginning, surrounded by glass eyes in jars, under the same flat light that the series uses for its most disorienting sequences. It is not a welcoming place. It is a place for confronting what humans make when they cannot accept loss: hollow surrogates with human faces.

[Yukito Ayatsuji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukito_Ayatsuji) is the founder of the shinhonkaku (neo-orthodox) mystery movement in Japan — a writer whose primary commitment is to the puzzle-box structure of Golden Age mystery fiction, [the tradition of fair-play deduction that treats the reader as a collaborator rather than a spectator](https://crimereads.com/the-honkaku-and-shin-honkaku-mysteries-of-seishi-yokomizo/). What makes *Another* unusual in his bibliography is that the supernatural element — the curse, the "Another," the memory suppression — is not an atmospheric flourish around a conventional mystery. It is the mystery's engine. The puzzle is: who is the hollow thing? The process of solving it follows honkaku fair-play principles exactly, with Koichi's erased memories functioning as the locked room. And the answer, when it comes, is illuminated by the doll shop and what Mei told us there.

Hollow things seek to fill their emptiness. The course of the series is the process of learning to see which thing is hollow — and discovering that the hollow thing has been sitting in plain sight, no more visible than a doll on a shelf.
