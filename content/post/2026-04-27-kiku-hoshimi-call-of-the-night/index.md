Ko Yamori wants to stop being human. Kiku Hoshimi wants to stop being a vampire. They are pursuing opposite transformations through structurally identical methods: they each have a theory about what makes the change possible, they each have a subject they need in order to test it, and they each are wrong about whether the theory will work.

This parallel is not coincidental. Kotoyama built Kiku specifically to function as Ko's dark mirror — a character who illuminates what Ko's project actually is by running the same experiment in reverse. The Kiku arc, which dominates the second half of the manga and the second season of the anime, is the point where *Call of the Night* stops being a romance about a boy and a vampire and becomes a story about what it means to want to be something other than what you are.

## The Theory and the Subject

Kiku Hoshimi is one of the older vampires in the series — ancient, powerful, and described consistently as sociopathic. She has spent considerable time studying vampiric transformation rules, specifically the pair that structures the entire series: a human must love a vampire to be turned, and a vampire who loves a human will kill them when they bite. The second rule is the one Kiku has made her obsession.

Her theory: if a vampire who loves a human cannot bite without killing, then the act of killing someone she loves might reverse her vampirism. She wants to become human. The mechanism she has identified requires her to fall genuinely in love with a human and then drink their blood — killing them in the process. {{< citation >}}Kotoyama. *Call of the Night* (Yofukashi no Uta), Volumes 14–18. Shogakukan/Weekly Shonen Sunday, 2022–2023.{{< /citation >}}

Mahiru Seki, Ko's friend from school who has become entangled in the vampire world, is Kiku's chosen subject.

The architecture here is a precise mirror of Ko's situation. Ko identified Nazuna as the vampire who could transform him. He knows the rule: he must love her. He is therefore engineering emotional conditions — or trying to understand them — in order to meet a transformation requirement. Kiku knows she needs to love Mahiru. She is therefore engineering emotional conditions in order to meet her own transformation requirement.

The difference that the series has built invisibly into both arcs is the same: you cannot engineer love. Ko has been trying to generate the correct internal state through accumulated experience. Kiku has been trying to generate it through deliberate exposure and proximity. Both are running research programs on living people. Neither research program produces the outcome it targets.

## What "Sociopathic" Actually Means

Kiku is described as sociopathic, and the series is careful about what this means in her specific case. She is not incapable of feeling. She is incapable of fully registering what it costs other people when she treats them as variables. Her centuries of life have produced enormous accumulated knowledge and essentially no updated capacity for empathy with her subjects — not because she is malicious but because the knowledge-accumulation process she has engaged in has consistently decoupled data from consequence.

What makes Kiku more complex than a simple villain is that the series takes her stated desire seriously. She genuinely wants to become human. The series does not position this as self-deception or as a cover for something darker. She has watched what immortality costs — she has seen Haru Nanakusa, Nazuna's mother, pursue the same goal — and she has concluded that she does not want to continue. The wanting is real. The method is the problem.

Her method requires Mahiru to be a person she can love. But she approaches Mahiru the way a researcher approaches a subject: she selects him because he has characteristics her theory requires, she monitors his responses, she manages their interaction to produce the conditions she needs. This cannot generate love, because love requires relating to someone as an end rather than as the occasion for your transformation.

Mahiru, meanwhile, develops genuine feelings for Kiku. This is the element Kiku did not account for: she selected a subject who could respond to her management with something that resembled the real thing, and she created conditions that produced, on his side, genuine emotional investment. The result is that Mahiru becomes willing to die for her — not as a research subject accepting a variable's outcome, but as a person choosing. This places Kiku in exactly the position her theory required, and she finds she cannot use it. The asymmetry is the series' most precise observation about how her method fails. She needed to fall in love with him. What happened is that he fell in love with her. These are not the same thing, and the method cannot correct for the difference.

## The Rule and the Reversal

The transformation rule in *Call of the Night* is elegantly constructed as a recursive trap. To be turned vampire, a human must love the vampire. To achieve the lethal bite that (Kiku theorizes) reverses vampirism, a vampire must love the human. Both rules require a genuine emotional state that cannot be manufactured on demand. Both protagonists — Ko and Kiku — spend their respective arcs trying to manufacture precisely that.

Ko's arc reaches its resolution sideways: he becomes a half-vampire not through deliberately achieving love but through accumulated life and genuine relationship. The transformation condition is met, but not as the result of Ko's strategic project. It happens in spite of his strategy, through the organic development of the thing he was trying to engineer. {{< citation >}}*Call of the Night*, Vol. 20 (final volume). Ko's partial transformation is completed not through deliberate effort but through circumstance and authentic feeling, which the series frames as the distinction the entire run has been building toward.{{< /citation >}}

Kiku's arc reaches a different resolution. She does develop something genuine for Mahiru. The series is careful not to call it love explicitly — the emotional vocabulary Kiku has access to after centuries of detachment is not adequate to name what changes in her. But something changes. And the change does not produce the lethal bite. The rule, if it is a rule, does not trigger the way she theorized.

This is the series' argument in concentrated form: the theory was based on a misunderstanding of what the rule is measuring. The lethal bite requires love, but love is not a state you arrive at on purpose. It is a condition that changes you before you recognize it. Kiku's research project assumed she could remain the researcher while falling in love. The actual experience of love — to the extent she gets there — requires her to stop being the researcher. And she cannot stop being the researcher without the entire project collapsing.

## The Ko That Kiku Clarifies

Reading Ko's arc through Kiku's illuminates what Ko was actually doing during the first half of the series that was obscured by the romance framing.

Ko's stated goal is to become a vampire by loving Nazuna. His method, from the beginning, is experiential: he spends nights with Nazuna, he experiences things, he checks against his emotional state to see if love has developed. This is not dramatically different from Kiku's approach — the difference is one of self-awareness. Ko is not as sophisticated as Kiku. He is not conducting a formal research program. But he is doing the same thing: trying to monitor the development of a feeling rather than simply having it.

The series' recurring motif of Ko taking stock of his emotions — asking himself how he feels about Nazuna, assessing whether what he experiences constitutes love — is actually a depiction of this contradiction. The attempt to observe the feeling is itself what prevents the feeling. When the feeling eventually arrives, it arrives precisely when Ko stops trying to verify it. {{< citation >}}This structural insight — that authentic feeling is incompatible with simultaneous self-monitoring — recurs across the series' secondary characters as well, particularly in the Nazuna arc's depiction of her engineered emotional distance.{{< /citation >}}

Kiku's arc makes this subtext explicit. Her version of Ko's monitoring is formalized into a research protocol. The failure of her protocol — not just because her theory about vampiric reversal is wrong but because the love-as-research-subject approach is structurally incompatible with love — retroactively clarifies what Ko was doing and why it could only resolve the way it did.

## The Character Who Wanted the Wrong Thing in the Right Way

There is a compassion in how Kotoyama writes Kiku's failure that complicates any straightforward reading of her as an antagonist.

She wants to be human. This is not presented as pathetic or deluded. The manga understands, through Kiku and through Haru Nanakusa before her, that immortality is not obviously desirable — that watching everything around you change while you remain static, that accumulating knowledge without being changed by it, that outliving every relationship you form, constitutes a kind of ongoing injury. Kiku's desire to escape this condition is legible.

What she gets wrong is not the goal but the method — and specifically the assumption that the method can be separated from the kind of person who uses it. Her research protocol works against her because it is the expression of exactly the pathology she is trying to escape. A being who relates to others as subjects of investigation cannot generate love by observing that she is supposed to generate love. The research orientation is not a tool she can put down when convenient; it is the shape her centuries have made her.

{{< citation >}}*Call of the Night*, Vol. 17–18. Kiku's arc resolution distinguishes her from the series' other antagonists by granting her a form of change that is not the transformation she pursued. Kotoyama's choice to give her ambiguous continuation — not defeated, not successful, changed in ways she did not intend — is the most honest outcome the series can offer her.{{< /citation >}}

What she achieves is not human. But it is not unchanged vampire either. The series offers her an ending that is neither her goal nor her prior state — which is to say, the kind of transformation that does not announce itself as a transformation. The same ending, arrived at the same way, as Ko's.

## What the Inversion Proves

Ko and Kiku mirror each other across a gap that is ultimately about direction rather than kind. He is moving toward the supernatural; she is moving toward the human. They are both running version of the same project and arriving at the same structural limit: you cannot get to a genuine emotional state by trying to produce it.

The series has built this parallel over its entire run, and the second season makes it explicit. What the inversion proves — what Kiku's arc adds that Ko's alone could not — is that the direction of travel is irrelevant. Whether you are trying to become more or less than human, the thing that gets you there is not the method. It is the relationship — the actual, unrepeatable, unmonitored experience of another person — that changes you. When the change happens, it is not the change you were looking for.

That is Call of the Night's thesis. Kiku is the proof that it applies in both directions.
