The professional cuddling job is the most honest thing about Nazuna Nanakusa.

She works as a paid snuggler for strangers — [dressing up, providing warmth, making clients feel temporarily wanted](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-episode-5-nazuna-professional-cuddler/), then taking the money and leaving before anything real has to happen. The transaction protects her as much as it serves them. She can be intimate without being present. She can be needed without being known. [As the RABUJOI review of episode five notes](https://rabujoi.wordpress.com/2022/08/04/call-of-the-night-05-feel-them-for-me/), this profession sustains her financially — or would, if she hadn't been neglecting it since meeting Ko, because suddenly she has something that makes the professional stuff feel thin.

That detail — the abandoned cuddling clients, the mild debt — is *Call of the Night* telling you who Nazuna actually is before she knows it herself.

## The Distance She Keeps

[According to the Nazuna wiki entry](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa), she appears to be in her early twenties but is actually thirty to forty years old, the born child of a vampire mother and a human father — an outcast among vampire society, unclassified, drifting. She survives by feeding on willing humans and, for extra income, by providing the cuddling services that have become her primary form of human contact.

But there's a hierarchy to the intimacy she allows. The cuddling clients get physical warmth, a professional performance of care, and no genuine access. The bite victims get something more intimate — blood draws on a level that is, within the series' mythology, genuinely vulnerable — but the relationship ends there. Nobody stays. Nobody knows her name unprompted. [Her daily life is empty apartment, video games, and booze](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2022/10/04/a-review-of-call-of-the-night/), interrupted by night-shifts of engineered closeness that she controls completely.

What Nazuna has constructed is a life where she is always the one providing the intimacy, which means she's always the one who could leave first. [As the Star Crossed Anime review notes](https://starcrossedanime.com/yofukashi-no-uta-anime-review-68-100/), this isn't unique to her temperament — the vampires in the series are 'lonely people' who 'lack meaningful relationships,' the isolation an occupational hazard of immortality. Nazuna's construction is just the most elaborately engineered version of it.

The series establishes this before the vampire mythology even enters the picture. When Ko Yamori appears on the bridge in the middle of the night, she could dismiss him. Instead, she takes him home and starts teaching him about the night. [As the Aftermath analysis of the series notes](https://aftermath.site/coming-of-age-anime-call-of-the-night-vampires/), she becomes "abrasive and guarded" whenever genuine feeling gets close — deflecting with jokes and dirty comments. It's a familiar defense mechanism, recognizable from every character who learned that being needed and being known are dangerous in the same proportion.

## The Taste of Emotion

Here is the detail that encodes the series' central tension: [Ko's blood tastes exceptional](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa). Not just good — "astonishingly delicious," the text says. And the reason is that she can taste the emotional texture of blood, and Ko's emotional life — his insomnia, his social alienation, his confused longing — has the richness of someone fully inhabiting his own interior. She can feel what he's feeling through the act of drinking from him.

This is a problem for Nazuna disguised as a pleasure. She has spent decades keeping emotional intensity at arm's length: the cuddling clients feel something but she doesn't, the bite victims provide blood but not intimacy. What she craves — she realizes this gradually — is exactly the thing she's been designing her existence to avoid: someone else's emotion, close enough to taste.

[As Game Rant's analysis of Nazuna's character notes](https://gamerant.com/call-of-the-night-things-that-make-nazuna-unique/), blood-sharing between vampire and human doesn't stay clinical — 'the heart ends up getting involved and not just in pumping the stuff through the arteries.' And within the series' mythology, this carries a specific consequence: if a vampire falls in love with their feedee, the human dies. Her emotional avoidance isn't merely a psychological defense. It is a matter of Ko's survival. She would rather conduct an entire professional emotional-support service for strangers than acknowledge what she feels for one specific person. The cuddle business is sustainable; love is fatal.

## The Trap That Was Always Hers

The transformation condition is widely understood as Ko's problem: he has to fall genuinely in love with Nazuna to complete the process. But that framing belongs to the early episodes, when the series is still presenting Nazuna as the one with information and power. What gradually becomes clear is that the condition is equally her trap.

[Kotoyama has stated directly in interview](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) that 'the idea of affection was the main point of Call of the Night,' and that 'the story would end instantly if Nazuna was experienced in being affectionate.' The transformation condition is the formal expression of that design: for the process to work, the vampire must genuinely love the human. Nazuna knows this. She also knows, at some level that she refuses to examine directly, that the condition requires her to do the one thing her entire constructed life is engineered to prevent. She has to want Ko specifically, not abstractly. She has to love him in the way that has a name, not in the way that can be disavowed as "it was just his blood."

The series stages this trap over its entire length. Every time Nazuna gets flustered when love is mentioned directly, every time she deflects Ko's genuine questions with a joke, every time she positions herself as the experienced one who doesn't need anything from him — she's running from the condition she set herself. She proposed the deal. She created the framework. And then she spent the rest of the story failing to meet her own requirements.

[The manga's final arc](https://epicstream.com/article/call-of-the-night-manga-ending-explained) resolves this precisely — [Chapter 200](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Chapter_200) positions the resolution entirely as Nazuna's failure to maintain distance: she walks away from Ko because she loves him, and because genuine love between them carries the threat of his death under the series' final mythology. She leaves. For three years she stays away. When Ko finds her, she tries to maintain the distance she's been maintaining since the first episode — and fails. The ending is her failing to keep the distance, which is the only thing the story needed her to do.

## What Immortality Does to You

[As the Star Crossed Anime review notes](https://starcrossedanime.com/yofukashi-no-uta-anime-review-68-100/), Nazuna 'had long since lost interest in the night and was just going through the motions' before Ko arrived — immortality is its own particular desolation: everything eventually loses its novelty, everyone you care about eventually dies or leaves, and the eternal solution is to stop investing enough to feel the loss. Nazuna has been a vampire long enough to have learned this lesson, probably several times. The professional cuddling is what thirty-plus years of that lesson produces: stay warm, stay available, stay detached.

What the series argues — through Ko, through his specific, annoying, emotionally excessive presence — is that the solution to the immortal's loneliness isn't to stop feeling. It's that someone whose blood tastes like that has to exist in the world before the taste makes the argument for them. Ko doesn't convince Nazuna that love is safe. He makes the case empirically: his blood tastes like someone who hasn't given up yet, and she keeps going back.

[The reviewer at Everything is Bad For You](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2022/10/04/a-review-of-call-of-the-night/) identifies the central paradox correctly: both Nazuna and Ko are "making all this up as they go," and Nazuna's apparent experience advantage is mostly performed. Beneath the decades and the late-night wandering, she's as adrift as he is — she's just had longer to construct infrastructure around the drifting.

## The Title She Was Always In

*Yofukashi no Uta* — staying up late's song. Ko's insomnia is the entry point, his rejection of the daylight world the premise. But Nazuna is also staying up late, in a way that predates Ko by decades. She has been up all night for years, drifting between apartments and clients and video games, feeding her way through an existence that she'd describe as carefree and that the series gradually reveals as hollow.

The neglected cuddling clients, the mild debt she accumulates since meeting Ko — those details are the series measuring what he costs her. She'd built an entire economy around the night because the night asked nothing back. She starts losing money the moment someone makes it mean something again.

She feeds everyone. For a while, she gets to call that enough.
