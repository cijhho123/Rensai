In chapter 337 of [Berserk](https://myanimelist.net/manga/2/Berserk), a high school boy slaps a god across the face.

Not metaphorically. Rickert — former member of the Band of the Hawk, blacksmith's apprentice, the one survivor who was nowhere near the Eclipse when it happened — walks into the court of Falconia, listens to Griffith ask if he still wants to help him achieve his dream, and hits him. In front of guards. In front of Locus. In front of the entire assembled court of the miraculous city Griffith has constructed.

Griffith waves off the guards preparing to execute Rickert. He looks at his changed emblem — the old White Falcon replaced by the new Falcon of Light. He says nothing.

The [fan analysis community has been parsing that silence ever since](https://ncfan-1.tumblr.com/post/99655319237/chapter-337-bridge-of-parting-initial-reactions). But the slap's significance isn't in what Griffith felt. It's in the structural position Rickert occupies — the one that makes his rejection the single human act in the entire manga that Griffith cannot absorb, rationalize, or survive as a complete self-narrative.

## The Unbanded Survivor

To understand why Rickert can do what no one else can, you need to understand what he is: the only surviving Band of the Hawk member who carries neither a brand nor a wound.

[Guts carries the Brand of Sacrifice](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Guts), which attracts malevolent spirits every night and keeps him in permanent physical torment. Casca carries the brand and the trauma of what was done to her during the Eclipse. Both of them experience Griffith's ascension as an ongoing violation — a wound that cannot close because its cause continues to exist. Their hatred of Griffith is visceral and organized by pain.

[Rickert's position is entirely different](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Rickert). He was sidelined by injury before Griffith's imprisonment, which put him outside the nexus when the sacrifice occurred. When Guts found him afterward and told him what happened, Guts explicitly said he couldn't take Rickert along on his quest — "You're too gentle a person to hate Griffith." This turns out to be both accurate and wrong in the most interesting way. Rickert doesn't hate Griffith with Guts' consuming fury. But he is not forgiving him either.

In the years between the Eclipse and his arrival in Falconia, Rickert becomes a blacksmith. [He forges swords](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_337_(Manga)) — not as weapons but as grave markers, planted in the hill outside Godot's house as a monument to the Band's dead. His grief becomes craft, his mourning becomes material. When he finally confronts Griffith, he references this act directly: the swords on the hill are his answer to the question of what the Band of the Hawk was and what it is now. They are his testament that the original Band is already memorialized, already gone, and that the new institution wearing its name is something else entirely.

## What Falconia Asks

[Falconia is real](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Falconia). This is the challenge Rickert faces on arrival that no prior confrontation with Griffith's legacy required. The city is miraculous — humankind flourishing, protected from the merged physical and astral world's supernatural threats, governed by a figure who has delivered on the largest scale any dream ever demanded. Rickert cannot simply hate this. He has to reason his way to a moral position that the brand's constant torment organizes for Guts automatically.

This is what makes Rickert's slap harder than anything Guts does. Guts fights Griffith from inside a wound Griffith inflicted. Rickert rejects Griffith from a position of relative clarity. He saw the city. He saw what Griffith became. He chose no anyway.

The core of his verbal argument [is carefully structured](https://bthump.home.blog/2018/09/29/what-are-your-thoughts-on-the-scene-of-rickert/): he acknowledges he was not at the Eclipse and therefore cannot completely hate Griffith the way Guts does. He notes the Band of the Hawk emblem has changed. He asserts that the original Band died with Griffith's humanity, and that what succeeded it does not have the right to the name. He does not disavow the human leader he once followed. He disavows the demon that leader chose to become.

## The Two Questions the Silence Raises

Fan discourse since chapter 337's release has centered on two interpretive problems.

**Did Griffith allow the slap?**

[The Tumblr analysis at bthump](https://bthump.tumblr.com/post/730902364230418432/how-was-rickert-able-to-slap-griffith-did) presents both readings seriously. Either Griffith deliberately permitted the act — accepting Rickert's judgment with a residual grace that suggests some part of him acknowledges its justice — or Rickert's rejection genuinely caught him off guard, which would mean that even a member of the God Hand has affective blind spots when the stimulus is sufficiently personal. Both readings are productive for Griffith's characterization. If he allowed it, he retains some residual conscience. If he didn't expect it, his omniscience has limits that love (or its destruction) can expose.

**What does his silence mean?**

The answer is embedded in where he looks. [Griffith's only response is to look at his changed emblem](https://doistillhategriffith.tumblr.com/post/113213308505/reread-chapter-337-what-rickert-is-saying-thats). The old White Falcon symbol — the Band's symbol — replaced by the Falcon of Light. The fan analysis that has accumulated around this beat consistently identifies the same thing: Griffith expected forgiveness from at least some former comrades. The God Hand's logic had "promised" him that the Band's sacrifice was necessary for his dream, and that fulfilling the dream retroactively justified the cost. Rickert was supposed to be the one who saw Falconia and understood. His physical rejection — not argument, not refusal, but a slap, a human-scale act of contempt — shatters that anticipated absolution.

[This is the reading that most initial-reaction analysis converged on](https://fandomwire.com/rickert-was-a-badass-on-that-one-one-of-the-most-humiliating-griffith-moment-in-berserk-was-also-a-satisfying-one-for-us/): the slap worked because fans had long wanted one moment of human-scale accountability, and it worked precisely because it came from the least expected source. Not the brandbearer consumed by hatred, but the ordinary member who had every structural reason to accept what Griffith built.

## The Wound the God Hand Can't Process

Here is the thesis underneath the scene. The God Hand operates through a philosophical system: causality as fate, every sacrifice as necessary, every nightmare as the destined path. The sacrifice of the Band of the Hawk was not cruelty — it was, within the system's own logic, the required payment for Griffith's dream. The payment is retroactively justified by the outcome. Everyone who was destroyed had to be destroyed for the world Griffith built to exist.

This logic requires something: acceptance. Not from Guts — Guts' hatred is factored in, already rendered as a cosmic struggle that confirms Griffith's importance. Not from Casca — her silence and trauma can be held at arm's length. But from Rickert, the unbanded survivor, the one who wasn't there, the one who might look at Falconia and see only the miracle — his acceptance would have completed the narrative. It would have meant that even someone who loved the original Band, who mourned it properly, who wasn't consumed by hatred, could recognize the dream's necessity.

Rickert's refusal proves the opposite. The dead cannot be retroactively converted into willing participants by the success of what their deaths funded. [The Band of the Hawk did not sacrifice themselves for Griffith's dream](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Eclipse). They were sacrificed without consent, without knowledge, without any choice in the matter. The slap says: the fact that it worked does not make it right. A god can be wrong.

This is what [Griffith's own silence confirms](https://doistillhategriffith.tumblr.com/post/113213308505/reread-chapter-337-what-rickert-is-saying-thats) — he has no rebuttal. He can dismiss guards. He can look at his emblem. He cannot argue with what Rickert says because arguing would require him to claim the sacrifice was just, which would require him to claim the dead consented, which [the Eclipse's own choreography makes impossible to assert](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_74_(Manga)). The silence is not mystery. It's a concession he cannot afford to make aloud.

## The Departure as Second Act

Rickert's defiance doesn't end with the slap. [Rakshas is immediately dispatched to assassinate him](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_338_(Manga)). Rickert negotiates with Silat and the exiled Bakiraka clan to escape Falconia with Erica. His departure reframes the scene: the slap was moral but the flight is strategic. He gets out, and in getting out he becomes [the one character operating outside Griffith's reach who has cultivated relationships Guts cannot](https://gamerant.com/berserk-rickert-vital-ally-guts/) — with Silat, with the Bakiraka, with communities that exist in the spaces Falconia doesn't govern.

![Rickert — Berserk](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/characters/11/285827.jpg)

This is what distinguishes Rickert's arc from pure moral gesture. He is the only surviving central character who can operate politically rather than physically. Guts can fight. Rickert can build coalitions, forge weapons, and move in spaces Griffith's image doesn't reach. [Game Rant's analysis of his future role](https://gamerant.com/berserk-rickert-vital-ally-guts/) identifies him as the character best positioned to approach Griffith's system from the outside — supplying alliances, intelligence, and weapons that Guts' direct-assault approach structurally cannot provide. The Bakiraka clan's antagonism toward Falconia gives Rickert an entry into organized resistance that [Guts' brand-driven isolation](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_339_(Manga)) makes impossible for him. The slap opened a door Rickert is now uniquely positioned to walk through.

## Why This Is the Scene

Berserk has more spectacular moments than chapter 337. [The Eclipse](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Eclipse), Guts losing his arm and eye, the [Berserker Armor's first activation](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Berserker_Armor), the fantasia merging physical and astral worlds — these are all larger in scale. But the slap is the moment the series has been building toward on a specific axis since the Eclipse: when does a human being act in a way that the God Hand cannot metabolize?

Guts' vengeance is metabolizable — it's the eternal struggle, the branded swordsman versus the angel who made him. It fits the cosmic frame. The [SkullKnight.net community has analyzed the causality system's logic extensively](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/some-thoughts-about-void-and-skull-knight-connection-and-some-other-things.14935/): the God Hand requires oppositions to sustain its narrative of inevitability. Guts as the branded avenger is an opposition the system can accommodate. But a slap from a blacksmith's apprentice who never got the brand, who mourned his friends by planting swords in a hill, who looked at a miraculous city and still said no — that doesn't fit. It's too small. It's too human. It's the kind of act that the grand narrative of fate and causality has no category for, because it's not heroic. It's just honest.

The God Hand can survive being hunted. It cannot survive being judged by someone it expected to forgive it.
