After the release of *Evangelion: 3.0 You Can (Not) Redo* in November 2012, Hideaki Anno said: *"I was broken. I fell into what's called a depressive state, the natural result of having spent six years grinding down my soul making Eva again."*

The film had just posted [the highest opening weekend box office in Japan that year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_3.0_You_Can_(Not)_Redo) — ¥1.13 billion across 224 screens in two days. Anno was incapacitated. He couldn't visit his own studio for most of 2013. The fourth film, which 3.0 ends on an explicit cliffhanger for, would take nine more years to arrive.

![Evangelion 3.0 theatrical poster](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/9e/Evangelion_3.0_poster.jpg)

This is the central paradox of *Evangelion 3.0*: it is the most commercially successful and most critically reviled entry in the Rebuild tetralogy. Yahoo! Movies Japan [gave it a 3.12 out of 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_3.0_You_Can_(Not)_Redo) — damning for a franchise event film with a built-in rabid audience. Rotten Tomatoes clocked it at 20%. The discourse that erupted in 2012 and has never quite died down frames 3.0 as a failure — a broken promise, a misstep, the film that almost killed the franchise. 

That reading is wrong. *Evangelion 3.0* is not a broken film. It is a film with a specific agenda, and Anno executed that agenda so successfully that it nearly killed him.

## The Plan, From the Beginning

Anno did not stumble into the choices that made *3.0* divisive. He designed for them. His stated plan for the Rebuild series was explicit: "The first [film] would be similar to the broadcast episodes. The second would gradually change, and the third and fourth to be totally different. From the outset, I didn't intend to do the same thing as the TV series."

*Evangelion: 1.0 You Are (Not) Alone* (2007) is essentially a high-budget remaster of the first six TV episodes — gorgeous, accessible, almost welcoming. *Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance* (2009) begins departing from canon, introduces new character Mari, and ends with Shinji triggering a Third Impact to save Rei. The ending of 2.0 is the most overtly hopeful moment in the history of the franchise: Shinji succeeds. He saves her. A god-like Yui descends. The film cuts to black.

This is the setup for 3.0's trap. Anno spent two films conditioning the audience — building goodwill, delivering emotional payoffs, giving Shinji actual wins. *3.0* opens fourteen years later, on a decimated Earth that Shinji apparently helped cause, surrounded by people who look at him with cold suspicion or outright contempt. Misato doesn't embrace him. She fits him with an explosive collar. NERV is gone. WILLE, the organization now fighting NERV, includes almost everyone Shinji trusted. He doesn't understand what happened. Neither does the audience.

That's not accidental storytelling sloppiness. That's the point.

## The Information Blackout as Experience

The criticism most often leveled at *3.0* is that it refuses to explain itself. Fourteen years of events are simply not shown. No flashbacks, no exposition, no catchup. Characters respond to Shinji's confusion with impatience or silence. The first thirty minutes feel, as multiple critics noted, like viewers have "skipped a movie."

But Anno originally planned something even more radical: [*Shinji wasn't supposed to appear in the film at all.*](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Evangelion_3.0:_You_Can_(Not)_Redo) The third installment was initially conceived as set entirely within the 14-year gap. What we got was a compromise version — and even that compromise was enough to fracture the fanbase.

The refusal to explain is Anno's way of synchronizing the audience with Shinji. The classic Evangelion technique — you experience what the character experiences — applied to narrative structure rather than psychology. Shinji wakes up fourteen years later into a world that has moved on without him, in which the relationships he understood no longer operate the way he remembers, in which his actions have had consequences he doesn't comprehend. The audience is placed in identical conditions. We don't know what WILLE is or how it formed. We don't understand what Rei Q is. We can't predict what anyone will do next because the rules have changed.

Most audiences experience this as the film's failure. It is actually its most sophisticated move.

## What the Film Actually Cares About

Strip away the worldbuilding confusion and *3.0* is about two things: the impossibility of return, and Kaworu Nagisa.

Kaworu gets more sustained screen time in this film than in the entirety of the original series. His relationship with Shinji — unhurried, tender, the only warmth Shinji receives from any direction for the film's entire runtime — is consistently singled out as the film's strongest element even by critics who dismissed everything else. Anno uses Kaworu to give Shinji (and the audience) a breathing space: these scenes function as the emotional center the film refuses to provide anywhere else.

The scene where Kaworu teaches Shinji to play piano in the ruins of a flooded city is the clearest example. There is no plot urgency in it. It's two people existing together. Shinji can't get the fingering right; Kaworu guides his hands. It is the only moment in the film where Shinji is not being interrogated, pitied, or treated as a threat. The intimacy feels stolen — because it is. Anno stages it in a world that has no room for it, which is precisely why its eventual obliteration registers as loss rather than plot mechanics.

That placement is deliberate. Kaworu is the one relationship that existed outside the timeline of collapse, the one figure who treats Shinji with uncomplicated care. And the film destroys it. Kaworu's death, when it comes, hits with a specific brutality because Anno has made it the only safe harbor in an otherwise alienating film.

This is the structural move of *3.0* in miniature: offer comfort, then take it. Give the audience something to hold, then demonstrate that holding on is the problem.

## The Cost

Nine years separated *3.0* from *3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time*. In the immediate aftermath of 3.0's release, Anno [could not enter his studio at all](https://soranews24.com/2015/04/02/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-opens-up-about-his-latest-bout-with-depression-movie-delays/) — not occasionally, but for the entirety of 2013. He made *Shin Godzilla* (2016) partly as a recovery project, something outside the Eva ecosystem that could remind him what making films felt like. He described *3.0*'s production as "six years grinding down my soul." His wife told him he remained in the world because of her and their friends.

What this reveals is that *3.0* was not the product of a filmmaker in control and executing a clean vision. It was the product of a filmmaker grinding himself to nothing on something he simultaneously needed to do and couldn't escape from. The brutality of the film — its refusal to console, its systematic dismantling of every support Shinji had built — reflects something of Anno's own psychological position during production. Not a confession, but a transfer.

## The Fourth Film's Shadow

*3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time* (2021) resolved the tetralogy in a way that largely redeemed the Rebuild series as a whole. Seen from the end, *3.0* functions as a necessary devastation — the nadir Shinji has to survive before the final film can be about something other than despair. [Anno, in a TIME interview](https://time.com/6090247/neon-genesis-evangelion-thrice-upon-a-time-hideaki-anno/), described Shinji's arc in the final film as learning to "save and care for those very close to him" — which only means anything because *3.0* first took everyone away from him.

But I don't think the correct way to appreciate *3.0* is retroactively, as setup. The film works — and fails — on its own terms, as a piece of deliberate audience confrontation. Anno knew what he was doing. He told you he was going to do it. The film's box office proved that audiences would show up regardless. And then it hurt them anyway.

That willingness to hurt — to follow an artistic logic at the cost of commercial goodwill, to prioritize an emotional argument over narrative satisfaction — is something close to integrity. Anno paid for it in depression and isolation and years of inability to work. The audience paid for it in confusion and betrayal and nine years of waiting.

Whether that trade was worth it probably depends on what you think art is for.
