In January 2001, three weeks after the original Tsukihime shipped at Comiket 59, [TYPE-MOON released Tsukihime PLUS-DISC](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihime_PLUS-DISC) at Sunshine Creation 11. It was a supplemental fan disc — two hours of side stories, bonus materials, and the first four chapters of what would become Kara no Kyoukai. Standard fan disc territory.

Except one of the short scenarios, [Geccha 2](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihime_PLUS-DISC), did something unusual. It placed a character named Satsuki Yumizuka in a meta-fictional space with a comedic cat-girl version of Arcueid — Neco-Arc — and had them discuss, directly and with evident sadness, the fact that Satsuki does not have a route in Tsukihime. Her route was planned. Her sprites exist. Her concept art is reproduced in the PLUS-DISC itself. It was cut. And now she is here, inside a bonus scenario in a fan disc, acknowledging that she exists in the margins of a story that left her behind.

This is extraordinary for a 2001 visual novel. It is the kind of meta-textual self-awareness that contemporary games reach for as a sophisticated narrative device. TYPE-MOON did it not because they were trying to be clever, but because Nasu apparently could not let the absence go without marking it somehow. And that impulse — to name the gap, to put a character in the gap and let her look at it — produced one of the most lasting creative decisions in TYPE-MOON's history.

## What PLUS-DISC Actually Is

Before getting to the ghost route, it helps to understand what PLUS-DISC contains. The main scenario, ["Alliance of Illusionary Eyes"](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Akira_Seo), introduces Akira Seo — a high school underclassman with precognitive abilities who witnesses a murder and becomes entangled with Ciel and the real Shiki tracking down a fake "Shiki Tohno." It is a self-contained story that places new characters in the established Tsukihime continuity, and Akira's future sight is the kind of perception mechanic that TYPE-MOON returns to repeatedly across their catalogue.

The Geccha scenarios are different in register: they are comedic fourth-wall breaks where the Tsukihime heroines sit around discussing the game's own internal inconsistencies. Geccha 1 has Arcueid, Ciel, and Akiha dissecting lore questions. [Geccha 2 is where Satsuki appears](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihime_PLUS-DISC), with Neco-Arc as her interlocutor — a chibi parody mascot who will go on to become, improbably, one of TYPE-MOON's most beloved characters.

The fan translation by [Mirror Moon, completed in 2008](http://mirrormoon.org/projects/complete/tsukihime/tsukihime-plusdisc/), brought this to English-speaking audiences and cemented the scenario's reputation among Western TYPE-MOON fans as PLUS-DISC's most important moment. Not because it is the longest or most dramatic — it is neither — but because it is unguarded.

## The Anatomy of a Ghost Route

Satsuki Yumizuka was designed as a heroine for Tsukihime. [Her route exists in documentation](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihime_PLUS-DISC): character design sheets, scenario sketches, a role in the original planned structure of the game. Nasu could not finish it in time for [Comiket 59](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsukihime) — a fact the community has documented extensively and that the PLUS-DISC itself implicitly confirms by publishing concept art for a route that does not exist. She was demoted to supporting character — she appears briefly in other routes, gets vampirized, appears to die, and is largely forgotten by the narrative that keeps her alive mainly as a background detail.

Geccha 2 gives her a space to acknowledge all of this. It is simultaneously a comic scenario and a genuine apology, mediated through the figure of Neco-Arc — who exists precisely because she is not Arcueid, who is a parody of the real heroine in the same way Satsuki is a parody of a real heroine's role. Two characters who are beside the point, discussing their own beside-the-pointness.

The concept art reproduced in PLUS-DISC is significant: TYPE-MOON is showing you what the route would have looked like. Not as a teaser for something coming, but as an honest acknowledgment of something that didn't. This is conceptually unusual. Most developers quietly bury cut content. TYPE-MOON put it in the supplemental disc and let Satsuki grieve it with you.

[The community at Beast's Lair documented this extensively](https://forums.nrvnqsr.com/showthread.php/4719-Timeline-of-Shingetsutan-Tsukihime-Kagetsu-Tohya-Melty-Blood-Series) — PLUS-DISC sits in a complex timeline relationship to the rest of the Tsukihime extended universe, and Satsuki's status as a ghost-heroine informs how subsequent works handle her. Melty Blood, set a year after Tsukihime's events, functions partly as a response to the missing route: Satsuki gets real combat presence and narrative weight in Melty Blood's story in ways the original game denied her. It is not the route that Geccha 2 mourned, but it is evidence that the creative wound did not close.

## How Absence Became a Mascot

The extraordinary thing about what PLUS-DISC did is where it led. Neco-Arc — born in Geccha 2 as a joke vehicle for Satsuki's mourning — became TYPE-MOON's unofficial mascot. She appears across [Melty Blood](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Melty_Blood) as a playable character, in [Carnival Phantasm](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Carnival_Phantasm) as the host of the Ahnenerbe café framing device — the catgirl MC of a [self-aware crossover OVA](https://www.cbr.com/fate-stay-night-crossover-anime-worth-watching/) that runs on TYPE-MOON's willingness to treat its own tragic cosmology as comedy material — in merchandise, in crossover content, in Grand Order event media. She is everywhere.

The irony: the character who exists to acknowledge TYPE-MOON's failure to finish Satsuki's route became the most reliably recurring symbol of TYPE-MOON's comedic brand identity. [Know Your Meme documents](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/neco-arc) how her popularity spiked further in 2021 when the Tsukihime remake announcement generated a wave of meme activity — a character born from an absence going viral in celebration of that absence finally ending. Every time Neco-Arc appears, she is a trace of the thing that wasn't finished. The mascot is built from the gap.

This is not an accident of development — it is the logical extension of TYPE-MOON's relationship to their own catalogue. Nasu has always been willing to hold his work at a certain critical distance, to treat his own fictional cosmology with affectionate irony, to acknowledge the mechanics and limitations of the VN form from inside the work itself. [The Wikipedia overview of Type-Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type-Moon) traces a creative history built on iterative refinement — Tsukihime begetting Kagetsu Tohya, Fate begetting hollow ataraxia, each supplemental work negotiating with the incompleteness of its predecessor. PLUS-DISC was the first and most explicit instance of this negotiation.

## Twenty-Three Years Later

In 2021, [TYPE-MOON released Tsukihime -A piece of blue glass moon-](https://typemoon.fandom.com/wiki/Tsukihime_-A_piece_of_blue_glass_moon-), the first part of a complete remake of the original game. It covers the Near Side routes — Arcueid and Ciel. Satsuki is not a heroine in this version either.

Then in 2022, TYPE-MOON announced [Tsukihime -The other side of red garden-](https://www.resetera.com/threads/the-next-tsukihime-remake-game-will-cover-the-far-side-routes-and-is-titled-tsukihime-the-other-side-of-red-garden-will-include-the-satsuki-route.477609/) — the Far Side routes remake, which will include a Satsuki route. [Noisy Pixel confirmed](https://noisypixel.net/tsukihime-the-other-side-of-red-garden-english-logo/) that an English logo appeared in the teaser trailer unlocked by completing the first remake, signaling international release plans alongside the Japanese announcement. The route that Geccha 2 mourned in 2001 is finally being written. Twenty-three years of Neco-Arc appearances, of Melty Blood cameos, of ghost-heroine status, ends with Satsuki getting the story she was always owed.

This arc is one of the strangest in visual novel history. A route was cut. A fan disc acknowledged the cut in an act of transparent self-awareness. A mascot was born from that acknowledgment and became more prominent than many actual heroines. And eventually the cut was healed, though it took two decades. The PLUS-DISC did not cause any of this — but it documented the wound at the moment it was fresh, and that documentation became canon, which is how the wound became something that demanded closing.

## What the Fan Disc Did That the Main Game Couldn't

[Reading order recommendations for the Tsukihime series](https://www.readtsukihi.me/) consistently place PLUS-DISC between the main game and Kagetsu Tohya — a deliberate position as a bridge text, two hours that decompress after the intensity of the main routes and prepare you for the longer supplemental game. In this function it is unremarkable: a fan disc doing what fan discs do.

What PLUS-DISC did that the main game couldn't — structurally, format-wise — is address the limits of the main game directly. Tsukihime proper cannot acknowledge its cut heroines; doing so would undermine the world's coherence. The fan disc is not the world; it is a space adjacent to the world where characters can be self-aware about their own fictional status. Geccha 2 only works because both participants know they are in a bonus scenario, not in the real story.

This is the formal contribution of PLUS-DISC that matters most: it established that the space beside the story is where TYPE-MOON does its most honest work. [Kagetsu Tohya](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Software:Kagetsu_Tohya) deepens this — its "Ten Nights of Dream" side stories use the same fourth-wall register as Geccha, letting characters address lore gaps from the main game with explicit awareness that they are in supplemental space. Carnival Phantasm perfects it. The entire tradition of TYPE-MOON's comedic meta-content — the chibi animations, the self-aware crossovers, the running jokes about character popularity and route selection — traces back to a two-hour fan disc released in January 2001 that decided the most interesting thing to do with Satsuki Yumizuka was to let her mourn herself.
