The X marks in [Koe no Katachi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(manga)) are usually described as a visualization of Shoya Ishida's social anxiety. [Large blue Xs](https://theoddapple.com/a-silent-voice/) appear over the faces of everyone around him; he can't look people in the eye; when he finally begins to heal and connect, the Xs peel away. It's a clean metaphor, and it's not wrong, exactly. But it's less precise than the manga is. The Xs aren't about social anxiety in the general sense — they're about specific people with specific moral claims on Shoya's attention. And the difference matters for what the manga is actually arguing about guilt and redemption.

Read carefully, the [X marks are an inventory](https://flawfinder.wordpress.com/2017/05/20/koe-no-katachi-a-silent-voice-review-the-road-to-redemption-is-long/). Not of Shoya's fear of the unknown, but of his known debts. The people whose faces are crossed out are not strangers. They're former classmates who participated in or witnessed the bullying of Shouko Nishimiya. They're people who later made Shoya the scapegoat for that bullying, who turned on him the same way he'd turned on her. They're people who know who he was and what he did. The X appears specifically on faces that have moral content for Shoya — people he has wronged, people who have wronged him, people who could name what he is. That's not generalized social anxiety. That's the specific face of guilt.

## Why the Distinction Matters

If the Xs were simply social anxiety markers, their removal would trace an emotional arc: Shoya becomes more comfortable, more open, more healed, and the Xs fall away as a byproduct of that wellness. That reading makes redemption look like therapy — an interior process that then manifests in relationships.

But that's not what [Oima Yoshitoki designed](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Long_Interview_with_Yoshitoki_Oima). Her stated intent for the manga was to portray how difficult it is for people to communicate their emotions — not bullying per se, not deafness per se, but the fundamental difficulty of genuine communication between people who have reasons to fear each other. The bullying and hearing impairment were vehicles for that theme, not the theme itself. What makes communication fail, in Oima's framing, is not just awkwardness or shyness but the specific accumulation of things we've done and had done to us that make certain conversations impossible.

The Xs are that accumulation made visible. They mark the people with whom Shoya has outstanding relational debts — unaddressed wrongs that sit between them and genuine communication. Their removal isn't emotional recovery; it's the discharge of specific obligations. And this means the order of their disappearance maps not Shoya's general healing but his specific accountability work.

## The Case of Ueno's X

[Naoka Ueno](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Naoka_Ueno) is the most revealing case in the system. In the [manga](https://myanimelist.net/manga/44347/Koe_no_Katachi), her X persists across most of the story — and when it finally comes off, the moment is ambiguous, linked not to a resolution of their conflict but to Shoya's general decision to try to accept people. In the [film adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Silent_Voice_(film)), her X disappears during a brief positive interaction, then comes back during their next serious confrontation.

The reappearance in the film is a more honest visual statement. Ueno is the character in the manga whose arc [deliberately doesn't resolve](https://diaryofcas.wordpress.com/2018/05/04/naoki-uenos-unrequited-love-in-koe-no-katachi/). She doesn't achieve the enlightenment or transformation that Shoya's arc is structured around. She remains invested in Shoya in ways that are complicated by her complicity in Shouko's bullying, her jealousy, and her refusal to acknowledge what she did. The X mark on her face is not fear — it's the register of an outstanding debt that neither party has properly acknowledged.

When the film shows her X flicker back during conflict, it's the visual system doing its most important work: demonstrating that reconciliation and accountability are not the same thing. Shoya can want to be friends with Ueno; he can feel warmth toward her. But her X can't stay off while the underlying debt remains unaddressed. The mark isn't about how Shoya feels. It's about what remains to be done.

## What Nagatsuka's Absence of an X Reveals

[Tomohiro Nagatsuka](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Tomohiro_Nagatsuka) is introduced in high school, when Shoya is at his lowest point — socially isolated, planning his death, going through the motions of a life he's decided to vacate. Nagatsuka sees Shoya perform a single act of decency (offering his own bike in place of Nagatsuka's when someone tries to steal it) and immediately, unilaterally declares Shoya his best friend. He extends unconditional acceptance based entirely on present evidence.

And Nagatsuka's face carries [no X](https://koenokatachi.fandom.com/wiki/Koe_no_Katachi_(Manga)).

This is the clearest sign that the marks are not social anxiety. Nagatsuka is a stranger in a context where Shoya struggles with everyone. If the Xs were generalized, Nagatsuka would be marked too. But he isn't, because he has no prior relationship with Shoya — no knowledge of what Shoya did in elementary school, no moral claim from the past, nothing to look past or forgive. Their friendship is constructed entirely in the present, without the prior moral claims — shared history, witnessed wrongs, the specific knowledge of who Shoya used to be — that make the other faces require something.

Nagatsuka represents what new connection looks like when it's not accountability work: you choose someone based on who they're being right now, and you build from there. His friendship is not less genuine for being predicated on ignorance of the past. But it also doesn't do the same work that reconnecting with Shouko, or confronting Ueno, or facing his former classmates does. [Nagatsuka is the proof that new bonds are possible](https://www.liveanime.org/post/silent-voice-manga-characters); he's not the proof that the old ones can be repaired.

## The Manga's Argument About Redemption

Most bullying narratives in manga and anime treat redemption as a function of the protagonist's interior state — if he's genuinely sorry, if he works hard enough, if he proves himself, the relationship heals. Think of [*My Hero Academia*](https://myanimelist.net/manga/75989/Boku_no_Hero_Academia), where Midoriya's unshakeable sincerity is itself the argument for why the world should open to him, or any number of shonen redemption arcs where heartfelt apology + demonstrated effort = restored relationship. The healing is presented as a natural consequence of the correct emotional state. Get the interior right and the exterior follows.

[Koe no Katachi is more specific than this](https://flawfinder.wordpress.com/2017/06/15/koe-no-katachi-and-the-difficulties-of-compromise-in-communication/). The X system reveals that redemption requires not general emotional recovery but specific relational work with specific people. You can't discharge a debt to Shouko by becoming a better person in general — you have to actually show up for Shouko, specifically, with the particular history you share. You can't clear your ledger with your former classmates by being nice to different people — you have to face each one in the specific context of what happened between you.

And some of that specific work doesn't complete in the manga's timeline. Ueno's arc stays unresolved. This is often discussed as a weakness — a subplot the manga doesn't close — but the X system suggests it's structural. Ueno's X stays because the debt between them stays. The manga is being precise: reconciliation is not guaranteed by desire. It requires what you did and what the other person is willing to do with what you did, and those two things don't always come together.

## What This System Says That Dialogue Can't

The X marks are [often cited as one of Koe no Katachi's most effective formal choices](https://cartoonvibe.com/a-silent-voice-movie/) — a way of putting the reader inside Shoya's perception without requiring dialogue to explain his emotional state. The film adaptation adopted them directly, which is unusual: Yamada Naoko's films typically translate the manga's visual grammar into motion rather than preserving still-panel devices. That she kept the Xs is a signal of how central they are to the story's legibility.

But the formal choice is more than expressive shorthand. Oima could have designed a system where Shoya sees Xs everywhere and they simply fade as he heals. That system would visualize depression or social anxiety cleanly — it would show a person getting better. What she designed instead is a system that tracks the specific architecture of guilt. The Xs don't appear on everyone; they appear on people with moral claims. They don't fade uniformly; they clear as each specific claim is addressed. And in Ueno's case, they don't clear at all — not because Shoya hasn't healed enough, but because the debt between them is genuinely unresolved.

The [manga was initially rejected for serialization](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2014-08-04/a-silent-voice-koe-no-katachi-manga-to-end-on-november-12/.77278) by multiple publishers, including Kodansha, before the one-shot found its audience online and the resulting interest forced a reconsideration. Part of what made publishers nervous was the content's honesty — an elementary school bully as protagonist, suicide ideation, a deaf character who is not simply a symbol of purity. The X system is that same honesty made formal: it refuses to let Shoya's emotional recovery stand in for the specific relational work his past requires. The distinction between "I feel better about people in general" and "I have addressed what I owe to this particular person" is uncomfortable to insist on. The Xs insist anyway.
