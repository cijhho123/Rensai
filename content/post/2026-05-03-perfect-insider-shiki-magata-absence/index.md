The murder mystery of *The Perfect Insider* hinges on a locked room. [Shiki Magata](https://myanimelist.net/character/87428/Shiki_Magata), programming prodigy, child murderer, theoretical genius, has been sealed inside a research facility for fifteen years. When her corpse emerges from that room — the arms and legs severed, dressed in a wedding gown — protagonist Sōhei Saikawa barely reacts with grief. He reacts with something more like awe.

That tells you everything you need to know about what *The Perfect Insider* is actually doing.

![Shiki Magata](https://myanimelist.net/images/characters/7/297578.jpg)

The show aired in fall 2015 on Fuji TV's [Noitamina block](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2015-10-08/sentai-filmworks-licenses-the-perfect-insider-anime/.93945), directed by Mamoru Kanbe with character designs by Inio Asano — yes, the Inio Asano who drew *Oyasumi Punpun* — and scored by Kenji Kawai. It's based on [Hiroshi Mori's 1996 debut novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subete_ga_F_ni_Naru), the first winner of the Mephisto Prize and the founding text of his "S&M series" (Saikawa and Moe, not what you're thinking). In mystery circles, the novel is credited as a [watershed work in the shin honkaku school](https://jmystery.fandom.com/wiki/The_Perfect_Insider), a movement that couched rigorous locked-room puzzles inside densely specialized, often scientific subject matter — Mori's engineering background writing itself into the fiction. The tradition proved influential: the novel's particular fusion of hard logic and psychological isolation shaped [NisiOisiN's *Zaregoto* series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaregoto_Series) and is visible in the DNA of the entire "mystery as intellectual character study" subgenre that followed.

None of that context explains why the show made so many viewers uncomfortable. [The IMDB score sits at 6.7](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt5247480/) and the persistent "pseudo-philosophical" complaints suggest something went sideways between premise and execution. But the critics who call the philosophical dialogue hollow aren't exactly wrong — they're just missing the point that it's *meant* to be hollow in exactly the way they mean.

## The Character Who Isn't There

Shiki Magata is the gravitational center of this show. Every scene orbits her. Every character has a relationship with her, a theory about her, a memory distorted by proximity to her brilliance. And she barely appears.

The [atelier emily essay on the show](https://formeinfullbloom.wordpress.com/2015/11/16/knowing-shiki-magata-four-perspectives/) identified this precisely: we encounter Shiki through four pairs of eyes, each one a funhouse mirror. Director Seiji Shindou — her uncle, her lover — presents her as burdened by existence, as someone who thought continuously about mortality. His niece/paramour becomes, in his memory, a kind of tragic philosopher. Saikawa, the show's protagonist, idolizes her as something post-human, repeating her philosophical aphorisms like scripture. Her aunt Yumiko recalls a "cute niece," which is almost certainly a defense mechanism over something she cannot bring herself to acknowledge. And Moe Nishinosono — the closest thing the show has to a peer for Shiki — processes her through rivalry and comparison, trying to locate herself in relation to this legend.

What's absent from this list is Shiki's own perspective. Her actual voice. The show gives us fragments — the famous video interviews where she delivers lines like "Every human starts as a genius. As they age, they become average. It's because they get stupid that they can work" — but these are performances. They are what Shiki constructed for her audience. The real Shiki, if she ever existed as something distinct from her observers' projections, never appears in the text. She's the locked room's most fundamental secret, and even the solution doesn't unlock it.

## What "Everything Becomes F" Actually Means

The title deserves more than the hand-wave it usually gets. In hexadecimal, the number fifteen is represented as F. [Shiki was sentenced to fifteen years of confinement](https://stemfromashes.blogspot.com/2018/06/subete-ga-f-ni-naru-perfect-insider.html) following the murder of her parents at age fourteen. "Everything becomes F" is, on one level, Shiki's own joke: her whole life has become that number, that single designation.

But the reduction has a deeper logic. Hexadecimal is a stripping operation — it converts complex, multi-digit decimal numbers into something more fundamental, more efficient, more machine-readable. "Everything becomes F" means: everything reduced to its base function. Surface stripped away. Performance dissolved. What's left is pure computation.

This is the ideal Saikawa holds Shiki to. She has achieved what he can only theorize: a life stripped of social performance, of human obligation, of the noise that prevents clear thought. That she accomplished this through murder and fifteen years of imprisonment is, to him, almost incidental. The content of her isolation matters less than its purity.

![The Perfect Insider key visual](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/9/76071l.jpg)

The visual direction reinforces this reading consistently. The [show's palette is famously desaturated](https://theglorioblog.com/2015/10/10/first-look-the-perfect-insider/), everything filtered through grey, the research island's sterility amplified into something approaching sensory deprivation. Inio Asano's character designs, [the first time his artwork was animated](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2015-05-14/subete-ga-f-ni-naru-anime-1st-promo-unveils-inio-asano-character-designs/.88158), lose some of his trademark weight in translation — reviewers noted the designs' "charisma doesn't fully survive animation" — but the peculiarity of his visual language still carries. These are people who look slightly wrong, slightly too composed, as though something human has been quietly subtracted from them.

## Saikawa's Worship as the Real Problem

The show is often accused of staging "pseudo-philosophical" dialogue that doesn't go anywhere. I'd argue this is the most precisely executed thing in it.

Saikawa's conversations — with Moe, with himself, with the ghost of Shiki — are philosophy as ritual. He doesn't use Shiki's ideas to understand her. He uses them to construct her. Every time he repeats one of her aphorisms, he's not engaging with a thinker; he's maintaining a shrine. The [Anime UK News review](https://animeuknews.net/2016/11/the-perfect-insider-review/) notes that Saikawa and Moe read as "very forgettable characters" against the magnetism of Shiki's backstory — but I think this is intentional design, not accident. Saikawa being fundamentally hollow is the point. His entire personality is an outgrowth of his relationship to Shiki-as-concept. Strip away the idol and there's not much left.

The show's uncomfortable age dynamic — Saikawa watched Moe grow up, she's transparently in love with him, he repeatedly deflects — makes more sense when you realize that Moe herself is, in some sense, a Shiki-substitute for Saikawa. Both are brilliant young women who lost their parents under traumatic circumstances. His resistance to Moe is the resistance of a man whose emotional life is organized around an absence. The real object of his desire is the Shiki he invented, not any actual human being.

This is what the [fan discourse most consistently misses](https://harperanimereviews.com/everything-becomes-locked-room-murders-the-perfect-insider-spoiler-review/): criticizing Saikawa's philosophizing for being pretentious is like criticizing a character's alcoholism for being unhealthy. The text knows it's unhealthy. That's the diagnosis.

## The Twist as Moral Verdict

The resolution is where the show makes its argument explicit, though in typically oblique fashion.

Shiki was not the corpse. Shiki raised a daughter inside the locked room — conceived before her imprisonment, born in captivity, raised in total isolation — and programmed that daughter to take her place at the research institute while she escaped. The corpse dressed in the wedding gown was that daughter, who Shiki presumably killed as part of her calculated exit. The woman who killed her parents at fourteen, imprisoned for fifteen years, had spent that imprisonment [manufacturing a replacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subete_ga_F_ni_Naru) rather than experiencing any of the transcendence her observers imagined.

This is the show's cruelest joke. Saikawa spent the entire series treating Shiki's isolation as philosophical purification. Shiki spent it raising a child in secret, manipulating her environment, working toward escape with the same cold-eyed pragmatism she's always displayed. She was never what he thought she was. She was never transcendent. She was just someone who, when given fifteen years alone, used them to survive.

The [moonlight detective review of the novel](http://moonlight-detective.blogspot.com/2023/09/the-perfect-insider-1996-by-mori-hiroshi.html) notes that what's most compelling about the story isn't the locked-room mechanics but "what really happened in that room all those lonely years" — and the answer is not enlightenment. The answer is a woman doing what she had to do to get out. Everything his idealization projected onto her was a fiction he needed, not a truth she embodied.

## The Asano Connection

It would be a mistake to treat Inio Asano's involvement as mere hiring of a prestigious name. [Asano's entire artistic project](https://animecollective.com/inio-asano-manga-guide/) — from *Solanin* through *Punpun* through *Dead Dead Demon's Dededede Destruction* — is about the catastrophic gap between how people are perceived and what they actually contain. In *Punpun*, the protagonist's father is idolized even while physically absent and emotionally destructive; when Punpun finally encounters him as an adult, what he finds is a small, pathetic man — nothing like the towering figure his memory had constructed. The act of idealization, Asano argues throughout his work, doesn't just misrepresent the subject; it destroys the viewer's capacity to see. Hiring Asano to design the characters for a show about exactly this mechanism isn't coincidence. It's editorial direction.

The [atelier emily analysis](https://formeinfullbloom.wordpress.com/2015/11/16/knowing-shiki-magata-four-perspectives/) closes with the observation that the mystery's resolution hinges on answering Shiki's own foundational question: "Who are you?" Not who killed her — who is she? The answer the show gives is: nobody knew. Everyone who thought they did was answering a question about themselves.

That's not pseudo-philosophy. That's the sharpest thing the show says, and it says it by hiding it inside eleven episodes of people talking around it.

## Why the Show Still Divides

The *Perfect Insider*'s reputation as a slow, philosophically murky mystery is partly deserved — [the pacing struggles are real](https://doubtandreason.wordpress.com/2017/03/31/review-and-analysis-subete-ga-f-ni-naru-the-perfect-insider/), and Mamoru Kanbe's direction sometimes fails to make the dialogue's intellectual play feel visually alive. A show that's fundamentally about people talking around an absence needs to find ways to make that absence feel present, and the anime doesn't always manage it. The desaturated palette is effective; the static shot composition sometimes just sits there.

But the underlying argument — that genius worshipped from a distance is a projection mechanism, and that what gets hidden inside the locked room of idealization is often something darker and more human than the worshipper can handle — is sharper than the discourse around the show acknowledges. The question *Subete ga F ni Naru* is asking isn't whodunnit. It's asking: what does it cost to treat a person as an idea?

The answer turns out to be: everything.
