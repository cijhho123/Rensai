Here is the central political fact of *Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood*: Riza Hawkeye, the most loyal and morally serious character in the series, tells Edward Elric in plain terms that she and Roy Mustang participated in the genocide of the Ishvalan people, that they did so following orders, and that this is no defense. She says that in a properly functioning country, she and Mustang would be tried as war criminals. She means it. Mustang means it too.

The show does not clean this up. The ending gives Roy a path toward political power and reform, but the Ishvalan people are not restored. The dead are not resurrected. The structural conditions that manufactured Mustang and Hawkeye's complicity — the military state, the "dogs of the military" system, the Amestrian empire's machinery of resource extraction through controlled violence — are not gone. They have been disrupted by the defeat of Father and Mustang's new position within the government. But disruption is not abolition.

This is what *FMA Brotherhood* is actually saying, and it is more politically serious than the show usually gets credit for.

## The "Equivalent Exchange" Misdirection

*Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood* is typically discussed through the lens of its central philosophical principle: the Law of Equivalent Exchange, which holds that [nothing can be gained without giving something of equal value](https://www.inverse.com/article/17504-fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood-anime-philosophy). This framing is encouraged by the show's structure, which uses equivalent exchange as the gravitational center of its world-building and the psychological key to the Elric brothers' tragedy: they tried to resurrect their mother, lost arm and body respectively as the "equivalent" cost, and have spent the series trying to reckon with what you owe and what can never be paid back.

This reading is not wrong. But it is incomplete. The show's actual thesis, demonstrated through everything the plot does rather than what the characters say, is that equivalent exchange is a lie — specifically, that powerful systems use the language of fair exchange to obscure the fact that they are extracting without compensation, sacrificing the many for the benefit of the few. The philosopher's stone, the show's MacGuffin, [is literally made of human lives](https://kotaku.com/fullmetal-alchemist-anime-manga-capitalism-alchemy-1850730164): an alchemical substrate produced by concentrating the souls of the murdered into a single object. The stone does not circumvent the Law of Equivalent Exchange; it outsources the cost to people who did not consent to pay it.

Alchemy in Amestris is [explicitly "labor under state power"](https://kotaku.com/fullmetal-alchemist-anime-manga-capitalism-alchemy-1850730164): State Alchemists must register with the military, receive government funding, and answer the call to arms when required. To use alchemy meaningfully within this society — to have access to resources, to advance in any career — is to be a tool of the state. Ed and Al call themselves "dogs of the military" because that is the formal designation of their position, and the show treats this designation with exactly the ironic weight it deserves. Their freedom to pursue their private goal (restoring their bodies) depends on their availability for use in state violence.

This is not philosophy-by-numbers. It is a specific, structural argument about how states manufacture complicity.

## The Ainu Connection

*Fullmetal Alchemist*'s political imagery does not come from abstraction. [Hiromu Arakawa has stated in interviews](https://gamerant.com/fullmetal-alchemist-imperialism-racism/) that the Ishvalan Civil War was directly inspired by the Japanese state's treatment of the Ainu — the Indigenous people of Hokkaido, who were systematically displaced by Japanese settler colonialism across the Meiji and post-Meiji periods. Arakawa grew up on a dairy farm in Hokkaido; [her own family were descendants of the settlers](https://fandomwire.com/hiromu-arakawas-farm-life-influenced-fullmetal-alchemists-most-important-element/) whose arrival was predicated on the Ainu's dispossession.

This biographical detail matters enormously. Arakawa is not writing about colonialism from a position of sympathetic distance — she is writing about a structure that her own family participated in, on the side of the colonizers. The Ainu, Hokkaido's indigenous people, were systematically dispossessed through the Meiji-era colonization of the island that Arakawa's family arrived as part of. She grew up on land that had a prior people. Writing the Ishvalan genocide is, in some sense, an act of self-examination: imagining what the descendants of the people who burned and displaced a culture might owe, and what accountability that requires.

The Ishvalans are a deliberately specific fictional people: desert-dwelling, monotheistic, brown-skinned with distinctive red eyes, followers of a faith that [prohibits alchemy as a usurpation of God's creative power](https://www.cbr.com/fullmetal-alchemist-the-ishval-civil-war-explained/). They are not generic victims. They are a culture with specific religious and aesthetic commitments that Amestris found threatening precisely because those commitments rejected the ideological foundation — alchemy as technology, the state as legitimate arbiter of life and death — on which Amestrian power rested.

The Civil War began, in Brotherhood's account, when Envy (disguised as an Amestrian soldier) shot and killed an Ishvalan child. The war that followed was not a response to an actual threat; it was manufactured. The genocide that concluded it — [Order #3066, signed by King Bradley, ordering the extermination of the Ishvalan population](https://fma.fandom.com/wiki/Ishval_Civil_War) — was not an emergency measure but a policy decision. State Alchemists were deployed as human weapons. Roy Mustang, who would later become the series' most explicitly redemption-seeking figure, burned Ishvalans alive.

The show insists on this history. [Episode 30's flashback to the Ishvalan War](https://www.toplevelcanon.com/reviews/fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood-s2e4-the-ishvalan-war-of-extermination) is one of the most sustained and uncomfortable sequences in a show not primarily designed to be uncomfortable. It does not allow Mustang or Hawkeye the dignity of ignorance or reluctance. They did this. They knew what they were doing. They did it anyway, because the machinery they were embedded in made refusal structurally costly and compliance structurally easy.

## Scar's Irresolvable Justice

The character who makes the show's political argument most precisely is Scar. A survivor of the Ishvalan genocide, Scar has spent the years since killing State Alchemists — targeting specifically the people who carried out the massacre. He is presented initially as an antagonist, then as a complex figure, then as a provisional ally whose motivations are never sanitized into anything comfortable.

[The Fandomentals analysis](https://www.thefandomentals.com/fma-revenge-forgiveness/) of Scar's arc identifies the key structural moment: when a character tells Scar that his killings were "senseless murder," he recognizes in those words the same language used to describe the Amestrian genocide of his people. He has become, in form if not in purpose, a mirror of what was done to Ishval. This recognition does not make him peaceful; it changes his tactics. He reorients from killing alchemists to working for accountability — demanding that the country that murdered his people be rebuilt in a way that cannot do it again.

[The show ends with Scar working alongside the new Amestrian government](https://www.thefandomentals.com/fma-revenge-forgiveness/) on the rebuilding of Ishval. This is not forgiveness. It is a transaction: what can he extract from this political opening that might protect what remains of his people? The Ishvalan people are not healed. Their homeland was destroyed. Their culture was nearly exterminated. The rebuilding project is necessary but not equivalent. Scar knows this. The show does not pretend otherwise.

This is the show's most morally honest position, and it is sustained against the narrative pressure to let good characters have good endings.

## What the Ending Refuses to Do

[In episode 30, Riza Hawkeye tells Edward Elric](https://www.thefandomentals.com/fma-revenge-forgiveness/) that following orders is not a moral defense. This is not a throwaway line. It is spoken by the character whose entire arc has been about the cost of putting her skill in service of someone else's vision — she tattoos the secrets of flame alchemy on her back and offers Mustang the right to burn it off her if he goes wrong. Her moral framework is built around the recognition that skills can be instrumentalized for evil and that the person holding the skill bears responsibility for who they allowed to use it.

Roy Mustang's ambition — to become Führer and reform Amestris from within, institute democracy, [make reparations for the Ishvalan genocide](https://www.thefandomentals.com/fma-revenge-forgiveness/) — is the show's most explicitly political project. It is also incomplete at the end of the series. He has achieved political position. He has not yet delivered accountability. The war criminals have not been tried. Riza's acknowledgment that they should be tried remains an unmet standard, hanging over the ending.

This is a deliberate choice by Arakawa. Conventional anime storytelling would provide Mustang with a clean redemption: atone, sacrifice, be forgiven, be elevated. *Brotherhood* gives him a beginning of a political project and a recognition that the project will be harder and longer than any individual moral renovation. The dead Ishvalans do not come back. [Scar does not forgive Mustang for burning his people](https://butwhytho.net/2022/08/fullmetal-alchemist-the-revenge-of-scar-live-action-review/); what they develop is something more like a cautious political alignment, which is less satisfying and more true.

## The Limitations Are Real

The political ambition of *FMA Brotherhood* is genuine and should be taken seriously. So should its limitations.

[CBR has argued](https://www.cbr.com/fullmetal-alchemist-fails-political-themes/) that the Ishvalans function more as a backdrop for the Amestrian characters' moral arcs than as autonomous people with their own interiority. Scar is the only named Ishvalan character who receives substantial development, and his arc is structured around his relationship to Amestrian alchemists rather than around Ishvalan culture or community. The genocide is depicted primarily through its perpetrators' guilt, not through the experience of survival.

The [ResetEra thread on whitewashing in Brotherhood](https://www.resetera.com/threads/whitewashing-in-fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood.354671/) notes that the anime's character designs, compared to the manga, sometimes soften the visual distinctiveness of non-Amestrian peoples. This is not nothing; representation in character design is how a visual medium signals which bodies are the norm and which are marked as other.

These are genuine criticisms. What they critique is not the presence of political content but the gap between the show's ambitions and its execution — the way that a story consciously engaging with genocide and colonialism can still center the emotional lives of the colonizers more than the colonized. *FMA Brotherhood* is not exempt from this critique simply because it tries harder than most shonen anime.

## What It Does Better Than Almost Anything

What separates *Brotherhood* from the standard of shonen storytelling on these themes is that it [does not let the political stakes evaporate at the end](https://ghostlightning.wordpress.com/2010/08/27/warfare-and-genocide-in-fullmetal-alchemist-brotherhood/). The Elric brothers' personal quest resolves: Ed sacrifices the Gate of Truth to restore Al's body, gives up alchemy permanently, and gets his brother back. This is emotionally satisfying in the way it should be.

But the country is still Amestris. [The MAL score of 9.09](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2024-03-20/frieren-knocks-full-metal-alchemist-brotherhood-out-of-the-top-spot-on-both-mal-and-ann/.208944) — which held the #1 spot for over a decade until *Frieren* displaced it in early 2024 — reflects the show's broad competence: its complete storytelling, its ensemble depth, its refusal to waste episodes. All of that is real.

What is also real is that the show's highest aspirations are in the political material: in Riza's acknowledgment that she is a war criminal, in Scar's refusal to accept that winning the war against Father constitutes justice for Ishval, in Mustang's unfinished project. These are the moments where *Brotherhood* stops being the greatest adventure anime ever made and starts being something rarer: an adventure anime that knows the adventure alone is not enough.

The Ishvalan dead are still dead. Equivalent exchange cannot restore them. That is what the show has been telling you the whole time.
