[Devilman Crybaby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby) premiered on Netflix on January 5, 2018, and the nihilism debate started almost immediately. The series ends with humanity destroyed, Akira Fudo dead and dismembered, and the world reset by a God who operates without apparent concern for what's been lost. "Nihilistic" attached itself to the series like a diagnostic label — a word that made the discomfort legible, that explained why the ending hurt in a way that felt larger than craft.

It was always the wrong label. What the series is doing with its ending is more precise than nihilism, and the mechanism for understanding it was embedded in the title for all ten episodes.

![Devilman Crybaby Netflix promotional artwork](https://static0.gamerantimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/devilman-crybaby-netflix.jpg)

## The Label That Calcified

[Critics split immediately on the ending](https://coherentcats.com/2018/12/17/12-days-of-anime-devilman-analysis-recommendations/). Daryl Surat of Otaku USA read the series as nihilistic. Kotaku's Chris Person rejected the label explicitly, arguing the series "has an emotional core and a thesis that it strongly believes in." [The Looper piece on the ending](https://www.looper.com/406546/the-ending-of-devilman-crybaby-explained/) focused on plot mechanics without engaging with the philosophical argument. [The Game Rant analysis](https://gamerant.com/devilman-crybaby-ending-explained/) described the finale's events without asking what they meant.

The nihilism reading is understandable as a reaction to the content. Humanity becomes a mob. [Social media broadcasts fear that tips ordinary people into mob violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby). Miki Makimura — the character who most clearly represents decency without supernatural enhancement — is [killed and dismembered by a human crowd, not by demons](https://globalanimenews.com/posts/devilman-crybaby-ending-destroys-every-bit-of-hope-you-had/). Akira fights Satan/Ryo across a ruined Earth, loses, and dies. [God sends angels to scour the planet and reset it](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Recap/DevilmanCrybabyEpisode10Crybaby). The arithmetic of the ending adds up to: everyone died, good didn't prevail, the universe moved on.

But the nihilism label describes the events, not the argument. The series is not saying loss is meaningless. It is saying loss is catastrophic — which is only true if the thing lost was worth having. These are opposite statements dressed in similar clothes.

## What the Title Does

The easier reading of Crybaby — that it is structurally Ryo/Satan's story, every violent scene building toward the moment he learns what love is too late — is correct as far as it goes. But it describes the story's shape rather than the formal argument the title is making. The distinction matters because the title is not an ironic twist waiting at the end. It is a structural promise that operates across all ten episodes.

[The title "Devilman Crybaby" appears to point at Akira](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=19982). He is introduced as the emotional one: he would cry on anyone's behalf, including strangers, including people who had never cried for themselves. Every time Akira appears in public — at the track, at school, around the Makimura family — his readiness to weep is presented as character definition. Ryo, by contrast, is cold and analytical, operating from a framework that treats the world as information. Where Akira weeps at the funerals of people he barely knew, Ryo watches without expression. The title's apparent subject is the protagonist who cannot stop crying. Ryo is the implicit contrast: the one for whom crying is impossible.

[Director Masaaki Yuasa confirmed that the title has a second meaning: "Akira/Devilman is a crybaby, and at the end he is able to make Satan cry."](https://yattatachi.com/exploration-ryo-devilman-crybaby) The crybaby the title was waiting for is not Akira. It is the being who denied love existed for ten episodes, weeping for the first time over the pieces of the only person he ever cared about.

This is the formal argument the title makes: every episode in which Akira is established as the crybaby is also an episode that is building Ryo's contrast — his inability to cry, his rejection of the emotional framework that crying requires. Ten episodes of Akira weeping is ten episodes of setting up the impossibility of what happens at the end: Ryo, who cannot cry, who has no framework for grief because he has no framework for love, weeping over a dead Earth. The title is a structural promise. The revelation that Ryo is its real subject is also the revelation of the series' thesis. What looked like it pointed one direction was always pointing somewhere else.

## What It Means That Ryo Cried

Ryo's denial of love is not background characterization — it is stated directly and structurally. [Ryo, along with Sirene and Kaim, explicitly asserts that love doesn't exist.](https://vampireseal.wordpress.com/2018/02/04/devilman-crybaby-episode-09-and-10-recap-and-review-learn-to-love-before-its-too-late/) His relationship with Akira, as he has processed it through ten episodes, is a form of possession — Akira is the one thing Ryo has determined is worth keeping. But he does not call this love. He has no framework for love because he has decided it isn't real.

[On the ruined beach, after Ryo has bisected Akira and the Earth is dying, he cries for the first time.](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Recap/DevilmanCrybabyEpisode10Crybaby) What the series is encoding in that moment is not a character's breakdown. It is a discovery. Ryo weeps because he has learned, through irreversible loss, that the thing he spent the series denying is real. His tears are his evidence. They are also the series' evidence.

In a genuinely nihilistic universe, tears over a loss would be biological — the nervous system's response to stimuli, no more meaningful than any other physiological event. But Devilman Crybaby treats Ryo's tears as a moral event. They are presented as the culmination of the series' argument rather than an incidental response. [Yuasa was direct about this intent: "I set out to tell the story, in the midst of all the tits, butts and spilled guts, of how love really matters by the end."](https://rodrovich.wordpress.com/2018/06/19/running-in-devilman-crybaby-passing-the-baton/) That is not the statement of someone making a nihilistic series. It is the statement of someone who used extreme content to ensure that the moment love reveals itself lands with full weight.

## The Universe Is Not Indifferent

Nihilism requires indifference. A nihilistic universe doesn't care what happens to anyone; it processes events without preference or weight. But [Devilman Crybaby's universe is not indifferent](https://medium.com/@natnie710/heavenly-satanic-and-technological-themes-in-devilman-crybaby-3d0f079f8182). God is characterized as cold and merciless — a deity who banished Satan from Heaven without love, and who treats the destruction of Earth as maintenance. And [the post-credits sequence complicates the reading of the ending further: the scorched Earth becomes a second moon, orbiting alongside the one Ryo tore in half during the fight](https://gamerant.com/devilman-crybaby-ending-explained/). The first episode of the series opens with a single moon. The post-credits shows two.

The implication embedded in Devilman's mythology is that this is not the first time. [Satan is trapped in a cycle — reincarnating, meeting Akira, forming the same attachment, making the same catastrophic mistake, losing Akira, and experiencing the reset again](https://an4t.com/devilman-crybaby-ending-explained-what-it-really-means/). Each iteration of Earth sports a different number of moons, which is how viewers can confirm the reset has already happened. The cycle is not random. It is, structurally, a punishment.

Here is where the nihilism reading collapses: you cannot torture someone with the thing they love if that thing is meaningless. If love were nothing — if Akira's death were just data — the eternal recurrence wouldn't hurt. The reset would be neutral, a system restoring itself. But the series presents it as anything but neutral. God's mechanism for punishing Satan is to force him to discover love through its loss, over and over, across iterations of the same world. That requires love to be real and devastating. Cruelty is not indifference.

And when the eternal recurrence is set alongside the title's structure, a further implication opens up: if Satan is the crybaby the title was waiting for in this iteration, he was the crybaby in the previous iteration too. And the one before that. The title isn't a one-time formal trick. It is the series' description of what the cycle keeps producing — a being who cannot cry, meeting a person who makes him cry, over and over, for as long as God chooses to keep running the loop. The universe organized to inflict grief is a universe that knows what grief is. A universe organized to keep producing the same crybaby is a universe that has decided love is the one reliable weapon.

## Akira's Name as Argument

This reading is reinforced by one of the series' subtler formal choices. [Akira's family name, Fudo, is a deliberate reference to Fudō Myōō](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akira_Fudo), the wrathful Buddhist deity among the Five Wisdom Kings — a manifestation of enlightened wisdom who destroys delusions and removes obstacles on the path to enlightenment. Fudō Myōō has a fearsome aspect because the destruction of delusion is itself violent; the obstacles being removed are the ones you built for yourself.

Akira's function in the series mirrors this exactly. He doesn't destroy Ryo's delusion by arguing with him or by surviving; he destroys it by dying. The enlightenment Ryo achieves — shattering, permanent, too late — is the recognition that the thing he most insisted wasn't real has been real all along, and is now gone. The series named its protagonist after a deity whose specific purpose is the destruction of delusion, and then had that protagonist fulfill that purpose through his death. This is not accidental.

## What Crybaby Is Actually About

[Crybaby is the first anime adaptation to feature the original ending from Go Nagai's 1970s manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devilman_Crybaby) — the ending where humanity is destroyed and Ryo mourns Akira's death. The 1972 television anime had a different ending; the catastrophic original was never animated before Yuasa's version. What Yuasa did was find the emotional argument latent in Nagai's structure and foreground it. The original manga's ending was not intended as nihilism either. It was a statement about war's cost, written during a period of sustained anti-war sentiment in Japan. Yuasa updated the social context but kept the emotional argument intact: love is proven by its loss.

[Yuasa stated the series' essential point directly](https://rodrovich.wordpress.com/2018/06/19/running-in-devilman-crybaby-passing-the-baton/): "The essential point of the story is what Ryo learns, and what he learns is love." That is not compatible with nihilism. Nihilism has nothing to learn. If love is real enough to be learned — real enough that its discovery happens even in the moment of its loss, real enough that it produces the first tears in a being who has existed across geological time — then love is the series' evidence against the reading it has been saddled with.

The nihilism label attached to Devilman Crybaby because the series is genuinely brutal, and because people who are hurt by a work sometimes reach for a framework that explains the hurt as the point. But brutal and nihilistic are different categories. [The series told a story about a being who spent his entire existence denying that love was real, and organized ten episodes of escalating catastrophe to produce the single moment where that denial became impossible](https://medium.com/@Krellitlikeitis/sympathy-for-the-devil-a-lesson-in-love-from-devilman-crybaby-a6c5947a787f). The crybaby the title had been waiting for cries at the end of the world. That weeping is not a concession to meaninglessness. It is the series' only argument, delivered at maximum volume.

The distinction matters because "nihilistic" and "tragic" are not synonyms, and collapsing them misreads what tragedy is for. Tragedy argues that loss is catastrophic. Nihilism argues that loss is nothing. Devilman Crybaby cares enormously about what it destroys — which is why watching it feels like being destroyed along with it.
