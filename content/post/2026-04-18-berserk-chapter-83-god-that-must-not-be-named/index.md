There is a chapter of Berserk that most readers have never encountered in the normal course of reading Berserk. It was published once, on [July 12, 1996, in Young Animal issue #14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Berserk_chapters), and then Kentaro Miura asked that it be omitted from Volume 13 and every subsequent collection. It has never been reprinted in official tankōbon format. You can find it online — the internet has no patience for authorial erasure — but it exists outside the canonical spine of any bookshelf.

The chapter is titled "God of the Abyss (2)." It is the moment Griffith, descending through the Eclipse toward his transformation into Femto, encounters the entity at the bottom of the Abyss: the [Idea of Evil](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Idea_of_Evil). A massive heart, covered in open eyes, breathing swirling gas. It explains to Griffith that it was born from humanity's collective unconscious — specifically from humanity's need to have *reasons* for suffering, death, and misfortune. It tells him that his entire lineage was arranged, that his dream of conquest was cultivated, that everything he is was prepared for this meeting. Then it asks whether he will become a God Hand and bring humanity either salvation or destruction.

It is one of the most significant pieces of world-building Miura ever wrote. And [he pulled it](https://screenrant.com/berserk-chapter-83-lost-miura-forbidden-factoid/).

The conventional framing is that Miura made a mistake — published too much, too fast, and had to retreat. The real story is more interesting. His removal of Chapter 83 was not damage control. It was an act of narrative philosophy, one that happens to perfectly mirror the themes of the chapter itself.

## What the Chapter Actually Says

The Idea of Evil's core claim is that *it exists because humans wanted it to*. Humanity collectively generated this entity out of the dark side of its consciousness — out of the universal human compulsion to find transcendent explanations for why bad things happen. The Idea of Evil is not a tyrant who seized power over humanity; it is a [projection of human psychology](https://epochemagazine.org/41/berserk-metaphysics-on-the-idea-of-evil/) given autonomous will. It operates through subtle causality, nudging events, preparing destinies, weaving fates — but it cannot determine everything absolutely. Characters like Skull Knight have always been able to [step outside the river of causality](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/the-lost-episode-and-the-idea-of-evil.10807/), to refuse beherits, to resist.

This is [Carl Jung's concept of the collective shadow](https://www.skullknight.net/forum/index.php?threads/jungian-collective-unconsciousness-and-berserk.5629/) made flesh — the accumulated darkness of human psychology, not repressed by one individual but shared across the entire species, given a physical form and a will. Jung's framework holds that the shadow does not disappear when suppressed; it manifests in projections, in the shape of monsters we then call external. The Idea of Evil is precisely that: the monster humanity created by refusing to own its own hunger for meaning in suffering. It reframes every torment Guts has endured not as the cruelty of a malevolent god but as the cruelty of *humanity's own desire to have a god*. The Idea of Evil is what humans built when they needed the world to make sense. The horror is that we made it.

## Why Miura Was Right to Remove It

In [2009, responding to questions from Skullknight.net](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon), Miura explained his reasoning: "It's because I wanted Berserk's world to be revealed just that far, not any more than that. The appearance of God in the manga conclusively determines its range. I thought that might limit the freedom of the story development."

He also said, pointedly: "I myself don't know if the Idea of Evil will show up in the manga or not."

This is a creator acknowledging that his story is bigger than his current understanding of it. The Idea of Evil, once named and given a face and a monologue, collapses the story's metaphysical open-endedness into a closed system. You no longer wonder whether there are supernatural forces at work — you *know*, and you know their shape, their motive, their origin. Every subsequent scene of religious devotion, every encounter with apostles and God Hand, every prayer to the God of the Abyss carries a different weight when you have already been told that God is a tumor of collective human sadness.

Consider [Father Mozgus](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Mozgus), the fanatical inquisitor who appears later in the Conviction arc. Mozgus believes in a God that demands blood and righteousness. Watching him is terrifying precisely because his God might be real, might be a genuine force in the universe — or might be the delusion of a man whose cruelty found a theology to justify itself. That ambiguity is crucial to how the arc functions. If readers enter the Conviction arc already knowing that the Idea of Evil exists and operates through causality, Mozgus becomes a puppet rather than a believer. The existential horror drains out.

The same logic applies throughout the manga. One of [Berserk's central tensions](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/09/28/fate-causality-why-was-the-lost-chapter-removed/) is between fate and individual will. Guts fighting against causality matters because it is not entirely clear what causality *is* or how absolute it is. If the mechanics of causality were fully explained in Volume 13, every subsequent scene where Guts resists his destiny becomes a debate with a known rulebook rather than a man throwing himself against the unknowable. The stakes change when the architecture is visible.

## The Ghost in the Margins

What makes the removal genuinely elegant is that the Idea of Evil doesn't disappear from the story — it retreats into the background where it was always supposed to operate. The entity appears at the end of [Episode 82, the chapter immediately preceding the lost one](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_82_(Manga)), but only as a looming presence in the Abyss. Its existence is established; its nature is not. It becomes exactly what the Eclipse sequence needs it to be: something vast, dark, and beyond understanding. The reader feels that Griffith's ascension has cosmological significance without having that significance explained to them.

This is the chapter working better as an absence than as a presence. The Idea of Evil that readers who read only the volumes encounter is more powerful than the Idea of Evil in Chapter 83, because it is the reader's own version — shaped by dread and implication rather than exposition. Miura intuited this. He published a chapter that told too much, recognized it, and pulled it back so that the story could breathe.

It's also worth noting what this decision tells us about Miura's craft. [His 2009 admission](https://www.thepopverse.com/comics-manga-berserk-chapter-83-idea-of-evil-god-reveal-not-republished-canon-noncanon) that he didn't know if the Idea of Evil would ever reappear is not a confession of poor planning. It's an acknowledgment that the best creative work remains responsive to itself as it develops. A creator who had locked Chapter 83 into the canonical spine of the manga would have been obligated to follow through on its implications in later volumes. By removing it, Miura kept the story's future structurally open.

## The Canon Question Nobody Can Quite Settle

The chapter exists in [a strange canonical limbo](https://mangasking.com/is-berserk-chapter-83-canon/) that the fan community has never fully resolved. The administrators at Skullknight.net have argued that it is not canon, since Miura effectively unmade it from the official story. Others point out that the Idea of Evil appears in Episode 82 (which is canonical) and is referenced obliquely through the rest of the manga — beherits and the God Hand's operations are consistent with the Chapter 83 explanation of how causality works, even if that explanation was never republished.

Miura himself never denied its canonicity when asked directly. He only said it would not be reprinted, and that he was uncertain whether the concept would return. This is a meaningful distinction. He didn't say the chapter was wrong or that the Idea of Evil didn't exist in his world. He said he wasn't ready to commit to *showing* it yet.

Now the question falls to Kouji Mori. [Berserk is continuing under Mori's supervision](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-new-writer-manga-continue/), drawn by Studio Gaga (Miura's former assistants), based on plans Miura shared before his [death in May 2021](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2022-06-07/berserk-manga-continues-under-kentarou-miura-assistants-kouji-mori-supervision/.186405). [Mori has stated](https://fandomwire.com/i-will-never-add-my-own-twist-koji-mori-knows-the-original-ending-kentaro-miura-planned-for-berserk-has-vowed-to-not-pollute-it/) he knows the ending Miura planned and will not add his own invention to it. Whether the Idea of Evil will finally be made explicit — whether Miura left notes for when to reintroduce it — is unknown. This is precisely what makes Chapter 83's canonical ghost story so alive: even in the hands of its continuation, that ambiguity has to be resolved by someone other than its author. What Miura refused to commit to becomes Mori's most loaded inheritance.

There is something almost poetic about the situation. A chapter about a god that operates from the background, never fully seen, shaping events through invisible causality — that chapter is itself operating from the background, shaping how readers understand the story without ever being formally present in it. If you've read it, it colors everything you encounter afterward. If you haven't, you feel its influence anyway in the story's texture, in the sense that something larger than the visible narrative is at work.

## What Gets Lost When You Name the God

The real cost of Chapter 83 being in the volumes would have been felt most in Berserk's emotional core: the question of whether suffering has meaning. [The Idea of Evil provides an answer](https://www.grimdarkmagazine.com/review-berserk-by-kentaro-miura/): suffering exists because humans needed it to have a reason, and their collective need generated a being to ensure it continued. It's a brutal, elegant answer. But it is an answer, and Berserk gains more from keeping the question alive than from resolving it at chapter 83.

Guts doesn't fight because he knows a cosmic entity arranged his misery. He fights because he refuses to accept that his misery is inevitable, regardless of what arranged it. The distinction matters enormously to the story's emotional force. If causality is a named mechanism with a known author, resistance becomes a debate with that mechanism. If causality is a felt weight with an unknown origin, resistance becomes an act of pure self-assertion against a world that might simply be cruel for no reason at all.

Miura gave readers the second version by taking the chapter away. He published a god, thought better of it, and put the god back where gods belong — in the dark, unnamed, felt but never confirmed.

That's not a mistake corrected. That's an artist who knew what his work needed.
