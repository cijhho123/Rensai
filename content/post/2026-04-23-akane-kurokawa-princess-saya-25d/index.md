There is a specific kind of creative failure that no one talks about because it doesn't produce a scandal. It produces nothing. A character exists in the source material — has a history, a relationship structure, a set of decisions that make her who she is — and then the adaptation begins, and the runtime pressures and the audience data arrive, and she gets edited down to three scenes and a sword. The result isn't bad. It's just not her. And if no one cares enough about her to complain, it doesn't even register as a problem.

This is exactly what happened to Princess Saya in the *Tokyo Blade* stage adaptation that forms the backbone of *Oshi no Ko*'s second season. And Akane Kurokawa's struggle to embody her is the series' sharpest argument about what commercial adaptation actually costs.

## What 2.5D Theater Is and Why It Matters

Japan's [2.5D musical industry](https://www.j25musical.jp/en/) — stage productions adapted from manga, anime, and games — is not a niche curiosity. It generates [approximately $175 million annually](https://gordoncox.substack.com/p/25d-musicals-japan-anime-manga-attack-on-titan), produces over [200 works per year](https://otakumode.com/news/674eae99f69446002854440d/The-World-of-2-5-Dimensional-Musicals-The-What-the-Why-and-the-How), and routinely draws audiences exceeding two million attendees per season. The genre launched with [*Musical: The Prince of Tennis*](https://www.japantimes.co.jp/culture/2015/03/31/stage/tenimyu-2-5-d-shows-net-2-million-tickets-sold/) in 2003, which became the blueprint for an entire industry: cast young actors in roles their fan communities recognize, replicate character designs and hairstyles with enough fidelity to trigger parasocial recognition, and let the established IP do the heavy lifting of audience investment.

The business model is specifically designed around characters that already have devoted followings. An [academic study on 2.5D adaptations](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/332477827_From_manga_to_stage_An_adaptation_study_of_25-D_musical_live_spectacle_naruto_-akatsuki_no_shirabe-) notes that audiences consume these productions primarily as fans of the original material — their investment precedes the show, and the show must justify itself against the mental model they already hold. This creates an unusual adaptation pressure: you must be legible to an audience that already knows the source material intimately, while fitting the whole thing into a two-hour runtime.

The result is predictable: major characters, whose fan bases are large enough to matter commercially, survive the adaptation. Secondary characters, whose fans are fewer, get cut or flattened. The character who was the protagonist's love interest in chapters five through eleven but got eclipsed by a more popular pairing by chapter twenty gets reduced to a set piece. Their scenes stay in just enough to check the fidelity box. Their interiority — why they made the choices they made, what their relationship to the story actually was — gets left on the cutting room floor, because there isn't a constituency large enough to demand it.

## The Princess Saya Problem

Princess Saya is exactly this character. In the original *Tokyo Blade* manga (a manga-within-the-manga in *Oshi no Ko*'s fiction), she was Touki's designated love interest. But as the manga's own fandom grew, audiences shifted their investment toward Touki's relationship with his rival Tsurugi — a [dynamic noted in TV Tropes discussions of the arc](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/posts.php?discussion=16252274490A24774800&page=37), where fan forums acknowledged that the Tsurugi x Touki pairing effectively displaced Saya's narrative function in the original text. One forum commenter noted that the manga was "sacrificing" Saya by reducing her to a romantic obstacle. Saya got sidelined in the source material itself before the adaptation even began. By the time the stage production enters development, she already has minimal scenes compared to other major characters.

Scriptwriter Goa must compress a complex manga into a two-hour production. He makes the economically rational decision: [he gives Saya fewer scenes and adjusts her character to fill the gaps a compact narrative requires](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/2.5D_Stage_Play). The character who enters the stage script is technically Princess Saya — same name, same sword, same position in the story — but her inner logic has been simplified to fit what the production needs from her.

Akane cannot perform this character. Not because she's incapable, but because her entire technique — [the profiling and possession-style acting](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Akane_Kurokawa) that she has built her career on — requires a coherent interior to inhabit. To become someone, she needs to understand why they made their choices, what they're afraid of, what they want. Goa's version of Saya doesn't have those answers, because the script didn't need them. [The character was written from the outside rather than from the inside](https://gamerant.com/oshi-no-ko-season-2-episode-1-review-tokyo-blade/).

When Akane brings this problem to Goa and the director, she's asking them to give her what any honest actor needs from a script: a person, not a function. [The Sakuga Blog's production analysis of Season 2](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/09/04/oshi-no-ko-stage/) identifies this as the arc's central argument: that "the true essence of a work may have little to do with the events it depicts, but rather on the nature of its characters," and that adaptations which prioritize plot fidelity while losing character texture are betraying the thing that made the source material worth adapting.

## The Conflict with the Original Author

The arc dramatizes this tension through the conflict between mangaka Abiko Samejima and the production team. Samejima is [demanding and rejects multiple revisions](https://oshinoko.fandom.com/wiki/Stage:_Oshi_no_Ko_2.5D_Stage_Play_Arc) — not because she's difficult, but because she knows the gap between what the script describes and what the original work actually is. From the production team's perspective, she's being impractical. From her perspective, they're agreeing to put her name on something that doesn't represent her work.

This is a real conflict in Japan's actual 2.5D industry. An [academic study of copyright in the Japanese manga industry](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17510694.2018.1563420) notes that while mangaka commonly retain their copyright, the practical exploitation of licensed works — including stage adaptations — involves negotiation dynamics that frequently favor production companies who control the capital, the venue relationships, and the casting infrastructure. The legal rights and the creative authority often diverge: a creator can technically refuse revisions, as the fictional Samejima does, but doing so risks killing the production entirely and damaging the commercial ecosystem around the original work. The popular assumption is that the industry has "utmost respect for the original work" — and many practitioners do operate with genuine care — but the commercial pressures of runtime, casting, and audience demographics create incentive structures that don't always preserve what made a character worth playing in the first place.

[The Sakuga Blog analysis](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2024/09/04/oshi-no-ko-stage/) frames *Oshi no Ko*'s position here as pro-adaptation but anti-negligence: the argument isn't that adaptations must be faithful shot-for-shot replications, but that the failures which damage characters usually stem from "systemic negligence" and "lack of truthful, eye-to-eye communication" rather than from the inherent requirements of the medium. Goa isn't a villain. He made rational decisions under constraint. The problem is that the system produced constraints that made rational decisions cost someone their character.

## The Meta-Irony of the Real-World Adaptation

In August 2024, [the *Oshi no Ko* manga's 2.5D Stage Play arc received a real-world stage adaptation](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-08-28/oshi-no-ko-manga-gets-stage-play-for-2.5d-stage-play-arc/.214883) — a 2.5D stage play about a 2.5D stage play, produced for December 2024 performances in Tokyo and Osaka. [The production included Tokyo Blade as a play-within-a-play](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2024/8/29/oshi-no-ko-new-stage-play-december-2024), with original script, lyrics, and music.

This is either the industry's most self-aware moment or its most perfectly oblivious one. A stage production adapting an arc that critically examines what stage productions do to secondary characters will face exactly the same decisions the fictional production faced. It too has a two-hour runtime. It too must select which characters justify cast slots. It too is commercially incentivized to center the characters whose names mean something to the audience coming in.

![Oshi no Ko promotional art](https://nxsnjqfqtwiaoedulnpr.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/image/articles/00000103_962662a4a259.jpg)

The real-world production [sold out its Tokyo and Osaka runs](https://richhippos.com/anime/theater-oshi-no-ko-2-5-dimensional-stage-version-opens-komiya-rio-who-plays-aqua-says-this-stage-performance-should-give-us-a-deeper-understanding-of-2-5-dimensional-stage-performances/), with Mirai Uchida cast as Akane Kurokawa. Rio Komiya, who played Aqua, stated that "this stage performance should give us a deeper understanding of 2.5-dimensional stage performances" — which is the most self-referential cast quote the genre has probably ever produced. Whether the production found a solution for its own version of the Saya problem — whether it gave Akane/Uchida's character enough interior to work with — is something only those who attended can answer. What's certain is that the meta-level irony is structural, not accidental: the series arguing that commercial adaptation loses secondary characters was itself commercially adapted, and had to navigate the same decisions in the real 2.5D industry it was critiquing.

## What Akane's Struggle Actually Diagnoses

Akane can't find Princess Saya because Princess Saya was edited away in service of the production's commercial needs. Her struggle isn't personal failure — it's accurate perception. She can tell the difference between a character who has been written with interior logic and a character who has been assembled from plot requirements. Her profiling technique, which allows her to inhabit the former, simply doesn't work on the latter.

The series uses her inability to find Saya to make an argument that extends well beyond *Tokyo Blade* and well beyond the fictional entertainment industry the characters inhabit. Every 2.5D production that has ever sidelined a love interest, merged two minor characters into one, or cut the scene that explained why someone made the decision they made has produced versions of this problem for the actors who have to play those roles. They have to stand on stage and be convincing as a person whose reasons for existing have been stripped out to save runtime.

Akane names this problem in a way that the industry usually doesn't: not as a production failure, not as an author's complaint, but as an actor's inability to perform a character who wasn't given a self to inhabit.
