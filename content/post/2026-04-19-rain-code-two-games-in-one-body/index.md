The [critical spread for Master Detective Archives: RAIN CODE](https://www.metacritic.com/game/master-detective-archives-rain-code/) tells an unusually coherent story. On one end: [DualShockers 9.5](https://www.dualshockers.com/master-detective-archives-rain-code-review/), [RPGFan 89](https://www.rpgfan.com/review/master-detective-archives-rain-code/), Pocket Tactics 9, Shacknews calling the Plus version something that "reaches heights the Danganronpa series never did." On the other: [GameSpot 7](https://www.gamespot.com/reviews/master-detective-archives-rain-code-review-blame-it-on-the-rain/1900-6418087/), [Eurogamer 6](https://www.eurogamer.net/master-detective-archives-rain-code-review), God is a Geek 6, Nintendo Life 7. Metacritic lands at 77.

This is not a disagreement about whether RAIN CODE is any good. It is two different groups of reviewers describing two different games that happen to share a body.

![Master Detective Archives: RAIN CODE key art](https://cdn.akamai.steamstatic.com/steam/apps/2231750/header.jpg)

## What Kodaka Said He Was Making

Kazutaka Kodaka left Spike Chunsoft in 2017 with a specific frustration. *"Danganronpa is good,"* he [told Gematsu at GDC 2023](https://www.gematsu.com/2023/03/master-detective-archives-rain-code-interview-with-kazutaka-kodaka-at-gdc-2023), *"but since it's a visual novel and you only read text, there will only be niche fans. I wanted to create a game where fans that don't often play visual novel games also come play."* He cited Life is Strange and Detroit: Become Human as the target audience. He wanted to make a game for people who wouldn't touch a visual novel.

The result of this ambition was RAIN CODE: a fully 3D open-world mystery adventure with level-up mechanics, skill trees, action boss fights, and what Kodaka described as [a Disneyland-attraction mystery dungeon](https://www.gematsu.com/2023/03/master-detective-archives-rain-code-details-kanai-ward-mystery-labyrinth-and-reasoning-death-match) that would make deduction feel physical and kinetic. The director later confirmed the switch to 3D was ["incomparably harder" than making Danganronpa](https://automaton-media.com/en/interviews/interview-from-abilities-that-transcend-human-understanding-to-fully-3d-scenes-that-left-no-room-for-error-master-detective-archives-rain-code-involved-challenges-incomparably-bigger-than-danganron/) — rebuilding everything from scratch, learning to build a navigable city rather than a single enclosed space.

What Kodaka actually built was a VN with corridors to walk down. The distinction matters.

## The Mystery Labyrinth Problem

The Mystery Labyrinth is where RAIN CODE's identity crisis is most visible. It is the game's signature mechanic: when enough evidence accumulates, Shinigami rips open a supernatural pocket dimension that physically manifests the unanswered questions of the case as environmental obstacles, enemies, and eventually a "Mystery Phantom" — the killer's desire to bury the truth made literal, fought via a "Reasoning Deathmatch" where you dodge false statements and slash the contradictions with your Solution Blade.

Kodaka explicitly [modeled the Labyrinth on Persona 5's Palaces](https://www.pockettactics.com/danganronpa/interview) — even joking that mentioning Persona in marketing helped move units. The design language is borrowed from action RPGs: spatial navigation, combat encounters, level progression. From that framework, the criticism writes itself. [Nintendo Life noted](https://www.nintendolife.com/reviews/nintendo-switch/master-detective-archives-rain-code) that "a large portion of these dungeons is spent just walking down a linear hallway." [Eurogamer found the Labyrinth](https://www.eurogamer.net/master-detective-archives-rain-code-review) "an obstacle to the story rather than an enhancement." The Reasoning Deathmatch is shallow by action game standards; the puzzle elements are thin by adventure game standards. On the action RPG rubric Kodaka was reaching for, the Labyrinth underdelivers.

But here is what reviewers who loved the game understood: the Labyrinth was never an action RPG mechanic. It is a VN conceit wearing action clothing. The "walking corridors" are the investigation made physical — the experience of sitting with a question before the answer arrives, given spatial form. The Phantom that attacks with false statements is Danganronpa's Class Trial Boss stripped of the social debate layer and rendered as a literal monster. What frustrated action RPG reviewers felt like padding is what VN readers recognize as the essential rhythm: accumulation and then rupture. The Labyrinth is only disappointing if you wanted the Palaces from Persona 5. If you wanted the Class Trial from Danganronpa given a body, it delivers.

The game [reached 300,000 units within its first month](https://www.gematsu.com/2023/08/master-detective-archives-rain-code-shipments-and-digital-sales-top-300000). That is neither a mainstream crossover nor a niche failure. It is a VN audience that showed up.

## What the Game Actually Is

RAIN CODE's strongest element is not its mechanics. It is Kanai Ward.

The city — perpetually rain-soaked, lightless, controlled at every level by the Amaterasu Corporation — is the most thematically dense setting in Kodaka's career. Every case illuminates a different facet of what it means to live inside a system designed to bury the truth: police who refuse to investigate because Amaterasu controls them, workers disappeared for knowing too much, the infrastructure of violence that keeps the powerful safe from consequence. [MoeGamer's long-form analysis](https://moegamer.net/2025/10/04/mystery-detective-archives-rain-code-what-price-justice/) reads RAIN CODE as "an expression of frustration, even outright anger at the state we've allowed the modern world to get into, and a fairly explicit statement of anti-capitalist intent." This reading is not a stretch. The corporate dystopia is the explicit villain of every chapter.

This is not Life is Strange. Life is Strange does not give you a death god and ask you to decide whether killing the murderer is justice or just a different kind of murder. RAIN CODE's moral engine — Shinigami must execute the culprit after each solved case, always, no exceptions — forces a question Danganronpa never did: what does truth cost when the mechanism of revealing it is also lethal? [Kotaku's review](https://kotaku.com/master-detective-archives-switch-review-danganronpa-1850592843) identified this as the game's most distinctive ethical dimension, the element that deepens as the overarching Amaterasu conspiracy becomes clearer and the executions multiply. Is Yuma any different from the system he is fighting? The game doesn't answer cleanly, and neither should it.

![Master Detective Archives: RAIN CODE Plus — Kanai Ward](https://cdn.akamai.steamstatic.com/steamcommunity/public/images/apps/2231750/9cd3f5e81bcfe4436f49a623524c148a4c08822d.jpg)

These are VN concerns. The questions RAIN CODE asks — about complicity, institutional truth-suppression, the ethics of justice — are not the questions Detroit: Become Human asks. They require patience with character interiority, tolerance for ambiguity, and willingness to sit inside a system's logic long enough for it to implicate the player. That is the VN skill set. The Life is Strange audience Kodaka wanted does not necessarily bring those tools.

## The Shinigami Dividend

None of this would hold together without Shinigami working. She does. A small purple ghost in the real world, a tall gothic woman inside the Labyrinth, she is brash, irreverent, frequently crude, and occasionally genuinely moving — the character Kodaka has called his [personal favorite in the entire game](https://gonintendo.com/contents/22536-master-detective-archives-rain-code-writer-kept-requesting-that-one-character-be). Her duality is the game's central image: the small, manageable version of death that can be kept at arm's length in ordinary life, and the full-scale confrontation with it that awaits inside any real investigation of what actually happened. The fan service in her Labyrinth design is real and the [GoNintendo headline](https://gonintendo.com/contents/22536-master-detective-archives-rain-code-writer-kept-requesting-that-one-character-be) about Kodaka repeatedly requesting she be made "sexier" did the rounds with appropriate raised eyebrows. But reducing Shinigami to the fan service misses how much the character holds the game's philosophical weight. Consider what she is structurally: every case ends with her killing the person whose crime Yuma just proved. She does it cheerfully. She is bound to him by contract and she will not negotiate. In the case that closes the first act, when the culprit turns out to be someone the cast trusted and genuinely cared about, Shinigami executes them anyway — and the game does not let you look away. [Kotaku's review](https://kotaku.com/master-detective-archives-switch-review-danganronpa-1850592843) identified this mechanic as what most clearly differentiates RAIN CODE from Danganronpa: Class Trials punished guilt with exile; RAIN CODE punishes it with death, performed by someone who will immediately make a joke about it. The dissonance is the point.

## The Version Problem

It is also worth noting that the canonical version of RAIN CODE — [RAIN CODE Plus for PS5 and PC](https://www.rpgfan.com/review/master-detective-archives-rain-code-plus/), released October 2024 — is substantially different from the game most 2023 critics reviewed. The Switch version was plagued with frame rate drops, blurry visuals, and [loading screens that consumed over an hour of total play time](https://www.nintendolife.com/reviews/nintendo-switch/master-detective-archives-rain-code). A game Kodaka explicitly designed to compete with cinematic mainstream titles was launched exclusively on Nintendo Switch — the platform with the least horsepower — and paid for it in performance. The enhanced version fixes virtually all of these problems. Shacknews [reviewed the Plus version](https://www.shacknews.com/article/141713/master-detective-archives-rain-code-plus-review-nothing-but-the-truth) and called it a game that "when it's on point it cooks to an absurd degree." That version — silky frame rate, full DLC included, proper presentation — is what RAIN CODE was supposed to be. Many of the mid-range scores from 2023 are partially reviews of platform limitations.

## The Game Kodaka Made Anyway

[Kodaka told Nintendo Life](https://www.nintendolife.com/features/everyone-just-so-happens-to-die-in-my-stories-danganronpa-creator-talks-master-detective-archives-rain-code) that he wants to make a RAIN CODE sequel alongside a Danganronpa return, though the path forward depends on fan response. [ResetEra's review thread](https://www.resetera.com/threads/master-detective-archives-rain-code-review-thread.736482/) and the sustained community enthusiasm around RAIN CODE Plus suggest the audience most invested in a sequel is not the Life is Strange crossover crowd Kodaka was courting — it is VN readers who recognized what he built as the sharpest, most politically coherent work of his career, sharper than Danganronpa precisely because the stakes are real and the antagonist is a corporation rather than a single mastermind.

The most honest reading of RAIN CODE is that Kodaka set out to escape his niche and ended up making the best version of it. The city that never sees the sun, the death god who executes the guilty, the amnesiac detective who erased himself to see clearly — these are not Life is Strange ideas. They are ideas that required the patience and interiority of a visual novel to land. The players who showed up with that patience understood. The critics applying an action RPG rubric to what is, at its core, a story about institutional suppression of truth were playing the wrong game.

Kanai Ward was always the argument. The Labyrinth corridors were just the walk to get there.
