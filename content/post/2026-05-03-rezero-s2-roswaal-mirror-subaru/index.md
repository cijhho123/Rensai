There's a scene in Re:ZERO Season 2 where Roswaal L. Mathers calmly explains to Subaru that he burned the Roswaal mansion to the ground. Everyone inside it. Because, he says, [the Gospel told him this was the optimal path](https://animehunch.com/what-is-roswaals-goal-and-motive-in-rezero/) toward his goal. He doesn't flinch. He doesn't perform regret. He simply presents it as arithmetic: sacrifice these people, advance toward the goal.

Subaru is horrified. He should be. But what the show is doing in that moment — what Season 2 has been building toward all along — is holding up a mirror. Because Subaru's entire approach to Return by Death runs on the same arithmetic. Die. Reset. Try different variables. Optimize the outcome. Repeat until perfect. The mansion is just what that logic looks like after 400 years.

Re:ZERO Season 2 is not, fundamentally, a story about defeating the Sanctuary's trials or stopping Elsa or rescuing Rem. Those are its plot. Its drama — the thing that gives the plot meaning — is the collision between Subaru and Roswaal as two men who've been handed the same weapon and made opposite choices about what to do with it.

## The Gospel and the Loop

[Roswaal has been at this for 400 years](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Roswaal_L._Mathers). The Roswaal we meet in the series is technically Roswaal A. Mathers, the original, who has spent four centuries possessing the bodies of his own descendants, one generation at a time, methodically working toward a single goal: the resurrection of Echidna. He possesses a Gospel — a book written by Echidna that describes the optimal future — and he has been following its instructions with absolute precision ever since she died. Every decision, every manipulation, every sacrifice: all of it is justified by the script. The Gospel says this is the way, so this is the way.

Subaru's Return by Death works differently in its mechanics but identically in its moral logic. [The ability, granted by the Witch of Envy Satella, rewinds time to a checkpoint whenever Subaru dies](https://fortresswilliam.com/2020/09/09/exploring-subarus-return-by-death-ability-rezero/), preserving only his memories. What Subaru retains across loops is the knowledge of what went wrong — who betrayed whom, which traps are laid, what the enemy's moves will be. This turns every death into data. Every tragedy becomes a variable to be adjusted. Every person becomes, in a certain mode of thinking, something between an obstacle and a resource.

This is not a flaw in Subaru's character, exactly. It's a structural temptation built into the ability itself. When you can undo deaths, death becomes cheap. When you can re-run scenarios, other people's pain becomes experimental. [As one analysis observes](https://gamerant.com/re-zero-subaru-flaws-make-him-a-better-protagonist/), Subaru's willingness to throw his life away over and over is framed initially as heroism — he's suffering so others don't have to. But it's also a form of control. He's the only one who knows the full shape of the situation. He's the one making decisions about which timeline gets to exist. That's power, and it corrupts.

What makes Roswaal such a devastating antagonist in Season 2 isn't his cruelty. It's his rationality. [As one essay puts it](https://fortresswilliam.com/2020/10/06/roswaal-mathers-the-perfect-villain-for-rezero/), Roswaal's plans "make sense when you remove the ethical elements and remove emotion from the equation." He's not deranged. He's simply solved the equation completely, all the way to its terminal point — which is a person who has no one left, pursuing a goal that will not bring him back what he lost.

## Echidna and the Architecture of the Trap

Between Subaru and Roswaal sits Echidna, the Witch of Greed, and she is the figure the show gives least credit for how much she's done.

The Tea Party scenes — where Echidna meets Subaru in a metaphysical dreamspace and interrogates him with an almost academic warmth — are among the best sequences in the season. [Echidna is written as genuinely fascinated by Subaru](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Echidna): by his Return by Death, by his suffering, by what she can learn from consuming his experience. She's brilliant, disarming, and she offers to help him. She positions herself as the one person who can understand his burden.

The other Witches of Sin warn him about this. Echidna's apparent warmth is intellectual appetite. She doesn't love people; she studies them. She finds Subaru compelling the way a scientist finds a novel organism compelling — and that distinction matters enormously, because [the show uses it to reveal something crucial about Roswaal](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Echidna/Relationships): he made a contract with her, fell in love with her, and devoted four centuries to her resurrection, not knowing — or not wanting to know — that she never looked at him the way he looked at her. The flashback scenes make this visible through a cinematographic tell: Echidna's eyes are always hidden. The love affair that defines Roswaal's entire existence was, from her side, a study.

Echidna gave Roswaal the Gospel. [She created Puck to watch over Emilia](https://rezero.fandom.com/wiki/Emilia). She designed the Trials that force Subaru to reckon with his past. She is, in a real sense, the architect of the situation Season 2 unfolds inside — and she constructed it by being exactly what she appears to be in the Tea Parties: someone who sees people as functions of interesting data.

This is the trap. Echidna built a world in which the most powerful ability a person can have is the ability to optimize, and she populated it with a man who would follow his optimization to its logical endpoint. Subaru stumbles into this world with a power that tempts him toward the same endpoint, and the show asks whether he'll recognize the trap before he falls all the way in.

## The Witch Trials as Diagnosis

[The Trials of the Sanctuary](https://animehunch.com/rezero-what-are-the-trials-of-the-sanctuary/) are structured as confrontations with one's past, present, and future. For Subaru, they function less as obstacles and more as diagnostics — each one forcing him to look at himself from an angle his loops have let him avoid.

The episode dedicated entirely to Subaru's backstory — his withdrawal from school, the inferiority complex he developed [in his father's shadow](https://gamerant.com/rezerosubaru-changes-season-1/), the self that decided it would be easier to disappear — is the show at its most psychologically precise. What we see is that Subaru came to this fantasy world already disposed toward self-erasure. He'd already decided he wasn't worth much. Return by Death didn't create his willingness to treat his own life as expendable — it gave that pre-existing tendency a mechanism.

The Trial's revelation isn't dramatic in the conventional sense. It's quietly devastating: Subaru sees the grief his deaths leave behind in alternate timelines. Emilia's face. Rem's face. People who are trying to hold on to him while he treats his existence as something to be discarded whenever the variables aren't right. The show makes the argument that what looks like heroism — dying to protect others — is also, at its core, a refusal to believe he deserves to stay alive when things are hard.

This is exactly the argument the show makes against Roswaal, framed differently. Roswaal's 400-year optimization project is also, underneath, a form of self-erasure: he stopped being a person and became a function, a carrier of the Gospel. The tragedy isn't that he sacrificed others. The tragedy is that he sacrificed himself first — his capacity to actually be present in a relationship, to want something rather than execute toward it.

## The Confrontation That Matters

The climactic confrontation between Subaru and Roswaal in Season 2 isn't a fight. It's an argument.

Roswaal demands that Subaru abandon his commitment to saving both the Sanctuary refugees and the mansion residents. [He wants Subaru to let go of his feelings and trample on Emilia's will in service of the optimal outcome](https://starcrossedanime.com/rezero-starting-life-in-another-world-2-anime-review-85-100/) — to treat her as a goal rather than a person. This is the moment where the mirror becomes explicit: Roswaal isn't offering Subaru a villainous bargain. He's offering him a methodology. Do what I do. Follow the script. Stop caring about the people in the timeline and start caring about the shape of the outcome.

Subaru refuses. What's interesting about his refusal is that it isn't pure altruism. The Witch Trials have already shown him the cost of his own self-erasure — the grief his deaths leave behind, the people who need him to stay rather than to succeed. His refusal to follow Roswaal's logic is, at its root, the show's central insight made flesh: [loving others before you can love yourself doesn't work](https://starcrossedanime.com/rezero-starting-life-in-another-world-2-anime-review-85-100/). The optimization breaks you before it saves anyone.

What the show ultimately argues — through the parallel between these two characters, through the Tea Parties, through the Trials — is that the problem with Roswaal's approach isn't that it's evil. It's that it's incomplete. He followed the logic all the way to its end and found nothing waiting there except the act of following. He's been executing the algorithm for four centuries and the algorithm is all he has left.

[Subaru wins not by being smarter or stronger but by being willing to lose](https://gamerant.com/rezerosubaru-changes-season-1/) — by making a bet with Roswaal on trust rather than certainty, by telling Emilia things he can't know will work, by treating his friends as actual people rather than variables in a scenario he's already run. [This is what the Witch of Envy meant](https://fortresswilliam.com/2020/09/09/exploring-subarus-return-by-death-ability-rezero/) when she gave him the ability and told him, in ways he couldn't understand at the time, to love himself. The ability is a curse that teaches you to see people as expendable. The only counter to it is insisting they aren't.

## Why This Season Still Divides People

[The Reddit Anime Awards named Re:ZERO Season 2 its Anime of the Year](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/interest/2021-02-22/reddit-anime-awards-crown-chihayafuru-3-re-zero-season-2-as-anime-of-the-year/.169817). The pacing critics weren't wrong that the season is slow. They were wrong about what the slowness means.

The case against Season 2 goes roughly like this: the second cour drags, the Subaru-Emilia scenes are [repetitive to the point of tedium](https://mistakescheerioforchesto.wordpress.com/2021/01/29/understanding-rezero-season-2-part-2-dead-on-arrival/), [White Fox pushed episode runtimes to 29 minutes and cut opening sequences](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/re-zero-season-2-delay-statement-white-fox-studio-anime/) and still couldn't make the material breathe, and [the production team was exhausted by the end](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/why-re-zero-season-2-took-so-long/). All of that is accurate. What the pacing argument misses is that the people making it are usually hoping for something different from what the show is doing.

Re:ZERO was never a power fantasy — but it keeps getting watched by people who want one. Subaru is too broken to be satisfying in the conventional isekai sense. His arc isn't triumphant; it's corrective. The pacing problems in Season 2 read very differently depending on whether you want the show to accelerate toward its dramatic payoffs or whether you want it to make you feel the weight that makes those payoffs mean something. [A season that compresses eight volumes of dense light novel material](https://litretreat.com/re-zero-light-novel-vs-anime/) into 25 episodes and still takes its time on Subaru's backstory is making a choice about what it thinks the story is. That choice isn't wrong — it's just not the one some viewers wanted.

The light novel makes this even more apparent. [Nagatsuki writes Subaru's internal monologue with a compulsiveness that the anime can't replicate](https://litretreat.com/re-zero-light-novel-vs-anime/) — the self-loathing that underlies every death, the specific texture of someone who genuinely believes he's disposable. The anime catches the emotional peaks. It loses the scaffolding underneath them, which is why the slow build sections feel to some viewers like stalling. [Star Crossed Anime noted that episodes 4–13 would rate 95/100 in isolation](https://starcrossedanime.com/rezero-starting-life-in-another-world-2-anime-review-85-100/); the problem is the 25 episodes it takes to get there, not the destination.

Arc 4 is the structural axis of everything Nagatsuki has built — the place where Subaru either becomes Roswaal or doesn't. Whether the season delivers on that depends entirely on whether you came to Re:ZERO for the question or the answer.

## What Roswaal Gets Right

There's a final, uncomfortable piece to the Roswaal mirror. He isn't entirely wrong.

His methodology is hollow and he's lost himself inside it. But the insight that drives it — that untargeted goodwill fails, that caring about everyone equally often means saving no one, that love without strategy is sometimes just a way of feeling good while people die — these aren't easily dismissed. [Subaru's response to this insight isn't to refute it but to find a different way to live with it](https://gamerant.com/re-zero-subaru-flaws-make-him-a-better-protagonist/): trust the people you love rather than optimize around them, accept the uncertainty rather than die until you've eliminated it.

That's not a complete answer. The show doesn't pretend it is. Roswaal will keep following his Gospel, at least for now. The Witch of Envy is still out there. The loops haven't stopped. But Subaru has learned, at significant cost, the difference between using Return by Death to run scenarios and using it to run toward people. Roswaal never made that distinction. That's the choice — and it's the only one that matters.
