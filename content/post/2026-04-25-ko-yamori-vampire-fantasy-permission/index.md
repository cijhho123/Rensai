Ko Yamori never needed to become a vampire. He already had everything he was chasing.

That's not a criticism of [*Call of the Night*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) — it's the series' actual argument. What Kotoyama spends twenty volumes building is not a story about a boy who wants supernatural power and earns it through love. It's a story about a boy who has already found the life he wants, but can't give himself permission to keep it. The vampire transformation he's chasing is a permission slip, not a destination. And the series — carefully, almost cruelly — refuses to let him cash it in.

## The Facade That Broke Before the Story Started

Before Ko ever steps outside at midnight, something is already over. He was, by [every external measure](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori), a model student — charismatic, popular, academically strong. That version of Ko was entirely constructed. He didn't enjoy school. He couldn't relate to his classmates. He understood none of what romance or connection were supposed to feel like, only that everyone around him seemed to understand it, and he was [performing the right responses](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/CallOfTheNight) without accessing any of the substance underneath.

What breaks him isn't failure. It's exhaustion from succeeding at something that cost him everything and gave him nothing. [He withdraws from school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night), develops insomnia, starts walking at night — and immediately, inexplicably, feels better. The nights aren't a problem to solve. They're the first thing that's ever actually fit.

This is the setup Kotoyama designed, and it's precise: Ko's disorder is a correct diagnosis of his situation, not a symptom of something broken. The nocturnal world is where he belongs. He just doesn't know yet that belonging somewhere doesn't require a supernatural excuse to stay.

## What "Becoming a Vampire" Actually Means

When Ko articulates his goal — get Nazuna to fall in love with him, complete the transformation, become a vampire — the logic sounds clear. But the reasons he gives are worth examining closely. [He doesn't want to die](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-episode-12-ko-final-decision-become-vampire/). Vampires don't need to go to school. They don't need to manage time. They're free from the compressed anxieties of a human life that has a deadline.

What Ko is describing is not a desire for power. It's a desire for the pressure to stop. And crucially, he frames vampirism as the only way to make that permanent — because if he stays human, there's always the implied possibility of going back. Of rejoining the structure. Of eventually becoming the model student again.

This is the adolescent logic of the irreversible act. By committing to something with no return path, Ko imagines he can finally stop being afraid that he'll capitulate. The bite isn't the goal. The bite is the lock on the door behind him.

Kotoyama himself framed the entire series as a "rebellious rom-com" — he [told a TSUTAYA interviewer](http://wsstalkback.blogspot.com/2020/07/call-of-sunday-kotoyama-tsutaya.html) that "a normal rom-com is embarrassing, so I decided to draw a rebellious one instead," and [confirmed later](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) that conveying that rebellious spirit to readers was exactly what he'd hoped for. Ko's exit fantasy is exactly that kind of difficulty: not a hero who wants power, but a kid who wants to stop having to want the things society tells him to want.

## The Series Dismantles the Fantasy Methodically

*Call of the Night* doesn't reject Ko's desire as stupid or childish. It takes it seriously and then [shows him what it actually contains](https://epicstream.com/article/does-kou-yamori-become-a-vampire-call-of-the-night). The vampire world, it turns out, is not a space of freedom. It has its own social hierarchies, obligations, and enforcement mechanisms. There are vampires who operate according to strict unwritten codes, factions with agendas, pressures that Ko couldn't have anticipated from the outside. The [series gradually makes clear](https://www.cbr.com/call-of-the-night-season-2-need-to-know/) that escaping human society by becoming a vampire would not mean escaping social obligation — it would mean trading one set of rules for another, less legible one.

The more surgical dismantling comes through Ko's half-vampire transformation. When Ko partially transforms — not through love but through [an act of protection during a dangerous moment](https://badendfriends.fandom.com/wiki/Half-Vampire_Kou) — the result is disturbing in a way that cuts directly at the series' argument. He becomes bloodthirsty and unhinged, and the first person he turns on isn't a stranger or an enemy: it's [Mahiru, his own childhood friend](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori), someone he cares about, nearly killing him before Nazuna intervenes. The transformation he's been treating as a solution to his loneliness threatens the exact connections that solved it. Whatever he imagined "vampire Ko" would be — free, finally himself — it isn't this. The half-vampire state doesn't liberate him from performance; it replaces his performed contentment with a performed violence that's even less him.

Meanwhile, the things Ko actually wanted — connection, nocturnal freedom, the feeling of being genuinely seen — he's been accumulating all along. He has friends now, real ones, [built from actual shared experience](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kou_Yamori) rather than performed social compatibility. He has Nazuna. He has nights that feel worth being awake for. The life he was trying to lock himself into through vampirism already exists. He just built it as a human.

## The Problem With Lust vs. Love (And Why It's Not a Subplot)

Ko's early misidentification of lust as love — his first kiss sends his heart racing and he mistakes the physical arousal for genuine romantic feeling — gets treated as a character quirk, a setup for later emotional growth. But it's actually the core diagnostic of his entire arc. Ko [confuses the feeling of finally wanting something with wanting that specific thing](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2022/10/04/a-review-of-call-of-the-night/). The excitement is real. Its object is uncertain.

The same dynamic runs through his vampirism wish. Ko feels genuine longing for a different kind of life — but vampirism is the first available form that longing could take, and he mistakes the container for the content. What he's chasing is the right to stop managing expectations that were never his to begin with. The vampire is just the first image that arrived.

The love condition for transformation — [Nazuna has to genuinely love him, not just bite him](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-11-15/call-of-the-night-manga-ends-with-200th-chapter/.204474) — functions as the series' mechanism for keeping Ko honest. He can't shortcut to transformation. He has to do the actual emotional work of being loved by someone who knows him. And that work is, slowly and sometimes painfully, what grows him into someone who doesn't need the transformation anymore.

## What the Ending Is Actually About

Ko doesn't become a vampire. By the final volume, [three years have passed](http://www.bostonbastardbrigade.com/2025/06/call-of-the-night-20-review/) and his feeling for Nazuna remains — not resolved into conventional romance, not cashed out into the supernatural. What Ko and Nazuna settle into is an arrangement that belongs to neither human convention nor vampire convention. [Neither a relationship in the standard sense nor a rejection of one](https://sonatano1.wordpress.com/2022/10/04/a-review-of-call-of-the-night/). Something they invented.

The series ends on "we're in this together" — which is, characteristically, exactly what Ko needed to hear and exactly what required the entire journey to become possible. Not permission from a transformation. Permission from another person, freely given, to keep showing up in a form that doesn't have a name.

This is what Kotoyama's "rebellious rom-com" was building toward all along. Not a boy who escapes his human life by becoming something else. A boy who learns that the life worth living is the one he was already building — piece by piece, night by night, without needing to lock the door.

Ko already had the night. What took twenty volumes was learning he didn't need to earn the right to stay in it.

![Call of the Night anime cover featuring Ko Yamori](https://s4.anilist.co/file/anilistcdn/media/anime/cover/large/bx141391-M3ZgUKTPENUk.jpg)

The reason that landing works is because the series never treated Ko's desire as pathological. It understood him. It knew that what he was chasing wasn't supernatural power but the right to be a person on his own terms. And it [showed him](https://aftermath.site/coming-of-age-anime-call-of-the-night-vampires/) — patiently, without condescension — that he'd been that person all along. He just had to stop waiting for something to make it official.
