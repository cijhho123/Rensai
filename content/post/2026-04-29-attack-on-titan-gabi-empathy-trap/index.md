The most revealing moment in Attack on Titan's Final Season didn't happen in the show. It happened in the comment sections, Reddit threads, and fan spaces where viewers declared — with genuine conviction — that Gabi Braun was the worst character in the series. Not Zeke, who engineered mass euthanasia. Not Eren, who killed eighty percent of the world's population. Gabi. The twelve-year-old child soldier raised from birth to believe her own people were devils.

[Attack on Titan Wiki: Gabi Braun](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Gabi_Braun) describes her as "a member of the Warrior Unit" trained from childhood to inherit the Armored Titan — a child conscripted into Marley's machinery of oppression before she could understand what any of it meant. The fandom largely responded to this with death threats and some of the most visceral character hatred in recent anime history. And in doing so, they proved [Hajime Isayama's central thesis](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-isayama-themes-explained/) better than anything in the text itself.

## The Structural Gambit

Most anime adaptations of ongoing manga begin where the manga begins. The Final Season does something stranger: it begins in the middle of a war, four years after the events of Season 3, in a country called Marley, following characters the audience has never met, including the boy who will inherit the Jaw Titan and a fiercely determined girl named Gabi who wants nothing more than to prove herself worthy of the Armored Titan. The Survey Corps — Eren, Armin, Mikasa, the people you've spent three seasons with — don't appear for nearly five episodes.

This is a conscious structural choice with real stakes. [Season 4's Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attack_on_Titan_season_4) notes the arc covers the manga's chapters 91–106 with the initial focal point entirely on the Marleyan side. You don't get your protagonists back until the show has made you sit with the people who were your series' monsters. Reiner Braun, who spent three seasons as a traitor and killer, becomes the nearest thing the Final Season Part 1 has to a point-of-view character. His PTSD. His survivor's guilt. His exhaustion. [The Reiner Braun wiki entry](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Reiner_Braun) notes that after returning to Marley, even his mother and cousin remarked he seemed like a completely different person — and this is the version of him we're asked to inhabit.

It's a narrative gamble: force the audience to see the enemy's children as children. Force them to understand that Marley raises its Warrior candidates the same way Paradis raises its soldiers — through propaganda, fear, and carefully manufactured hatred of an outgroup. Isayama has since [admitted that the ending left him feeling a sense of insincerity](https://animehunch.com/attack-on-titans-ending-felt-insincere-admits-hajime-isayama/) — that some of what he wanted to say didn't fully land. The anime's script revisions, most notably a reworked Armin/Eren confrontation where [Armin explicitly condemns the Rumbling's cost](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-finale-fixes-manga-problem-eren-armin/) in a way the manga never delivered, represent his attempt to clarify the argument. But the opening gambit — starting in Marley — was never something that needed fixing. It was the most precisely executed structural choice in the series.

## Gabi Braun Is Eren Yeager

The parallels between Gabi and Eren are not subtle. [Multiple analyses have traced them explicitly](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-gabi-eren-parallel/), and even the visual language of the manga spells it out: in Chapter 106, Gabi ties her hair up with fierce determination in front of a mirror — a panel that deliberately echoes the moment Eren does the same thing. Same posture. Same fire. Same conviction that the monsters on the other side deserve death.

Consider the structural identities. Eren at twelve: his home destroyed, his mother devoured, his entire understanding of the world organized around an enemy he has been taught to hate since birth. Gabi at twelve: raised in Liberio, told that the Eldians of Paradis are devils responsible for the suffering of her people, her entire self-worth measured by how efficiently she can destroy that enemy. A [comparative analysis published in Granthaalayah Journal](https://www.granthaalayahpublication.org/journals/granthaalayah/article/download/6051/5863?inline=1) traces how both characters function as mirrors of each other — same fuel, same tunnel vision, same willingness to sacrifice everything for a cause they didn't choose — arguing that their parallel indoctrinations are structurally central to how the series dramatizes cycles of violence. The essay notes the crucial divergence: Gabi ultimately questions and dismantles her conditioning, while Eren calcifies his into apocalypse.

The difference the fanbase drew was not ideological. It was personal. They loved Eren because they had been with him for three seasons. They hated Gabi because she killed Sasha.

[As Screen Rant's defense of Gabi argues](https://screenrant.com/attack-on-titan-gabi-not-bad-op-ed/), the double standard is glaring: Annie Leonhart — who slaughtered Survey Corps soldiers in cold blood for years — received an almost entirely sympathetic sendoff by the Final Season's close. Gabi, who fired a rifle from a blimp in what she understood as an act of war, became one of the most reviled characters in recent anime fandom. The difference is proximity and duration. Fans had been inside Annie's perspective. They had not been inside Gabi's yet.

## The Empathy Test the Fandom Failed (On Purpose)

Here is what makes the fan response to Gabi not just ironic but structurally instructive: it demonstrates the exact cognitive mechanism the series is trying to dismantle.

[Attack on Titan's political subtext](https://8bitdigi.com/political-interpretations-of-attack-on-titan/) has been examined extensively. Isayama has confirmed that his core intent was to dramatize the way victims become perpetrators — that this "was pretty much there from the beginning." But the ideological point only lands if you can feel it rather than just understand it. A viewer who reads that "hatred is cyclical" as an abstract thesis and agrees with it has not actually confronted their own selective empathy. A viewer who hates Gabi for killing Sasha while cheering Eren on through the Rumbling has demonstrated that mechanism operating inside themselves in real time.

[The Rumbling](https://attackontitan.fandom.com/wiki/Rumbling) — Eren's decision to unleash the Wall Titans and kill eighty percent of the global population — is the act the show asks you to weigh against everything else. [From the Völkerrechtsblog analysis of AoT and genocide law](https://voelkerrechtsblog.org/how-attack-on-titan-complicates-the-genocide-convention/), the Rumbling represents one of fiction's most direct engagements with the ethics of elimination: is the survival of your people ever worth the annihilation of everyone else? Fans who embraced Eren through this — or at least mourned him with genuine grief — while remaining unable to extend any empathy to a twelve-year-old brainwashed soldier have located exactly the border the show is trying to make visible.

## The Cultural Fault Line

The Western/Japanese reception divide sharpens the argument further. [Anime Senpai's reporting on the cultural difference](https://www.animesenpai.net/attack-on-titan-unlike-abroad-gabi-is-loved-in-japan/) documents that Japanese fans largely did not hate Gabi — they responded to her as a child caught in an impossible system, which is how the text presents her. Western fans, particularly in the English-speaking internet spaces on Reddit and Twitter, generated the bulk of the character's toxicity.

This gap is worth sitting with. Isayama has confirmed that one of his inspirations for the series' political landscape was [the history of Japanese imperial militarism](https://www.thedailystar.net/shout/news/historical-allusions-attack-titan-fascist-subtext-3220951) — the machinery that turned ordinary people, including children, into instruments of state violence. Japanese audiences live in closer cultural proximity to that history, to the documented reality of families sending child soldiers into a war built on manufactured conviction. They may read Gabi the way that history is taught in its aftermath: not as a monster but as a casualty of a system. Western audiences, who arrived at the series through entertainment culture rather than inherited national trauma, were faster to process her through the lens of what she did rather than what was done to her.

There's a second factor: parasocial attachment operates at a particular intensity in English-language fandom spaces, and Sasha's death functioned less as narrative event than as personal grievance. The people who posted death threats about Gabi weren't engaging with the story's argument — they were reacting to a loss they experienced as their own. That reaction, whatever its human causes, is precisely what the show is examining: the way personal investment in "our side" makes the violence of "their side" feel qualitatively different even when the mechanism is identical.

As her arc progresses — as she encounters Falco's quiet decency, as she lives among the people she was taught to despise, as she [chooses not to double down the way Eren did](https://gamerant.com/attack-on-titan-gabi-eren-parallel/) — many viewers found their hostility toward her shift into something more complicated. That recalibration is the show working exactly as intended.

## What Gabi Is Actually For

It is tempting to frame Gabi as a "mirror to Eren" and leave it there — the structural foil, the parallel arc, the thematic echo. But her function in the Final Season is more precise than that. Gabi is an empathy audition: a test of whether the viewer has actually absorbed the series' argument about perspective and tribalism, or merely agreed with it in the abstract.

[The anonymous Tumblr essay tracking the War for Paradis arc](https://ackermom.tumblr.com/post/677386907549925376/i-wanted-to-do-a-little-reflection-on-the-war-for) makes the point that the Marley arc exists to set a trap — to make you care about people on the other side before the war fully ignites, so that by the time the Alliance is fighting Eren, there are no clean lines left. Gabi is the sharpest edge of that trap, because she's the hardest character to sympathize with. She's loud, she's zealous, and she shoots the character everyone loves.

But she is also a child who was handed her convictions before she could have formed any of her own. That is the same sentence you would write about Eren Yeager in Season 1. The question the show is asking — and most fans' reactions have already answered — is whether you apply that sentence consistently or only when you already love the person it describes.

Gabi needed no revision. The audience's response to her was precisely calibrated. The hatred was the point.

## The Cycle, Embodied

What's left when you put Gabi and Eren side by side and ask who deserves your sympathy more is not a rhetorical question. It's the exact question Isayama built the entire Final Season around.

Eren killed eighty percent of the world's population and [won nominations for both Best Protagonist and Best Antagonist](https://medium.com/@zeliche/eren-yeager-deep-character-analysis-hero-villain-or-victim-of-fate-38e17ab3ab37) in the same Crunchyroll Anime Awards cycle — ultimately taking the antagonist prize. The fandom couldn't agree on what he was because the show refused to make that agreement easy. Gabi, by contrast, had her moral category decided for her by a fanbase that needed someone uncomplicated to resent.

That asymmetry doesn't mean the Final Season failed to make its case. It means the case was made on the audience's skin rather than on the screen. The series' thesis about how indoctrinated children become enemies, how empathy operates selectively along tribal lines, how the cycle of violence reproduces itself through exactly the kind of passionate certainty Eren and Gabi both embody — all of that was demonstrated live, in real time, by the people watching it.

You were supposed to hate Gabi. Not because hating her was right. Because hating her, and not noticing you were doing the same thing to her that Marley did — deciding she was a devil based on where she came from and who she hurt — was exactly the mechanism the show had been diagnosing for four seasons.

The show ended. The comments section is still going.
