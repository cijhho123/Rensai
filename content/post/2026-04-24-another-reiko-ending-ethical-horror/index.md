The moment Kouichi drives a pickaxe into his aunt, PA Works' grey aesthetic wrings everything it can from the act. The music swells. Kouichi weeps. Reiko smiles — suddenly remembering who she is, what she was, that she had already died — and then she is gone. The curse shatters. Class 3-3 survives another year. Roll credits.

The show presents this as tragedy. A necessary evil. The only way. I want to argue it's actually the show confessing its own ethical collapse — and doing so without realizing it.

![Another (2012) key visual](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/anime/6/74879.jpg)

## What the "Another" Actually Is

Before discussing the ending, let's be precise about the mechanics. Each year, the [curse of Class 3-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)) inserts a dead student or faculty member back into the living classroom. This person appears fully human, carries memories and relationships, attends class, goes home to family. Crucially, they have no awareness that anything is wrong. They believe themselves alive because, from the inside, they experience everything a living person experiences.

The Another is not a ghost in the traditional J-horror sense — not a revenant filled with resentment, not a specter with unfinished business, not something you can reason into peace. They are not malevolent. They [haven't chosen their condition](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Another_Novel). They wake up every morning and live what they sincerely believe is their life. The horror the series codes into this concept is not supernatural dread but something closer to epistemological tragedy: a person can exist, believe they exist, and be completely wrong.

This is a genuinely rich premise. The question it raises — how do you weigh the claim of someone who insists they are alive when they are not? — has no clean answer. [Fan discourse](https://drunkenanimeblog.com/2018/07/23/reiko-and-the-dilemma-of-the-extra/) has engaged this directly. The Drunken Anime Blog's analysis of Reiko's case puts it plainly: "the fundamental dilemma of the 'solution' is that there's no tangible way to differentiate the Another from a person, and the Another themselves think they're alive. In terms of their own actions, the Another has done nothing wrong."

The show sets up a moral puzzle without a moral answer. And then it gives one anyway.

## The Curse's Original Sin

To understand why the ending is ethically bankrupt, you need to remember what caused the curse in the first place. In 1972, [a student named Misaki died mid-year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Another_(novel)). The class, unable to accept the loss, collectively agreed to pretend he was still alive. They left his desk intact, spoke to his empty chair, included him in the graduation photo. They refused, as a group, to let someone be dead.

The curse is the supernatural consequence of that refusal. Yomiyama turns the class's denial into literal reality: every year, a dead person is actually inserted back into the class, and everyone's memories adjust to accommodate them. The horror is rooted in the act of insisting someone exists when they cannot — of overriding the finality of death through collective will.

This is not subtle. Another is quite deliberately a story about what happens when you refuse to acknowledge death. The original class was so devastated by loss that they decided, together, to simply not accept it. The curse is that choice given teeth.

## Reiko

In the final revelation, Kouichi learns that his aunt Reiko — the woman who raised him after his mother's death, who lives in the same house, who has shared meals and conversations and grief with him — is the "Another" for this year's class. She is the dead person who has been reinserted. She died years ago and doesn't know it.

The show handles this reveal with genuine emotional craft. We have spent twelve episodes with Reiko. [She's warm, tired, faintly melancholic](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/Reiko_Mikami) — a woman still processing old grief while managing a difficult homelife. We like her. The audience has built a relationship with her the same way Kouichi has. When the reveal lands, it hurts precisely because the show has made her real to us.

But now examine what happens next. Kouichi kills her. With a pickaxe. This is presented as the solution — the only way to break the curse — and the show frames it as an act of tragic necessity, the kind of burden heroes carry. [Episode 12](https://randomc.net/2012/03/26/another-12-end/) is the emotional climax of the series, and that climax is Kouichi doing something terrible to someone he loves, and being right to do it.

Is he? Is it right?

## The Ethical Blind Spot

Reiko did not choose to come back. She did not petition some curse-mechanism for a second life, did not claw her way back through supernatural ambition. She was inserted, against her will (insofar as she had will), into a situation she cannot perceive or escape. She believes she is alive because the curse gave her that belief. She has no defense against the accusation — "you are the Another" — because she cannot understand it.

The show's logic runs: the curse must be broken; breaking the curse requires killing the Another; therefore killing the Another is the right thing to do. This is a utilitarian calculus, and Another accepts it without examination. The class's survival requires Reiko's death. Q.E.D.

But notice what this calculus does. It takes a person who believes they are alive, who has done nothing wrong, and assigns their continued existence as the cost that others must pay. It says: your existence is the problem, and we are the ones who will solve it. This is precisely what the original 1972 class did to dead Misaki — they insisted his non-existence was unacceptable and overwrote it with collective will. The curse is the same logic reversed: now we insist that this person's existence is unacceptable, and we overwrite it with violence.

The mechanism is identical. The direction is flipped. The show doesn't notice.

## What Fan Discourse Found

The Drunken Anime Blog piece, which engages the Reiko dilemma directly, identifies the core problem: the Another's culpability, or total lack of it. But there's a further wrinkle that even that analysis glances past. The [Another Wiki's entry on the curse's mechanics](https://another.fandom.com/wiki/The_Curse) specifies that the Another cannot be told the truth — informing them of their status triggers amnesia, as if the curse actively protects its own stability. The show treats this as a practical obstacle. It is actually the most damning detail: the Another cannot be reasoned with, appealed to, or given a choice. The only available option is killing someone who will never consent to their own death because they cannot be made to understand it. The show presents this as the tragedy. What it actually is, is the elimination of all possible ethical alternatives — a narrative structure that forces the audience to accept murder as the only option by removing every route to a different one.

The [Anime Herald's survey of horror anime](https://www.animeherald.com/2023/09/09/the-big-three-of-horror-anime-the-indelible-legacy-of-shiki-another-and-when-they-cry/) positions Another alongside Shiki and When They Cry as foundational to the genre. Shiki is worth the comparison in detail. When Shiki's humans begin hunting and staking the Shiki (vampires), the show depicts it as a mob action: torches, pitchforks, one character burning the village while laughing. The young female Shiki Sunako — sympathetic throughout — is killed in sunlight while trying to escape. Shiki frames its resolution as collective violence, makes the audience feel the horror of a community turning on something different from itself, and refuses to let the human characters off morally. Another's humans are weeping and noble. Shiki holds the contradiction in tension. Another dissolves it.

What [CBR's analysis of Another's visual language](https://www.cbr.com/another-stylistic-elements-make-best-horror-anime/) identifies as PA Works' signature — characters designed to resemble "life-like dolls," slightly wrong in their movements, drained of the warmth of natural color — is usually read as horror atmosphere. But it applies to everyone in the show, not just the Another. Every character moves like something not quite real. If the aesthetic was meant to signal "this person is the dead one," it fails completely. If it was meant to suggest that everyone in Yomiyama is somehow compromised by proximity to the curse — that the entire class exists in a kind of moral unreality — then the direction is doing something more honest than the screenplay acknowledges.

## The Resolution Resolves Nothing

Here's the sharpest version of my argument: Another's solution breaks the curse without answering the curse's question.

The curse asked: when a community refuses to accept death, what happens? The answer it provides is: the dead come back, and people get killed by falling umbrellas. But the question underneath — what does the community owe someone it has refused to let go? — goes untouched. Killing Reiko doesn't answer it. It just terminates the case.

[Critics have noted](https://anime-ashoka.medium.com/another-the-anime-thats-redefining-the-horror-genre-4dc624d862da) that Another's first half is significantly stronger than its second, with the shift from psychological dread to [Final Destination-style gore sequences](https://www.slashfilm.com/570174/another-anime/) representing a tonal surrender. This is true, but the deeper surrender is the ending's philosophical one. The show had assembled all the pieces to say something genuine about collective guilt, about the community's responsibility toward those it has pushed out, about whether survival justifies the cost. Instead it delivers a pickaxe and a tearful goodbye.

I don't think the show earned that goodbye. Reiko wept and smiled because the narrative needed a cathartic beat. But she hadn't consented to being brought back, hadn't chosen to be the thing that had to die, and was killed by someone who loved her because she was inconvenient to the living. If that sounds harsh: it should. Horror that wants to be taken seriously has to take its own premises seriously.

## J-Horror and the Right to Remain

Classic J-horror gives its dead a reason to linger. [Sadako and Kayako](https://www.cbr.com/ring-the-grudge-sadako-kayako-deadliest-ghost/) are malevolent because they were wronged — their anger is a claim on the living, a demand that the injustice be acknowledged. They are threatening, but they have standing. You can understand why they are here even as you run from them.

Another's Another has no such claim. Reiko isn't angry. She isn't haunting anyone. She's just alive — or thinks she is — and that's the problem. Japanese horror has a complicated relationship with the line between the living and the dead, and Another sits at an interesting position in that tradition: a ghost story where the ghost is the most sympathetic figure, and the horror is that everyone agrees she has to go.

What the show never asks is whether that agreement is moral or just pragmatic. Whether "the curse demands it" is an ethical justification or just a description of the situation. Whether Kouichi's guilt, which the ending aestheticizes so carefully, was the right feeling to have — and whether it was sufficient.

It wasn't. But the show doesn't know that. And that, finally, is where Another's real horror lives: not in the [umbrella impalement](https://screenrant.com/another-most-disturbing-death-scenes-ranked/), not in the paranoia, not in the creaking corridors of the school. It lives in the ending's certainty that Kouichi did what had to be done, and its total comfort with that certainty.
