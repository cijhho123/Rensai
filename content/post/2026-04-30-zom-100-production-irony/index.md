In the first episode of Zom 100: Bucket List of the Dead, protagonist Akira Tendo spends three years getting ground into nothing by a company called ZLM — a studio with a logo nearly identical to OLM, Inc. (the Pokémon animation studio), housed in a building modeled directly on OLM's actual office. The satire is not subtle. [According to Epicstream's reporting](https://epicstream.com/article/zom-100-animators-attack-olm-easter-egg), the animators behind the show were former OLM employees who had recently left to form BUG FILMS, a new studio built specifically to do things differently. They embedded a mockery of their previous employer into the very first scene of their debut production.

Three months later, Zom 100's final three episodes were on indefinite hiatus. The studio couldn't finish its own show.

The irony here is almost too neat to be real. Zom 100 is explicitly, deliberately, proudly a critique of Japan's "black company" culture — the [karoshi-adjacent world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zom_100:_Bucket_List_of_the_Dead) of mandatory overtime, soul-crushing expectations, and employees treated as disposable machinery. Its animators staged an open revolt against their former workplace by inserting it into the show as a site of dystopian labor exploitation. Then the production collapsed under exactly the kind of crunch the show was criticizing. There is a lesson in here about the anime industry's structural conditions that no one in power seems particularly interested in learning.

## Where the Manga Comes From

Haro Aso, who also created Alice in Borderland, conceived Zom 100 from a specific, personal observation. [In his Variety interview](https://variety.com/2023/tv/news/aso-haro-interview-zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-zombies-1235479562/), he describes listening to a friend — an office worker — complain about his job over drinks, and thinking: "If this is the case, I'd still rather be a zombie." From that moment came the premise: what if the zombie apocalypse was not the disaster but the liberation? What if the end of civilization was the first time someone like Akira Tendo had been allowed to breathe?

The [Wikipedia entry for the series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zom_100:_Bucket_List_of_the_Dead) describes it as serialized in Shogakukan's Weekly Big Comic Spirits since 2018, written by Aso and illustrated by Kotaro Takata. The manga ran until 2024, completing its story while the anime was still mid-collapse. By 2023, it had acquired a devoted enough readership for Crunchyroll to greenlight an anime adaptation, with BUG FILMS — founded in September 2021 by Hiroaki Kojima, a former OLM producer — taking the production.

The setup Aso builds is deliberately simple: Akira is 24, three years out of college, working at a production company where the hours are inhuman and the management is worse. He has given up on everything — his friendships, his hobbies, a woman he liked — in service of a job that returns nothing. He is, as [Incluvie's analysis puts it](https://www.incluvie.com/articles/zom-100-critiques-predatory-capitalism), a zombie before the zombies arrive. When civilization collapses on the morning of episode one, he does not feel horror. He feels joy.

The genius of this premise is that it inverts the usual zombie metaphor. George Romero used zombies to represent consumerist groupthink — the mindless herd. Aso uses them to represent liberation from it. [Japan Powered's examination of the show](https://www.japanpowered.com/anime-articles/zom-100-work) describes the "black company" concept that structures Akira's pre-apocalypse life: a workplace where unpaid overtime is mandatory, bosses function as petty tyrants, and health — mental or physical — is irrelevant. The zombies don't end Akira's suffering. They just remove the last remaining obstacle to his noticing it.

## BUG FILMS and the OLM Easter Egg

The choice to rename ZLM and model the building on OLM's actual office was not in the manga. [Game Rant's explainer on BUG FILMS](https://gamerant.com/bug-films-studio-zom-100/) notes the studio's animators came substantially from OLM, where conditions had become notoriously difficult — franchise overload, schedules described as nightmarish, and the kind of production-line mentality that leaves no room for creative risk. They left to build something new. Hiroaki Kojima's stated founding philosophy was to focus on "works that people found valuable" rather than output volume.

Then they made Zom 100's production team insert a caricature of their old studio into the show's villain company. The easter egg [Screen Rant called "brutal"](https://screenrant.com/zom-100-roasts-pokmon-anime-studio-crunchyroll/): a logo nearly indistinguishable from OLM's, a building that replicates OLM's office building, the whole package designed to be legible to anyone in the industry. Whether it was cathartic, satirical, or both, it was unmistakably deliberate. These were people saying something specific about the place they had come from.

The problem is that systems don't change just because individuals change studios. They carry the industry's structural problems with them.

## What Happened Next

Episode four of Zom 100 aired July 30, 2023, in Japan. [ANN reported](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-08-24/zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-anime-6th-episode-delayed-on-some-streaming-services/.201585) that episode six was delayed by 24 hours on Crunchyroll, Netflix, and Hulu due to "production circumstances." Episode five was pushed back a week earlier for similar reasons. These are the standard euphemisms: the show is not done, and no one wants to say why.

By late September, [Screen Rant confirmed](https://screenrant.com/zom-100-anime-indefinite-hiatus-after-delays/) that episodes ten through twelve were on indefinite hiatus — the studio could not commit to release dates. Studio Shaft was reportedly brought in to help stabilize the production, [as Epicstream noted](https://epicstream.com/article/zom-100-shaft-reportedly-leaves-as-production-issues-mount), before eventually departing the project. Three episodes of a twelve-episode series were held back for months. [ComicBook.com reported the indefinite delay](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/zom-100-anime-indefinite-hiatus/) in matter-of-fact terms, but the fan reaction — documented across threads and in [The Mary Sue's piece on discovering the show mid-collapse](https://www.themarysue.com/i-discovered-my-favorite-anime-of-the-summer-right-as-it-was-slammed-with-production-delays/) — was a mixture of frustration and grim recognition.

Episodes ten through twelve eventually aired as a Christmas package on December 25, 2023 — [Screen Rant's report on the return](https://screenrant.com/zom-100-new-episodes-return-christmas-crunchyroll/) made it sound like an announcement at a celebration, but the reality was that the show had simply been unable to maintain a weekly schedule. A first-season anime airing its final three episodes as a holiday bundle, months after the broadcast window, is not a success story.

## The Structural Argument

[Mechanical Anime Reviews' sharp piece on the production](https://mechanicalanimereviews.com/2023/09/15/the-production-farce-of-zom-100/) makes the structural case plainly: "the show suffers from the forced labor that the show is thematically against." Zom 100 was BUG FILMS's first full series production. The studio had done in-between animation on other projects, but had never managed a complete cour of weekly television. The industry's entry-level expectations — twelve episodes, weekly broadcast, simultaneous streaming across multiple platforms — are designed around studios that have existing infrastructure. A two-year-old studio of twenty-one people, however talented, was building that infrastructure while also producing the show.

The Sakuga Blog's production notes, [cited in industry coverage around the premiere](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2023/07/14/zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-production-notes-01/), praised director Kazuki Kawagoe and the creative team effusively for what they accomplished in episode one — calling the premiere brilliant while also noting the structural darkness of BUG FILMS's position. Kawagoe came from a background of episode direction on lower-profile productions; his talent had, as the notes framed it, been constrained by the kind of long-running franchise work OLM specialized in. BUG FILMS was supposed to free that talent. The system had other plans.

[The AV Club's piece on Zom 100 and contemporary labor politics](https://www.avclub.com/zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-anime-netflix-labor-strike-similarities) made the connection to the post-pandemic "Great Resignation" — the moment when workers across industries started calculating whether the deal was worth it. Akira's extreme circumstance (it takes literal zombie apocalypse to free him) is, the piece argues, a commentary on how thoroughly social and economic pressure can trap someone even when they know the trap exists. The irony is that the animators who built BUG FILMS were doing exactly what the show recommends: leaving the bad situation and building something better. The industry didn't let them.

## What the Irony Actually Reveals

It would be easy to frame the Zom 100 production collapse as a story about one studio's misfortune, an underdog that bit off more than it could chew. That reading is too kind. BUG FILMS's failure was not idiosyncratic — it was structural. [Dexerto's breakdown of why the show kept getting delayed](https://www.dexerto.com/anime/why-is-zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-on-hiatus-2310253/) traces it to the fundamental misalignment between weekly broadcast demands and realistic production capacity, a misalignment that afflicts far more established studios than BUG FILMS. Zom 100 was simply the case where the irony was maximally visible because the show itself had something specific to say about labor exploitation.

The anime industry's production model — simultaneous worldwide streaming across Netflix, Crunchyroll, and Hulu; weekly episode requirements; global promotional pressure — is built for studios large enough to weather disruption. [ANN's examination of anime delays as a 2023 phenomenon](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2023-08-24/zom-100-bucket-list-of-the-dead-anime-6th-episode-delayed-on-some-streaming-services/.201585) noted that Zom 100 was far from the only show in summer 2023 struggling with the demands of same-season delivery. The production pipeline that exists primarily to serve streaming platforms' release schedules does not bend for studios that are simultaneously building their organizational infrastructure and trying to maintain broadcast quality. BUG FILMS was trying to build something different. The system absorbed them.

The ZLM logo still appears in episode one. The building is still modeled on OLM's office. The satire is still there, embedded, specific, signed by people who knew what they were critiquing. But finishing twelve episodes in twenty-six weeks while maintaining the quality of that premiere turned out to require exactly the conditions — the crunch, the overextension, the gap between what was promised and what could be delivered — that the show was supposed to be indicting.

Haro Aso wanted to say something about what modern Japan does to people who give too much to a job. The production of Zom 100 ended up saying the same thing, just differently, and mostly by accident.
