The most quietly devastating thing about [Call of the Night Season 2](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2025/5/15/call-of-the-night-season-2-anime-release-date-key-visual-haru-nanakusa-cast) is not what it reveals about Nazuna. It's that Nazuna doesn't know any of it.

The [Cigarette Case Arc](https://animecorner.me/call-of-the-night-season-2-prepares-for-cigarette-case-arc-with-new-visual-maaya-uchida-joins-cast/), which comprises the bulk of Season 2, is nominally about Ko Yamori and Nazuna Nanakusa continuing their project — he wants to fall in love with her so she can turn him into a vampire; she wants to make him love her for reasons she doesn't fully understand yet. But the arc's real subject is the backstory the series had been withholding since its first chapter: what Nazuna is, who her mother was, and why neither fact is something she has access to.

In the episodes covering Kabura Honda's flashbacks, the series reveals: [Nazuna was born, not turned](https://gamerant.com/call-of-the-night-truth-about-nanakusa-nazuna/). She is the daughter of Haru Nanakusa, a vampire who believed that conceiving a child with a human could transform her back into mortality. Haru tried it. She had Nazuna. She stopped drinking blood — out of love, or belief, or both — and died. Nazuna grew up without her, raised loosely by Kabura (who was in love with Haru and had to carry the grief of that while also raising Haru's daughter, who looks exactly like her).

And Nazuna remembers none of it. She has amnesia about her origins. She woke up in her apartment one day, already a vampire, with no template for what she is or where she came from.

## The Season 1 Reading, Revised

In [Season 1](https://myanimelist.net/anime/50346/Yofukashi_no_Uta), Nazuna reads as deliberate. Her emotional distance has the quality of a choice: the professional cuddling arrangements, the blood-drinking as transaction, the condition she placed on Ko's transformation (he has to genuinely love her — not infatuation, not need, but love). She seemed like a predator who had learned to create distance because closeness was dangerous, or a loner who had simply decided intimacy wasn't worth the complication.

Season 2 collapses that reading. If Nazuna has never known who she is, if she grew up without her mother, if she is a born vampire with no human period and no memory of her earliest years — then her emotional inexperience is not a defense mechanism. It is a condition of origin. She doesn't know how to love because she has never had a model for it. The series establishes, by the end of the Kabura flashbacks, that the closest relationship Nazuna ever had was with a woman who was simultaneously grieving someone else and who looked at Nazuna and saw the person she had lost.

That is the parental template Nazuna grew up with, if she grew up with anything: a godmother who loved her at a remove, through the face of someone she'd lost.

## Haru's Theory and Its Cost

[Haru Nanakusa's belief](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/call-night-season-2-episode-5-kabura-s-past-reveals-promise-haru-nazuna-s-origins-anko-picks-next-target) — that conceiving a child with a human could make her human again — is not presented as wisdom. It is presented as a theory held desperately, by someone who wanted something badly enough to gamble on it. She fell in love with a human man. She stopped drinking blood. She had Nazuna. And whatever she was hoping would happen, it did not — she died.

The show does not say exactly what took her. Only that her choice to stop drinking blood, combined with her feelings for Nazuna's father, eventually cost her her existence. She left Kabura a letter asking her to look after her daughter, and then she was gone.

The irony the series sets up — though it does so quietly, without underlining it — is structural and exact. [The love condition Nazuna imposed on Ko](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Nazuna_Nanakusa) (fall in love with her and she'll turn him into a vampire) is functionally the same theory her mother tried. Love as a threshold. Love as the thing that transforms you from one state into another. Love as the mechanism that lets you cross the divide between human and vampire, in one direction or the other.

Haru tried to use love to cross from vampire to human. Nazuna is trying to use Ko's love to carry him from human to vampire. Both are treating love as a transformation vector, as something that can be earned and then applied like a key to a lock. Both are wrong about what love is. And both are doing it without fully understanding the stakes.

What [Kotoyama's series argues love actually is](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) — something the manga's final arc made explicit — is that it doesn't arrive through engineering. It accumulates sideways, through presence and time and the specific texture of nights shared, and by the time you try to measure whether it qualifies, it has already changed the question. The love condition treats love as a destination; the series treats it as something you're already inside before you notice. This is why the transaction structure always fails: you cannot manufacture the thing that invalidates the transaction.

The difference is that Haru knew she was running an experiment. Nazuna doesn't know she's repeating one.

## What Kabura Carries

The character who reveals the most about this dynamic is not Nazuna. It is [Kabura Honda](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kabura_Honda).

Kabura was turned by Haru. Kabura was in love with Haru. When Haru got pregnant and then died and then sent a letter from a direction Kabura hadn't expected, asking her to look after her daughter, Kabura became a godmother to the child of the person she loved most, who had chosen a human over her, gotten everything she wanted from that choice, and died anyway.

The Kabura scenes in Season 2 do not ask for sympathy in the way that flashback exposition usually does — they do not score themselves dramatically or dwell on her suffering. What they achieve instead is the sense of what a life built around grief looks like from the inside: functional, persistent, and shot through with an absence that never resolved itself. Every time Kabura looks at Nazuna — who looks exactly like Haru — she is looking at the evidence of everything she lost.

This is the family Nazuna came from, even without knowing it. Not dysfunction, exactly. Something stranger: a web of love and loss so tangled that the person at its center (Nazuna) couldn't receive any of it clearly even if she had the memory to access it.

The [fan discussion around Season 2](https://x.com/Chinatsu_BB/status/1951535418698244469) kept returning to this element — the Haru/Kabura relationship and how it recontextualizes Kabura's sometimes cold demeanor toward Nazuna in Season 1. What read as mild antagonism is grief redirected. Kabura has Haru's face to look at every time she sees Nazuna, and has had it for however long Nazuna has been alive.

## The Amnesia Is the Point

The fact that Nazuna has no memory of any of this is not a plot convenience. It is the series' central formal argument about its own premise.

[Nazuna woke up one day in an apartment with no memory of her past](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/call-night-season-2-episode-4-kabura-s-past-reveals-new-face-likely-key-nazuna-s-origins), already a vampire, and started building a life from scratch. The professional cuddling. The blood economy. The elaborate indirection around intimacy. These are not the behaviors of someone who chose distance — they are the behaviors of someone who had no template for closeness and improvised something functional in its place.

And the love condition — which Season 1 presents as Nazuna's gambit, her safeguard — becomes something stranger under this light. She set a condition requiring that Ko love her. But she didn't know what love looked like. Her model for it was Kabura's grief, which is love expressed through absence and substitution. Her model was her own situation as someone whose mother loved her enough to arrange guardianship by letter, which is love expressed through bureaucratic self-removal.

The condition she imposed is the one she is least equipped to recognize being fulfilled, because the love she's seen is always mediated through loss, distance, and proxy. She can't receive it directly even when it arrives, because her only templates for it are structured around someone being gone.

This does not entirely invalidate the Season 1 reading of Nazuna — she is deliberately maintaining distance, she is running a professional transaction structure, and she is doing it consciously within the series' established frame. What Season 2 changes is the source of that behavior. What looked like a choice built on experience is revealed as a pattern built on absence. The behaviors are the same; the explanation for them is completely different.

## Anko, Watching

The other piece Season 2 adds is [Anko Uguisu's backstory](https://www.sportskeeda.com/anime/call-night-season-2-episode-8-nazuna-makes-offer-tragedy-strikes-meijiro-family-anko-uguisu-born) — and it is not the background the series seemed to be promising.

Anko (real name Kyouko Mejiro) was [Nazuna's closest friend in high school](https://call-of-the-night.fandom.com/wiki/Kyouko_Mejiro). They were each other's only friends. On Kyouko's eighteenth birthday, Nazuna offered to turn her — to make her into a vampire offspring, as a way of helping with the emotional turmoil she was in. And on that same night, Kyouko's father — who had been turned into a vampire by someone else — attacked and killed her mother. Kyouko killed him in self-defense.

The timing is unbearable. Nazuna's offer came on the same night vampirism destroyed Kyouko's family. [She left Nazuna after a short exchange](https://www.dropintomanga.com/post/719406679758897152/anko-uguisu-past-finding-peace-night), dedicated the next decade to eradicating vampires, and became Anko Uguisu — a professional identity built entirely out of her worst night.

What this makes of Anko's presence in the current story is something more specific than "structural mirror." She was almost Ko. She was the person standing at the threshold of vampirism for someone she cared about, and then that threshold became the mechanism of her family's destruction. She is not watching Ko and Nazuna from a safe analytical distance — she is watching someone else do the thing she almost did, and she cannot decide whether to stop it or let it play out, because the person she would be stopping it to protect is the person she also still cares about.

[The Season 2 Cigarette Case Arc](https://anitrendz.net/news/2025/05/15/call-of-the-night-season-2-reveals-cigarette-case-arc-visual-maaya-uchida-casting-july-4-premiere/) builds this structure of tragic rhyme deliberately: every character who surrounds Ko and Nazuna's central relationship is carrying a version of the same story. Love across the vampire/human divide. An attempt at transformation. A cost that someone paid.

This is not the series saying Ko and Nazuna will fail. It is saying that the stakes are real, that the history of this specific kind of attempt is one of failure and grief, and that the two people at the center of the series are doing it without full knowledge of that history. Nazuna cannot access the history. Ko doesn't know enough to understand what he's asking for. Anko knows exactly what it cost and cannot stop watching.

## The Condition Rewired

What [Kotoyama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_of_the_Night) has done across these two seasons is something more structurally sophisticated than the setup-and-payoff of a typical romcom. The love condition was introduced in Season 1 as a rule of the world — this is how vampire transformation works, this is what Ko and Nazuna have to accomplish. By Season 2, the condition has been revealed as a family inheritance that Nazuna enacted without knowing it was one, a theory her mother held about transformation that killed her mother, and a threshold that everyone around the central relationship has evidence is more dangerous than it looks.

The [Season 2 ending](https://pinnedupink.com/blogs/the-cel-block-anime-reviews-analysis-for-the-gen-x-fan/call-of-the-night-season-2-review-story-characters-and-what-s-next) doesn't resolve this — Ko and Nazuna are still in the middle of it. But the revelation of Haru's story changes what "the middle" means. This is no longer just a romance about two people with an unusual shared project. It's a romance in which one of the two people is unknowingly repeating her mother's fatal experiment on a different person's behalf.

Whether Nazuna ever learns this — whether she ever gets access to who she came from, what Haru tried to do, why Kabura looks at her the way she does — is the question Season 2 leaves open. What it does not leave open is whether the history exists. It does. It is just that Nazuna can't see it, and Ko doesn't know to look.

That is the shape of the series now: two people in the dark, running an experiment that has already been run once, trying to find something that has already cost someone everything.
