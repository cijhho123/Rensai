The Human Instrumentality Project is not a villain's plan. That's the thing most readings of *The End of Evangelion* get slightly wrong. It is, by any honest philosophical accounting, a coherent solution to the problem of human suffering — and the film's entire moral weight rests on the fact that Hideaki Anno knew this, and chose to reject it anyway.

![End of Evangelion poster](https://myanimelist.net/images/anime/1404/98182l.jpg)

## What AT Fields Actually Are

In the show's mythology, AT Fields are typically glossed as "energy barriers" — the thing Angels have, the thing Evangelions can cancel out. But the Evangelion universe is consistent about what they actually represent at a deeper level: [the metaphysical barrier between self and other](https://wiki.evageeks.org/AT_Field), the thing that keeps one consciousness from dissolving into another. They're the walls of individual identity. Every human being has one. It's what makes you *you* rather than an undifferentiated piece of the collective.

The problem — and this is where Anno is doing something philosophically serious — is that those walls are also the source of all human loneliness. You cannot fully know another person. You cannot fully be known. The gap between self and other is permanent and unbridgeable, and every human relationship is an attempt to communicate across a void that cannot be closed. Gendo Ikari retreated into work to avoid this. Asuka built an armored persona to make it irrelevant. Rei, who barely had an AT Field to begin with, barely had a self at all.

The Human Instrumentality Project's core proposal is simple: dissolve the walls. Merge all human souls into a singular undifferentiated consciousness. No more loneliness, because there is no longer anyone to be lonely. The problem is solved by eliminating the thing that has the problem.

## The Instrumentality Sequence as Honest Temptation

What makes *The End of Evangelion* philosophically serious — and what separates it from the kind of anime that just uses "psychological" as an aesthetic — is that it doesn't stack the deck against Instrumentality. The visions Shinji experiences inside the Instrumentality sequence aren't obviously false or grotesque. They're gentle. There's a version of his life where Rei is a classmate who likes him, where Misato is an ordinary woman who smiles at him, where things are simple and warm and nobody is in an Evangelion.

Anno presents this version of happiness with real tenderness, which is what makes Shinji's rejection of it land as a genuine choice rather than a heroic reflex. He's not choosing suffering over comfort because suffering builds character. He's choosing something harder to articulate: [he wants these people as they actually are](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Human_Instrumentality_Project), not as projections of his own desire. He wants Rei to be Rei, even the parts of Rei he doesn't understand, even the version of Rei that looked at him with oceanic indifference and pushed Instrumentality forward herself. The merged version isn't the real one. The real one is separate from him, and he cannot have her any other way.

This is a distinction that sounds simple and isn't. What Shinji is rejecting is the logic that says the purpose of other people is the feelings they produce in you. Instrumentality is the ultimate expression of that logic — it literally absorbs other people into your emotional interior. His "no" is a refusal to treat other people as instruments of his own longing, even at the cost of never truly reaching them.

## The Strangling Scene and the Thesis

The strangling scene — Shinji with his hands around a catatonic Asuka's throat in Unit-02's cockpit — is frequently misread as evidence of Anno's contempt for his audience or his protagonist. It's neither. It's the moment the film's argument crystallizes.

Shinji has just rejected Instrumentality. He has chosen the world of separate selves, which means he has chosen to exist next to Asuka as she actually is: a person who spent the entirety of the film's first act catatonic from psychological collapse, whose body was torn apart by Mass Production Evas while she screamed a dying rally cry, who cannot be reached and cannot reach him. He grabs her throat because he has no idea what to do with the terror of her being there, being real, being other. He is trying to confirm that she exists — and that his existence, which required her non-absorption, was worth the cost she paid.

When she lifts her hand and touches his face, he releases her and breaks into sobs. This is not a tender moment in any comfortable sense. It is the experience of contact across the unbridgeable gap: the most you can actually have with another person, which is still almost nothing. Her final line — *kimochi warui*, "I feel sick" or "this is disgusting" — is [not scripted contempt](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Kimochi_Warui). Voice actress Yuko Miyamura has described it as emerging from the actual emotional texture of the scene, a genuine response to the situation rather than a planned line. It is the sound of being touched by another person's mess and finding it repellent and human and real all at once.

Anno ends the film there. Not on a note of hope, not on reconciliation, but on *kimochi warui*: the specific, irreducible feeling of being in the presence of another person who is not you, whose existence you cannot control, whose responses you cannot predict. It's the exact opposite of what Instrumentality offered.

## Why Anno Says Yes to the Mess

Anno's stated intent was to "convey the difficulty of these feelings" — not to offer a resolution to them, but to make their weight felt. *The End of Evangelion* was made while he was working through clinical depression, and the film is deeply marked by that context: the flatness of Shinji's early scenes, the sense that even catastrophe fails to produce feeling, the way the film keeps framing apocalypse from the perspective of someone who cannot quite locate himself in it.

The depression matters because depression is, among other things, a condition in which other people stop feeling real. They're present but unreachable. The AT Field becomes absolute. And what Anno is documenting, through the film's catastrophic journey to that final shoreline, is the process of finding other people real again — finding them real enough to choose, real enough to be repelled by, real enough to grieve.

The film offers no promise that the world Shinji re-enters is a good one or that connection will come. [The production notes make clear](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff) that only Shinji and Asuka have returned to physical form; what the rest of humanity's choice looks like remains open. What the film promises is only that individual existence — with all its loneliness, its opacity, its kimochi warui — is the necessary condition for any genuine encounter with another person.

The AT Field is not the enemy. It is the price of admission.
