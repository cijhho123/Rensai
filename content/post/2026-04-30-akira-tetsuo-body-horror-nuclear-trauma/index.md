In 1945, the American occupation of Japan imposed a censorship policy called the "Press Code." [Among its prohibitions: any discussion of the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki that could be construed as critical of the United States](https://theconversation.com/the-little-known-history-of-secrecy-and-censorship-in-wake-of-atomic-bombings-45213). The experience of the hibakusha — the survivors — was classified. Their testimonies were suppressed. [Medical reports and scientific documentation collected by Japanese professionals were confiscated by American officers and remained classified for years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Censorship_Detachment). Japan was not permitted to look directly at what had happened to it.

By 1988, when Katsuhiro Otomo released [Akira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akira_(1988_film)), the Press Code was forty years in the past. But the trauma it had suppressed doesn't disappear when the policy ends. It finds other containers. It migrates into other forms. And what Otomo did with his ¥1.1 billion production — the [most expensive Japanese animated film of its era](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0094625/) — was construct a container so visually overwhelming that audiences couldn't look away from what they'd spent decades being told they couldn't see.

Tetsuo Shima's body horror is that container.

## What the Critics Usually Say

The critical consensus on Akira runs something like this: it is a landmark of animated cinema, a technical achievement, a gateway to Western anime fandom, and a film saturated with nuclear allegory. Tetsuo's mutations symbolize the dangers of uncontrolled power. Neo-Tokyo represents Japan's fraught relationship with modernization. The espers — the child psychics — embody the legacy of nuclear experimentation. All of this is true. None of it is quite precise enough.

[The standard nuclear allegory reading](https://lwlies.com/long-read/akira-and-the-spectre-of-nuclear-war) treats Akira as a political film that smuggles critique inside science fiction. That reading is correct but incomplete. What it doesn't account for is why the film's body horror sequences feel so specifically correct — why Tetsuo's flesh expanding beyond his control, his tumors overwhelming him, his body absorbing everything around it, reads not like symbolism but like documentation.

The answer is that Otomo wasn't writing allegory. He was writing experience.

## The Keloid Logic

[The hibakusha of Hiroshima and Nagasaki developed keloid scars](https://hpmmuseum.jp/modules/exhibition/index.php?action=DocumentView&document_id=479&lang=eng) — thick, raised, disfiguring tissue that grew at wound sites and continued growing beyond normal boundaries. Unlike ordinary scar tissue, [keloids expand past the original injury, appearing in 50 to 60 percent of those exposed to thermal heat rays within two kilometers of the hypocenter](https://www.hiroshimapeacemedia.jp/abom/97abom/peace/e/06/bakugeki.htm). They don't know where to stop. They become a condition of the body that the body cannot regulate.

[Critics have noted](https://lwlies.com/long-read/akira-and-the-spectre-of-nuclear-war) that Tetsuo's mutating tissue follows exactly this logic. As his psychic power grows beyond his ability to contain it, his body begins to expand in the same pattern: uncontrolled growth that does not respect the edges of the self. The flesh swells past the skin. It incorporates whatever is adjacent — metal, concrete, organic matter. It cannot stop because the signal driving it has no off switch.

This is not allegory. This is the direct visual encoding of what radiation does to tissue. Otomo grew up in postwar Japan, in a culture that had absorbed the experience of the bombings through censorship and silence. The hibakusha were present in Japanese society — disfigured, marginalized, feared — but the official discourse of American occupation and subsequent Cold War geopolitics meant their suffering could not be named directly. What Otomo did was render that suffering in a form that could: animation, science fiction, a teenager with psychic powers who tears himself apart in Neo-Tokyo.

The tablets Tetsuo chokes down in a desperate attempt to control his abilities have been [specifically paralleled by critics](https://lwlies.com/long-read/akira-and-the-spectre-of-nuclear-war) to the ineffective vitamin treatments administered to bomb survivors in the weeks following the attacks. [Medical science had no framework for radiation sickness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hibakusha) — the first weeks brought burns and blast wounds, the third through eighth weeks brought hair loss, anemia, hemorrhage, and death, none of it predictable or treatable with what Japanese medicine had in 1945. The tablets didn't work. Nothing worked. The body just kept doing what the explosion had set in motion.

Tetsuo's pills don't work either.

## Why It Had to Be Animation

This is the part of the argument that's usually left implicit, but it matters.

[Akira was produced with a budget of approximately ¥1.1 billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akira_(1988_film)), making it the most expensive Japanese animated film of its era. Otomo insisted on complete creative control and pioneered techniques that had not been used in anime before: pre-recorded dialogue (normally animation was produced to fit post-recorded audio), which allowed lip sync that matched the actual spoken words; a quick action recorder to test animation fluidity in real time; 24 frames per second rather than the 12-frame standard, giving the film a motion quality closer to live action than anything audiences had seen in animation.

All of this technical investment served a single purpose: the film needed to show things that could not be shown any other way.

Here is the thing about photographs of the hibakusha: they exist. Some have become iconic. The American occupation initially suppressed them, but by the time Otomo made Akira, photographs of keloid scars, of burn victims, of radiation sickness had been published. [It was not until 1951 — the end of the occupation — that Japanese newspapers could print photographs of hibakusha showing keloids on their bodies](https://theconversation.com/the-little-known-history-of-secrecy-and-censorship-in-wake-of-atomic-bombings-45213). Photographs exist and they are horrifying. But they show surfaces. They document aftermath in a form that can be filed, classified, contemplated at a distance. They do not embed you inside the experience.

Animation can. Specifically, animation at the level of technical ambition that Otomo was operating at — 24 frames per second, pre-recorded dialogue, a quick action recorder testing each sequence for fluidity — can place you inside a body that is losing its boundaries in real time. Live-action cinema couldn't have done this: prosthetics show you a surface, a simulation of mutation. Animation can render flesh as it is becoming something else, from the inside. The transformation sequences in Akira are not special effects. They are phenomenological. You are Tetsuo as his body stops being his. There is nowhere to look away to, because the frame is his perspective and his perspective is collapsing.

This is why the Press Code's suppression matters to how Akira works. Photography, journalism, testimony — these were what the censorship targeted. They are propositional modes: they say "this happened, here is evidence." Animation operates differently. It doesn't propose. It creates an experience. The censors had no category for what Otomo was making, because there had never been anything like it, because this specific form — technically ambitious Japanese animation depicting the body failing on this scale — did not exist when the Press Code was active.

## The Title Character Who Isn't There

There's a structural choice in Akira that no one who hasn't thought about it would notice: the title character barely appears.

In the film, Akira himself exists only as frozen body parts, preserved beneath a stadium. He is shown in flashback — a small, frightened child who destroyed Neo-Tokyo in a psychic explosion forty years before the story begins. He is the catastrophe that already happened. He is not an active presence in the plot; he is the absence around which the plot orbits.

[In the manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akira_(manga)), Otomo develops Akira further — he is revived by Tetsuo, used as a figurehead emperor, and ultimately plays a more significant role in the political chaos that follows the second Neo-Tokyo destruction. The film, adapted before the manga concluded, had to compress this into something more contained. What Otomo chose to compress it into was this: Akira as pure absence, as the wound that precedes the story.

This is the film's deepest structural statement about nuclear trauma. The bomb isn't in the film as a visible event. It already happened. What the film depicts is the society that rebuilt on top of the detonation site — the gleaming, crowded, techno-spectacular Neo-Tokyo that was constructed directly over the crater. Japan rebuilt after 1945. The economy recovered. The infrastructure was rebuilt, then exceeded. By 1988, Japan was in the midst of its economic bubble, one of the wealthiest nations on earth.

The crater was still there underneath it. The explosion was still reverberating. Akira — who detonated World War III in the film's backstory — is the name for something that society tried to contain underground. What Otomo shows is what happens when the containment fails.

## Tetsuo Is Not the Villain

The conventional reading casts Tetsuo as the antagonist and Kaneda as the hero. This is the reading 4Kids-era Western audiences brought to the film, shaped by years of "clear good guy, clear bad guy" anime framing. It is wrong.

[Tetsuo is a product of systemic failure](https://www.collider.com/akira-1988-shows-corrupting-influence-of-power-anime/). He grew up in the same orphanage as Kaneda, the same broken institutional care system, the same lack of parental attachment. The difference between them is that Tetsuo's inferiority complex — which the film establishes clearly — gave the military scientists something to work with. Kaneda's bravado made him less susceptible to manipulation; Tetsuo's wounded pride made him the perfect subject. The military took a damaged teenager and gave him power he could not contain. [The "Akira program" mirrors real-world government exploitation of marginalized populations in postwar Japan](https://lwlies.com/long-read/akira-and-the-spectre-of-nuclear-war), including the treatment of war orphans as disposable subjects.

[Otomo himself noted](https://gamerant.com/akira-anime-movie-changes-manga/) that the film's compressed timeline required him to sacrifice some of the moral complexity present in the manga. In the manga, Kaneda is more morally ambiguous — willing to kill Tetsuo rather than save him, less heroic in his motivations. The film romanticizes Kaneda into a cleaner protagonist role. But the core of Tetsuo's tragedy survives: he is not a person who chose evil. He is a person that an exploitative system chose for destruction.

The parallel to the hibakusha, again, is not metaphorical. The atomic bomb survivors were people that geopolitical systems chose to destroy — or more precisely, who were treated as an acceptable cost of a strategic decision made at enormous distance from them. Their bodies paid the price. Tetsuo's body pays the price for a military program that used him as raw material.

## The Manga's Political Resolution

[The film covers only the first half of Otomo's manga](https://gamerant.com/akira-anime-movie-changes-manga/), which ran from 1982 to 1990 across 2,000+ pages. The manga continues into explicit political territory that the film can only gesture toward: competing military factions, a revived United Nations, the "Great Tokyo Empire" that forms around Akira as a figurehead, and Kaneda's ultimately pragmatic, even brutal, response to Tetsuo's threat.

Otomo reportedly regretted what was sacrificed: [he said in 1991 that "part of the original was sacrificed"](https://gamerant.com/akira-anime-movie-changes-manga/) for the film version, though he acknowledged he had come to appreciate the two works as "two different but similar versions of the same story."

The manga's political dimension is not incidental. Otomo was writing in the context of Japan's student revolt of the 1960s, the Anpo protests against the U.S.-Japan Security Treaty, the history of leftist organizing that had been suppressed by the postwar order. The teenage biker gang in Akira — the Capsules, Kaneda's crew — are not just convenient protagonists. They represent the disenfranchised youth who have inherited a society built on concealed trauma and capitalist reconstruction. [Academic analysis has framed Akira as a meditation on "capitalist modes of destruction"](https://www.academia.edu/16357877/Born_of_Trauma_Akira_and_Capitalist_Modes_of_Destruction) — the way each economic catastrophe is processed as raw material for further growth, the crater of Neo-Tokyo's destruction becoming the foundation of a new city, the same logic playing out again.

## What the Film Actually Changed

When Akira reached Western audiences — [opening on Christmas Day 1989 in the United States](https://www.nme.com/features/film-features/akira-anime-rerelease-2776694), airing on BBC Two multiple times between 1994 and 1997 in the UK — it landed in a cultural context that had no frame for it. American animation was Saturday morning cartoons. Adult animation meant Heavy Metal, the Ralph Bakshi tradition. Nothing looked or felt like Akira.

What Western audiences received was an enormous technical achievement about psychic teenagers in a dystopian city. The nuclear trauma reading, the hibakusha parallel, the political critique — none of that was accessible without context most Western viewers didn't have. What landed instead was the pure visual impact: motion that looked real, violence that had consequence, a world that had texture and depth and history.

[The film became, in the phrase critics have used, the "gateway drug" to Western anime fandom.](https://www.cbr.com/akira-dystopian-sci-fi-western-pop-culture-influence/) It proved that animation was not a format constrained to children's entertainment. It cascaded into the early 1990s anime boom in the West, creating the market that allowed Dragon Ball Z, Ghost in the Shell, Evangelion, and everything that followed to find Western audiences.

But the gateway opened mostly onto the spectacle, not the content. Most Western fans absorbed Akira as a technically astonishing action film. The film's actual argument — that the body is a site of political history, that uncontrolled power is specifically the power that states create and cannot regulate, that Japan in 1988 was still inside the detonation — remained largely invisible.

Which is, in a dark way, historically appropriate. The experience of the hibakusha was suppressed once and survived in other forms. The film about that suppression arrived in the West and was experienced primarily as a spectacle. The trauma migrated again, into appreciation without comprehension.

Tetsuo's body kept telling the story. Most people were watching the bike.
