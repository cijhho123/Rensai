The argument about Mari Makinami has been going on since her teased appearance at the end of *Evangelion: 1.0*'s theatrical preview. Fans who wanted *Rebuild* to honor the original series resented her as a cynical commercial insertion — [critics described her](https://gamerant.com/evangelion-who-was-mari-makinami-really/) as added purely to strengthen audience appeal and generate merchandise. Defenders argued she had an arc. Neither camp quite lands on what's actually interesting about her, which is this: [Anno deliberately did not design her](https://www.cbr.com/mari-makinami-hideaki-anno-evangelion-creator-theory/).

Every other major pilot in the Evangelion franchise is, at some level, a projection of Hideaki Anno's psychology. Shinji is his paralysis. Rei is his idea of an ideal object. Asuka is his aggression and self-loathing in female form. These characters were built from the inside out, from an interior so claustrophobic that *The End of Evangelion* collapsed under its own weight. Mari is different. [Anno explicitly handed her off](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious) to assistant director Kazuya Tsurumaki to develop, requesting something "un-Evangelion-like." He wanted a character he couldn't have made himself. That's not a creative failing. It's the whole point.

## What *Ha* (破) Actually Means

[*Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/3784/Evangelion_Movie_2__Ha) takes its Japanese title from the kanji 破 — *ha* — which carries two relevant meanings simultaneously: "interlude" (as in the classical Japanese dramatic structure *jo-ha-kyū*) and "break." Anno was playing on both. As a narrative interlude, 2.0 opens the door to whatever the *Rebuild* series was going to become. As a break, it announces that what follows will not simply be a remake of the original NGE.

[Mari's character was explicitly designed](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious) to embody that *ha*. Tsurumaki described her as representing the structural departure — the foreign element inserted into a closed system. The opening of *2.0* makes this literal: we begin not in Tokyo-3 but somewhere cold and industrial, with dialogue in Russian and English, Sakura Nagashi absent, Mari in a plugsuit that hasn't been established, fighting an angel in a location that means nothing to the audience's established geography. Before Shinji appears, before anything familiar from *1.0* reasserts itself, you get five minutes of a character you don't know doing something competently. That's a deliberate reset.

The problem is that after this opening, 2.0 largely doesn't know what to do with her. [Tsurumaki himself admitted](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious) Mari was "a different character in each scene" with "multiple personalities," describing the difficulty of making her consistent through roughly forty script revisions. She's charming in scattered moments — the pre-battle humming, the LCL sniff — but her role in the film's central action amounts to: arrive, pilot Unit-02 in Beast Mode, nearly lose, get benched, let Shinji have his heroic moment against Zeruel. She's a placeholder until Shinji needs to run in.

This is why the fan criticism has some traction. In *2.0* specifically, Mari functions more as a demonstration than a character. What's she demonstrating? That someone can pilot an Eva without trauma as the motivating engine.

## The Character Who Actually Enjoys It

All three of the original pilots — Shinji, Rei, Asuka — [pilot for reasons that have nothing to do with wanting to](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=20956). Shinji does it out of guilt and obligation; he cannot refuse his father and cannot articulate any positive desire. Rei does it as self-annihilation, because she has been constructed to exist for this purpose and has no framework for refusal. Asuka does it as competitive self-proof, filling an interior absence with external achievement. Their piloting is, in every case, the expression of something broken.

[Mari pilots because she finds it interesting.](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious) She hums pop songs. She sniffs the LCL to gauge the Eva's emotional state the way a cook might taste a sauce. She activates Beast Mode on Unit-02 with the casual professionalism of someone running a known protocol. When the fight goes badly, she doesn't spiral into self-recrimination — she adapts. She's the only character in the franchise who appears to experience piloting as an activity with positive valence, who engages with the Evangelion as a system to understand rather than a parent to please or an identity to prove through.

This is a quiet argument the film makes without stating it. The other pilots are defined by their relationship to dependency — emotional, parental, institutional — and their inability to separate their existence from the machine's demands. [One critic's reading](https://veemignon.wordpress.com/2017/11/21/evangelion-2-22-you-can-not-advance/) frames this as a simplification: *2.0* strips the psychological complexity of the original series, turning Rei into a damsel and removing ambiguity. That's a legitimate critique of the film as a remake. But it misses what Mari's presence does alongside that simplification — she's the character the film doesn't need to simplify, because she was never complicated in the way Eva characters are complicated. She has no trauma bond with the machinery. Mari isn't immune to the machine's costs (she's been aged-frozen by the "[curse of Eva](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious)" by the events of *3.0+1.0*, as becomes clear from her apparent age across decades of in-universe history). But she isn't constituted by those costs. She was already a person before Eva, and she remains one after. That's the demonstration.

## The Moyoco Theory and What Anno Actually Said

The fan theory that Mari is based on Moyoco Anno — the director's wife, a prominent manga artist — originated with [Gainax co-founder Toshio Okada](https://gamerant.com/evangelion-who-was-mari-makinami-really/) and persists because it's emotionally coherent as a reading. If Anno was working through his depression in the original NGE and Shinji represents his paralyzed self, then a character representing his recovery and his marriage could function as the symbol of genuine outside connection — which is exactly what Mari becomes by 3.0+1.0.

[Anno denied it emphatically](https://www.cbr.com/mari-makinami-hideaki-anno-evangelion-creator-theory/), calling it "baseless assumptions" and stating that "Mari's personality was created by director Kazuya Tsurumaki's hand." [Moyoco Anno herself asked readers of her paid newsletter](https://www.crunchyroll.com/news/latest/2022/3/9/evangelion-creator-hideaki-anno-confirms-mari-wasnt-based-on-his-wife): "Please don't compare me to the work any more than you have to. Please don't make me feel uncomfortable about it." Studio Khara's official position is that the comparison "would have been impossible" during production.

The denial doesn't kill the reading, but it does reframe it. Even if the parallels are unintentional, what they've converged on is accurate: Mari functions in the Rebuild as the representation of something outside Anno's psychological closed system. Whether or not she's literally based on Moyoco, she occupies the structural position that someone from outside that system would occupy. She is the relationship with reality that made the recovery possible — not depicted as a character study, but present as a narrative role.

## Why "Unnecessary" Is the Wrong Charge

The most common serious criticism of Mari — beyond the fan-service complaint — is that she's structurally unnecessary. [She has little bearing on *2.0* or *3.0*'s plots](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=20956). She doesn't change Shinji. She doesn't have a relationship with any established character that carries emotional weight. You could cut her from both films and the plot would proceed intact.

This is true. And it's precisely the wrong standard to apply to her.

Mari isn't in *Rebuild* to drive plot. She's in *Rebuild* to exist alongside the plot as a different possibility. The function of a character who genuinely enjoys piloting, who enters the story without psychological preconditions, who can be present without being damaged — is not to change anything in *2.0*. It's to sit next to everything that happens in *2.0* and establish, quietly, that an alternative exists. That the world of Eva doesn't have to be constituted entirely by trauma.

[One EvaGeeks analysis](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=20956) calls her "the anti-otaku ending, the 'it's time to grow up and move on beyond this cartoon' ending" — which is accurate but sounds harsher than the function deserves. She's not a rebuke. She's an open door. Shinji in *2.0* walks through a different door (into disaster, into Near Third Impact, into everything that cascades through *3.0*). He can't reach her yet. But she's there.

The [payoff comes in *3.0+1.0*](https://www.cbr.com/evangelion-3-01-01-mari-illustrious-makinami-explained/), when it's Mari's hand Shinji takes at the station as the world rebuilds itself. [One analysis describes her](https://ramenpunk.medium.com/the-curious-case-of-mari-makinami-illustrious-41dfe20f4b19) as representing Anno's "falling in love with reality after being King of Otaku for most of your young life" — and while that reads as too clean, the structural function holds. She can be the exit because she was never inside. The critics who said she was purposeless in 2.0 were right that she was purposeless *then*. But the series was playing a longer game than one film — and you cannot build that payoff using a character who shares damage with the protagonist. That's why she had to be made by someone other than Anno, from outside his system, inconsistent across scenes. Her externality is not a flaw. It's her qualification.

## What Anno Let Tsurumaki Make

There's a kind of creative honesty in the production history of Mari that gets overlooked. Anno built the original NGE from his own pathologies, and the result was both singular and, by his own account, nearly destroyed him. He knew *Rebuild* required something he couldn't supply: a character who wasn't drawn from his interior. Someone who didn't already carry the weight of everything Eva had been.

He [handed the task to Tsurumaki](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Statements_by_Evangelion_Staff) and acknowledged the result wasn't fully consistent. He accepted a character with "multiple personalities" across scenes because perfecting her would have meant bringing her back inside his control — and the moment she came back inside his control, she'd have started becoming one of the other pilots. The inconsistency is a preservation of her externality.

[Toshimichi Ōtsuki, the King Records producer](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Mari_Makinami_Illustrious), originally requested a new main female character for commercial reasons. Anno agreed. And in that agreement — in the admission that something commercially motivated and externally requested could serve the film's purposes — there's a different kind of honesty than the confessional authenticity of the original series. *Rebuild* is a collaboration between Anno's personal artistic project and the commercial apparatus that surrounds it. Mari is the character who embodies that collaboration rather than pretending it doesn't exist.

She was added to help sell the film. She was designed to break Eva. Both of those things are true, and they are not in contradiction.

The fandom spent fifteen years asking whether she worked. She does — just not in the way any one film makes legible. She works as the space that had to exist before something genuinely different could grow there.
