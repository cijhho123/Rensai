Himari Inuzuka tells Yuu Natsume that love is a hassle. She says it's poison. She proposes they become "fate-bound partners" on the explicit condition that no romantic feelings are allowed to enter the arrangement. She is cheerful, direct, and completely convinced of her own position.

She also wears a choker around her neck, every day, that Yuu made for her by hand — a small pendant housing an *Anemone flaccida*, a flower [traditionally associated with forsaken love and fading hope](https://danjoru.fandom.com/wiki/Himari_Inuzuka). She wears the symbol of exactly what she's told herself she doesn't have.

That contradiction is the most interesting thing about [*Can a Boy-Girl Friendship Survive?*](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=26206), the J.C. Staff spring 2025 adaptation of [Nana Nanana's light novel series](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2024-08-09/danjo-no-yujo-wa-seiritsu-suru-iya-shinai-anime-reveals-2025-premiere-visual/.214106), which ran for twelve volumes before [concluding in late 2025](https://namicomi.com/c/news/article/14083/danjo-no-yuujou-wa-seiritsu-suru-iya-shinai-light-novel-series-ends). The show frames its central question — the one in its title — as if the answer might be open. But it's not the question that matters. What matters is why Himari needs so badly to believe the answer is yes.

## The Philosophy Is the Tell

Himari isn't oblivious. That's the crucial distinction from the standard romcom lead. She's not an idiot about feelings — she's encyclopedic about them. She reads emotional situations quickly, she's perceptive about other people's relationships, and she's described in the source material as [deeply popular with a natural social charisma](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive), a presence so effortless that other people are drawn in before she has to do anything at all. She's navigated attraction before. She's not missing the signals. She's actively choosing not to name them.

When she declared that love was a hassle and a poison, she wasn't stating a naive belief. She was installing a firewall. [As the ANN episode 5 review documented](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/episode-5/.224143), what drew Himari to Yuu in the first place was that "he came off as somebody who wouldn't try to take advantage of her" — she was, specifically, "tired of people using affection as a weapon against her." The friendship pact isn't idealism. It's a control mechanism. As long as she defines the relationship as purely platonic, she retains complete authority over it. The moment romantic love enters the vocabulary, she becomes vulnerable to the same exploitation she's spent her whole life navigating around.

[Fan discourse around the series](https://www.anime-planet.com/forum/threads/danjo-no-yuujou-wa-seiritsu-suru-iya-shinai.347076/) has frequently noted that Himari's situation runs on what might be called *philia* — a classical conception of friendship-love — rather than *eros*, and that the show is unusual in treating this as a genuine philosophical position rather than just a gap in self-awareness. The distinction matters. Himari isn't confused about what love is. She's chosen the kind she's willing to risk.

The choker is the tell because it proves she already knows. You don't wear a handmade object every day from someone whose feelings you're genuinely indifferent to. You don't anchor an entire philosophy of love-as-poison around a person and then display their craftwork as a pendant. The *Anemone flaccida* — forsaken love — isn't subtle symbolism. It's the show being direct about exactly what Himari is doing: performing a belief she has already quietly abandoned.

## What the Pact Is Actually For

The "friendship pact" as a romcom device is basically furniture at this point. [The childhood-friends-to-lovers pipeline is one of the most durable structures in the genre](https://www.cbr.com/childhood-friends-to-lovers-anime/), and the "I don't want to ruin what we have" hesitation has been a standard speed bump for decades. [The central setup draws comparisons to *When Harry Met Sally*](https://beneaththetangles.com/2025/04/04/first-impression-can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/), a film that posed the same question about heterosexual friendship and romantic tension — though the anime's answer is already written into its structure before the first episode ends.

What *Danjoru* does differently — at its best — is give Himari a coherent reason for the pact that isn't just genre convention. She didn't make it because she was scared of her own feelings. She made it because she had already decided that this specific friendship was too valuable to expose to the wreckage of failed romance. The show earns that weight in its early episodes: the easy physicality between Himari and Yuu, the way she talks about loving to watch his face when he works on flower accessories, the sense that she genuinely sees him — his dream of building a business around floral accessories, his quiet persistence — in a way nobody else has bothered to. [Community viewers noted early on](https://forums.sakurajima.moe/threads/danjo-no-yuujou-wa-seiritsu-suru-iya-shinai-%E2%80%A2-can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive-episode-2-discussion.3456/) that the first few episodes put in real work establishing what's worth protecting before anything threatens it, which is more than a lot of romance anime bother to do.

The problem is that the pact was never really about protecting the friendship. It was about protecting herself. And when Rion Enomoto shows up — Yuu's first love from elementary school, composed and direct where Himari is chaotic and evasive — Himari's entire constructed frame starts cracking at the edges. [By episode 6, she has technically confessed to Yuu four times](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/episode-6/.224315) while insisting she hasn't confessed at all. The architecture she built to keep herself safe is fighting her own feelings for its structural integrity.

## Rion and the Exposure Problem

Rion is a more interesting rival than she initially appears, and understanding why requires looking at what she actually represents to Himari rather than just to Yuu.

Rion is [openly honest about her feelings from her first significant appearance](https://anibase.net/en/character/1b2yZ/Rion-Enomoto). She confesses directly, more than once, without strategic ambiguity. She is, [as the TV Tropes character breakdown describes her](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Characters/CanABoyGirlFriendshipSurvive), an Ice Queen on the surface and a genuinely caring, emotionally straightforward person underneath — which makes her an almost point-for-point inversion of Himari, who projects warmth and accessibility while maintaining a much colder inner architecture of self-protection. Rion hides her shyness; Himari hides her feelings. But Rion still confesses. That's the difference.

![Himari and Yu facing each other in a later key visual](https://static.animecorner.me/2025/06/1749827598-ba3a820dc4d611a765e74ac93e33dfd8.jpg)

The threat Rion poses isn't that she might win Yuu. It's that her emotional directness makes Himari's entire strategy look like what it is. When someone is simply honest about loving someone and nothing catastrophic happens — when the person they love doesn't use that admission as a weapon, doesn't weaponize it — Himari's foundational assumption about how affection works gets quietly undermined. [Reviews noted that Rion is "more stable and healthier" as a teen anime character](https://www.japanpowered.com/anime-articles/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive), which is exactly what makes her presence so destabilizing. She's evidence that the world Himari built her defenses against isn't the only world available.

[RABUJOI's episode analysis](https://rabujoi.wordpress.com/category/anime-reviews/spring-2025/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/) put it well: Himari "built a towering fortress of 'just friendship'" through her teasing and strategic retreats, while Rion's presence gradually exposes the seams. Not by being a better competitor, but by being a different kind of person — one who leads with honesty and survives it.

## The Show's Best and Worst Instincts

The series is at its most compelling when it treats Himari's position as something other than a simple phase to outgrow. [The ANN episode 4 review noted the show was "walking a very delicate tight rope" with her character](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/review/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/episode-4/.223959) — not because she was unsympathetically written, but because her behavior exists in a zone where both "she's protecting herself" and "she's being unfair to Yuu" are simultaneously true. The hallway argument where her choker gets broken was praised specifically because it felt real from both perspectives: each of them genuinely believed the other had betrayed something.

That's good character writing. It doesn't give you a scoreboard. It gives you two people who care about each other doing damage through the mechanisms of that caring.

Where the show struggles is when it defaults to manufactured friction — [the artificial miscommunication loops that multiple reviewers flagged as the series' weakest instinct](https://yoshianimereviews.wordpress.com/2025/06/20/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/). When Himari is petulant without cause, the show is wasting its own strongest card. Her defensiveness is compelling when it's rooted in the psychology the first arc established; it reads as incoherent when it's just deployed to generate an episode's worth of conflict. [The overall audience score](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt35346400/) reflects this split: the premise and the character's core are solid, but the execution oscillates between genuine emotional insight and genre autopilot.

[Anime-Planet users noted a similar divide](https://www.anime-planet.com/anime/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive/reviews) — people either stick with Himari through the rough patches because they understand what the show is trying to do with her psychology, or they check out because the behavior reads as inexplicable rather than motivated. The show doesn't always do the work to make the latter group stay.

## The Title Is a Misdirection

The show's title is a misdirection. Not because the answer is obviously "no" — though it probably is, and [at least one commentator framed this as cause for celebration rather than lament](https://medium.com/@steven.wyman.blackburn/can-a-boy-girl-friendship-survive-no-it-cant-and-that-s-a-good-thing-c2b0c84b1dc2) — but because the question implies the show has genuine stakes around it when it doesn't. From episode one, the narrative architecture points in one direction. [The AniList synopsis](https://anilist.co/anime/153554/Danjo-no-Yuujou-wa-Seiritsu-suru-Iya-Shinai) doesn't even pretend otherwise: the relationship "slowly turns into something else" is the premise, not the destination.

The more honest question the show is asking — and occasionally actually engages — is: *what does it cost to maintain a position you've already secretly abandoned?* Himari is paying a real price. She's expending enormous emotional labor — the performative teasing, the careful management of moments that could become confessions, the constant redrawing of lines she herself keeps crossing — to sustain a fiction that she's comfortable with the arrangement. The *Anemone flaccida* sits at her throat as a kind of permanent receipt for a transaction she's pretending never happened.

What makes this worth paying attention to isn't the romance. It's that Himari's situation is legible beyond the genre. People build elaborate philosophical frameworks to justify positions they've already emotionally vacated. They construct principles around what they're afraid of rather than what they actually believe. The show's best episodes understand this. Its worst episodes forget it. The question isn't whether the friendship survives. It's whether Himari can survive admitting she doesn't want it to.
