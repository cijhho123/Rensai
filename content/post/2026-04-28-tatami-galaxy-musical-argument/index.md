Most writing about [*The Tatami Galaxy*](https://myanimelist.net/anime/7785/Yojouhan_Shinwa_Taikei) focuses on what you can see: the flat-perspective backgrounds, the text cascading down the screen, the racing monologue that makes each episode feel like being trapped inside someone's anxious skull. These are the obvious things. But the show constructs a parallel argument through sound — an argument that arrives at the same conclusions as the visual one while approaching them from a completely different angle. The OP, the score, and the ED aren't decoration. Together they form a three-part philosophical essay about what Watashi actually is, what each character's psychology actually looks like, and what question the whole series is trying to answer.

If you miss the music, you miss half the show.

## The Lost Dog Already Knows

Asian Kung-Fu Generation's "[Maigoinu to Ame no Beat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maigoinu_to_Ame_no_Beat)" — "A Lost Dog and the Beats of the Rain" — was the 15th single from a band known for angular, urgent indie rock. It was also the first AKFG song to feature brass instruments, which is notable: something new was called for, something that could hold a certain weight without collapsing into generic anime-rock anthemics.

The image the title hands you is the whole show in four words. A stray dog, in the rain, wandering. That's Watashi. Across [eleven episodes](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=11200) he cycles through tennis circles, cycling clubs, film societies, and secret societies, each time convinced he's finally going to find his "rose-colored campus life" and his "raven-haired maiden." Each time he ends up exactly where he started — wetter, more bitter, no closer to the connection he claims to want. He is the stray dog. He is in the rain. He caused the rain himself.

![The Tatami Galaxy key visual](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/db/Yojouhan_Shinwa_Taikei.jpg)

The specific genius of this image is that a lost dog doesn't know it's lost. It thinks it's searching. It has a direction in mind; it is heading somewhere. The wandering looks, from the inside, like purpose. Watashi narrates his failures with such voluminous self-justification — blaming Ozu, blaming the clubs, blaming the universe of unchosen possibilities — that the OP's choice of the stray dog metaphor arrives as a diagnosis the protagonist can't hear himself receiving. The viewer hears it every episode before Watashi opens his mouth. The opening frames every episode's circular disaster in advance.

What makes the AKFG choice interesting beyond the image is the tone. The song is not despairing. It's searching, urgent, a little ragged, but fundamentally forward-moving. The stray dog is crying in the rain "on the street corner at night" but the song doesn't end there — it ends with movement, with "let's go day by day." The OP acknowledges the condition and refuses to let it be terminal. That refusal is doing structural work: it tells you, before Episode 1 has started, that the show believes there is a way out of the dog's condition, even if the dog doesn't know it yet.

There's also an additional layer worth naming. Yusuke Nakamura, who designed the characters for *The Tatami Galaxy*, is also the artist who [illustrates Asian Kung-Fu Generation's CD covers](https://yattatachi.com/portrait-of-an-artist-yusuke-nakamura). His distinctive flat-perspective, woodblock-influenced visual style runs through both the anime and the band's visual identity. The OP isn't just musically commissioned — the visual hand behind the show and behind the band's imagery is the same hand. That continuity of vision is why the opening feels so native to the show rather than slapped on.

## The Score Knows What Each Character Is

Composer Michiru Ōshima's [original soundtrack](https://vgmdb.net/album/20951) for *The Tatami Galaxy* is built on three leitmotifs that track character psychology rather than emotional atmosphere. This is a meaningful distinction. A lot of anime scoring works atmospherically — it tells you how to feel about what you're watching. Ōshima's score tells you what each character *is*. Her compositional approach — [she describes jumping straight into orchestral scoring "in a flash of inspiration," after reading the source material and discussing the director's vision](http://www.jpopexchange.net/Michiru_Oshima_JPop_Exchange_Exclusive_Interview.htm) — suits a show about the gap between interior experience and exterior reality. Her output for this series has been described as [orchestrating "chamber strings, woodwinds, and light electronics to match rapid-fire dialogue and surreal campus detours"](https://ffshrine.org/2010/09/the-tatami-galaxy-michiru-oshima-2010-score/) — but the instrument choices for each character are not incidental. They're character arguments.

Watashi's theme is carried by violin. This is precise. The violin in this context speaks to aspiration, to romanticized emotion, to a kind of reaching that is beautiful but also inherently vulnerable. It depicts, as [one review of the OST put it](https://blog.animeinstrumentality.net/soundtrack-reviews/yojouhan-shinwa-taikei-tatami-galaxy-original-soundtrack-review/), "an optimist, a dreamer." But violins can also be overwrought, can tip from expressive into melodramatic, and Ōshima leans into that ambiguity. Watashi's theme captures exactly the gap between how he experiences his own interiority — rich, feeling, genuinely moved by things — and how his behavior actually looks from outside. He is a dreamer who cannot convert the dream into action. The violin plays that contradiction.

Ozu's theme is clarinet plus accordion, and the combination is unsettling in a way that rewards paying attention. The clarinet "slinks" — [the Anime Instrumentality review uses that word](https://blog.animeinstrumentality.net/soundtrack-reviews/yojouhan-shinwa-taikei-tatami-galaxy-original-soundtrack-review/) and it's right — and the accordion adds what the same piece calls a "slimy touch." This is a choice about ambiguity, not villainy. Clarinet is not a monstrous instrument; it's agile, capable of warmth, but also of unsettling registers when pushed. Ozu's theme doesn't tell you he's evil. It tells you his nature is *slippery*. You can never quite pin down whose side he's actually on. The music knows what Watashi refuses to accept: Ozu is not a monster, but he is genuinely strange, and his strangeness is not separable from his friendship.

Akashi's theme is calming and melodically clear — [a "soothing and charming" melody](https://blog.animeinstrumentality.net/soundtrack-reviews/yojouhan-shinwa-taikei-tatami-galaxy-original-soundtrack-review/) that captures her function in the show more accurately than Watashi's narration ever manages. He intellectualizes her, abstracts her into the "raven-haired maiden" ideal, but the score refuses that reduction. Her theme is grounding, present, attentive to things as they are. She is, as the [Chicago Review of Books noted about the novel's construction](https://chireviewofbooks.com/2023/02/20/multiversal-revelations-in-the-tatami-galaxy/), someone who accepts the present rather than chasing hypothetical better versions of it. Her music already knows that before the narrative gets there.

This matters because it means the score is not narrating from Watashi's point of view. The visuals and the monologue are deeply subjective — we see the world as Watashi filters it. The music does not. The score sees each character clearly even when Watashi cannot, and when you listen with that in mind, the score functions as the reliable narrator the protagonist refuses to be.

## What the God Is Actually Dictating

The ending theme is where the argument gets sharpest. "[Kamisama no Iu Toori](https://kafkafuura.wordpress.com/2010/06/03/kamisama-no-iu-toori/)" — "As God Dictates" — is performed by Etsuko Yakushimaru of Soutaiseiriron, alongside composers Junji Ishiwatari and Yoshinori Sunahara. Yakushimaru's voice is [precise and cool](https://www.discogs.com/release/13581727-Michiru-Oshima-The-Tatami-Galaxy-Original-Soundtrack), not warm, not emotionally pleading. It sounds like a statement being made rather than a feeling being expressed.

The title poses the show's central question in four characters. Is Watashi's situation — the repetitive failures, the inability to change, the circling through the same basic mistake in different settings — fated? Or has he chosen it? "As God Dictates" could mean: *this was always going to happen, your freedom is an illusion*. Or it could mean something else entirely, because "god" in this show has a specific referent: Higuchi, the slacker deity figure who claims to govern romantic fate, who at the show's crucial moment [tells Watashi](https://chireviewofbooks.com/2023/02/20/multiversal-revelations-in-the-tatami-galaxy/) that "the root of all his evil is in always relying on other possibilities" — that he must accept the person he is here and now, rather than gambling everything on better hypothetical selves. What this god dictates is not predestination. What he dictates is *acceptance of the present*. The kamisama tells you to stop looking for the better timeline and live in the one you have.

![Etsuko Yakushimaru promotional image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Etsuko_Yakushimaru_2019.jpg/400px-Etsuko_Yakushimaru_2019.jpg)

The song's lyrics make the connection explicit if you look at them directly. As [translated by kafka-fuura](https://kafkafuura.wordpress.com/2010/06/03/kamisama-no-iu-toori/), the opening lines run: *"In order to go to heaven / every day, softly, I did good things in secret."* This is the song's narrator performing virtue — doing good deeds not because of genuine goodness but to accumulate merit for entrance to paradise. The performance continues: the narrator maintains a persona of purity while, as [one reading of the lyrics](https://sonichits.com/video/Junji_Ishiwatari_%26_Yoshinori_Sunahara_%2B_Etsuko_Yakushimaru/Kamisama_no_Iu_Toori) notes, harboring jealousy and darker feelings underneath. The final verse's claim — that the narrator deserves heaven — arrives as the song's punchline: a person who has suppressed all authentic feeling in favor of performed goodness, now demanding divine reward for the performance.

That is Watashi's whole problem, stated in three minutes of electronics and cool vocals. He performs the role of wronged idealist — the man who deserved the rose-colored campus life and didn't receive it — while refusing to acknowledge his own agency in his failures. He claims he was cheated. What god dictates, if you're listening, is that the claim is a lie.

The ending's cold, staccato electronic texture — so different from the organic AKFG rock of the opening — closes each episode in a register that refuses comfort. You don't walk away from the ED feeling resolved. You walk away feeling the problem has been precisely named. That's the correct feeling to have after eleven episodes of watching someone fail to name it themselves.

## Why the Three Layers Add Up to Something

What's unusual about *The Tatami Galaxy*'s musical architecture is that the three components are not harmonized — they're triangulated. The OP knows what Watashi is and describes it from the outside. The score sees each character clearly from a position the protagonist can't access. The ED poses the central philosophical question and refuses to answer it too easily.

Together they create a situation where the viewer is always in possession of more information than the narrator provides. The show [won the Grand Prize at the 14th Japan Media Arts Festival](https://www.cgarts.or.jp/v1/outline/press/2010/img/1208_e.pdf) — [the first television series ever to do so](https://www.nishikata-eiga.com/2011/02/14th-japan-media-arts-festival.html) — with the jury citing it as a work that "overflows with expressiveness" and "defies the commercial limitations usually put onto such works." Part of what that means in practice is that Yuasa found ways to layer meaning across multiple channels simultaneously — visual, verbal, and acoustic — so that the show's argument arrives whether or not you're following the rapid monologue at full speed.

Shingo Natsume, who directed Episode 6 of the series and later went on to direct the sequel *Tatami Time Machine Blues*, has spoken about how Yuasa builds ["a sturdy structure that won't collapse even when young creators experiment in their own ways"](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2022/10/07/from-tatami-galaxy-to-time-machine-blues-masaaki-yuasa-shingo-natsume-and-the-context-of-unique-animation/) — and that he wished his time within that team had never ended. That sturdiness applies to the sound design too. The music isn't decoration on top of the visual argument; it's load-bearing. The show can afford to move so fast, to overwhelm you with text and narration, because the score and the themes are quietly making the same case in a slower register underneath.

The lost dog in the rain. The three instruments tracking three very different psychologies. The god who turns out to dictate acceptance rather than fate. These aren't background music. They're a second reading of the same book, running in parallel — and the book argues that the present you have, including the 4.5 tatami room and the ambiguous friend and the woman you keep failing to see clearly, is not the consolation prize for missing the rose-colored life. It is the life. The music has known that all along.
