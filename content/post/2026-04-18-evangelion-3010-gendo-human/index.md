For twenty-five years, Gendo Ikari existed in Evangelion as a fixed point: the distant father, the architect of suffering, the man who abandoned his son at age three and spent the rest of his life using that son as a weapon for his own ends. He was not a mystery to be solved. He was a verdict. Every fan who ever watched Shinji reach for connection and get a cold shoulder, every viewer who watched Gendo place a bomb around a fourteen-year-old's neck, understood that some characters are not meant to be understood — only to be survived.

*Evangelion: 3.0+1.0 Thrice Upon a Time* disagrees. And that disagreement is the film's most controversial and most necessary move.

![Gendo Ikari - Evangelion](https://wiki.evageeks.org/images/3/35/Op_C013_gendo.jpg)

## What the Film Asks You to Do

The climax of Thrice Upon a Time sends Shinji into a confrontation with Gendo inside an anti-universe — [a space where LCL gives shape to perception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_3.0+1.0_Thrice_Upon_a_Time), and the past, the present, and the mind's eye all become equally real. What begins as a battle becomes something stranger: Gendo simply talking. Explaining. Showing Shinji — and the audience — the footage Anno had been withholding for three decades.

Gendo as a child, alone. [A boy fond of piano who was not good at being liked](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Gendo_Ikari), who had no idea how to let people close. A young man who met Yui and experienced, for the first time, someone who chose him — and who was so unprepared for that experience that losing her shattered something irreparable. A father who [believed that abandoning Shinji was a just punishment for his own failures](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Gendo_Ikari), who feared that contact with his son would only cause more harm. A man who spent his entire adult life running from the terror of being someone worth loving.

"I was afraid," Gendo says. He was afraid of his own child.

The film asks you to receive this. Not to excuse it — [he cannot be totally absolved of his crimes](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=13297) — but to understand it. In the [SoraNews analysis of the film's Japanese reception](https://soranews24.com/2021/03/10/my-dad-was-like-gendo-how-does-the-final-evangelion-movie-resolve-the-series-true-conflict/), one reporter described watching Shinji and Gendo reach understanding not as enemies becoming friends, but as "two adults who understand and respect each other, and leave the past in the past" — a distinction that matters. This is not reconciliation as erasure. It is the acknowledgment that the man who hurt you was also, somewhere, afraid.

## The Radical Empathy of the Villain's POV

What makes this so divisive is also what makes it the bravest thing the Rebuild series does. The original NGE [gave viewers psychological access to Shinji's interior in extraordinary detail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon_Genesis_Evangelion) — his loops of self-recrimination, his hunger for approval, the terror underneath every withdrawal. The series structured itself around understanding *why* Shinji is the way he is. Gendo was always exempt from this. He was the cause, not the subject.

Thrice Upon a Time revokes that exemption. [As the Medium/AniTAY essay on the film notes](https://medium.com/anitay-official/evangelion-3-0-1-0-emotional-violence-and-ultimate-catharsis-1bf2f2a79ddb), Gendo is revealed as "a flawed, fearful human being who ran away from his son because he couldn't accept he was worthy of love from anyone after losing his wife." This is, structurally, the same portrait the original series painted of Shinji. The son inherited the father's psychology almost exactly — the same inability to reach for connection without simultaneously expecting rejection, the same withdrawal masquerading as control.

Anno [spent three decades building one of anime's most compelling portraits of a traumatized teenager](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/anime.php?id=49) while keeping the source of that trauma opaque. The final film's argument is that trauma does not originate with the person who inflicts it. Gendo hurt Shinji in the same structural way he himself was hurt, by people and by loss, and the hurt was not calculated cruelty — it was unprocessed fear dressed up as remoteness. This does not let him off. It does, however, make him legible.

[Some fans find this unearned](https://forum.evageeks.org/viewtopic.php?t=13297). The objection is reasonable: Gendo's cruelties are too specific, too sustained, too much a product of active choice to be fully explained by personal psychology. He did not accidentally create the Human Instrumentality Project. He deliberately used his teenage son as a pilot, fitted him with a kill-switch, and pursued reunion with his dead wife through an extinction event. These are not the acts of a frightened man operating unconsciously. They are goals.

The film's answer — which the viewer must decide whether to accept — is that none of this is incompatible. [Gendo can be a person who loved and feared and was shaped by loss *and* a person who made choices that hurt others irreparably](https://scenikeight.tumblr.com/post/710956590413529088/the-cycle-of-rebuild-of-evangelion-understanding). Understanding the former does not excuse the latter. But refusing to understand the former because the latter is unforgivable is a choice about what stories are allowed to show — and it is a choice that NGE spent its entire run arguing against, when the subject was Shinji. [The Atelier Emily essay on the film](https://formeinfullbloom.wordpress.com/2021/08/19/hundreds-of-ways-to-say-hell-is-other-people-and-also-love-is-other-people-evangelion-3-01-0-thrice-upon-a-time/) frames this as the film's refusal to separate "hell is other people" from "love is other people" — they are the same statement.

## What the Hug Means

There is a moment near the end of their confrontation where Gendo hugs Shinji. [This is the first time in the entire franchise](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Gendo_Ikari) — across the TV series, the films, the manga — that Gendo Ikari has touched his son in anything other than utility. It is a brief, awkward embrace between two people who barely know how to occupy the same space, and it carries more weight than almost any action sequence in the four films.

![Gendo - original design reference](https://wiki.evageeks.org/images/1/1b/Proposal_22_gendo.jpg)

This matters because the original series spent enormous energy on the hedgehog's dilemma — the idea that the closer you draw to another person, the more you hurt each other, and that connection and pain are inseparable. [*End of Evangelion* ended on a vision of this that was deliberately unresolved, possibly irresolvable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_End_of_Evangelion). Thrice Upon a Time does not cancel that argument. It proposes a next chapter: that the people who taught you the hedgehog's dilemma were also subject to it, and that understanding this is different from forgetting what they did.

The hug does not fix anything. It cannot. Gendo dies shortly afterward — [ultimately consumed by the very project he set in motion](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Gendo_Ikari). But the hug acknowledges something the series spent twenty-five years avoiding: that Gendo Ikari was, underneath everything, a man who did not know how to be close to his son and who found this out too late.

## Anno's Closing Statement

The film earned [¥10.2 billion in Japan](https://www.siliconera.com/evangelion-3-0-1-0-almost-10-billion-yen-japanese-box-office/), [breaking franchise records globally](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/2021-04-12/final-evangelion-film-breaks-franchise-box-office-record-globally/.171641). But the more significant datum is what Anno did with the ending. The film closes at an animated version of [Anno's own hometown — the Ube train station in Yamaguchi Prefecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_3.0+1.0_Thrice_Upon_a_Time) — with Shinji living in a world without Evangelion. The choice is not accidental. Anno put his own city at the end of the story because the ending belongs to the person who made it: he did not send Shinji to a fictional town, he sent him home. The Gendo-Shinji confrontation and its resolution are, in this reading, autobiographical — not literally, but structurally. They are what it looks like when a creator finally makes peace with the work he built in pain.

[Megumi Ogata, Shinji's voice actress for thirty years, was surprised when she read the line "Goodbye, all of Evangelion"](https://mechacatalogue.com/2021/08/17/goodbye-all-of-evangelion-evangelion-3-01-01-thrice-upon-a-time-2021-movie-review/). Anno told her: "Goodbye to your current feelings." He was not just asking her to deliver a line. He was telling her to let go of who Shinji had been to her for three decades — the boy she had inhabited through two television endings and three films — and say goodbye to that version of him. This is what the Gendo scene made possible. You cannot say goodbye to what you have not understood.

This is what Thrice Upon a Time is finally doing: not just ending a franchise, but demonstrating what Anno believes endings are for. The original series ended with Shinji confronting himself. *End of Evangelion* ended with Shinji confronting what he had made. Thrice Upon a Time ends with Shinji confronting his father — and finding, in the ruins of that confrontation, someone recognizable.

The hardest scene Anno ever wrote is not the most spectacular thing in the film. It is a frightened old man, in the last moments of his life, saying: I was afraid. I did not know how to be your father. I am sorry.

Anno gave the villain the same interiority he gave the hero. That is the radical act. Whether you believe it was warranted is a question about what you think empathy is for.
