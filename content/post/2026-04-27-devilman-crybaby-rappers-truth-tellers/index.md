There is a creative decision in *Devilman Crybaby* that goes mostly undiscussed, folded into the general praise for Masaaki Yuasa's visual extremity and Kensuke Ushio's relentless score. In Go Nagai's original 1972 manga, Akira Fudō is introduced surrounded by a gang of delinquents — standard-issue manga lowlifes who bully him, represent social disorder, and function as the human debris the story moves past on its way to apocalypse.

In Yuasa's 2018 adaptation, those delinquents are gone. Their replacements are a rap crew.

Yuasa was direct about the reason. "[I feel like rappers are the people who speak what's on their minds today](https://blog.sakugabooru.com/2018/01/08/devilman-crybaby-interview-with-director-masaaki-yuasa/)." That's it. That single substitution — delinquents out, rappers in — is the most politically loaded decision in a series famous for political provocation. Understanding what it means, and what Yuasa does with it, explains why *Crybaby* is a different kind of horror from its source material.

## What Delinquents Meant and What Rappers Mean

In 1972 shōnen manga, delinquents were a recognizable social type: *yankii*, youth outside institutional order, embodiments of the threat that lurks at the edge of respectable society. They were visible antagonists — the thing your parents worried you'd become if you drifted too far. For Nagai, they served a precise narrative function: Akira is their victim, and his victimhood establishes the social stakes before the supernatural ones arrive.

The substitution of rappers for delinquents doesn't just update the aesthetic. It inverts the moral valence entirely. Delinquents, in the manga framework, are the problem — social disorder made flesh. In Yuasa's rereading, rappers are the opposite: they are people who, by their nature and practice, insist on saying what institutions prefer left unsaid. The threat they pose is not violence but honesty.

Wamu, Kukun, Hie, and Babo — voiced respectively by actual hip-hop artists [Ken the 390, Young Dais, beatboxer AFRA, and Hannya](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt6660498/) — are not Akira's antagonists. They become, across the series' ten episodes, something closer to its conscience. As [ComicBook.com noted at the time](https://comicbook.com/anime/news/devilman-crybaby-anime-netflix-rappers-all-raps/), the rap crew "serve as a narrator throughout the course of the series, hiding the dark messages of the series into their lyrics." They rap the subtext. They say what the narrative is afraid to say directly.

This is a formal choice, not just a thematic one. Yuasa uses the rap performances — genuine freestyle sequences performed by professional artists, with [Ken the 390 serving as supervisor of all the rap sequences](https://hypebeast.com/2018/1/netflix-devilman-crybaby-anime-series-hip-hop-moments) — to articulate the social critique that the horror imagery can only gesture at. Where the demon transformations show what humans are capable of, the raps say it plainly, in real language, in contemporary rhyme schemes that have nothing to do with 1972.

## The Logic of Who Dies First

Here is where the choice becomes devastating: the rappers die.

Not quickly, not easily. [Wamu is killed when an ice pick is driven into his back](https://devilman.fandom.com/wiki/Wamu). His death comes during the period of humanity's worst violence — not the demons' violence, but the humans'. The mob phase, when social media has turned Nagai's Cold War paranoia about hidden enemies into a real-time global panic, is when Wamu and his crew are killed. They are not killed by demons. They are killed by people like them.

This is not incidental. Yuasa constructed it deliberately. The people who speak plainly — who say what's on their mind, who refuse the institutional fiction that everything is fine — are the first to go when social panic arrives. [The mob that eventually destroys Miki — chasing her down, stabbing her in the back, parading her body parts on sticks](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2019-04-24/interview-devilman-crybaby-director-masaaki-yuasa/.146067) — is the same social force that eliminates the rappers. In both cases, the mechanism is the same: a community decides that certain people are dangerous, and the rest of the community agrees, and the dangerous people are destroyed.

What makes the rappers' deaths different from Miki's — both of which are genuinely horrifying — is that their deaths are explicitly cultural. When you kill the truth-tellers, you kill the songs. The soundtrack goes quiet in a different way.

## Kensuke Ushio and the Music That Refuses to Be Period

The rappers are one register of the series' musical argument. The other is Kensuke Ushio's score — and it's doing the same thing in a different language.

Ushio, who performs electronic music under the moniker [Agraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kensuke_Ushio), composed 48 tracks for the series — a full double-album's worth of music that ranges from synthwave-adjacent industrialism to orchestral horror to quiet piano figures. What the rappers do vocally — refusing to let the story be historical, insisting on the contemporary — Ushio's score does instrumentally. There is nothing in his palette that would locate the series in 1972. No prog rock riff, no psychedelic fuzz, no period markers. The music insists, relentlessly, that whatever is happening in the frames is happening now.

This matters because [Nagai's Devilman was, at its core, a Cold War allegory](https://gigazine.net/gsc_news/en/20180116-devilman-crybaby-go-nagai-masaaki-yuasa-interview/) — the paranoia about hidden demons stood in for the paranoia about hidden communists. The 1972 manga felt precisely of its moment. Yuasa's reread is built on stripping that historical specificity away and replacing it with contemporary urgency. The demons aren't Communists anymore. They're whoever your community decided was dangerous this week. The social media mob in *Crybaby* doesn't need an ideology — it needs a target. And the mechanism for transferring that alarm to 2018 audiences is exactly what Ushio's synthwave does: it makes the same paranoia sound like something you've heard on your commute.

When Yuasa deploys the score under the party sequence that transitions into massacre, or under the social media montages where demonic accusations multiply across screens, [the music fuses the grotesque with the recognizable](https://coolestsound.jp/Denki_Groove_Man_Human_Man_Human_konya_dake_Ushio_/2018.01.02/012125/). Ushio's music doesn't comment on the action from a historical distance. It lives inside it — the way the rappers' lyrics live inside the story.

[The opening theme, "Man Human" by Denki Groove](https://vgmdb.net/album/105510) — an unreleased track created specifically for the series — announces this from the first frame: foreboding, pulse-driven, more nightclub than anime, placing the audience inside a sonic space that belongs to the present. When that theme ends and Ushio's score takes over, the continuity is deliberate. The whole audio architecture of *Crybaby* is arguing that this story is yours.

## What Netflix Made Possible

The distribution context matters more than it usually does for anime. [*Devilman Crybaby* was released January 5, 2018, simultaneously in 90 countries and 23 languages](https://sciencesaru.com/en/works/DEVILMANcrybaby_en), with [90% of its viewership coming from outside Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_Saru). This was unprecedented for Science Saru — a studio that had operated with 20–25 staff before the project — and unprecedented for Yuasa.

It also meant the series' mob-violence parable, its social-media critique, its argument about what happens to truth-tellers in a panic — all of it landed simultaneously everywhere, on a platform that its audience watched through the same algorithms the show was depicting. People streaming Crybaby on Netflix in January 2018 were watching a story about what social media does to communities while living inside that story in real time. The [3rd Crunchyroll Anime Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3rd_Crunchyroll_Anime_Awards) gave it Anime of the Year in 2019, which suggests the global audience understood what they had seen — and partly that was because the global audience had been in the streets, online, and watching phone-filmed violence in real time in ways that made the mob sequences feel documentary rather than fantastical.

This timing was not coordinated. But it gave the rappers' function an extra layer of resonance that Yuasa could not have planned. The people who speak what's on their minds, in a series distributed to 90 countries simultaneously by a platform built on attention metrics — their death hit differently than it would have in a niche theatrical release.

## The Adaptation Choice That Explains Everything

The critical discourse around *Devilman Crybaby* fixates, understandably, on its extremity: the explicit violence, the graphic sexuality, the series' refusal of comfort. These are real and important. But the rappers are the decision that tells you what the extremity is for.

Nagai's delinquents are a passive social threat — background disorder that the narrative moves past. Yuasa's rappers are an active moral presence. They rap the truth. They move through the series with their own interiority, their own relationships, their growing awareness of what's happening to the world around them. And then the world kills them for it.

What Crybaby is really doing — beyond the splatter, beyond the [queer love story at its core](https://www.animefeminist.com/feature-devilman-crybaby-legacies-queerness-diversifying-remakes/), beyond the nihilism that isn't actually nihilism — is asking a simple and devastating question: what does a society do with the people who say what everyone else is thinking? The answer, across the series' ten episodes, is: it kills them, one by one, while documenting the killings on its phones.

That's not a metaphor for 1972. That's a description of 2018. The rappers are how you know.
