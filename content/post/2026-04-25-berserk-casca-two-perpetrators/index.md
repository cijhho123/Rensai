When Casca recovers her memories at the end of chapter 355 and sees Guts standing beneath the cherry blossoms, she screams. Not in relief. Not in recognition. In terror.

The fandom spent years debating what this scream means. The most common answer is that she was overwhelmed by the memories of the Eclipse — that seeing Guts triggered the horror of watching Femto violate her, of everything breaking apart in that howling moment under the Godhand. That reading is not wrong. But it is incomplete. And the part it misses is the part *Berserk* has been circling for hundreds of chapters without the audience fully absorbing what they were seeing.

![Berserk manga cover](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/manga/1/157897.jpg)

## Who Casca Was Before

It is worth beginning there, because the discourse around Casca so often starts at the Eclipse rather than at the person who walked into it.

[Casca is the only daughter of desperately poor parents](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Casca) who surrendered her to a nobleman who intended to assault her. She killed him. Not metaphorically — she grabbed his dagger and used it. Griffith happened to be there; he handed her a sword and she ran toward the Band of the Hawk, toward a life where her survival was her own achievement rather than a man's gift. [She worked for years to earn a position that most of the Band never fully accepted from a woman](https://www.gamerant.com/berserk-how-guts-casca-and-griffith-dealt-with-trauma-differently/), becoming one of the most capable commanders in Griffith's forces.

The argument that *Berserk* undermines gender stereotypes "about as much as she perpetuates them" — [a fair-minded conclusion from the fan analysis community](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2020/04/04/why-casca-is-a-well-written-female-character/) — does not change the foundational fact: Casca, as Miura wrote her in the Golden Age arc, is one of the most complete female characters in dark fantasy manga precisely because she entered her own story as an agent. She had established a self before the violence began.

This matters because what the Eclipse takes from her is not just memory or sanity. It is the specific self that fought for years to exist.

## The Eclipse Was Not Hers

The critique from feminist readers is exact: [during the Eclipse, Griffith/Femto's gaze was on Guts](https://nox-artemis.tumblr.com/post/61194794422/berserk-and-feminism-women-in-refrigerators). The assault is staged as an act performed *at* Guts — a final humiliation, a destruction of everything Guts cherished, with Casca as the instrument. The horror Guts experiences watching is the emotional center of the scene. What Casca experiences during it is largely unrepresented.

[Miura himself later acknowledged discomfort with how he handled sexual violence in early *Berserk*](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-violence-women-guts-controversial/), and the manga's trajectory after the Golden Age shows a clear movement toward less gratuitous use of these scenes. But the Eclipse remains what it is: a narrative event that happens *to* Casca primarily as a vehicle for what it does *to* Guts. Her regression to near-catatonia after it is treated as Guts' burden to bear, Guts' quest to reverse, Guts' wound to carry.

This is where the "Women in Refrigerators" critique applies with genuine force. [The classic formulation](https://nox-artemis.tumblr.com/post/61194794422/berserk-and-feminism-women-in-refrigerators) — female characters harmed not for their own story development but to catalyze a male character's — describes exactly what the Eclipse does to Casca structurally, even if the manga contains more complexity than the formula allows for.

But here is where the story becomes more uncomfortable than the critique alone can account for.

## Volume 23 and the Second Perpetrator

During the years Casca spent in her regressed state — the Lost Children arc and its aftermath — Guts carried her. He protected her from apostles and swordsmen and the constant pressure of the Brand. He did this because he loved her. He also, in Episode 190 ("Fangs of Ego") within [Volume 23](https://comic-watch.com/comic-book-reviews/berserk-volume-23-welcome-to-the-new-berserk), during the winter journey, attacked her. The Beast of Darkness — the manifestation of his hunger for violence and vengeance — consumed him while evil spirits swarmed the Brand. He choked her. Then he assaulted her sexually, pinning her down in a deliberate structural echo of what Femto did during the Eclipse, [the Beast of Darkness explicitly goading him to mirror that act](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Episode_190_(Manga)).

The counter-argument the fandom reaches for is: "but he wasn't in control." This is true and irrelevant. The Beast of Darkness is not a foreign entity — it is Guts' own suppressed rage, his own darkness made appetitive. And the physical act, regardless of what generated it, was real. Casca's body registered it as real. [The berserkanalysis community has documented the consequence clearly](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2019/08/25/why-is-casca-screaming-seeing-guts-in-episode-359/): "this is us witnessing her forming a trigger: namely Guts' face." The chapter in which Casca, now recovered, sees Guts in episode 359 and screams again — that scream is *not* about the Eclipse. It is about Volume 23. It is about a specific act of violence committed by the person who claims to love her, while she was incapable of resistance or consent.

Guts' own psychological history makes the manga's architecture legible: [he is himself a survivor of childhood sexual assault](https://www.bitoll.com/berserks-exploration-of-ptsd-and-trauma/), sold by his adoptive father to the mercenary Donovan when he was nine. The parallels between Guts' rape by Donovan and his experience watching Femto violate Casca — both involving being held down, both involving betrayal by a father figure — are intentional design. But *Berserk* also draws the line between victimhood and perpetration. Guts carrying his own trauma does not cancel what he did to Casca. [The manga does not let it cancel.](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-guts-lowest-low-failure-manga/)

## What the Scream Is Made Of

[The chapter 355 scream is partly about the Eclipse](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2019/05/21/why-did-casca-scream-at-the-end-of-chapter-355/) — the sight of Guts' scars and missing eye triggering the catastrophic memories she has just recovered. But [the chapter 359 scream, which the analyst distinguishes as "similar, yet different,"](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2019/08/25/why-is-casca-screaming-seeing-guts-in-episode-359/) has a different architecture. It is a conditioned fear response to Guts' physical presence. Not to Griffith. Not to the Godhand. To Guts.

This is what most fandom discourse cannot absorb. The community's energy around the question — "why is she screaming, does she actually still love him, when will they reconcile" — treats the scream as a puzzle to be solved rather than as a statement to be heard.

Consider the specific scene the fandom constantly cites as evidence that the relationship can be mended: [when Farnese asks Casca if she remembers Guts, she smiles and blushes](https://berserk.fandom.com/wiki/Casca), eyes filling with tears. Her conscious memory of him is warm — embarrassed, touched. Then she sees him in person and screams in terror. These two responses are not contradictory. They describe, with clinical precision, the experience of loving someone who has harmed you. The warm memory is real. The fear response is real. They coexist in the body of every survivor who has been hurt by someone they love, and Miura's decision to render both — rather than collapsing them into one coherent feeling the reader can be comfortable with — is the most honest thing the arc does.

![Berserk manga volume cover art](https://cdn.myanimelist.net/images/manga/1/157897l.jpg)

The question *Berserk* is actually asking is not "will Casca and Guts get together." It is: after what he did, can she be in the same room with him? And what does it mean for a narrative about endurance and love that the answer is, initially, no?

## The Continuation's Evasion

[Since Miura's death in 2021](https://mangaquesty.com/2025/04/27/unseen-scars-trauma-and-ptsd-in-kentaro-miuras-works/), the continuation under Studio Gaga and Kouji Mori has carried forward the surface structure of his plot while — inevitably — losing certain things. The specific reckoning that Casca's recovery arc was building toward is one of them.

[Chapter 372](https://berserkanalysis.wordpress.com/2023/07/25/analysis-of-berserk-episode-372-spoilers/) finds Casca living in a trance-like state within Falconia, Griffith's capital, before a memory surge prompts an escape attempt. The continuation treats her primarily as a captive to be rescued — which cycles her back into the structural role she occupied for hundreds of post-Eclipse chapters, prior to Elfhelm. [CBR has documented how Guts himself is reduced in the continuation](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-guts-lowest-low-failure-manga/) — the man who dedicated decades to protecting Casca "failed to protect Casca" in Elfhelm, failed even to injure Griffith, and now contemplates death. This is framed as rock-bottom heroism, as necessary humiliation before a comeback. What neither the continuation nor most of its coverage asks is: what does Casca see, watching this man whose face is her trigger pursue the same vengeance that has already cost her everything?

[Screen Rant's reading](https://screenrant.com/berserk-return-eclipse-worst-scar-casca-mind-break/) frames the criticism as wanting Casca to "return to her former glory" — her combat capability, her agency. That is a version of the concern. It misses the specific nerve. The issue is not whether Casca gets to fight. The issue is whether the narrative acknowledges what Guts did to her as something she has the right to reckon with on her own terms — not as a complication of their romance, not as a wrinkle to be smoothed by his heroic actions elsewhere.

## What Miura Built

What is remarkable about the scream — in either chapter — is that it exists at all. A lesser version of this story resolves Casca's recovery with a reunion and a tearful embrace. The fact that Miura wrote it as a scream of terror suggests he understood exactly what he had done in Episode 190. He knew — [having acknowledged his discomfort with how he handled sexual violence in early Berserk](https://www.cbr.com/berserk-violence-women-guts-controversial/) — that letting Casca run back to Guts after Elfhelm would retroactively excuse Volume 23. He wrote the scream as an honest consequence, not a dramatic obstacle before the expected resolution.

[The manga's trajectory with Casca](https://medium.com/@berserkforever/women-of-causality-a-feminist-analysis-of-berserk-42c4ab726275) is, in this reading, more sophisticated than the Eclipse-centric criticism allows. Yes, the Eclipse used her as an instrument of Guts' suffering. But the subsequent four hundred chapters have been, in part, about what that usage costs her — and about the fact that the person designated as her protector created a separate wound that she is now required to survive independently. The Eclipse happened to her. Volume 23 happened to her. Both perpetrators are real. Only one of them is the protagonist.

That is the question the scream is asking. Whether the continuation — or the fandom — is willing to sit with it is another matter.
