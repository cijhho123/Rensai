For fifteen years, the internet had a consensus about Shinji Ikari: he should have just gotten in the robot. Not metaphorically — people meant it literally. They meant he should have stopped whimpering, stopped retreating into himself, stopped letting his psychology get in the way of doing the heroic thing. The frustration was so pervasive it became a meme, a shorthand for a certain kind of perceived cowardice in fiction. And Hideaki Anno, when he came back to Evangelion with the Rebuild tetralogy, had heard all of it.

*[Evangelion: 2.0 You Can (Not) Advance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelion:_2.0_You_Can_(Not)_Advance)* is his answer. The film hands you the Shinji you asked for — active, decisive, willing to fight for someone he loves — and then, in its final act, shows you exactly what that Shinji costs the world.

![Evangelion 2.0 key visual](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2e/Evangelion_2.0_poster.jpg)

## The Pivot

Anno has been direct about what changed between the original series and the Rebuild films. The production record for *2.0*, translated at [EvaResources](https://evaresources.wordpress.com/books/2-0-crc/2-0-crc-part-1/), quotes him explicitly: "from the second part onwards, I intended to abandon those elements fixated on the original series and proceed with the feeling that I was making an entirely new work." After witnessing the audience response to *1.11/Prelude* — the enthusiasm, the hunger — he scrapped the existing drafts and restarted with a three-day creative retreat.

What this means thematically is that *2.0* is the first Evangelion made from the other side of the depression that built the original. Multiple critical analyses have identified this shift. As one piece noted at [Anime News Network](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/feature/2016-02-19/the-evolution-of-evangelion-rebuild-vs-tv/.98581), if the original series represents the "inside" perspective of someone in the midst of depression, the Rebuild films come from the "outside" perspective of someone who has survived it. Anno married Moyoco Anno, found stability, and returned to Evangelion not as a man drowning but as one who had learned how to swim.

You can feel this in every frame of *2.0*. The film is warmer. The sun breaks through in a way it almost never did in the TV series. When Shinji makes Rei a bento and she accepts it — genuinely, warmly, like a person who has discovered that receiving kindness is possible — the scene plays with a lightness that the original NGE never permitted itself. The [Evangelion fandom wiki](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Evangelion:_2.0_You_Can_(Not)_Advance) describes the dinner party Rei organizes as an attempt to heal the fractured relationship between Shinji and his father. It's a gesture the original Rei, a blank avatar of longing, could not have conceived.

## The Promise in the Delivery

The entire middle section of *2.0* is built on a dramatic question: what if these characters were allowed to try? Rei tries to connect. Shinji tries to be present for his father. Asuka, rewritten from the TV series' one-dimensional tsundere into something more complicated and isolated, still reaches toward combat competence as her form of love for herself. The [Wonders in the Dark review](https://wondersinthedark.wordpress.com/2011/01/29/the-more-things-change-hideaki-annos-evangelion-2-0-you-can-not-advance/) notes that Anno "deepens characterization through selective changes" and points to a "new emphasis on the attempts by the three children to connect with one another that was absent in the original."

Even the action sequences carry this energy. When Asuka fights the Angel in Paris alone in the film's bravura opening set piece, the sequence establishes her competence without needing male backup — a deliberate structural shift from the TV series' ensemble battles. And when she volunteers for the Unit-03 test, she does it so that Rei's dinner party won't be cancelled. This version of Asuka — [documented at EvaWiki as meaningfully different from her original counterpart](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Asuka_Shikinami_Langley), with a changed surname, changed background, and notably *more* willingness to be emotionally vulnerable — still uses combat as her primary language. But the combatant is serving the dinner party. The heroism is in service of something domestic and connective. The film is threading courage through care in a way the original refused to do.

So when the climax arrives, the viewer has been primed. Shinji has been allowed to become someone you can root for. The dinner party scene, the bento, the moments of almost-normalcy — they all function as collateral. When Rei gets absorbed by the Tenth Angel, Shinji's refusal to abandon her isn't a lapse in judgment. It's the most human thing the film has shown him doing.

And it ends the world.

## The God-Mode Trap

The climax of *2.0* is the film's real argument, not its action centerpiece. When Shinji shouts "Give back Ayanami!" and Unit-01 reactivates on zero power — regrowing its severed arm from light, sprouting a halo, channeling something beyond its own mechanical limits — the film is fulfilling the fantasy of the audience who wanted a Shinji who *fights*. This version of Unit-01's awakening is notably distinct from the original TV series' berserk mode: as documented at [EvaWiki](https://wiki.evageeks.org/Berserk), the TV series depicted Unit-01 acting autonomously, outside Shinji's control, while in *2.0*, the Eva is under the pilot's will. This time, it's *him*. His determination. His love.

![Evangelion Unit-01 awakening, halo scene](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Rebuild_of_Evangelion_2.22_-_Unit-01_awakens.jpg/640px-Rebuild_of_Evangelion_2.22_-_Unit-01_awakens.jpg)

The [Near Third Impact sequence](https://evangelion.fandom.com/wiki/Near_Third_Impact) is spectacular precisely because it feels like victory — a cross-shaped energy wave, angelic imagery, Rei's giant form rising from the light. And then the horror sets in. The people across Tokyo-3 begin dissolving. The Anti-A.T.-Field expands. Shinji's emotional resolve, his love, his refusal to let go, is the direct mechanism for the near-extinction of humanity.

The EvaGeeks forum has debated this dynamic at length. One [thread specifically addressing Shinji's "improvement"](https://forum.evageeks.org/thread/9161/Shinji-20-an-improvement/) cuts to the point: "A less depressed Shinji isn't necessarily an improvement considering in 2.0 he speeds up the end of the world." The argument is that he took action and set his own goals and fought for what he believed in — and one of those goals was worth near-apocalypse.

What Anno built in the climax is a structural irony that only works because the film earned your sympathy for this Shinji. The TV series Shinji was easy to criticize because he retreated into himself. He was passive. The criticism was that he needed agency. Well: *2.0* gives him agency. His agency is the most destructive force in the film.

## Recovery vs. Resolution

The distinction matters. Anno making this film from a healthier psychological position does not mean the film argues that health is the solution. What it argues — and this is the sharper thesis — is that the same emotional core that made Shinji paralyzed in the original series is still catastrophic when it becomes active. The problem was never simply that he wouldn't act. The problem was the structure of his need.

Anno has been explicit about what that structure is. In a widely-cited interview, [he described Shinji as "a melancholic oral-dependent type"](https://www.evamonkey.com/writings/wong-amos-interview-with-hideaki-anno-director-of-neon-genesis-evangelion.php) — directly after acknowledging that the character reflected "my character, both the conscious and unconscious parts." The oral-dependent personality, in the Freudian framework that saturates the original series, is driven by unmet early needs for nurturing and validation. It treats other people as sources of reassurance rather than as independent beings. Love, for an oral-dependent, is fundamentally about filling the void — which is why Shinji's love for Rei in the climax of *2.0* cannot coexist with her consent, her safety, or the continued existence of Tokyo-3. He is not saving her. He is refusing to exist in a world where she is gone. The difference is everything.

[Wikipedia's breakdown of Shinji's psychological design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinji_Ikari) notes that the original series "Episode 24" is explicitly titled in reference to Freudian oral stages. Anno was not deploying this psychology as backdrop — it was the blueprint. And when Rebuild hands Shinji agency without resolving the blueprint, the result is the same structure operating at full force.

[Game Rant's retrospective on Rebuild](https://gamerant.com/rebuild-evangelion-movies-compared-to-original-anime/) frames the optimism precisely: "Rebuild allows characters to make their own mistakes, while the original series frames the world around them as completely harsh and unforgiving." That's right, but the mistake Shinji makes in *2.0* is not a small one. It's the mistake the audience was asking him to make for fifteen years. The film is saying: you wanted him to fight, to act, to prioritize the person he loves over his own paralyzing self-doubt. Here. Watch what that looks like.

The final image — Kaworu descending with the Lance of Longinus, having halted the Near Third Impact, telling Shinji "This time, I will show you happiness" — functions as both promise and warning. Every attempt to be happy in Evangelion leaves devastation behind it. The happiness that Kaworu is gesturing toward is real. But so is the scale of the wreckage that Shinji's love already generated to get here.

## What This Changes About the Original Debate

The fan consensus about NGE Shinji has always rested on a category error: treating passivity as the character's flaw, when the original series understood passivity as *symptom*. The wound that made Shinji unable to act was the same wound that, left unhealed, would make his action catastrophic. The original series explored the paralysis side. *2.0* explores the other side of the same knife. A [psychological analysis of Neon Genesis Evangelion at Anime Rants](https://animerants.net/2023/07/27/neon-genesis-evangelion-thematic-analysis/) makes this point directly: the show is "not about Shinji failing to be brave enough, but about whether connection itself is survivable."

What makes *2.0* such a precise piece of filmmaking is that Anno clearly loves this version of his characters. [The CBR analysis of Mari's role](https://www.cbr.com/evangelion-3-01-01-mari-illustrious-makinami-explained/) notes that the Rebuild films reflect "Anno's attempt to give his cast a second chance." He is not mocking the audience for wanting a more decisive Shinji. He is genuinely exploring what that Shinji becomes. The compassion in *2.0* is real. The dinner party is real. The warmth between Shinji and Rei is real. And the near-apocalypse that warmth generates is also real.

The [IMDB user reviews](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0860906/reviews/) and [Letterboxd discussions](https://letterboxd.com/film/evangelion-20-you-can-not-advance/) tend to converge on *2.0* as the "fun one" — the exciting middle chapter where things actually happen and Shinji stops being insufferable. That reading isn't wrong, exactly. The film is fun. The action sequences are extraordinary. But it's the kind of fun that, if you've been paying attention, should make you deeply uncomfortable by the final act.

## The Deeper Architecture

In retrospect, *2.0* is the cleverest structural move in the entire Rebuild tetralogy, more so than the existential rupture of *3.0* or the redemptive scope of *3.0+1.0*. It works precisely because it lets you have what you want. The [Midwest Film Journal review](https://midwestfilmjournal.com/2021/08/19/touching-a-nerv-evangelion-2-0-you-can-not-advance/) observes that the film released to wide acclaim in Japan, [grossing ¥4 billion at the Japanese box office](https://www.screendaily.com/-evangelion-rules-the-japanese-box-office/5002949.article) in 2009 and premiering at number one. The audience was satisfied. They got their heroic Shinji.

Anno knew that. He was counting on it.

The film's title — *You Can (Not) Advance* — takes on a different texture once you've seen the ending. The "you" is Shinji, but it's also the audience: you cannot advance to the resolution you want, the clean catharsis of watching love conquer all, without paying the price that love demands in this universe. The warmth that Anno genuinely brought to the Rebuild films from his own recovery is real and present in *2.0*. But that warmth does not change the fundamental architecture of Evangelion's tragedy. It just shows you a different entry point into the same collapse.

Kaworu will show Shinji happiness. And the cost of that happiness is everything that comes after.
